Orientation

What PowerPy is and the problem it solves

PowerPy is a Python framework that predicts how a satellite’s solar array will perform electrically and survive thermally across its mission — and what happens when cells fail — then writes the answer into engineering reports.

The thing being analysed

A satellite gets its power from a solar array: thousands of small solar cells glued onto flat panels and wired together. Each cell is a postage-stamp-sized semiconductor that turns sunlight into electrical current. On its own a single cell makes only about 2.7 volts and roughly one amp — useless for running a spacecraft. So cells are wired in long chains to add up their voltage, and many chains are placed side by side to add up their current, until the array delivers the hundreds of volts and many amps the satellite bus needs.

Two hard truths make this more than simple arithmetic:

  • Space is a brutal environment. In sunlight a cell bakes; in the shadow of the Earth (an eclipse) it freezes. High-energy particles slowly damage the silicon (radiation degradation), so the same array that delivers 100% of its power on day one (begin-of-life) might deliver only 80% fifteen years later (end-of-life). The design must still close the power budget at end-of-life, at the worst temperature, after the worst radiation dose.
  • Cells fail, and a failed cell gets dangerously hot. When one cell in a chain dies, the healthy cells behind it keep pushing current — but now there is nowhere for that current to flow except backwards through the dead cell, which turns it into a little heater. That hot-spot can climb past the temperature that melts the panel. Engineers add bypass diodes to limit the damage, and they must prove the worst realistic failure still stays below the melt limit.

In one sentence: PowerPy takes a description of an array (which cell, how it is wired, the mission’s temperatures and radiation, the panel’s materials) and produces the array’s current–voltage behaviour, each cell’s temperature, the worst-case hot-spot under failures, and a report a reviewer can read.

The job behind the job — closing the power budget

Why compute all this? Because a satellite must close its power budget: at every moment of the mission, the array must supply at least as much power as the spacecraft draws, with margin to spare. The hard moment is usually end-of-life, hot, just after the longest eclipse — when the array is degraded, running warm (so making less voltage), and the battery has just been drained. PowerPy exists to answer, quantitatively, “does the array still close the budget then, and does it survive the worst credible failure?” If the answer is no, the design grows — more cells, more panels — which costs mass and money, so the prediction must be accurate, not conservative-by-guesswork.

Margin, not just a number Engineers never design to exactly 100 %. They carry margin (say, the array makes 115 % of the required power at the worst case) to absorb modelling error, manufacturing spread and surprises. PowerPy’s job is to make the prediction trustworthy enough that the margin can be tight — every unnecessary percent of margin is wasted launch mass.

The shape of the framework — three layers

It helps to picture PowerPy as a pipeline with three stages. Data comes in on the left, gets turned into physics in the middle, and leaves as reports on the right.

Analogy — a wind-tunnel for solar panels Think of PowerPy as a numerical wind tunnel. You don’t fly the real satellite to see if the array survives; you build a faithful mathematical model and “fly” it through every temperature, every radiation dose, and every failure you can imagine — cheaply, repeatably, on a laptop — until you trust the design.

Why this is built the way it is

Two design choices shape everything downstream, and both come from the office reality the tool lives in:

  • It must run without installing anything. The engineering environment forbids pip install. So PowerPy is pure Python (numpy, scipy) and even the circuit simulator, ngspice, is vendored — copied into the source tree — rather than installed. More importantly, the entire default electrical model is analytic: it never needs ngspice at all (Part 14 explains the SPICE story).
  • It must be auditable. Every input is a typed, validated object; every output traces back to an input row. A reviewer can open the spreadsheet, the JSON, and the report and follow the chain. That is why the data model (Part 3) is so deliberate.

Foundations — the words you need first

Before the physics, here is the small vocabulary the rest of the handbook leans on. Skim it now; each term is also repeated where it is used, and collected in the glossary.

Solar cell The smallest unit — a semiconductor chip that converts light into electric current. PowerPy’s reference cell is a triple-junction 3G30LARS: short-circuit current ≈ 1.23 A, open-circuit voltage ≈ 2.68 V, area ≈ 70 cm².
I–V curve (current–voltage curve) The signature of a solar cell. It answers: “if I hold the cell at voltage V, how much current I does it deliver?” It is a falling curve — high current near zero volts, zero current at the open-circuit voltage. Every electrical question in PowerPy is ultimately a question about this curve.

Newton’s method & the Jacobian A way to solve equations you can’t rearrange by hand. Guess, measure how wrong you are, use the slope to make a better guess, repeat. When there are several unknowns at once, the “slope” becomes a small matrix of slopes called the Jacobian. This is where the “2×2 vector” comes from (Part 7).

The data model — how the inputs get in

Before any physics runs, PowerPy turns messy human inputs (a spreadsheet, some JSON files) into clean, typed, validated objects. This part is the “left edge” of Figure 1.1.

Two kinds of input

  • The workbook — a single Excel file (params.xlsx) that describes this array: which cell, how it is wired, the mission’s operating points, the loss factors, the radiation fluxes, and the report’s table of contents.
  • Reference files — JSON files shipped with the package describing the cell (its BOL points and degradation curves), the diode, and the substrate materials. These rarely change; the workbook points at them by name.

One function ties it together: load_report_data(params.xlsx, data_dir). It reads each sheet with a dedicated loader and returns one immutable ReportMetadata object holding nine sub-objects — cell, mission, losses, fluxes, layout, structure, document.

The two sheet conventions

Every sheet is one of two shapes, which keeps the loaders simple:

  • Key-value sheets (singletons like the document cover or the cell parameters): columns param | name | value | unit | type | source — one fact per row.
  • Long-format sheets (collections like losses or fluxes): one row per item, with identifier columns, a value, context columns, and an include flag so rows can be switched off without deleting them.
Design choice — strings, not enums A recent change replaced rigid enumerations (a fixed list of allowed words baked into the code) with plain strings that each workbook defines for itself — phase names like BOL_ATC or End_of_Life, launch configs like single/dual. Cross-sheet matching is done case-insensitively. Why? Because every project names its phases differently, and the tool should adapt to the spreadsheet rather than force the spreadsheet to adapt to the tool.
Analogy — the customs desk The loader layer is the customs desk of the framework. Nothing enters the physics engine until its passport is checked: numbers are really numbers, fractions are in [0, 1], keys are unique, file references resolve. Once stamped (validated and frozen), an object is trusted everywhere downstream and can never be quietly mutated.

Deep dive ① · the electrical heart

The single-diode equation

Everything electrical in PowerPy starts here: a compact equation that reproduces a solar cell’s entire I–V curve from just four datasheet numbers. This part builds it from scratch, names every symbol, gives an analogy, shows why it needs Newton’s method, and works the real numbers.

What a solar cell looks like to electricity

Hold a solar cell in the dark and it behaves like an ordinary diode — a component that lets current flow easily one way and blocks it the other. Shine light on it and something is added: the light knocks electrons loose and drives a steady current. So a lit cell is modelled as a current source and a diode wired together, plus two resistors that account for real-world imperfections. This picture is the single-diode model (“single” because it uses one diode; more elaborate models use two).

The equation

Writing down “current in = current out” at the terminals of Figure 4.1 gives the single-diode equation. This is exactly the relation coded in DiodeParams:

I = Iph − I0 · ( e(V + I·Rs)/Vt − 1 ) − (V + I·Rs) / Rsh terminal current = light current − diode current − leakage current

I0Diode saturation current — a tiny number setting how hard the diode “leaks back”.A
VtThermal voltage of the diode, n·kT/q — the voltage scale of the exponential.V

Analogy — a pump, a relief valve and leaks Picture water, not electrons. Iph is a pump pushing a fixed flow (the sunlight). The diode is a one-way relief valve: as you raise the back-pressure (the voltage V), the valve cracks open and dumps more and more of the pumped flow back internally — and because a diode is exponential, once you near Voc it dumps almost everything, which is why the current collapses to zero there. Rs is friction in the output pipe; Rsh is a small permanent leak. The current you actually collect at the tap is the pump flow minus what the valve dumps minus the leak — exactly the three terms of the equation.

Why we can’t just “plug in V and read off I”

Look closely: the unknown I appears on both sides — once on the left, and again inside the exponential as the I·Rs drop. You cannot algebraically isolate I; the equation is implicit. This is the whole reason a numerical method is needed. PowerPy uses Newton’s method: for each voltage on the sweep, guess I, see how badly the equation fails to balance, and use the slope to correct the guess — repeating until it balances.

In the code single_diode_iv() does this for the whole voltage sweep at once (a vectorised Newton step over a numpy array of voltages). The residual f is the equation’s imbalance; df is its slope dI; the update is I ← I − f/df. For physical parameters it converges in a handful of steps — in fact, just 2 iterations for this cell.

Where the four parameters come from — the closed-form fit

A datasheet does not give you I0, Vt, Rs. It gives the four operating points: Isc, Imp, Vmp, Voc. PowerPy’s fit_rseries() inverts the model algebraically (taking the shunt as open) to recover the hidden parameters from those four numbers:

Vt = Vmp(Isc−Imp) / Imp   ·   I0 = Isc / ( eVoc/Vt − 1 )   ·   Rs = (Vt/Imp)·ln((Isc−Imp)/I0) − Vmp/Imp three hidden parameters, recovered in closed form from four datasheet points
Worked example — fitting the real 3G30LARS cell

The shipped cell file gives, at begin-of-life: Isc = 1.2311 A, Imp = 1.18425 A, Vmp = 2.375 V, Voc = 2.679 V.

  1. Thermal voltage. Vt = 2.375·(1.2311 − 1.18425)/1.18425 = 2.375·0.04685/1.18425 = 0.09396 V. (Reasonable — a triple-junction cell’s effective Vt is around 0.05–0.25 V.)
  2. Saturation current. The exponent Voc/Vt = 2.679/0.09396 = 28.51. So I0 = 1.2311/(e²⁸·⁵¹ − 1) = 1.2311/2.4×10¹² ≈ 5.1×10⁻¹³ A — a tiny back-leakage, as expected for a good cell.
  3. Series resistance. Rs = (0.09396/1.18425)·ln((0.04685)/(5.1×10⁻¹³)) − 2.375/1.18425. The log term is ln(9.2×10¹⁰) ≈ 25.2, so Rs ≈ 0.0793·25.2 − 2.005 = 1.999 − 2.005 ≈ 0 Ω (the code clamps small negatives to 0).
  4. Check. Feeding these into single_diode_iv() and reading the curve back gives Isc = 1.231 A and Voc = 2.679 V — the very points we started from — and a peak power of 2.809 W at the MPP. ✓ the fit reproduces the datasheet.

From “a cell” to “this cell, now” — environment corrections

The fit above is the BOL cell at its reference temperature. Real cells run hot or cold, and age. operating_points() scales the four datasheet numbers before fitting, multiplying by three gains:

  • Temperature. A factor 1 + (coeff/100)·ΔT, where ΔT is the cell’s temperature above reference. Current rises slightly with heat (+0.043 %/°C here); voltage falls (−0.0062 %/°C). This is why a hot cell makes less useful power.
  • Radiation. A remaining factor ≤ 1 read off the degradation curve at the accumulated dose — interpolated in log-fluence. More dose, lower factor.
  • Losses. A pre-multiplied product of mission loss factors (coverglass, assembly, etc.) on the current and voltage axes.

The current axis (Isc, Imp) gets the current gains; the voltage axis (Voc, Vmp) gets the voltage gains. Then fit_rseries() runs on the corrected points, and the I–V curve reflects this cell at this age and temperature. That single curve is the building block for the entire array.

Why this cell makes ~2.7 V — triple-junction cells

An ordinary silicon cell makes only ~0.6 V because one semiconductor can harvest only one band of the solar spectrum. Space cells are triple-junction: three different semiconductor layers stacked on top of each other, each tuned to a different colour band (roughly: a top layer for blue/UV, a middle for green/yellow, a bottom for red/infrared). Because the three junctions are in series within the single cell, their voltages add — giving the ~2.68 V open-circuit you see for the 3G30LARS. This is also why these cells reach ~30 % efficiency versus ~18 % for silicon: less of the spectrum is wasted.

The dark curve Turn off the light (Iph → 0) and the same equation describes the cell in the dark: it becomes a plain diode. PowerPy keeps a dark-curve path because a failed, reverse-biased cell behaves like a (leaky) diode rather than a generator — exactly the regime that sets a hot-spot’s reverse power (Part 11).
Worked example — current at a chosen voltage

Once the curve exists, reading off the current at any voltage is just interpolation. For the fitted BOL cell:

  1. At V = 1.0 V (well below the knee): I = 1.2311 A — essentially the full Isc; the diode is barely conducting, so almost all the photo-current reaches the terminals.
  2. At V = 2.0 V: I = 1.2302 A — still nearly flat. P = V·I = 2.46 W.
  3. At V = Vmp = 2.375 V: I = 1.183 A, P = 2.81 W — the peak.
  4. At V = 2.6 V (past the MPP, near Voc): I has collapsed to 0.70 A, P = 1.82 W. ✓ the exponential “relief valve” is now dumping most of the current internally — the curve’s steep right-hand cliff.

This collapse near Voc is the whole reason arrays are operated at the MPP, not at higher voltage: a little past the knee and the power falls off a cliff.

The physics

Building the array — series & parallel combination

One cell’s curve is the atom. An array’s curve is built by joining many curves with exactly two rules, applied over and over up the hierarchy.

The two rules

  • Series (a string of cells): they share one current; their voltages add. Stack them and the curve gets taller (more volts) at the same current.
  • Parallel (strings side by side): they share one voltage; their currents add. The curve gets wider (more amps) at the same voltage.
Analogy — people carrying water up a hill Series is a bucket brigade up a staircase: the same bucket (current) passes hand to hand, and each person adds a step of height (voltage). Parallel is several brigades climbing side by side to the same height: each adds its own bucket flow (current) at that same height. Series adds height; parallel adds throughput.

Both rules live once, in combine.py. To add curves in series you flip each to V(I), pick a shared current grid, and sum the voltages. To add in parallel you keep V, pick a shared voltage grid, and sum the currents. The composite levels — string, section, panel, array — are then just a few lines each, calling these two functions.

The weakest-link rule

A subtlety the code handles: when you put cells in series, the chain can only pass as much current as its weakest cell — so the combined curve is limited by the smallest Isc among the children. In parallel, the stack only reaches as high in voltage as its weakest member — limited by the smallest Voc. (In the code: isc_min = min(...) for series, voc_min = min(...) for parallel.)

Why this matters for failures The weakest-link rule is the seed of the hot-spot problem. A shaded or failed cell has a tiny Isc; in series it throttles the whole string, and the surplus current from the healthy cells has to go somewhere — backwards, into the weak cell. Part 11 picks this up.
Worked example — two cells in series, two in parallel

Take our cell (Isc = 1.23 A, Voc = 2.68 V, Pmax = 2.81 W) as the unit.

  1. Two in series. Same current, voltages add: the pair still tops out at Isc = 1.23 A but now reaches Voc = 5.36 V. Peak power doubles to ≈ 5.62 W. A string of 22 such cells would reach ≈ 59 V.
  2. Two in parallel. Same voltage, currents add: Voc stays 2.68 V but Isc doubles to 2.46 A. Peak power again ≈ 5.62 W — the same total power, delivered as more amps at lower volts.
  3. The mismatch case. Put a strong cell (1.23 A) in series with a weak one (0.6 A). The series pair can pass only 0.6 A — the weakest link — even though one cell could give twice that. The “lost” current is what gets forced into reverse bias in a real fault. ✓ series → add volts; parallel → add amps; the weakest child caps the chain.

The composition tree

PowerPy mirrors the wiring as a tree of nodes that all share one interface (SimNode): a leaf CellModel produces a curve; a String combines its cells in series; a Section combines strings in parallel; a Panel and the Array continue up. Ask the top of the tree for its curve and the request flows down to the leaves and the curves combine back up — the same shape as the real hardware.

The physics

The space environment — sunlight, albedo, IR, orbit

A cell’s curve and its temperature both depend on its surroundings. This part is the heat-and-light bookkeeping that feeds the thermal solver in Part 7.

The three heat inputs

  • Direct sunlight — the dominant input, about 1361 W/m² at Earth’s distance (the solar constant). It scales with inverse-square distance from the Sun and with the cosine of the panel’s tilt to the Sun.
  • Albedo — sunlight bounced off the planet below. A fraction (the albedo, ≈ 0.3 for Earth) of the solar input, hitting the rear/underside.
  • Planetary infrared (IR) — the warm planet glows in the infrared; the panel absorbs some of that too.
Analogy — sunbathing near a wall Stand in the sun by a pale wall on a warm day. The Sun on your face is the direct flux. The glare bouncing off the wall is albedo. The heat radiating from the warm wall itself is planetary IR. All three warm you; the thermal model adds up exactly these three, scaled by how absorptive each face is.

Orbit and eclipse

Over an orbit these fluxes change: the panel tilts relative to the Sun, and for part of each orbit the satellite passes into the planet’s shadow — an eclipse — where the direct flux drops to zero and the panel rapidly cools. PowerPy carries a dependency-free Keplerian orbit model that produces the orbital period, the Sun direction, the beta angle (how tilted the orbit plane is to the Sun), and the eclipse fraction, and turns these into a flux timeline — a list of (time, sun, albedo, IR, tilt) points. That timeline is exactly what the transient solver (Part 10) marches through to watch the panel heat and cool.

Two fidelities The built-in analytic circular-orbit model needs no external libraries and covers the common case. A higher-fidelity path (via the optional hapsira orbit library) is sketched in the code but intentionally left as a seam — the analytic baseline is what runs by default.

The flux equations

The direct solar flux reaching the panel is the solar constant scaled two ways:

Worked example — how much sun, and how much tilt costs you
  1. Distance. At Earth (1 AU) the flux is the full 1361 W/m². At Mars (1.524 AU) it falls as 1/r²: 1361/1.524² = 586 W/m² — already less than half. At Jupiter (5.2 AU): just 50 W/m², which is why deep-space missions need huge arrays or nuclear power.
  2. Tilt. Facing the Sun squarely (θ = 0) gives cos 0 = 1.000 — full flux. At θ = 30°, cos = 0.866 (a 13 % loss). At θ = 60°, cos = 0.500 — half the sunlight, just from the angle. ✓ this cosine factor is the tilt multiplier on qF in the thermal balance (Part 7).

Eclipse and the beta angle

How much of each orbit is sunlit depends on the beta angle β — the angle between the orbit plane and the Sun line. At high β the satellite skims the edge of the shadow and may be in continuous sunlight; at low β it plunges through the planet’s shadow each orbit for a long eclipse. The orbit model computes the eclipse fraction from β and the orbit geometry, and the flux timeline drops psun to zero during eclipse — which is what makes the transient solver (Part 10) show the panel cooling sharply, then re-heating at sunrise.

Deep dive ② · the thermal heart

The 2-node thermal model & the 2×2 vector

This is the part you specifically asked about: why the 2×2 vector? The short answer is that each cell has two unknown temperatures (front and rear), and solving for both at once means inverting a 2×2 matrix at every Newton step. Here is the long answer, built slowly.

Why temperature is a whole separate problem

A solar cell in space has no air to carry heat away — it can only shed heat by radiating it to cold space. It also absorbs heat from the Sun and the planet (Part 6), and it generates or dissipates electrical power. Its temperature settles where all of these balance. That settling temperature feeds straight back into the I–V model (Part 4), which is why thermal and electrical can’t be fully separated (Part 9).

Why two nodes?

A mounted cell is a sandwich: a sunlit front face and a rear face, with the substrate in between. The two faces are not at the same temperature — the front sees the Sun, the rear sees the planet — but they are linked by heat conducting through the thickness. So PowerPy models each cell with two temperature nodes: T₁ (front) and T₂ (rear). Two unknowns per cell.

The two balance equations

For each node we write “net heat = 0” at steady state. These are f1 and f2 in solve_thermal():

f₁ = qF − εF·A·σ·(T₁⁴ − Tsp⁴) − Pelec + C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0
f₂ = qR − εR·A·σ·(T₂⁴ − Tsp⁴) − C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0 front balance (top) and rear balance (bottom); each is heat-in minus heat-out

The key nonlinearity Radiated heat goes as T⁴ (the Stefan–Boltzmann law: a hot body radiates as the fourth power of its absolute temperature). That T⁴ is what makes these equations nonlinear — you cannot rearrange them to “T = …”. Hence, again, Newton’s method.

From two equations to a 2×2 matrix — what a Jacobian is

Newton’s method for one unknown says: new guess = old guess − f / f′, where f′ is the slope. With two unknowns (T₁, T₂) and two equations (f₁, f₂), the single slope becomes a 2×2 table of slopes — the Jacobian J. Each entry is “how much does this equation change if I nudge this unknown”:

J =   ⎡ ∂f₁/∂T₁   ∂f₁/∂T₂ ⎤  =  ⎡ a   b ⎤
       ⎣ ∂f₂/∂T₁   ∂f₂/∂T₂ ⎦      ⎣ c   d ⎦ the 2×2 “vector” you asked about: the matrix of how each balance reacts to each temperature

Analogy — two tangled dials Imagine a machine with two dials (T₁, T₂) and two gauges (f₁, f₂) that must both read zero. The dials are tangled: turning the front dial moves both gauges, because the faces share heat. The Jacobian is the little 2×2 “turning the front dial by 1 moves gauge-1 by a and gauge-2 by c; turning the rear dial moves them by b and d” cheat-sheet. Knowing that cheat-sheet, you can compute exactly how far to turn both dials in one move to drive both gauges toward zero — that’s one Newton step.
Worked example — one sunlit cell, cold start

Take a single cell radiating to space under full sun. Start the solver at a deliberately wrong −120 °C and watch the energy-balance residual |f₁|+|f₂| at each Newton step (Figure 7.2 plots exactly this run):

  1. Step 1: huge imbalance — the guess is far off, the T⁴ term is wildly wrong.
  2. Steps 2–6: the residual falls fast as Newton uses the 2×2 slope to leap toward balance.
  3. Steps 7–10: it drops to ~10⁻¹⁴ W — machine-zero — and the loop stops. ✓ converged to a front temperature of 3.59 °C for this case.

The takeaway: even from an absurd start, the 2×2 Newton step finds the balance in about ten iterations, because each step uses the true local slopes.

Worked example — one Newton step, every number shown

Let’s do a single step by hand for one cell, so the 2×2 is concrete. Take area A = 0.0016 m² (a 4 cm tile), front absorptivity αF = 0.91, emissivities εF = εR = 0.83, conduction C·A = 1000·0.0016 = 1.600 W/K, full sun psun = 1361 W/m² and a healthy cell extracting Pelec = 1.1 W. Absorbed front flux qF = αF·A·psun = 0.91·0.0016·1361 = 1.982 W. Start both nodes at 28 °C = 301.15 K.

  1. Residuals (how unbalanced are we?). Plugging into f₁ and f₂: f₁ = +0.2623 W, f₂ = −0.6194 W. Not zero — the guess is wrong.
  2. Jacobian entries. a = −4·εF·A·σ·T₁³ − C·A = −1.6082; d = −1.6082 (same form); b = c = +C·A = +1.6000. So J = ⎡−1.6082, 1.6000; 1.6000, −1.6082⎤.
  3. Determinant. det = a·d − b·c = (−1.6082)(−1.6082) − (1.6)(1.6) = 2.5863 − 2.5600 = 0.02639. (Small, because the two rows are nearly equal — the faces are tightly conduction-linked.)
  4. Corrections (Cramer’s rule). ΔT₁ = −(d·f₁ − b·f₂)/det = −((−1.6082)(0.2623) − (1.6)(−0.6194))/0.02639 = −21.57 K; ΔT₂ = −21.84 K. Both faces are too hot, so Newton cools them.
  5. Update & check. New T₁ = 301.15 − 21.57 = 279.58 K (6.4 °C). One more step brings the residual to ~0.018 W, a third to ~0.0003 W, settling at a front temperature of 3.59 °C. ✓ three steps from a 28 °C start.

That is the entire thermal solver for one cell: build f, build the four Jacobian numbers, invert the 2×2 by hand, step, repeat. The array version does this for every cell at once.

Why “vector” — solving the whole array at once

An array has thousands of cells. Crucially, in the simplest case the cells are independent: each one’s two equations involve only its own T₁ and T₂. So the giant system is block-diagonal — a stack of independent 2×2 blocks, one per cell. PowerPy never loops over cells in Python; it stores T₁ and T₂ as numpy arrays and computes a, b, c, d, the determinant, and the updates as array operations — every cell’s 2×2 solved simultaneously in one vectorised pass. This is the “vectorised 2×2” in the docstrings: the 2×2 is the per-cell physics; the vectorisation is doing all of them at once for speed.

Where c_cond comes from The conduction coefficient C is the substrate’s c_cond = conductivity / thickness (W/m²K) — derived once in Substrate.c_cond. A thinner, more conductive board ties the two faces together more tightly; an infinitely insulating board (C → 0) would let front and rear settle completely independently.

Deep dive ③ · heat that spreads

Lateral conduction & the graph Laplacian

So far each cell was thermally an island. In reality the panel’s metal facesheet carries heat sideways from a hot cell to its cooler neighbours. Turning that on is what the g_lat switch does — and it changes the mathematics from “many independent 2×2 blocks” to “one big coupled system”, organised by a structure called the graph Laplacian.

The concept — heat leaks to the neighbours

Picture the panel as a grid of tiles. Each tile sits on a continuous metal skin. If one tile runs hot (say a failed cell dissipating power), heat doesn’t just radiate away — it also flows through the skin into the four tiles touching it (up, down, left, right). Those warm up a little; their neighbours warm a little less; and so on. The hot-spot’s peak drops because the heat is shared, but a patch of neighbours gets warmer.

Analogy — a rumour through a crowd A failed cell is a person who suddenly shouts (a burst of heat). With no lateral conduction, only that person hears it — a sharp, isolated spike. With lateral conduction, they tell their four neighbours, who tell theirs: the “news” spreads outward, getting quieter with distance. The total noise is the same, but it’s smeared over a neighbourhood instead of concentrated in one spot. g_lat sets how loudly each person passes it on.

How strong is the sideways flow? — the geometry

lateral_conductance() turns panel geometry into a single number glat (W/K) for the heat shared between two adjacent tiles:

glat = kfacesheet · (edge_width · thickness) / pitch conductivity × cross-section of the heat path ÷ distance between tile centres

This is just the standard “conduction = conductivity × area ÷ length” applied to the thin strip of facesheet bridging two tiles. A thicker, more conductive facesheet → larger glat → more spreading. (For a typical aluminium facesheet this comes out around 0.045 W/K.) When glat = 0 the cells are islands again, so the lateral model is a strict superset of the independent model.

Now the cells are coupled — enter the graph

Once heat flows between neighbours, a tile’s balance equation contains its neighbours’ temperatures. The neat block-diagonal structure of Part 7 is gone; everything is tied together. To organise “who is next to whom”, PowerPy builds the panel’s adjacency: from the grid layout, neighbours() lists every touching pair (each tile linked to the one on its right and the one below — that covers all 4-neighbour bonds without duplication).

What the graph Laplacian does

The graph Laplacian L is a matrix built from the adjacency: L = D − A, where A marks which tiles touch and D counts each tile’s neighbours on the diagonal. Its magic is what it does when multiplied by the temperature vector T: the i-th entry of L·T equals the sum over neighbours of (Tᵢ − Tneighbour) — precisely “how much hotter am I than the tiles around me”. Multiply by glat and you have each tile’s net sideways heat loss. So the whole neighbour-by-neighbour bookkeeping collapses into a single matrix–vector product g_lat · L·T — no Python loop over pairs.

Worked example — the Laplacian on three tiles in a row

Take the simplest panel: three tiles in a line, 0–1–2 (so 0 touches 1, and 1 touches 2).

  1. Adjacency A (1 where tiles touch): row 0 = [0,1,0], row 1 = [1,0,1], row 2 = [0,1,0].
  2. Degrees D (neighbours each): tile 0 has 1, tile 1 has 2, tile 2 has 1 → diagonal [1,2,1].
  3. Laplacian L = D − A = ⎡1,−1,0; −1,2,−1; 0,−1,1⎤.
  4. Apply it to a temperature vector. Suppose tile 0 is hot: T = [100, 50, 50] °C. Then L·T = [1·100−1·50, −100+2·50−50, −50+50] = [+50, −50, 0].
  5. Read the meaning. Tile 0’s entry is +50 → it is hotter than its neighbour, so it loses heat sideways. Tile 1’s entry is −50 → it gains. Tile 2’s entry is 0 → it’s in balance with its neighbour. Multiply the whole thing by glat and you have the exact sideways watts each tile exchanges. ✓ one matrix multiply replaces a pair-by-pair loop.
In the code solve_thermal() builds the adjacency and Laplacian once (it never changes), as a sparse matrix (mostly zeros — a tile touches only ~4 of thousands). Each Newton step it assembles a (2N × 2N) Jacobian = a constant part (conduction + glat·L) plus the only changing piece, the radiative diagonal −4εAσT³, and solves it with a sparse linear solver. Same Newton idea as Part 7, just one big coupled system instead of N tiny ones.

The payoff — and the worst-case it reveals

Lateral conduction is not just realism for its own sake — it changes which failures are dangerous. Because neighbouring hot cells reinforce one another, the worst case is no longer a single failed cell but a cluster of adjacent failures whose heat piles up faster than it can spread away. That insight drives the worst-case search in Part 12.

A subtlety Whether spreading helps depends on the question. For a lone failure it dramatically lowers the peak (good). But it also means a tight group of failures can create a broad, stubborn hot region that a single-cell analysis would completely miss — which is exactly why PowerPy models it.

Electro-thermal coupling — the chicken-and-egg loop

The electrical model needs the temperature; the thermal model needs the electrical power. Neither can go first. This part is how PowerPy breaks the circular dependency.

The circularity

From Part 4, a cell’s I–V curve — and therefore the power it makes or dissipates — depends on its temperature. From Part 7, a cell’s temperature depends on the electrical power leaving it (Pelec appears in the front balance). So:

  • To find the power, I need the temperature.
  • To find the temperature, I need the power.
Analogy — adjusting two shower taps It’s like finding the comfortable setting of a shower with separate, interacting hot and cold taps. You can’t compute the perfect setting in one shot; you nudge one, feel the result, nudge the other, and converge. Crucially, you make small nudges — yank a tap and you overshoot into scalding then freezing forever. PowerPy makes small, damped nudges too.

The damped fixed-point loop

couple() hunts for the self-consistent (temperature, power) pair by iterating:

  1. Guess a temperature field T.
  2. Ask power_fn(T) for the per-cell electrical power at that T (in production this builds the circuit, runs it, reads back V·I per cell).
  3. Run the thermal solve (Part 7) with that power to get a new temperature Tnew.
  4. Step only part way toward it: T ← T + ω·(Tnew − T), with ω ≈ 0.5.
  5. Repeat until the step is tiny (converged).
Why the ω (under-relaxation) ω is the damping factor in (0, 1]. Taking the full step (ω = 1) can oscillate or diverge when the two physics push hard against each other; halving each step (ω = 0.5) trades a few extra iterations for reliable convergence. It is the mathematical version of “small nudges.”

The loop is written so the electrical solve is injected as power_fn. That keeps the coupling logic independent of how the power is obtained — a synthetic function for tests, or the real ngspice read-back in production (Part 14). The loop itself is pure, standalone, and tested.

Worked example — why damping converges and full steps oscillate

Imagine the true self-consistent temperature is 70 °C, and suppose (for illustration) that each thermal solve “overshoots”: if you feed it the power at temperature T, it returns a new temperature that lands on the far side of 70 °C by about the same distance.

  1. Full step (ω = 1). Start at 60 → solve returns 80 → returns 60 → returns 80… it oscillates forever around 70 and never settles.
  2. Damped step (ω = 0.5). Start at 60; the solve suggests 80, but you move only halfway: 60 + 0.5·(80−60) = 70 region. Next round suggests ~72, you move half: ~71. Then ~70.5, ~70.2… each move is smaller and the sequence closes in on 70.
  3. Stop. When a whole round moves the temperature by less than the tolerance (1e-3 °C), the loop declares convergence and does one final clean thermal solve at that power. ✓ damping trades a few extra rounds for guaranteed settling.

Transient thermal — watching a hot-spot climb in time

The steady solver finds where temperature ends up. The transient solver shows the journey: how fast a hot-spot climbs after a failure, or how the panel cools entering eclipse.

Adding thermal mass

Temperature doesn’t jump instantly because materials store heat. Add a heat capacity C (J/K) to each node and the steady balance becomes a differential equation:

C · dT/dt = (heat in) − (heat out) = f(T, t) at steady state dT/dt → 0, recovering f = 0 — the Part 7 problem

So the transient model contains the steady model as its long-time limit. That is a built-in sanity check: let time run long enough and the transient answer must equal the steady answer (the code has a parity test pinning exactly this).

Analogy — a bathtub filling Heat capacity is the size of the bathtub. Heat-in is the tap, heat-out is the drain. The water level (temperature) rises until inflow equals outflow. A bigger tub (more mass) fills more slowly — which is why a chunky aluminium-backed cell heats up gradually rather than snapping to its final temperature.

Why backward (implicit) Euler

To march forward in time you must turn dT/dt into steps of size dt. The naive “forward” way (estimate the rate now, step) is unstable for this kind of stiff radiative system — small time-steps blow up. PowerPy uses backward (implicit) Euler, which is unconditionally stable: you can take large steps without blow-up. The price is that each step must solve a nonlinear equation (the new temperature appears on both sides) — and that is, once again, a 2×2 Newton solve per cell, with the same Jacobian as Part 7 plus a C/dt term on the diagonal.

Worked example — how slowly does a cell heat up?

The “speed” of the transient is the thermal time constant τ = C / G, where C is the node’s heat capacity and G is its effective heat-loss conductance (W/K). It is the time to cover ~63 % of the gap to the final temperature.

  1. Heat capacity. An aluminium-backed cell, ρ ≈ 2700 kg/m³, cₚ ≈ 900 J/kgK, 0.8 mm thick, area 0.0016 m²: C = ρ·cₚ·thickness·A = 2700·900·0.0008·0.0016 ≈ 3.1 J/K.
  2. Loss conductance. Near 300 K the radiative loss slope is ~4εAσT³ ≈ 4·0.83·0.0016·5.67×10⁻⁸·300³ ≈ 0.008 W/K per face.
  3. Time constant. τ ≈ C/G ≈ 3.1/0.016 ≈ 190 s — a few minutes. That matches Figure 10.1, where the failed cell takes minutes (not seconds) to climb to its steady peak. ✓ thermal mass sets the pace; radiation sets the destination.

This is why the “2×2” keeps reappearing: it is the per-cell, two-node physics, and it shows up in the steady solve, inside each lateral Newton step, and inside each transient time-step.

Bypass diodes & hot-spots — the failure physics

Now we use the machinery. This part explains why a failed cell gets hot, and how a bypass diode clamps that heat — turning “how close must the diodes be?” into a number.

Why a failed cell becomes a heater

Recall the weakest-link rule (Part 5): cells in series share one current. If one cell fails or is shaded, it can no longer pass the string’s current. But the other healthy cells in the string are still illuminated and still want to push current. With nowhere else to go, that current is forced backwards through the failed cell, driving it into reverse bias — a large negative voltage. Power is voltage × current, and a reverse-biased cell absorbs this power as heat: P = V·I with V negative means heat into the cell. That is the hot-spot, and it feeds the thermal solver as a negative Pelec.

The bypass diode as a relief valve

A bypass diode is wired across a group of series cells. While they generate, it is reverse-biased and off. The moment a cell in the group blocks the current, the rest of the string drives the group into reverse and the diode turns on, carrying the current at its small forward drop and clamping the voltage. That caps how negative the failed cell’s voltage can swing — and therefore caps its reverse power, and therefore its temperature.

|Vfailed| ≤ Vforward + (cells_per_diode − 1) · Vcell the clamp: a failed cell can’t be pushed more negative than the diode drop plus its group-mates’ forward drops

With no diode (or one diode spanning a whole long string), the failed cell sees nearly the entire live stack — perhaps −(N−1)·Vcell ≈ −20 V — and dissipates a large reverse power. The fewer cells a single diode protects, the smaller that worst-case reverse voltage, and the cooler the hot-spot. spacing_scan() tabulates this trade directly.

Analogy — pressure-relief on a pipe The healthy cells are a pump that keeps pushing flow. A blockage (failed cell) makes pressure spike right at the blockage. A bypass diode is a relief valve just upstream: it opens and routes the flow around the blockage, capping the pressure (voltage) the blocked section ever sees. More relief valves, spaced closer, mean any single blockage sees less pressure — and runs cooler.
Worked example — the clamp in numbers

String of 22 cells, Vcell ≈ 2.4 V, fault current I ≈ 1.18 A, silicon bypass diode Vforward = 0.7 V.

  1. Unprotected. The failed cell faces the other 21 live cells: |V| = 21·2.4 = 50.4 V. Reverse power = 50.4·1.18 = 59.5 W dumped into one cell — catastrophic (this is the orange bar in Figure 11.1).
  2. One diode per 4 cells. The clamp caps it at |V| = Vforward + (4−1)·Vcell = 0.7 + 3·2.4 = 7.9 V. Reverse power = 7.9·1.18 = 9.3 W.
  3. The win. Tighter spacing cut the worst-case heat from 59.5 W to 9.3 W — a factor of ~6 — which is the difference between melting the panel and surviving. ✓ “how close must the diodes be?” is now a number you can design to.

Failure analysis — Monte-Carlo & worst-case search

A panel has many cells; failures can land anywhere, in any combination. PowerPy offers three complementary ways to ask “how bad can it get?”, all built on the lateral thermal solver of Part 8.

The common engine

Every study works the same way underneath: take a pattern (a set of failed cell indices), build a per-tile power map where healthy cells extract ~1.1 W and failed cells dissipate a reverse power (~−9.6 W), solve the panel thermally with lateral conduction, and record the peak temperature and how many tiles exceed the melt limit. The studies differ only in which patterns they try.

① Position sweep

Fail each single cell in turn — “which one lonely failure is worst?” Cheap, and a good first map of the panel’s sensitive spots.

② Worst-case greedy search

Because lateral conduction makes adjacent failures reinforce each other (Part 8), the truly dangerous case is a cluster. worst_case_search() hunts for it greedily: start with no failures; at each step, try adding each remaining cell and keep whichever single addition pushes the peak temperature highest; repeat up to a budget of failures. It deliberately builds the nastiest cluster — the one random sampling usually misses.

Analogy — building the worst possible bonfire You have a budget of logs (failures) and want the hottest fire. Greedy search places one log, then asks “where does the next log make the fire hottest?” — always next to the existing flames, never scattered. Step by step it assembles the worst cluster, because heat from adjacent failures stacks up.

③ Auto-stopping Monte-Carlo

For a realistic average picture, auto_monte_carlo() draws random failure patterns (each cell fails with some probability) and tracks the mean peak temperature. It is self-stopping: after each batch it computes the standard error of the mean and quits once the estimate is tight enough — rather than running a fixed, arbitrary number of trials.

Why standard error, not a fixed count The uncertainty in an average shrinks like 1/√N — so the first hundred samples buy you a lot, and the ten-thousandth buys almost nothing. Stopping when the standard error falls below a target (e.g. 2 °C) spends exactly as much computation as the required confidence needs, no more. The runs are independent, so they parallelise across threads (numpy releases the GIL during the solve).
Worked example — when does the sweep stop?

The standard error of the mean is SE = s/√N, where s is the spread (standard deviation) of the peak temperatures and N is the number of runs.

  1. Suppose the peaks have spread s ≈ 15 °C. After N = 200 runs: SE = 15/√200 = 15/14.1 = 1.06 °C. If the target is 2 °C, we have already stopped — 200 runs was enough.
  2. Want the estimate twice as tight (SE = 0.53 °C)? Because SE ∝ 1/√N, halving it needs four times the runs — 800. Tightening confidence gets expensive fast.
  3. This is why a fixed “run 10 000 trials” is wasteful: you might hit the target at 200, or need 800, depending on the spread. ✓ the auto-stop measures, then decides.
Position vs. worst-case vs. Monte-Carlo — which to use Use the position sweep to map sensitive spots, the worst-case search to certify the absolute worst cluster against the melt limit (the safety question), and Monte-Carlo to estimate the typical/expected behaviour and the probability of exceeding the limit (the reliability question). They answer different questions and are meant to be used together.

PowerPy can, in principle, run a real circuit simulator for the electrical solve. Understanding when it does — and why it usually doesn’t need to — explains a lot about the framework’s structure.

Current state (honest status) The analytic path is production-ready. Appendix · the numerics behind the deep dives

Numerical methods primer — Newton from one unknown to many

The single-diode solve (Part 4), the 2-node thermal solve (Part 7), the lateral solve (Part 8) and every transient step (Part 10) are all the same idea — Newton’s method — at different sizes. This appendix builds that idea from one unknown up to many, so the recurring “2×2” stops looking like a coincidence.

Rung 1 — one unknown, one equation

Suppose you must solve f(x) = 0 but cannot rearrange it (exactly our diode and thermal cases). Newton’s recipe: stand at a guess x, look at how wrong you are (f(x)), and look at the slope f′(x) — the local steepness. The slope tells you which way and how far to step:

xnew = x − f(x) / f′(x) one unknown: divide the error by the slope

Geometrically you slide down the tangent line to where it crosses zero, and repeat. Near the answer the error squares each step (0.1 → 0.01 → 0.0001…) — quadratic convergence — which is why a handful of steps suffices.

Analogy — finding the bottom of a valley in fog You can’t see the valley floor (the solution), but you can feel the slope under your feet. Newton says: from the steepness, estimate where the ground levels out, walk straight there, re-check the slope, repeat. Each look-and-leap gets you dramatically closer.

Rung 2 — two unknowns, two equations

Now you have two knobs (x, y) and two gauges that must both read zero: f₁(x,y) = 0 and f₂(x,y) = 0. The catch is that the knobs are coupled — turning x moves both gauges. A single slope can’t capture that; you need four slopes — how each gauge responds to each knob. Arrange them in a 2×2 table, the Jacobian:

J = ⎡ ∂f₁/∂x   ∂f₁/∂y ⎤ ,   and the step solves   J · ⎡Δx⎤ = −⎡f₁⎤
     ⎣ ∂f₂/∂x   ∂f₂/∂y ⎦                        ⎣Δy⎦    ⎣f₂⎦ two coupled unknowns ⇒ a 2×2 system to solve for the two corrections

Instead of “divide by the slope”, you now “multiply by the inverse of the slope-matrix.” For a 2×2 that inverse is a one-liner using the determinant det = ad − bc, which is exactly the Cramer’s rule PowerPy hard-codes (Part 7). This is precisely why the framework keeps using a 2×2 vector: each cell has two coupled temperature unknowns, so each Newton step is a 2×2 solve.

Rung 3 — many unknowns: block-diagonal vs. coupled

An array has N cells, hence 2N unknowns and a 2N×2N Jacobian. Two situations:

  • Cells independent (glat = 0). A cell’s two equations only touch its own two temperatures, so the giant Jacobian is block-diagonal — N separate 2×2 blocks with nothing connecting them. You never form the big matrix; you solve all N tiny 2×2s in one vectorised array operation (Part 7).
  • Cells coupled (glat > 0). Lateral conduction links neighbours, so off-diagonal terms appear. Now it really is one 2N×2N system — but a sparse one (each tile touches ~4 of thousands), assembled with the graph Laplacian and solved with a sparse linear solver (Part 8).
The unifying picture Diode I–V: 1 unknown per voltage point → scalar Newton. One cell’s temperature: 2 unknowns → 2×2 Newton. A coupled panel: 2N unknowns → sparse Newton. Same algorithm, three sizes. Learn it once and the whole solver stack reads the same way.

Why Newton and not just “try values until it works”

You could sweep guesses blindly, but that is hopeless in many dimensions and slow even in one. Newton uses gradient information — the slope/Jacobian — to head straight for the root, so it converges in single-digit iterations instead of thousands of blind trials. The cost is needing the derivatives; for these physics they are simple closed forms (the −4εAσT³ radiative slope, the ±C·A conduction terms), so Newton is both fast and exact.

Appendix · seeing it all work together

A full analysis, end to end

To tie the parts together, here is the path a single question takes through PowerPy: “On a 9×9 panel, what is the worst hot-spot if up to five cells fail, and does it stay below the melt limit?” Every step below maps to a part of this handbook.

Step by step

  1. Describe the array (Part 3). A layout file gives the 9×9 grid, the cell type, the substrate (which fixes c_cond and the optical α/ε), and the tile pitch. The loaders validate it into typed objects.
  2. Set the environment (Part 6). Choose the operating phase — full sun (1361 W/m²), the relevant temperature and radiation dose, plus the loss factors.
  3. Establish the healthy baseline (Parts 4–5). Each cell’s I–V curve is fit from the datasheet points corrected to this environment; cells combine up the string/section tree to the panel curve. Healthy cells extract ≈ 1.1 W each.
  4. Model a failure (Part 11). A failed cell is driven into reverse bias; its bypass-diode clamp sets how negative its voltage can go, giving a reverse power ≈ −9.6 W that enters the thermal solver as heat.
  5. Solve the panel thermally with spreading (Parts 7–8). For any failure pattern, build the per-tile power map and run the lateral 2-node solver. With glat>0 a lone failure peaks near 47 °C (Figure 8.2), not the 255 °C an isolated model would predict.
  6. Search for the worst cluster (Part 12). worst_case_search() greedily adds the failure that raises the peak most, five times over — assembling the adjacent cluster whose heat reinforces fastest. It returns the failed-cell set, the peak temperature, and the trajectory of the peak after each added failure.
  7. Judge and report (Part 13). Compare the peak against the melt limit for a PASS/FAIL verdict; render the heat-map figure, the trajectory, and the numbers into a PDF, plus an HTML/JSON heat-map for the record. The CLI command powerpy worst runs exactly this.
Analogy — a flight test on paper Steps 1–2 are describing the aircraft and the weather; steps 3–5 are the physics of the flight; step 6 is deliberately flying the worst manoeuvre; step 7 is the test report. PowerPy lets you fly the worst case hundreds of times, on paper, before bending any metal.

What you can trust, today

The full path above — describe → fit → combine → fail → lateral thermal → worst-case → report — runs end to end with the analytic engine and is covered by the test suite. The only optional piece not live is the real-ngspice electrical validation (Part 14), which the framework treats as a bonus, not a dependency. In other words: the question this appendix poses is one PowerPy answers out of the box.

Appendix · knowing the model’s edges

Assumptions, limitations & validity

A model is only trustworthy if you know where it stops being true. This appendix lists what PowerPy assumes, why those assumptions are reasonable for a space solar array, and the situations where you should be careful.

Electrical model

  • Single-diode, shunt-open. The fit takes Rsh as effectively infinite (≈10⁹ Ω). Valid for healthy space cells whose leakage is genuinely tiny; a cell with a real shunt defect (a manufacturing flaw, micro-crack) would need the two-diode or explicit-shunt model. The framework keeps a shunt term in the equation precisely so this can be re-enabled.
  • Operating points drive everything. The whole curve is reconstructed from four datasheet numbers plus coefficient/degradation curves. This is exact at those four points and physically-shaped between them, but it is a model of the curve, not a measurement of every point. The optional ngspice path exists to cross-check it.
  • Linear temperature & log-fluence degradation. Temperature coefficients are applied linearly about the reference temperature; radiation factors are interpolated in log-fluence. Both match how vendors publish the data, but extrapolating far outside the supplied range is unreliable.

Thermal model

  • Two nodes per cell. Front and rear are each treated as a single uniform temperature. This captures the dominant front/rear split but not within-cell gradients (e.g. a temperature ramp across a single large tile). For hot-spot peak prediction the lumped node is the standard engineering simplification.
  • Radiation to deep space, gray bodies. Each face radiates as a gray body (constant emissivity) to a 2.7 K background. There is no atmosphere, so no convection — correct for orbit, wrong for a ground test in air.
  • Lateral conduction is first-order. Sideways heat is a single conductance glat between 4-neighbour tiles, derived from facesheet geometry. It captures in-plane spreading well; it does not resolve the honeycomb core’s 3-D detail or radiative coupling between tiles.
  • Steady vs transient. The steady solver assumes conditions hold long enough to settle. Near rapid events (failure onset, eclipse entry) use the transient solver — the steady answer is the destination, not the path.

Failure model

  • Failures as fixed reverse power. A failed cell is represented by a clamped reverse power into the thermal solver. This is the right driver for the hot-spot temperature, but it abstracts the detailed reverse I–V characteristic of a specific failure mechanism.
  • Greedy worst-case is a heuristic. The greedy cluster search is fast and finds a very damaging cluster, but it is not a mathematical proof of the global worst case. For a small panel it is typically the true worst; for very large panels treat it as a strong lower bound on the danger.

Numerics

  • Newton needs a sane start. Convergence is fast and robust for physical parameters, but a wildly nonphysical input (negative areas, zero conductivity) can stall it; the solvers warn (or, in strict mode, raise) if a solve does not converge.
  • Determinant guard. The 2×2 inverse guards against a near-zero determinant by flooring it — a safety net for degenerate inputs, not a licence to feed nonsense.
The honest summary PowerPy is a system-level tool: it predicts array performance, panel temperatures and hot-spot survival to engineering accuracy, fast enough to sweep thousands of failure cases. It is not a device-physics simulator or a 3-D finite-element thermal model, and it does not pretend to be. Its assumptions are the standard, defensible ones for early- and mid-phase solar-array design — and each one is isolated in the code so it can be refined when a project needs more fidelity.

Appendix · the subtle questions answered

Conceptual FAQ — the “but why” questions

These are the questions that tend to nag after a first read. Each is answered in full here, drawing the threads of the handbook together.

Why two thermal nodes — why not one, or ten?

One node would force the sunlit front and the planet-facing rear to share a single temperature, which is physically wrong — they see very different environments and are only loosely linked by conduction through the board. Ten nodes (a fine 3-D mesh) would be more accurate within a cell but enormously slower, and would need material data the early design doesn’t have. Two nodes capture the dominant effect (the front/rear split) at the cost of a trivial 2×2 solve — the classic engineering sweet spot of enough fidelity, minimum cost. That balance is exactly why the “2×2” is everywhere in the code.

Why is the front usually hotter than the rear?

The front absorbs the full solar flux (hundreds of watts per m²) while the rear only sees the weaker albedo and planetary IR. Both faces radiate to cold space, but the front has far more heat to shed, so it settles hotter — and conduction through the substrate drags the rear part-way up toward it. In the worked example of Part 7 the front converged to 3.59 °C and the rear to 3.32 °C: close, because the board conducts well, but the front leads.

If lateral conduction lowers the peak, isn’t more of it always better?

For a single failure, yes — spreading the heat is purely protective (255 °C → 47 °C in Figure 8.2). But spreading also shares heat, so a tight cluster of failures can build a broad, stubborn warm region, and in a healthy array strong lateral conduction can warm cells that would otherwise run cool. It is a redistribution, not a removal, of heat — which is why PowerPy models it explicitly rather than assuming it always helps.

Why an analytic model when a real SPICE simulator is right there?

Because the analytic single-diode model answers the questions PowerPy asks — array curves, power, hot-spot drivers — directly, fast, and with zero external dependencies, which matters in an environment that forbids installs. SPICE is a validation luxury, not a necessity: it lets you cross-check the analytic curves against full circuit physics. Designing the framework so the must-run path is pure Python, and SPICE is optional, is what keeps it robust (Part 14).

Why does the radiated heat go as T⁴, and why does that matter so much?

It is the Stefan–Boltzmann law: a body radiates power proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature. The practical consequence is twofold. First, it makes the balance equations nonlinear, which is the entire reason Newton’s method appears. Second, it is a powerful self-limiting effect: a cell that gets hotter radiates dramatically more, so temperatures don’t run away without bound — they find a (possibly dangerous, but finite) balance.

Why vectorise — isn’t a loop over cells simpler to read?

A Python loop over thousands of cells, each doing a tiny 2×2 solve, would be readable but slow — and the Monte-Carlo and worst-case studies run the panel solver thousands of times. Expressing the per-cell arithmetic as numpy array operations lets the same maths run on all cells at once in compiled code, turning minutes into milliseconds. The physics per cell is unchanged; only the execution is batched (Part 7).

What actually fails when a cell “fails”?

In PowerPy’s abstraction, a failed cell simply stops generating and is driven into reverse bias by its still-healthy series neighbours, turning into a resistive heater. Real-world causes — micro-cracks, interconnect breaks, coverglass damage, shadowing — differ in detail, but they share this electrical signature, which is what sets the hot-spot. The model targets the consequence (reverse power → temperature) rather than the specific cause.

What decides how many cells go in series versus in parallel?

The spacecraft bus runs at a fixed voltage (often ~28, ~50 or ~100 V). Cells are put in series until the string reaches that bus voltage — that sets the series count (e.g. ~22 of our 2.4 V cells for a ~50 V bus). Then strings are placed in parallel until the array delivers the required current (and hence power). So series count is dictated by voltage and parallel count by current — and both flow from the power budget of Part 1. PowerPy reads this topology from the layout and builds the tree of Part 5 to match.

Why is the maximum-power point where it is — at the knee?

Power is V·I. Near V = 0 the current is high but the voltage is almost nothing, so the product is small. Near Voc the voltage is high but the current has collapsed, so the product is small again. Somewhere between — right at the knee of the I–V curve — the rising voltage and the still-healthy current multiply to a maximum. That sweet spot is the MPP, and it is where array regulators are designed to hold the operating point, because a few hundred millivolts either side throws away power (the V = 2.6 V case in Part 4 lost a third of the peak).

How do I know any of these numbers are right?

Three ways the framework earns trust: the analytic curve reproduces the datasheet points it was fitted from (Part 4 check); the transient solver reduces to the steady solver as time → ∞ (a parity test); and the independent thermal path matches the legacy per-cell solver exactly (a single sunlit cell → 65.26 °C in the regression test). A 92-test suite pins these and the rest. The figures in this handbook were generated by those same solvers.
