Chapter 4
Letting Heat Spread
Across the Panel

Today the thermal solver treats every cell as a sealed island — front and rear only, no sideways sharing. This chapter answers, in plain words: can we let heat flow sideways through the panel too? What would it take, is it a rebuild or a bolt-on, and does it still plug into everything we already built?

You asked three sharp questions: (1) is expanding the model to in-plane heat transmission possible or impossible, and what's the work; (2) is it independent of the current framework or does it tear it up; and (3) will the new results still be fed back into the earlier layers (breakdown, coupling, Monte-Carlo). This chapter answers each, slowly, with the same kitchen-English pictures as Chapter 3 — bathtubs with pipes, a city of linked ponds — and a ladder of small questions before every hard one.

Words You Need First

Six words carry this whole chapter. Meet them plainly; each is used properly later and all are in the Glossary.

In-plane / lateral conduction Heat flowing sideways, across the flat face of the panel, from a hot cell to its neighbours — as opposed to front-to-rear through a cell. "Lateral" = sideways.
Node A single point we track one temperature at. Today each cell has two nodes (front, rear). A bigger model just has more nodes.
Conductor (thermal) A "pipe" between two nodes that lets heat flow between them. A fat conductor (low resistance) shares heat fast; a thin one barely shares. Sideways sharing = adding conductors between neighbouring cells.
Nodal network / resistor network The standard way engineers model heat: a web of nodes joined by conductors, with "heat-in = heat-out" written at every node. It's literally how spacecraft thermal tools (ESATAN, Thermal Desktop) work.
Adjacency The map of "which cell touches which" — who each cell's neighbours are. You need this so the solver knows where to put the sideways pipes.
Sparse matrix A big grid of numbers that is mostly zeros (because each cell only touches a few neighbours, not all of them). Special "sparse" solvers exploit all those zeros to stay fast.

1What You Have Now vs What You Want

One picture makes the whole upgrade obvious. It's the bathtub from Chapter 3 — but now we ask what happens between neighbouring tubs.

The picture Now: every cell is its own sealed bathtub. Water (heat) can slosh between its front end and rear end, but the tub has solid walls — nothing leaks to the tub next door. That's why a hot-spot piles up: the heat has nowhere sideways to go. What you want: drill a pipe between each tub and its neighbours. Now a tub that's filling fast (a hot cell) bleeds some water into the tubs beside it. The peak level drops; the neighbours rise a little. That pipe is lateral conduction.
Why this is the crux In Chapter 3 we said the solver vectorises because the cells are independent (sealed tubs). The moment you drill the pipes, the cells stop being independent — each one's temperature now depends on its neighbours'. That single change is the entire story of this chapter: gain realism, lose the "every cell is its own little problem" shortcut.

2Is It Possible? — Short Answer First

Verdict Yes — entirely possible, and it's a standard, well-trodden upgrade. It is not exotic research; in-plane conduction is exactly how professional spacecraft thermal models work. What you have now is the simplest case of a bigger, completely normal model. You are not inventing a new physics — you are switching on a term we deliberately left at zero.

The reason it feels like a big jump is only that it changes how the numbers are solved (Section 4), not whether they can be. The governing physics — heat flows from hot to cold — is the same one you already use for front-to-rear; we just also apply it side-to-side.

Ladder · "possible" from the ground up
Q1. Is sideways heat flow real, or are we inventing it?
Completely real. Aluminium honeycomb has metal face-sheets that conduct heat sideways quite well. Today's model just ignores that path for simplicity. Switching it on makes the model more faithful to reality, not less.
Q2. Is the maths for sideways flow different from the front-to-rear flow we already do?
No — it's the same law (heat ∝ temperature difference × conductance). We already compute one such flow (front↔rear). Lateral flow is just more of the same links, pointing sideways.
Q3. Then what actually makes it harder than what we have?
Only this: the links now tie cells together, so you must solve all cells at once as one big joined puzzle instead of N tiny separate ones. The difficulty is bookkeeping and solving a bigger system — not new physics. (Section 4 shows exactly what that means.)
Q4. Has anyone solved problems like this before, or are we pioneers?
It's textbook. The "nodes joined by conductors, balance at every node" recipe is the bedrock of thermal engineering and of solving heat-flow on any grid. Mature, fast, free libraries (scipy's sparse solvers) do the heavy lifting. So: well-charted territory.

3The Physics — Heat That Flows Sideways

The whole model is one tiny rule, applied at every node. Learn the rule once and the rest is repetition.

The one rule (Fourier's law, in words) Heat flows from a hot node to a cooler one at a rate equal to the temperature difference times a "conductance" (the fatness of the pipe). Big difference or fat pipe → lots of heat moves. Equal temperatures → no flow.
heat from node A to node B = GAB × (TA − TB)
GAB = the conductance of the pipe between A and B (watts per °C) · T = each node's temperature. Note: if TA = TB, the flow is zero — heat only moves on a difference.
The picture — a city of ponds Imagine each cell is a pond, and pipes join neighbouring ponds. Pour water into one pond (a hot cell) and it spreads to its neighbours, which spread to their neighbours. Eventually the whole field of ponds finds a set of levels where every pond's inflow equals its outflow at once. There's no "first pond" — they all settle together. Solving the panel is finding that whole settled landscape of temperatures in one go.
What gets written down At every node you again write the bathtub balance "heat-in = heat-out", but now "out" includes a term for each sideways pipe to a neighbour. With N cells × 2 faces you get ~2N balance equations, all tangled together through the shared neighbour temperatures. That tangled set is what the solver cracks.
Ladder · the rule, then the network
Q1. Two cells, one at 187°C, one at 40°C, joined by a pipe of conductance 0.05 W/°C. Which way does heat flow and how much?
From hot to cold (187 → 40). Amount = 0.05 × (187 − 40) = 0.05 × 147 ≈ 7.4 W leaving the hot cell into the cold one. That 7.4 W is heat the hot cell no longer has to radiate alone — so it runs cooler.
Q2. If we made that pipe ten times fatter (G = 0.5), what happens to the two temperatures over time?
They rush toward each other — lots of heat shared, so the gap shrinks fast. A very fat pipe makes neighbours nearly equal in temperature (the panel "smears" the hot-spot out).
Q3. If the pipe had zero conductance (G = 0), what model are we back to?
Exactly today's model — no sideways sharing, sealed islands. So the current solver is just the special case "all lateral conductances = 0". The upgrade contains what you have.
Q4 (top rung). Why can't we still solve each cell on its own once the pipes exist?
Because each cell's balance now contains its neighbours' temperatures, which aren't known until their balances are solved, which contain their neighbours'… it's one connected web. Pull one node and the whole net moves. So the cells must be solved together, as a single system. That's Section 4.

4The One Thing That Changes in the Maths

Everything you learned in Chapter 3 about Newton's method stays. Exactly one ingredient changes shape. Here it is, with no hand-waving.

What stays / what changes Stays: Newton–Raphson (guess → correct → repeat until settled). Still the engine.
Changes: the "slope" Newton uses. Today it's N tiny independent 2×2 blocks (one per cell), so we invert each by a closed-form formula and blast them all at once. With sideways pipes the slope becomes one big matrix where each node is linked to its neighbours — a sparse matrix — solved in one shot by a sparse linear solver instead of N little closed-form inverses.
In one line of code-speak Today: a closed-form 2×2 inverse, vectorised over cells. Tomorrow: build a sparse matrix (each node + its neighbour links) and call scipy.sparse.linalg.spsolve once per Newton step. Sparse solvers chew through 10⁴–10⁵ nodes in milliseconds, so it stays fast even for a big array.
Ladder · why "sparse" keeps it cheap
Q1. A panel of 1000 cells — could each cell, in principle, link to all 999 others?
In principle the matrix has 1000×1000 = a million slots. But a cell only touches ~4 neighbours, so only a handful of slots per row are non-zero. The matrix is ~99.6% zeros.
Q2. Why does all those zeros being there actually help us?
Sparse solvers skip the zeros entirely — they store and compute only the non-zero links. So the cost grows with the number of pipes, not with N². A near-empty matrix is cheap to solve.
Q3. Did we lose Newton's method by switching to a matrix solve?
No. Newton still wraps the whole thing: guess all temperatures, build the sparse slope, solve for the correction, update, repeat until settled. We only swapped the inner "invert the slope" step from a 2×2 formula to a sparse solve.
Q4 (top rung). So is "no per-cell loop" still true after the upgrade?
Yes — even better. We never loop over cells in slow Python; we build one big sparse system and solve it in one compiled call. The vectorised spirit of Chapter 3 survives; it just graduates from "N independent 2×2s" to "one sparse system."

5Q2 — Is It Independent of the Current Framework?

Verdict Largely yes — it is a contained swap, not a rebuild. The change lives almost entirely inside the thermal layer. The layers above it don't care how a temperature was computed — they just receive an array of temperatures. That's the whole reason it was built in layers.
The picture Think of the framework as a kitchen with stations. The thermal solver is the "oven." Breakdown, coupling, and Monte-Carlo are the chefs who use whatever comes out of the oven. If you swap the oven for a better one that bakes more evenly (spreads heat), the chefs' recipes don't change — they still take "the baked tray" and carry on. You upgraded one appliance, not the kitchen.
The one honest "but" Two small things do ripple outward, and both are additive (you add info, you don't rewrite): (a) the cells now need a geometry — which cell sits next to which (a grid position), because "sideways" only means something once cells have neighbours; and (b) the substrate/ panel gains one new number, the lateral conductance. Neither breaks existing code; they're new inputs the new solver reads. Everything already written keeps working with lateral conductance set to zero.
Ladder · how "contained" it really is
Q1. What does the Monte-Carlo layer actually receive from the thermal solver?
An array of per-cell temperatures (and the derived flags). It doesn't know or care whether those came from sealed islands or a connected network. So a smarter solver is invisible to it.
Q2. If the interface (inputs in, temperature array out) stays the same, what must we change upstream?
Nothing structural. The breakdown check, the coupling loop, the results store, the sweep — all keep their code. They consume the same shape of output. That's the payoff of layering.
Q3. So why isn't it perfectly independent — what leaks out?
Just two new inputs: cell geometry (the neighbour map) and the lateral conductance value. These are additions, not edits — you feed the solver more, you don't tear anything down.
Q4 (top rung). Could we ship it so the old behaviour is still available?
Yes — set lateral conductance = 0 and the new solver reproduces the current sealed-island answers exactly (Section 3, Q3). So it's a strict superset: old model = new model with the pipes closed. You never lose what you have.

6Q3 — Does It Feed Back Into the Earlier Layers?

Verdict Yes — and it flows through the exact same channels you already have. The new, more realistic temperatures pour straight into breakdown, into the coupling loop, and into Monte-Carlo, because those layers were built to read "a temperature per cell" — which is precisely what the new solver still produces (just better numbers).

Let's trace the journey of one number so "feed back" is concrete.

Two kinds of "feed back" — both already exist (1) Forward feed: thermal → breakdown → results. The new temperatures are simply better inputs; the melt decision and the stored numbers update automatically. (2) The loop feed-back: the electro-thermal coupling already sends temperatures back to the electrical model so the two agree (Chapter 3 §11). With lateral spreading, the temperatures it sends back are the spread-out ones — so the loop converges to a more honest joint answer. You don't add a new feedback path; the existing one just carries better data.
Ladder · trace the number, see the feedback
Q1. The new solver says the hot-spot is now 150°C (spread), not 187°C (sealed). Where does that 150 go first?
Into the breakdown check — "is 150°C above the melt limit?" Same check as before, just fed a more realistic number. If the limit is, say, 160°C, the verdict can flip from "melts" to "survives." That's the upgrade earning its keep.
Q2. Does the coupling loop need rewriting to accept spread-out temperatures?
No. It already takes "temperatures in, power out, repeat." It doesn't care that the temperatures now include sideways sharing — it just keeps looping until electricity and temperature agree, landing on a better fixed point.
Q3. And Monte-Carlo — does running thousands of these change?
Not in structure. Each run still calls the solver and records the worst temperature. The histogram simply shifts to more realistic values (often a cooler, narrower danger tail). The sweep code is untouched.
Q4 (top rung). So in one sentence: does the new physics reach the old layers?
Yes — it reaches all of them, through the same wires, because every later layer speaks one common language ("a temperature per cell"), and the upgraded solver still speaks exactly that language — just more truthfully.

7What You'd Actually Need to Add

Strip away the theory and the to-do list is short — three ingredients. None of them is a rewrite; each is an addition.

#IngredientIn plain wordsWhere it lives
1Geometry / adjacencyGive each cell a position on the panel (row, column) so the solver knows who its neighbours are. Without neighbours, "sideways" is meaningless.the Circuit class (add grid coords to each cell)
2Lateral conductanceOne new number: how fat the sideways pipe is, from the face-sheet's conductivity, thickness and cell spacing. One value (or a small table) for the panel.the substrate / panel parameters
3Sparse-network solverBuild the big sparse "slope" matrix (each node + its neighbour links) and solve it once per Newton step with a sparse solver.a new thermal_panel.py (today's solver is the G=0 case)
The shape of the work Ingredients 1 and 2 are small, additive inputs. Ingredient 3 is the real coding — but it's a known recipe (nodes + conductors + sparse solve), guarded by an easy test: with all lateral conductances set to zero, the new module must reproduce today's verified numbers (65.26°C / 187.48°C) exactly. Pass that, and you know you only added spreading without breaking the physics you trust.
The one thing to get right The conductance value (ingredient 2) is where realism is won or lost. Too fat and the panel looks falsely uniform (hot-spots vanish that really exist); too thin and you barely improve on sealed islands. It's worth grounding that number in the real face-sheet material and, if possible, one measured or reference data point — otherwise you have a more elaborate model that's confidently wrong. Garbage pipe-width in, garbage spreading out.

8A Worked Picture — Two Linked Cells

The smallest version of the whole idea: a hot cell joined to one cool neighbour. Watch the pipe pull the peak down. (Numbers chosen to be followable by hand, not exact solver output.)

Worked example — the pipe cools the hot-spot
Sealed (today): the dead cell dumps 9.6 W and must radiate all of it alone. Balance lands high — call it 187°C. Its healthy neighbour sits at ~40°C, ignoring each other.
Now open a pipe of conductance G = 0.05 W/°C between them. Heat flows hot→cold: 0.05 × (187 − 40) = 0.05 × 147 ≈ 7.4 W tries to cross.
That 7.4 W is heat the hot cell no longer radiates by itself — so its balance temperature drops. Meanwhile the neighbour now receives extra heat, so its temperature rises.
They meet somewhere in between — the hot cell maybe ~150°C, the neighbour maybe ~60°C (the real numbers come from re-solving the joined balance, which is what the sparse solver does in one shot).
Check the direction of every move: peak went down (good — heat had somewhere to go), neighbour went up (it absorbed some), and total heat radiated by the pair is unchanged (energy is conserved — we only moved where it leaves). If the melt limit is 160°C, this cell just crossed from "melts" to "survives". That flip is the entire engineering value of the upgrade.
Ladder · read the result correctly
Q1. Did opening the pipe create or destroy any heat?
Neither. The 9.6 W is still produced and still radiated to space in total. The pipe only redistributes where it sits before it leaves — peak down, neighbour up, sum the same. Pure conservation.
Q2. Why is "peak down, neighbours up" usually good news for survival?
Melting is about the hottest single point, not the average. Shaving the peak — even while nudging the average neighbourhood up a little — is exactly what keeps the substrate under its limit.
Q3. Could spreading ever be bad?
Occasionally — if a neighbour was already near its own limit, the extra heat could tip it over, or a cluster of failures could pool heat. That's precisely why you'd run Monte-Carlo on the new model: to catch those subtler, spread-out danger patterns the sealed model can't see.
Q4 (top rung). Why is today's sealed 187°C still a useful number even after the upgrade?
Because it's the worst case — "what if no heat spreads at all." It's a safe, conservative bound. The lateral model gives the realistic case. Engineers like having both: design to the conservative bound, report the realistic one. You're not throwing 187°C away; you're adding a truer companion to it.

Glossary

Adjacency The neighbour map — which cell sits next to which. Needed before "sideways" means anything.
Conductance (thermal, G) How easily heat flows between two nodes, in watts per °C. The "fatness of the pipe."
Conductor A heat path joining two nodes; carries G × (temperature difference) watts.
Fourier's law Heat flow = conductance × temperature difference; heat only moves on a difference.
In-plane / lateral conduction Heat flowing sideways across the panel face, cell to neighbour.
Interface (of a layer) The agreed "inputs in, outputs out" contract; here: conditions in, a temperature array out.
Newton–Raphson The guess-correct-repeat solver; unchanged by the upgrade — only its inner slope-solve changes.
Nodal network / resistor network Modelling heat as nodes joined by conductors, balanced at every node.
Node A point at which one temperature is tracked.
Sparse matrix A mostly-zero grid of numbers; sparse solvers skip the zeros to stay fast.
spsolve scipy's sparse linear solver — solves the big network in one compiled call per Newton step.
Superset (G=0 case) The new model with all sideways pipes shut reproduces today's sealed-island model exactly.
Worst case / conservative The sealed 187°C — "what if no heat spreads"; a safe upper bound to design against.
