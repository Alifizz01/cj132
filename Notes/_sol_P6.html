P6-1. Dissipation with and without a bypass diode. Heat in the failed cell is |V|·I.
(a) Protected:  |V| = 0.7 V,  P = 0.7 V × 0.50 A = 0.35 W. A barely-warm patch.
(b) Unprotected (driven to breakdown):  |V| = 20 V,  P = 20 V × 0.50 A = 10.0 W concentrated in one ~30 cm² cell — easily enough to scorch.
(c) Reduction factor:  10.0 W ÷ 0.35 W ≈ 28×. The factor is the same as in Worked 6A/6B because it is just the ratio of the two voltages, 20 V ÷ 0.7 V ≈ 28.6: the current appears in both numerator and denominator and cancels, so the reduction depends only on how far the clamp holds the voltage below the breakdown level, not on the value of the current.
P6-2. Worst case from the layout. Use  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (K−1)·Vcell,  Vcell = 2.35 V,  I = 0.48 A, cap at 20 V.
(a) K = 6:  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (6−1)×2.35 = 0.7 + 11.75 = 12.45 V. This is below the 20 V breakdown cap, so the formula applies directly (the diode group does not quite drive the cell to breakdown). The N = 24 figure does not enter the worst single-cell case — only the group size K does, because the pile-on comes from the (K−1) healthy cells inside the same bypass group.
(b) Worst-case heat:  P = |Vfail|·I = 12.45 V × 0.48 A = 5.98 W ≈ 6.0 W. Roughly 18× the protected (K = 1) bookend of 0.34 W — uncomfortably hot, though short of the full 9.6 W breakdown case.
(c) Re-wire to K = 2:  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (2−1)×2.35 = 3.05 V;  P = 3.05 V × 0.48 A = 1.46 W ≈ 1.5 W. What the designer bought: the worst-case hot-spot dropped from ~6.0 W to ~1.5 W (about 4× cooler), sliding the failed cell toward the gentle −0.7 V clamp. What it cost: three times as many bypass diodes — 12 groups instead of 4 — i.e. more mass, more solder joints, more complexity. This is the §6.4 trade-off in numbers.
P6-3. Why parallel strings make it worse. In §6.3's picture the failed cell is the single sink and every healthy generator is a donor. In scenario A the only donors are the healthy cells of the failed cell's own string, so the current forced through the bad cell is limited to roughly one string's worth. In scenario B the three extra parallel strings share the same bus, and their current can be pushed backwards across the bus into the weak string and through the failed cell on top of its own string's contribution. More donors feeding one sink means a larger current I forced through the bad cell, and since its heat is |V|·I, a larger I means more dissipation for the same reverse voltage — the hot-spot runs hotter. The component that limits this: the blocking diode (§6.6), placed in series with each string, which stops current from the bus flowing backwards into a weak or shaded string and thus caps the extra push that parallel strings would otherwise add.
