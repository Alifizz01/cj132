P8-1. Runs needed for a target accuracy. The governing rule is SE ∝ 1/√N, so the standard error falls only as the square root of the trial count. Given N1 = 500 trials at SE1 = 6 W, target SE2 = 1.5 W.
(a) Required shrink in SE:  SE1/SE2 = 6 W / 1.5 W = 4× (the W units cancel — a ratio of errors is dimensionless).
(b) Required growth in N:  Since SE ∝ 1/√N, cutting SE by 4× means raising √N by 4×, i.e. raising N by 4² = 16×.
(c) Total trials:  N2 = N1·(SE1/SE2)² = 500 · (6/1.5)² = 500 · 16 = 8000 trials.
(d) Why "slow":  Because error falls as 1/√N, accuracy is bought at a squared price — here a mere 4× improvement in error demanded 16× the work, and in general 10× more accuracy costs 100× more runs. That punishing exchange rate is Monte Carlo's defining weakness.
P8-2. Estimate π from a stated hit count. Darts uniform in a 1×1 square with an inscribed quarter-circle of radius 1; 1562 of 2000 land inside the arc.
(a) Formula and the factor of 4:  π ≈ 4 × (inside / total). The 4 appears because the inside-fraction approximates (area of quarter-circle)/(area of square) = (π/4)/1 = π/4; solving that for π multiplies by 4. (A quarter-circle is one-quarter of a full circle, hence the π/4, hence the ×4 to undo it.)
(b) Compute the estimate:  inside/total = 1562/2000 = 0.781, so π ≈ 4 × 0.781 = 3.124.
(c) Compare with the truth:  true π = 3.14159, so the error is |3.124 − 3.14159| ≈ 0.018 — close, from 2000 random darts and no calculus. (For N = 2000, 1/√N ≈ 0.022, and the error sits comfortably within that scale.)
(d) Halving the error:  Since the error scales as 1/√N, to roughly halve it you must quadruple the darts (√4 = 2): throw about 4 × 2000 = 8000 darts. Each halving of error costs four times the work — the 1/√N rule again.
P8-3. Why 2N enumeration is hopeless for 200 cells.
(a) Number of patterns:  Each of the 200 cells is independently OK or failed (2 states), and the states multiply, giving 2200 distinct failure patterns.
(b) Why simulating them all is impossible:  2200 ≈ 1.6 × 1060 — a 1 followed by sixty zeros, vastly more than the number of atoms in the Earth. And each single pattern is not free: it requires a full coupled electro-thermal solve (Section 7), the iterative per-cell temperature/power calculation, which costs real compute time. Multiplying ~1060 patterns by the cost of one solve gives a total that no computer could finish in any feasible time — the age of the universe is roughly 1017 seconds, so even a trillion solves per second would not make a dent. Enumeration is therefore flatly impossible.
(c) What PowerPy does instead, and which mode:  It uses Monte Carlo — sample a manageable subset of failure patterns at random, simulate each, and infer the statistical picture from the sample rather than the full 2200. To obtain the full distribution of power loss you would use the RANDOM sampling mode (mode 3): many random failure patterns, accumulated into a histogram of total power loss. (The position sweep finds the single worst spot; the count sweep charts decline versus number of failures; only random sampling delivers the full distribution.)
