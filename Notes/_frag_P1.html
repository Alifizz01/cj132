A satellite is a machine that must keep itself alive in one of the most hostile places we know — the vacuum of space — for years at a time, with no fuel line, no power cable, and no repair crew. Almost everything it does, from firing its radio back to Earth to keeping its own electronics from freezing, costs electrical power. That power has to come from somewhere, and for the overwhelming majority of spacecraft it comes from sunlight, captured by a solar array. This section builds your mental model of a solar array from the single building block — one tiny solar cell — and introduces the four measured numbers an engineer uses to describe it. Everything later in PowerPy is built on these four numbers, so we will go slowly and define every word the moment it appears.

1.1 What a solar array is for, and what PowerPy does

Let us start with the mission, because the numbers only make sense once you know what they are protecting. A spacecraft in orbit lives through conditions that would destroy ordinary electronics:

  • Vacuum. There is no air. That sounds harmless, but it means there is no convection — no breeze, no air to carry heat away. The only way a satellite can shed heat is by radiating it as infrared light. This is why temperature is such a central concern, as we will see.
  • Radiation. High-energy particles from the Sun and from deep space steadily damage the semiconductor crystal in each solar cell. Over years this degradation slowly lowers the power the array can produce.
  • Thermal cycling. Each orbit the spacecraft swings from full sunlight (hot) into the Earth's shadow (cold) and back, sometimes dozens of times a day. The array expands and contracts with every cycle.
  • Eclipses. When the spacecraft passes behind the Earth, the Sun is blocked and the array produces nothing. The satellite must run on batteries until it comes back into the light.

The engineer's job is to design an array that is big enough to power the spacecraft and robust enough to survive all of the above for the whole mission. To do that without launching a real satellite to find out, we simulate it. That is what PowerPy is.

Term PowerPy — a software tool that simulates a satellite solar array. Crucially, it predicts two linked things at once: the array's electrical output (how many volts and amps, hence how many watts, it delivers) and its temperature. Both matter, because — as we will learn — a solar cell's electrical behaviour depends strongly on how hot it is. PowerPy lets an engineer size an array (choose how many cells, and how to wire them) and verify that it keeps working even when something goes wrong (a "fault").
Note A solar cell is fundamentally a heat engine living in a fridge that has no door: sunlight pours energy in, only ~30 % leaves as electricity, and the rest becomes heat that can only escape by radiating to cold space. So an electrical model that ignores temperature is only half a model. Keep this dual nature in mind — it is the reason PowerPy computes both.

A real array is not one cell. It is a hierarchy, assembled from the bottom up. You will meet each of these objects by name later in the notes; for now just see the ladder so the vocabulary is not a surprise:

Term cell → string → section → panel — the four levels of a solar array, smallest to largest. A cell is one photovoltaic device (our subject this section). Cells wired end-to-end form a string. Strings grouped together form a section. Sections mounted on a physical wing form a panel. (Each is taught in its own later section — here we only name them so you have the map.)
Key idea Master the single cell first. A string is just cells in a row, a section is strings grouped, a panel is sections mounted — so every higher level is a combination of the level below it. If you truly understand one cell's four numbers, the rest of the array is bookkeeping built on top of them.

1.2 How a solar cell makes electricity — the photovoltaic effect

Before the numbers, the physics — in plain words. The word photovoltaic literally splits into photo (light) and voltaic (electricity, after Volta): "light → electricity." A solar cell turns light directly into electrical current with no moving parts. Here is the mechanism, one step at a time.

A solar cell is made of semiconductor material — a crystal (classically silicon; in space, usually gallium arsenide) whose electrons are almost free to move but need a small kick of energy to break loose. The cell is built as two layers pressed together:

Term p–n junction — the boundary where a layer of p-type semiconductor (doped so it has a shortage of electrons, i.e. spare positive "holes") meets a layer of n-type semiconductor (doped so it has spare electrons). At this boundary an internal electric field forms, like a one-way ramp. "Junction" simply means this boundary. ("Doped" means a tiny, deliberate amount of another element has been added to the crystal to give it extra electrons or extra holes.)

Now the effect itself, in four micro-steps:

  1. A particle of light — a photon — strikes the cell. A photon is a single packet of light energy; sunlight is a torrent of them.
  2. If the photon carries enough energy, it knocks an electron loose from the crystal, leaving behind a hole. Now you have one free negative charge (the electron) and one free positive charge (the hole).
  3. The internal field at the p–n junction sweeps the electron one way and the hole the other — the "one-way ramp" separates them so they cannot simply recombine.
  4. Connect a wire between the cell's two terminals and the separated electrons flow through it to get back to the holes. That flow of charge is an electric current. Put a load (say, a radio) in that wire and the current does useful work.
Key idea Light in, current out, no moving parts: a photon frees an electron, the junction's built-in field pushes it around an external circuit. More light striking the cell means more electrons freed per second, which means more current. Hold on to that last sentence — it is exactly why the first of our four numbers depends on sunlight.

Space cells are special. Sunlight contains many colours (energies) of photon, and any single semiconductor only efficiently catches a narrow band of them. To catch more of the spectrum, space cells stack three sub-cells:

Term triple-junction GaAs cell — a high-performance space solar cell made of gallium arsenide (chemical symbol GaAs) built as three stacked sub-cells, one on top of another. Each sub-cell is tuned to absorb a different part of the sunlight's colour spectrum (roughly: top catches blue, middle catches green, bottom catches red). Stacking three means much less sunlight is wasted, which is why these cells reach an efficiency of about 30 % — far higher than the ~15–20 % of an ordinary silicon panel on a house roof.
Note "Efficiency ~30 %" is a given property of this cell type — it means about 30 % of the sunlight energy landing on the cell leaves as electricity. We will not calculate it here, because that needs the strength of sunlight as a separate input number, which we introduce later. Treat 30 % as a stated fact for now.

1.3 The four numbers that describe a cell EXAM-HOT

An engineer does not describe a solar cell with a wall of physics. They describe it with four measured numbers, plus one special point those numbers define. Get these four straight and keep them distinct — confusing them is the single most common beginner mistake. We will define each precisely, with its defining condition.

Number 1 — Short-circuit current, Isc

Term Short-circuit current (Isc) — the current that flows when you connect the two terminals of the cell directly together with a plain wire, so the voltage across the cell is zero (V = 0). This is the most current the cell will ever push. Its value is set by how much sunlight is hitting the cell and how big the cell is — more light or more area means more electrons freed per second, hence more current. For our cell, Isc ≈ 0.50 A.
Note "Short circuit" sounds like a fault, but for a solar cell it is harmless and is a standard test condition: a solar cell is a current source, so shorting it just lets its natural current flow with nothing held back. It will not burn out from being shorted the way a battery would.

Number 2 — Open-circuit voltage, Voc

Term Open-circuit voltage (Voc) — the voltage measured across the cell's terminals when nothing is connected (the circuit is "open"), so no current flows (I = 0). This is the most voltage the cell will ever show. Its value is set by the physics of the p–n junction and — very importantly — by temperature: a hotter cell has a lower Voc. For our cell, Voc ≈ 2.67 V.
Key idea The two "ends" of a solar cell's behaviour are opposites: Isc is "all current, no voltage" (V = 0); Voc is "all voltage, no current" (I = 0). Sunlight mainly drives Isc; temperature mainly drives Voc. This split is worth memorising — it explains most of what an array does in orbit.

Numbers 3 & 4 — Max-power voltage and current, Vmp and Imp

Between the two extremes lies the operating point an engineer actually cares about. A cell can be operated anywhere along its curve — you choose, by choosing the load. At one special place the product of voltage and current (which is power) is as large as it gets. The voltage and current at that place are our third and fourth numbers.

Term Max-power voltage and current (Vmp, Imp) — the voltage and the current the cell delivers when it is operating at the single best point on its curve, where output power is greatest. For our cell, Vmp ≈ 2.35 V and Imp ≈ 0.48 A. Notice each is a little below its extreme: Vmp < Voc and Imp < Isc. That "give a little of each" is the whole secret, as we will see.
Key idea — the count of four The four numbers are exactly: Isc, Voc, Vmp, Imp. That is all four. The MPP, Pmp and the Fill Factor we meet next are not extra members of the four — they are derived from these four. Keep the headcount honest: four measured numbers, everything else computed from them.

The point those last two numbers define — the MPP

Term Maximum Power Point (MPP) — the single point on the cell's curve, located at the coordinates (Vmp, Imp), where the output power P = V · I reaches its largest value. The power there is called the maximum power, Pmp = Vmp · Imp. "MPP" is the point (a location on the curve); "Pmp" is the number of watts at that point.
Pitfall Do not confuse the point with the power. The MPP is a place (a voltage paired with a current). Pmp is a single number of watts. Saying "the MPP is 1.13 W" is sloppy; the MPP is at (2.35 V, 0.48 A), and the power there is Pmp ≈ 1.13 W. Examiners love this distinction.

1.4 The IV curve and the power curve

The four numbers are really four landmarks on one continuous picture: the cell's IV curve. This single graph is the heart of solar-cell engineering, so we will read it carefully.

Term IV curve (current– voltage curve) — a graph that plots the cell's current I (vertical axis) against the voltage V (horizontal axis) for every possible operating point. You read it as: "if the cell is held at this voltage, it will deliver that current." The curve runs from the point (0, Isc) on the left — zero volts, full current — across to (Voc, 0) on the right — full volts, zero current.

The shape between those two endpoints is the cell's signature. Reading left to right:

  • Flat stretch. Starting at the left, as voltage rises from zero the current barely drops — the curve runs almost horizontally near Isc. Across most of its working range the cell behaves like a near-constant current source.
  • The knee. At a certain voltage the curve bends sharply downward — like the knee of a bent leg. This bend sits right around Vmp.
  • The steep drop. Past the knee the current falls fast, plunging to zero at Voc.

Now overlay a second curve: power. For every point on the IV curve we can compute P = V · I and plot it. This power curve is not flat — it is a hump that starts at zero on the left, rises to a peak, and falls back to zero on the right. The peak of the hump sits exactly above the knee of the IV curve, at the MPP.

Why is the power curve a hump that touches zero at both ends? Because power is the product of two things, and at each end one of them is zero:

P = V · I
where P is power in watts (W), V is voltage in volts (V), and I is current in amperes (A). One volt times one amp equals one watt.
Key idea — why P = 0 at both ends At the left end (short circuit) the voltage is zero, so P = 0 · Isc = 0 W: plenty of current, but no voltage to push it through, so no power. At the right end (open circuit) the current is zero, so P = Voc · 0 = 0 W: plenty of voltage, but no current flowing, so no power. A product is zero whenever either factor is zero. Real power only appears between the ends, where both V and I are sizeable at once — and it is biggest where their product peaks, the MPP.
Think it through — climbing to "why not at the ends?"
Q1. What does the IV curve plot, in one sentence?
The current the cell delivers (vertical axis) at each possible voltage you could hold it at (horizontal axis), from (0, Isc) on the left to (Voc, 0) on the right.
Q2. How do we turn a point on the IV curve into power?
Multiply that point's voltage by its current: P = V · I. Every point on the IV curve has a power, and plotting those powers gives the power curve.
Q3. What is the power at the far left end of the curve (V = 0)?
Zero. P = 0 · Isc = 0 W. The current is at its maximum, but with zero voltage there is no power. All flow, no push.
Q4. And at the far right end (I = 0, at Voc)?
Also zero. P = Voc · 0 = 0 W. The voltage is at its maximum, but with zero current there is no power. All push, no flow.
Q5. So why is the maximum power NOT at Voc or Isc, but somewhere in between?
Because power needs both a healthy voltage and a healthy current at the same time — it is their product. At each extreme one factor collapses to zero and drags the product to zero. As you move inward from the left, voltage climbs while current stays high, so the product grows; keep going and eventually current starts dropping faster than voltage rises, so the product shrinks again. The best trade — the largest product — sits at the knee, in between. That balance point is the MPP, at (Vmp, Imp). You sacrifice a little voltage off Voc and a little current off Isc to keep both high together.

1.5 Fill Factor — how "square" the curve is EXAM-HOT

We now have everything we need for the most important derived figure of merit in solar engineering: the Fill Factor. It answers a natural question: an ideal cell would deliver its full Isc right up until its full Voc — a perfect rectangle of power, worth Voc · Isc. Real cells round off at the knee and fall short of that rectangle. How close does a real cell get? That ratio is the Fill Factor.

Term Fill Factor (FF) — a pure number between 0 and 1 that measures how "square" the IV curve is, i.e. how close the cell's actual maximum power comes to the ideal rectangle Voc · Isc. It is the ratio of the real maximum power to that ideal rectangle. Good space cells score about 0.8 (sometimes higher). A higher FF means a squarer curve and a better cell.
FF = (Vmp · Imp) / (Voc · Isc) = Pmp / (Voc · Isc)
where Pmp = Vmp · Imp is the real maximum power (W), and Voc · Isc is the "ideal rectangle" power (W). The watts cancel, so FF has no units — it is a bare ratio.
Note Picture two rectangles drawn under the IV curve from the origin. The big one has corners at Voc and Isc — the ideal. The smaller one has its far corner at the MPP, (Vmp, Imp) — the real maximum power. FF is simply the area of the inner (MPP) rectangle divided by the area of the outer (ideal) rectangle. A perfectly square curve would make them equal and FF = 1.
Worked example (a) Compute Pmp and the Fill Factor for our reference cell: Isc = 0.50 A, Voc = 2.67 V, Vmp = 2.35 V, Imp = 0.48 A.
Step 1 — maximum power. The MPP is at (2.35 V, 0.48 A). Multiply: Pmp = Vmp · Imp = 2.35 V × 0.48 A = 1.128 W. (Volts × amps = watts.)
Step 2 — the ideal rectangle. Voc · Isc = 2.67 V × 0.50 A = 1.335 W. This is the power the cell would give if its curve were a perfect rectangle.
Step 3 — divide. FF = Pmp / (Voc · Isc) = 1.128 W / 1.335 W = 0.845 (≈ 0.84). The watts cancel, so the answer is a bare number with no units, as it must be.
Check: FF came out 0.845, comfortably inside the legal range 0–1, and right around the "~0.8" we expect for a good space cell. Both sanity tests pass. A value above 1, or with units left on it, would mean an arithmetic slip.
Worked example (b) Power away from the MPP. Suppose we read the IV table and find that at V = 2.0 V the cell delivers I ≈ 0.49 A. How much power is that, and how does it compare with Pmp?
Step 1 — power at this point. P = V · I = 2.0 V × 0.49 A = 0.98 W.
Step 2 — compare with the maximum. From example (a), Pmp = 1.128 W. So here we get 0.98 W ÷ 1.128 W ≈ 0.87, i.e. about 87 % of the maximum.
Step 3 — the surprising part. Look closely: the current here (0.49 A) is actually higher than the current at the MPP (Imp = 0.48 A). Yet the power is lower. Why? Because we dropped the voltage from 2.35 V to 2.0 V — a loss of 0.35 V — to gain only 0.01 A of current. The voltage fell by much more (in proportion) than the current rose, so the product V·I went down.
Check: 0.98 W < 1.128 W, so operating off the MPP gives less power than the MPP — exactly as it must, since the MPP is by definition the maximum. Running at higher current did not help: power is the product, and we lost more on voltage than we gained on current. This is the IV curve's central lesson in one number.

1.6 Why the MPP matters in practice

Why obsess over a single point? Because every watt counts on a spacecraft. A solar array is heavy and expensive to launch, so engineers want the most power from the fewest cells. That means operating the array as close to its MPP as possible.

  • Some spacecraft use a dedicated electronic circuit, a maximum power point tracker, that continuously adjusts the load to sit the array right at its MPP — squeezing out the most watts at every moment.
  • Others use a simpler scheme: they hold the array at a fixed bus voltage chosen to be near Vmp under typical conditions. Cheaper, slightly less optimal.
Pitfall The MPP is not nailed down — it moves. Recall that temperature strongly affects Voc (a hotter cell gives less voltage), and that radiation slowly degrades the cell over years. Both shift the whole IV curve, and with it the MPP. An array sized for a cold, brand-new cell can fall well short when the cell is hot and aged. PowerPy exists precisely to predict these shifts; we study how temperature and degradation move the curve in later sections. For now, just hold the warning: the MPP you design for is not the MPP you will always have.
Key idea Operate near the MPP to get the most power per cell — but design with margin, because the MPP drifts with temperature and age. "Size for the worst case (hot, old), not the best case (cold, new)" is an engineer's habit you will use again and again.

1.7 Meeting these ideas in PowerPy — the cell API

Everything above maps onto a handful of PowerPy commands. You do not need to run anything now; just connect each command to the concept it computes, so the code feels familiar when you reach it.

Term API (Application Programming Interface) — the set of named commands a piece of software gives you to ask it to do things. PowerPy's cell API is just the list of instructions you type to build a cell and ask about it.
  • cell("3G30") — create a cell object. The string "3G30" names the cell type to model (a triple-junction, ~30 cm² space cell — the very cell whose numbers we have been using).
  • cell.calcQuad() — returns the four numbers plus the maximum power, as a bundle: {isc, voc, vmp, imp, pmp}. (Our four landmarks, with Pmp handed to you for free.)
  • cell.calcMP() — returns the Maximum Power Point: the (Vmp, Imp) where power peaks.
  • cell.calcIVCurve(step) — returns the IV table: a list of (V, I) points marching along the curve, where step sets how finely spaced the voltage samples are (a smaller step gives a denser, smoother table).
  • cell.IVCurve() — plots that table as the picture you saw above: current versus voltage.
Note Two methods sound similar but differ: cell.calcQuad() hands you the summary numbers (the landmarks), whereas cell.calcIVCurve(step) hands you the whole curve as a table of many points. Use the first when you only need Isc/Voc/MPP/FF; use the second when you need the full shape — for example to find the power at an arbitrary voltage, as in worked example (b).

Key idea — section recap A satellite runs on sunlight, captured by a solar array built up as cell → string → section → panel; PowerPy simulates that array's power and temperature so it can be sized and proven to survive orbit. One cell is described by four measured numbers — Isc (current at V=0), Voc (voltage at I=0), and the operating pair Vmp, Imp. Their product Pmp = Vmp·Imp is the maximum power, located at the MPP. The Fill Factor FF = Pmp / (Voc·Isc) ≈ 0.8 says how square the curve is. Power is zero at both ends of the IV curve (because it is the product V·I, and one factor vanishes at each end) and peaks in between, at the MPP — the balance point where voltage and current are both kept high together.
