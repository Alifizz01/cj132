3 How a cell changes over its life: temperature and radiation, and the prepareModel pipeline

In Section 2 we treated a solar cell as four fixed numbers feeding an equivalent circuit. That was a convenient lie. The moment you put a cell in orbit, those four numbers start to move — minute by minute as the cell heats and cools, and year by year as space radiation slowly wrecks the crystal. This section is the story of where the four numbers come from at any given instant, and of the small assembly line — PowerPy calls it prepareModel — that builds them from a fixed data file, a temperature, and a radiation dose.

Key idea A space solar cell's four headline numbers — Isc, Imp, Vmp, Voc — are not constants. They shift with temperature (cold makes a cell push out more voltage; hot makes it push out less) and they degrade as the cell soaks up radiation damage over the mission. PowerPy keeps, per cell type, a JSON file holding the room-temperature Beginning-of-Life values plus lookup curves that describe both shifts.

3.1 A one-line reminder of the four numbers

Before we move them around, recall what they are (Section 2 derived them from the IV curve):

Recap — the four operating-point numbers
  • Isc — short-circuit current (A). The current when the cell's terminals are shorted; the top-left of the IV curve.
  • Voc — open-circuit voltage (V). The voltage with no load drawn; the bottom-right of the IV curve.
  • Imp, Vmp — the current and voltage at the maximum-power point (the "knee"), where I·V is largest.

Everything in this section is about computing these four values for a specific temperature T and a specific accumulated radiation dose, then handing them to the circuit model.

3.2 Beginning of Life and End of Life

Term — BOL / EOL

BOL (Beginning Of Life) — the cell as it leaves the factory: fresh, undamaged, zero accumulated radiation. The numbers printed on a datasheet are BOL numbers.

EOL (End Of Life) — the same cell after it has absorbed the entire mission's radiation dose. Same silicon, same area, but the crystal is now peppered with defects, so it produces less current and less voltage. A mission is sized so that even the weakest, end-of-life array still meets the power budget — so EOL, not BOL, is usually the number that decides whether a satellite survives.

The gap between BOL and EOL is the whole reason this section exists. If cells never aged, you would size a solar array once and forget it. Because they do age, the simulator must know how far down each number has drifted by the time the cell reaches the dose your orbit delivers.

3.3 The cell configuration: what the JSON file holds

Each cell type ships as a small data file. It records the fixed, measured BOL properties of that cell plus the housekeeping PowerPy needs. The keys are:

{
  "name":           " exact part name of the cell ",
  "reference":      " datasheet / source the numbers came from ",
  "alpha":          0.91,      // solar absorptivity  (thermal: how much sunlight is absorbed)
  "epsilon":        0.85,      // IR emissivity       (thermal: how readily it radiates heat away)
  "isc":            0.50,      // short-circuit current        [A]
  "imp":            0.48,      // current at max-power point   [A]
  "vmp":            2.35,      // voltage at max-power point   [V]
  "voc":            2.67,      // open-circuit voltage         [V]
  "r_interconnect": 0.030,     // series resistance of the cell interconnect [ohm]
  "t_ref":          28,        // reference temperature the numbers were measured at [degC]
  "area":           30.18      // cell area [cm^2]
}
Reading the keys
  • alpha (solar absorptivity) and epsilon (IR emissivity) are thermal, not electrical. They decide how hot the cell gets: alpha is the fraction of incoming sunlight the cell absorbs (heats it up), epsilon is how efficiently it re-radiates that heat to space (cools it down). A cell's equilibrium temperature is set by the balance of the two. We only need them here to remember that T is itself a computed quantity, not a free knob.
  • t_ref (reference temperature) is the temperature at which isc, imp, vmp, voc were measured — typically 28 °C. Every temperature correction in this section is measured relative to t_ref: the correction is zero exactly at T = t_ref.
  • r_interconnect is the small series resistance of the metal tab joining cells; it feeds the series-resistance term of the Section-2 circuit.
  • area lets PowerPy scale a cell up or down (a half-size cell delivers half the current). The base isc/imp belong to this reference area.

3.4 Radiation dose, and why it is one number

Space is full of trapped electrons and protons — charged particles held by the planet's magnetic field (the Van Allen belts are the famous example). When one of these particles slams into the cell, it can knock an atom out of place in the crystal lattice. Each displacement is a new defect that traps charge carriers before they reach the contacts, so a little current and a little voltage are lost. Over years, the damage accumulates.

The awkward part: the particles arrive at a huge range of energies, and a 2 MeV proton does not do the same damage as a 0.5 MeV electron. Tracking the whole spectrum cell-by-cell would be hopeless. So the field uses a standard trick:

Term — equivalent 1 MeV electron fluence

Fluence is the total number of particles that have passed through one square centimetre over the whole mission (units: particles per cm²). The equivalent 1 MeV electron fluence collapses the entire messy spectrum of electrons and protons of all energies into one single number: "the dose that would have done the same damage if it had all been 1 MeV electrons." It is the universal currency of radiation damage — a single scalar standing in for the whole spectrum.

PowerPy lets you set this dose, and it does something subtle: it tracks the dose affecting current and the dose affecting voltage separately, because current and voltage degrade at different rates under the same radiation.

cell.setDose(dose_i, dose_v)
sets the current-degrading dose dose_i and the voltage-degrading dose dose_v.
Inputs are in units of 1×1014 e/cm². The code multiplies what you pass by 1e14 internally. So to model a fluence of 1×1015 e/cm² you pass 10, not 1e15.
Pitfall — the units-of-1e14 trap EXAM-HOT

The dose argument is pre-scaled: PowerPy multiplies it by 1×1014 for you. If you read a real fluence of 1e15 e/cm² off a radiation report and type cell.setDose(1e15, 1e15), you have just asked for 1×1015 × 1×1014 = 1×1029 e/cm² — a dose that would vaporise the solar system. The crystal "dies" instantly and every remaining factor collapses.

Right way: divide the real fluence by 1e14 first. 1×1015 e/cm² → pass 10. 5×1014 → pass 5. 3×1013 → pass 0.3.

3.5 Regressors: turning dose into a number

How does the simulator know that a dose of, say, 5×1014 e/cm² leaves 92 % of the current intact? It looks it up. For each cell type the JSON file carries a set of curves — PowerPy calls them regressors — and each is just an interpolation lookup: hand it a dose, it returns a number.

Term — regressor

A regressor here is a stored curve, evaluated by interpolation, that maps a radiation dose to a coefficient. Two families exist, and they answer two different questions.

Family 1 — remaining factors  risc, rimp, rvmp, rvoc. Each is the fraction of the BOL value still surviving after a given dose. At zero dose every remaining factor is exactly 1.0 (nothing has degraded yet); as dose climbs they fall, reaching roughly 0.8–0.9 at end of life. You multiply the BOL value by its remaining factor to get the radiation-degraded value.

Family 2 — temperature coefficients  isc_dt, imp_dt, vmp_dt, voc_dt. Each is a slope: how much the parameter changes per degree Celsius, d(param)/dT. Crucially, these slopes themselves depend on dose — a radiation-damaged cell responds to temperature slightly differently — which is why they are stored as regressors too, not as plain constants.

Signs you must memorise
  • isc_dt is small and POSITIVE. Heating a cell raises its current a little — a hot cell absorbs photons slightly more readily (the band gap narrows). Magnitude is tiny, order 10−4 A/°C.
  • voc_dt and vmp_dt are NEGATIVE and larger in magnitude. Heating a cell drops its voltage, and this is the effect that dominates. A typical figure is voc_dt ≈ −6 mV/°C per junction (a multi-junction cell stacks several, so the cell-level number scales up — treat −6 mV/°C as an illustrative per-junction scale, not the whole-cell value).

One sentence to carry the whole section: cold helps voltage, heat hurts it, and current barely cares.

3.6 The prepareModel pipeline

Now we can assemble the operating-point numbers. prepareModel is the routine that, given the cell's fixed JSON values, a temperature T (in °C), and the two doses, produces the four numbers at this instant. Read it as an assembly line — each step takes the running value and adjusts it.

Two inputs arrive from elsewhere in the simulator and we simply carry them through:

Pipeline — steps 1–2 (pointing & losses, taught in other sections)
1. Tilt (pointing).   tilt = cos(angle_alpha + season_angle) · cos(angle_beta). This is how squarely the sun strikes the panel; 1.0 means dead-on. (Geometry section.)
2. Combine loss factors.   lossI = product of all current-loss multipliers (including an inherited tilt and season factor);   lossV = product of all voltage-loss multipliers. (Losses section.) These are held aside and applied last.

Steps 3–7 are the heart of this section — the temperature-and-dose correction of each of the four numbers. Notice carefully which dose keys which parameter: Isc and Imp use dose_i; Vmp and Voc use dose_v.

Pipeline — steps 3–7 (the dose + temperature correction)
3. Look up the slopes.   imp_dt = imp_dt(dose_i),   isc_dt = isc_dt(dose_i). (When T < 0 °C a special low-temperature blend of the slope is used instead, because the response goes slightly non-linear in the deep cold — mentioned for completeness; we will not dwell on it.)
4. Short-circuit current:   isc = isc·risc(dose_i) + isc_dt(dose_i)·(T − t_ref)
5. Max-power current:   imp = imp·rimp(dose_i) + imp_dt·(T − t_ref)
6. Max-power voltage:   vmp = vmp·rvmp(dose_v) + vmp_dt(dose_v)·(T − t_ref)
7. Open-circuit voltage:   voc = voc·rvoc(dose_v) + voc_dt(dose_v)·(T − t_ref)
Every line has the same shape: (BOL value × remaining factor) + (slope × temperature offset from t_ref). The first term is the radiation hit; the second term is the temperature shift. At T = t_ref the second term vanishes, and at zero dose every remaining factor is 1.0 — so a fresh cell at its reference temperature returns exactly the datasheet number. ✓
Pipeline — step 8 (apply losses, taught in the losses section)
8. Apply the illumination/season voltage corrections and the loss factors held from step 2:
isc ·= lossI;   imp ·= lossI;   vmp ·= lossV·vmp_corr;   voc ·= lossV·voc_corr
The result — (isc, imp, vmp, voc) at this temperature and this dose — is exactly the four-number set that the Section-2 equivalent-circuit model consumes to draw the IV curve. The pipeline's whole job is to feed that model honest, life-stage-correct numbers. ✓
Mental model

Steps 4–7 move each number to the right place for the cell's age and temperature. Step 8 then shaves off the optical and wiring losses that the rest of the system imposes. The worked examples that follow stop at the end of step 7 — at the bare dose-and-temperature operating point — because the loss factors are a separate downstream multiply taught elsewhere. Do not apply lossI/lossV to them.

3.7 Worked examples

These walk steps 4–7 with real numbers and units. They stop before step 8 (no loss factors applied).

Worked 3A — Voc of a cold cell

Given: base voc = 2.67 V at t_ref = 28 °C;   voc_dt = −6.0×10−3 V/°C;   rvoc(dose) = 0.95;   the cell is cold at T = −80 °C. Apply step 7.

Radiation term:   voc · rvoc  =  2.67 V × 0.95  =  2.5365 V.  (radiation alone has pulled it down from 2.67 V)
Temperature offset:   ΔT = T − t_ref = (−80) − (28) = −108 °C.  (the cell is 108 degrees colder than the reference)
Slope term:   voc_dt · ΔT  =  (−0.006 V/°C) × (−108 °C)  =  +0.648 V.  (negative slope × negative ΔT → a POSITIVE bump: cold adds voltage)
Sum:   voc = 2.5365 V + 0.648 V = 3.18 V.
Sanity check on the sign. The cell started at 2.67 V; radiation tried to lower it, but the deep cold raised it all the way to 3.18 V — above the BOL value. That is exactly right: going cold RAISES the voltage, and at −80 °C the temperature gain (+0.648 V) easily beats the radiation loss (−0.13 V). If you had gotten a number below 2.5365 V you would have flipped a sign somewhere. ✓
Worked 3B — Isc of a hot cell at EOL

Given: base isc = 0.50 A;   risc(dose) = 0.97 (end-of-life dose);   isc_dt = +0.30×10−3 A/°C;   the cell is hot at T = +70 °C. Apply step 4.

Radiation term:   isc · risc  =  0.50 A × 0.97  =  0.485 A.  (EOL radiation has knocked the current down from 0.50 A to 0.485 A)
Temperature offset:   ΔT = 70 − 28 = +42 °C.
Slope term:   isc_dt · ΔT  =  (+0.0003 A/°C) × (+42 °C)  =  +0.0126 A.  (positive slope × positive ΔT → heat adds a little current)
Sum:   isc = 0.485 A + 0.0126 A = 0.498 A.
Read the reference point carefully. The current barely moves, and it moves up — but "up" here means relative to the radiation-degraded 0.485 A, not the BOL 0.50 A. The full story: radiation drops it from 0.50 A to 0.485 A, then the +70 °C heat nudges it back up to 0.498 A — still slightly under the BOL value. Current is almost temperature-insensitive (the slope is tiny and positive), which is exactly why isc_dt is the small positive coefficient. ✓
Worked 3C — the formula collapses to the BOL number

Claim: at T = t_ref and zero dose, every step 4–7 line returns the original datasheet value. Take Voc with base voc = 2.67 V.

Zero dose ⇒ remaining factor = 1.0:   voc · rvoc(0) = 2.67 V × 1.0 = 2.67 V.
T = t_ref ⇒ ΔT = 0:   voc_dt · (t_ref − t_ref) = voc_dt × 0 = 0 V, whatever the slope.
Sum:   voc = 2.67 V + 0 V = 2.67 V — the BOL datasheet value, unchanged.
This is the consistency guarantee: the same one formula that corrects for cold, heat, and radiation reduces to "do nothing" at the conditions where the numbers were measured. A correction that did not vanish at its own reference point would be a bug. ✓

3.8 Why colder means more voltage — a Socratic ladder

Ladder — Why does a solar cell make MORE voltage when it is COLDER?
Q1. What is a "temperature coefficient" in the first place?
It is a slope: how much a quantity changes for each 1 °C change in temperature, d(param)/dT. For voltage we wrote it voc_dt, in volts per °C. Multiply it by how far the temperature is from t_ref and you get the voltage shift. It is the bridge between "temperature changed" and "the number changed."
Q2. What is the SIGN of that coefficient for voltage, and what does the sign mean physically?
It is negative (e.g. voc_dt ≈ −6 mV/°C per junction). A negative slope means: as T goes up, voltage goes down; and as T goes down, voltage goes up. So cold raises voltage follows directly from the minus sign — exactly what Worked 3A showed numerically (ΔT went negative, and −×− gave a positive bump).
Q3. But WHY is the sign negative? What is actually happening inside the cell?
The cell is a diode in the light. Its open-circuit voltage is set by a tug-of-war between the light-generated current and the diode's own saturation current I0 — the tiny leakage current that flows backwards through the junction. I0 depends extremely strongly on temperature: it is governed by how many charge carriers have enough thermal energy to cross the band gap, which is an exponential function of temperature. When the cell gets colder, far fewer carriers have that energy, so I0 plummets. A smaller I0 means the diode leaks less, so the light current can hold the junction at a higher voltage before the two balance — Voc rises. Heat does the reverse: I0 climbs, the junction leaks more, Voc sags.
Q4. Tie it back to Section 2 — where did we already meet this I0?
It is the i0_cell term in the Section-2 equivalent-circuit model — the diode's reverse saturation current that sets where the IV curve bends down to meet the voltage axis. Temperature does not appear as a separate knob in that circuit; instead, prepareModel bakes the temperature effect into the value of Voc it hands the circuit (step 7). So the "cold → more voltage" physics and the circuit's i0_cell are two views of one mechanism: cold suppresses i0_cell, which raises Voc.
Q5. (Trap-spotter) A classmate says: "voltage comes from kT/q, and that term gets smaller when it's cold, so surely cold should LOWER the voltage." Where is the error?
The kT/q prefactor does shrink in the cold, taken alone — but it is not the dominant term. Voc ≈ (kT/q)·ln(Ilight/I0 + 1), and I0 sits inside the logarithm with an exponential temperature dependence. As the cell cools, I0 falls so steeply that the logarithm grows faster than the kT/q prefactor shrinks. The exponential collapse of I0 wins decisively, so Voc rises overall. The classmate fixated on the small prefactor and missed the big exponential — the classic student trap.

3.9 The API you will actually call

PowerPy cell methods
  • cell.setCell(type) — load a cell type's JSON config (the ten keys of §3.3).
  • cell.setTemperature(T) — set the operating temperature T in °C (drives steps 3–7).
  • cell.setDose(di, dv) — set the current and voltage doses, in units of 1e14 e/cm² (§3.4 pitfall).
  • cell.resizeCell(area) — scale the cell to a new area in cm² (rescales the currents).
  • cell.showTempCoefficients() — print the temperature-coefficient regressors (the isc_dt … voc_dt slopes).
  • cell.showRemainingFactors() — print the remaining-factor regressors (the risc … rvoc curves).

A typical setup is: setCell → resizeCell → setTemperature → setDose, after which prepareModel has everything it needs to produce the four operating-point numbers.
