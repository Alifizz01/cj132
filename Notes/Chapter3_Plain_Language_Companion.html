Words You Need First

Meet these once, in plain words. Every one is explained properly later — this is just so no sentence ahead of you contains a word that's still fog. They're all in the Glossary too.

Voltage (volts, V) The push. How hard the electricity is being shoved. Think water pressure. Higher voltage = harder shove.
Current (amps, A) The flow. How much electricity is actually moving past a point each second. Think litres of water per second through a pipe.
Power (watts, W) How much work per second is being done. Power = push × flow = volts × amps. A 10 W bulb turns 10 joules of energy into light+heat every second.
Solar cell A flat wafer that turns sunlight into electricity. One cell makes a small push (a couple of volts). We wire many together to get a useful amount.
Series Devices connected in a line, one after another, so the same flow goes through all of them and their pushes add up. (Cells in series → more volts.)
Parallel Devices connected side by side, sharing the same two end-points, so their flows add up but the push stays the same. (Cells in parallel → more amps.)
Bias Which way the voltage is pushing a device. Forward bias = pushed the way it likes (it makes power). Reverse bias = pushed backwards (bad — it eats power as heat).
Hot-spot One small place that gets dangerously hotter than everything around it. A failed cell being pushed backwards becomes a hot-spot.
Substrate The board the cells are glued to. Here it's an aluminium honeycomb panel — light and stiff, but it softens and melts if a hot-spot cooks it.
Flux Just "how much energy per second lands on each square metre" of a surface. Sunlight is a flux; the Earth glowing below you is another flux.
Albedo The fraction of sunlight a planet bounces back. Earth's albedo ≈ 0.3 → about 30% of the sun hitting Earth reflects back up and can warm your panel from below.
Iterate / iteration To repeat a step over and over. Two very different meanings hide in this word — Section 10 untangles them, because that's exactly what tripped you up.
Vectorise To do the same sum on a whole list of numbers in one fast go, instead of looping through them one at a time. Section 10.

1Electricity as Water

Almost every confusing thing later is really a statement about electricity. If you carry one picture the whole way, carry this one: electricity is water in pipes.

The picture Voltage = water pressure (how hard it's pushed). Current = flow rate (how much moves per second). Power = pressure × flow (how much actual work the water can do — spin a wheel, lift a bucket). A tall water tower gives high pressure (voltage); a fat open pipe gives high flow (current). To get a lot of work done you want both.
Power  P = V × I
P = power in watts (W) · V = voltage in volts (V) · I = current in amps (A)
Worked example — a single cell making power
A healthy cell pushes at V = 2.3 V and flows I = 0.48 A.
Power = 2.3 × 0.48 = 1.1 W. So this one cell delivers about 1.1 joules of energy every second.
Check: both numbers are positive (push and flow point the same way), so the power is positive — the cell is a giver of energy. Good. Remember the sign; in Section 4 it flips.
Ladder · getting the three words solid
Q1. If voltage is "push" and current is "flow", which one is litres-per-second?
Current. Flow = how much moves per second = amps. Voltage is the pressure behind it.
Q2. I double the voltage but the current stays the same. What happens to the power?
Power = V × I, so doubling V doubles the power. Twice the push, same flow → twice the work.
Q3. A device has lots of voltage across it but zero current flowing. How much power?
Zero. P = V × 0 = 0. A pressure with nothing flowing does no work — like a capped pipe under pressure: nothing moves, nothing gets done.
Q4. Now the hard one: the voltage across a device is negative (pushing backwards) while current still flows forward. Is the device giving power or eating it?
Eating it. P = V × I with V negative gives a negative number. Negative "given" power means it's actually absorbing energy — taking energy out of the circuit and turning it into heat. Hold this thought: it is the whole reason a dead solar cell becomes a heater (Section 4).
Practice 1 A cell runs at V = 2.0 V and I = 0.50 A. (a) What is its power? (b) Is it giving or eating energy?

2A Solar Cell, and What "Failing" Means

A solar cell is a one-way energy door. Light comes in; electricity comes out. To use it we must know two things: how it behaves when healthy, and what "failed" actually does to it.

Solar cell A flat wafer that, when light hits it, pushes electricity out one terminal and pulls it in the other. One of these triple-junction space cells pushes about 2.3–2.6 V. On its own that's tiny — a single AA battery is 1.5 V — so we wire many together (Section 3).
The picture Think of each cell as one person on a rowing team. Healthy, they pull their oar and add to the boat's speed. But a cell can fail: crack from a micro-meteorite, lose its connection, or just die of old radiation damage. A failed cell is a rower who has stopped pulling. The problem isn't only that the boat loses one rower — it's what happens when the others keep pulling and drag the dead one along backwards. That backward drag is the villain of this whole story.
Why we care A satellite can't be repaired. So before launch we want to predict: if a cell dies here versus there, how bad is it? Does it just lose a little power, or does it cook a hole in the panel? That prediction is what the whole new code is for.
Ladder · what "healthy" and "failed" mean
Q1. A healthy cell in sunlight — is it giving energy to the circuit or taking it?
Giving. Light energy goes in, electrical energy comes out. Its power (V × I) is positive.
Q2. A cell "fails open" (its wire breaks). Can current still flow through it?
No — an open wire is a cut pipe. No path, no flow. But the cells around it are still pushing, and that push has to go somewhere…
Q3. A cell sits in the shade while its neighbours are in full sun. It can't make its own push. What do the bright neighbours do to it?
They push current through it the wrong way — backwards. The shaded/dead cell becomes a roadblock the others shove against. We'll see in Section 4 that "shoved backwards" = reverse bias = heat.
Q4. So which is worse for the panel: a cell that makes less power, or a cell that gets pushed backwards?
Pushed backwards, by far. Less power is just a smaller giver. Pushed backwards turns the cell from a giver into an eater — and everything it eats becomes heat in one tiny spot. That's the hot-spot.

3Series & Parallel — Cells Working as a Team

One cell is too weak. So we connect many. There are exactly two ways to connect them, and the difference between them is the difference between "add the push" and "add the flow".

The picture Series = people standing in a line, each pushing the person in front. One person's shove plus the next plus the next — the pushes add up, but the same single line of motion runs through everyone. Parallel = several people pushing the same heavy cart side-by-side. They all push at the same one "pressure", but together they move more cart per second — the flows add.
Remember Series adds voltage. Parallel adds current. That's the entire electrical rule of this chapter. Everything the Circuit class does is repeat this rule at four levels: cells→line (series), lines→module (parallel), modules→block (series), blocks→array (parallel).
Ladder · build up to "why one dead cell hurts a whole line"
Q1. Ten cells in series, each pushing 2.3 V. Total push?
10 × 2.3 = 23 V. Series adds the voltages.
Q2. In that line of ten, how much current flows through cell #1 versus cell #7?
Exactly the same. In a series line there is only one path, so one single current runs through every cell. (This is the key fact for the next rung.)
Q3. Cell #7 dies and stops making its push, but the other nine are still pushing 2.3 V each. The current still wants to flow through the whole line (Q2). What must happen at cell #7?
The nine live cells force the line's current through the dead cell #7. But #7 isn't pushing that way anymore — so the current is being shoved through it backwards. #7 is now reverse-biased.
Q4. The nine good cells together push about 9 × 2.3 ≈ 21 V. If almost all of that lands across the one dead cell, what is the voltage across cell #7 — and which sign?
Around −20 V (negative = backwards). One little cell now has the whole line's push dumped across it, the wrong way. Next section: that negative voltage, times the current still flowing, is a big negative power — i.e. a big heater.
Practice 2 A "module" is 2 lines in parallel; each line is 5 cells in series at 2.3 V and 0.48 A per cell. (a) Voltage of one line? (b) Voltage of the module? (c) Current of the module?

4Reverse Bias — How a Dead Cell Becomes a Heater

This is the heart of the whole project, and it's just the sign-flip from Section 1 made real. Let's earn it slowly.

Reverse bias A device is reverse-biased when the voltage across it points the opposite way to the direction it normally pushes. For a solar cell, forward = "making power"; reverse = "having power forced into it". Same word "bias" you met up front — it just means "which way it's pushed".
Worked example — the cell turns from giver to heater
Healthy: V = +2.3 V, I = 0.48 A → P = +1.1 W. Positive = it gives 1.1 W to the circuit. Light in, electricity out.
Dead & reverse-biased: the line forces V = −20 V across it while I = 0.48 A still flows.
P = (−20) × (0.48) = −9.6 W.
The minus sign means it is no longer giving — it is absorbing 9.6 W. Energy can't vanish, so where does it go? Into heat, right there in the wafer.
Check the size: 9.6 W in a thumbnail-sized cell is like pressing a 10 W soldering-iron tip onto the panel and leaving it. That is exactly why it can melt things.
Ladder · from "negative number" to "physical heater"
Q1. Power came out negative. Numbers don't burn anything — what does a negative power physically mean?
Our sign convention is "positive = the cell gives energy out". So negative = the cell receives energy from the rest of the circuit instead of giving it.
Q2. The cell receives 9.6 W of electrical energy every second. It isn't storing it (it's not a battery) and it isn't making light. By conservation of energy, where must it go?
It must come back out as some other form. The only option for a resistor-like lump of silicon is heat. Electrical energy in with nowhere to go → temperature up.
Q3. Why is reverse-bias heat so much worse than the cell simply making 1.1 W less?
Two reasons. (1) The magnitude: −9.6 W is nearly 9× bigger than the 1.1 W it used to give. (2) The concentration: a whole line's worth of energy is dumped into one cell-sized spot instead of spread out. Big energy, tiny area = very high temperature.
Q4. The reverse voltage was about −20 V. Where did that exact number come from?
From Section 3, Q4: the other ~9 live cells in the line each push ~2.3 V, and with the dead cell being the only "gap", nearly the whole stacked push (≈21 V) lands across it. More cells in the line → more reverse voltage available → a worse hot-spot. That's a layout lever we can design with.
Practice 3 A dead cell is forced to −15 V while 0.50 A flows through it. (a) Its power? (b) Is it giving or absorbing? (c) How many watts of heat does it produce?

5Hot-Spots — Why a Heater Can Melt the Panel

We now have a 9.6 W heater stuck on the back of a satellite. Why is that a catastrophe and not just a warm patch? Because of where the heat is and what it's sitting on.

Honeycomb substrate The cells are glued onto a panel built like a honeycomb of thin aluminium walls sandwiched between two skins — incredibly light and stiff, which is why spacecraft use it. But aluminium softens around 400–600 °C and the glue/adhesive gives up far sooner. Concentrate enough heat and you get a melted pit, a delaminated cell, even a hole.
The picture Spreading 9.6 W over a whole dinner plate barely warms it. Putting the same 9.6 W under a single coin makes that coin scorching. The reverse-biased cell is the coin: small area, big power, and in space there's no air to carry the heat away — it can only radiate. So the temperature climbs until the cell glows enough to shed 9.6 W. In the model, that lands near 187 °C — hot enough to threaten the adhesive and the honeycomb.
Where we are Dead cell → forced backwards (reverse bias, Section 4) → absorbs ~9.6 W → all of it becomes heat in a tiny spot (this section) → temperature soars → can melt the honeycomb. The rest of the chapter is about predicting that temperature number, because the temperature is what tells us "safe" vs "melted".
Ladder · why "watts" alone don't tell you if it melts
Q1. Two heaters both dump 9.6 W. One is spread over 1 m², the other over 1 cm². Which gets hotter?
The 1 cm² one, dramatically. Same total power, far less area to get rid of it through → it must reach a much higher temperature to shed the same energy.
Q2. On Earth a hot panel is cooled by air blowing past. In orbit there's no air. So how does a cell in space get rid of heat at all?
Only by radiating — glowing it away as infrared light. No conduction to air, no convection. This is slow, so the cell has to get quite hot before it can radiate the heat away as fast as it's made.
Q3. Why does "it can only radiate" make the temperature go up?
Because radiating faster requires being hotter (hot things glow more — Section 6). The cell heats up until its glow finally carries away 9.6 W per second. If that balance point is above the melt limit, the panel is damaged before it ever reaches balance.
Q4. So what's the single number that decides "safe" vs "melted"?
The cell's balance temperature — the temperature at which heat-out equals heat-in. Compute it, compare it to the melt limit. Computing that number for every cell is exactly what the thermal solver does.

6Heat as a Bathtub — Energy In, Energy Out

To find that balance temperature we need one idea: in steady state, heat-in equals heat-out. The bathtub makes it obvious.

The picture A bathtub with the tap running and the drain open. If water pours in faster than it drains, the level rises. If it drains faster than it pours, the level falls. The level sits still only when in-rate = out-rate. Temperature is the water level. Heat flowing in (sunlight, the reverse-bias heat) is the tap. Heat radiated away is the drain. The cell's temperature climbs or falls until in = out, then holds. That holding value is the balance temperature.
The Stefan–Boltzmann law (the "drain") A warm surface radiates heat away at a rate proportional to its temperature to the fourth power: heat-out ∝ T⁴. The T⁴ is why the drain is a gentle trickle when cold and a torrent when hot — double the (absolute) temperature and the glow jumps 16-fold. This is the "hot things shed heat much faster" fact we kept leaning on.
heat radiated per second = ε · σ · A · T⁴
ε = emissivity (how good a glower the surface is, 0–1) · σ = a fixed constant of nature (5.67×10⁻⁸) · A = area · T = absolute temperature in kelvin (°C + 273.15)
Ladder · the bathtub, then the T⁴ twist
Q1. Tap pours 5 litres/min, drain removes 5 litres/min. Is the level rising, falling, or still?
Still. In equals out, so nothing changes. This "still" state is what we mean by steady state / equilibrium / balance.
Q2. Translate that to heat: what is true about heat-in and heat-out when the temperature has stopped changing?
They're equal. Heat-in = heat-out. Solving the thermal problem just means: find the temperature that makes that equation true.
Q3. The drain depends on temperature (T⁴): hotter cell → faster drain. If we suddenly turn the tap up (more heat in), what does the cell do?
It warms up — which speeds the drain — until the faster drain again matches the bigger tap. It finds a new, higher balance temperature. More heat in → hotter equilibrium.
Q4. Why is the T⁴ (not just T) actually good news for the cell's survival?
Because the drain ramps up so steeply with temperature that even a big tap is matched without the temperature running away to infinity. A small temperature rise buys a large increase in heat shed. It's a self-limiting brake — the reason a 9.6 W hot-spot lands at ~187 °C and not 5000 °C.
Practice 4 In words (no arithmetic): a cell is sitting at its balance temperature. You add a second, smaller heat source on it. Describe what happens to its temperature and why it doesn't rise forever.

7Two Temperatures — the Front/Rear Model

A real cell-on-panel isn't one temperature — the sunlit front and the shaded rear differ. So the model tracks two temperatures and lets heat trickle between them. That's the "two-node" model.

Node Just "a point we track a temperature at". Two nodes = two points: the front face (T₁) and the rear face (T₂). It's the simplest model richer than "one lump".
The picture A house wall on a sunny day. The outside is hot, the inside is cooler, and heat seeps through the wall from hot side to cool side. Front face = outside (sun hits it, it glows to space). Rear face = inside (it glows toward Earth/space behind). The panel between them lets heat conduct through — fast if the panel conducts well, slow if it insulates.
Three taps and drains per face For each face the bathtub gets a few inlets/outlets: sunlight + earthshine + the electrical/reverse-bias heat are taps; its own glow to space is a drain; and the conduction through the panel is a pipe connecting the two tubs. The model writes "in = out" for the front tub and "in = out" for the rear tub — two balance equations, two unknown temperatures.
Ladder · why two, and why they're linked
Q1. Why not just use one temperature for the whole cell?
Because the two faces really do differ — the front bakes in sun while the rear faces cold space or a dim Earth. One number would hide a difference that matters for where the cell sits thermally.
Q2. Heat conducts through the panel from front to rear. Which direction does it flow — and what sets how fast?
From the hotter face to the cooler one (heat always flows "downhill" in temperature). How fast = the panel's conductivity divided by its thickness; the code calls this c_cond. Thin, conductive panel → fast sharing → the two temperatures stay close.
Q3. If the panel conducted heat perfectly, what would happen to T₁ and T₂?
They'd become equal — perfect sharing erases the difference, and the two-node model collapses back to one temperature. The two-node model matters precisely because real panels share heat imperfectly.
Q4. We now have two balance equations (front tub, rear tub) and two unknowns (T₁, T₂), and they're tangled together because the conduction term has both. Can we just solve T₁ first, then T₂?
No — each equation contains both temperatures, so they must be solved together. And each equation has a T⁴ in it, so it's not a simple straight-line equation either. That "two tangled, curved equations at once" is what Newton's method (Section 9) is built to crack.

8The Chicken-and-Egg — Why We "Solve" Instead of Just Computing

Here's the subtlety that makes this hard, and the reason a loop appears at all. The heat-out depends on the temperature — but the temperature is the very thing we're trying to find.

The picture Imagine a thermostat with no display. You want to know the room's final temperature, but the heater's output depends on the room temperature, which depends on the heater's output… You can't write the answer in one line because the unknown appears on both sides. You have to hunt for the value that's consistent with itself.
Why can't we just plug in numbers and get T?

Because the balance equation looks like  heat-in = ε·σ·A·T⁴. To get T out of T⁴ you'd take a fourth root — fine if heat-in were a fixed number. But heat-in also contains T (the conduction between faces depends on the temperatures, and the electrical heat depends on temperature too). So T is on both sides, wrapped inside a fourth power. There is no clean "T = …" you can write down. We must solve it numerically — guess, check, improve.

This is the iteration you keep hearing about "Iteration" here means: make a guess for T, see how wrong it is, nudge it, repeat until it stops changing. That repeating is unavoidable for a T-on-both-sides equation. Hold this thought — in Section 10 we'll separate this good, necessary iteration from the bad, slow kind that we deleted.

Ladder · what "solve" even means
Q1. "Compute" vs "solve" — what's the difference?
Computing is one-directional: inputs → formula → answer (like 2+2). Solving is for when the unknown appears on both sides, so you can't go straight through; you search for the value that makes the equation balance.
Q2. Why does T appearing on both sides force a search?
Because you can't isolate it. Every attempt to move T to one side leaves another T (inside T⁴ or inside the conduction term) on the other. No rearrangement frees it, so we test candidate values instead.
Q3. A naive search: "try 20 °C, too cold; try 200 °C, too hot; try 100…". Would that work?
Yes, eventually — that's just hot-and-cold guessing (bisection). It works but is slow. We'd like a smarter guesser that uses how wrong it was to jump near the answer. That smarter guesser is Newton's method.
Q4. So is the loop in our thermal solver a sign the code is inefficient?
No! The loop is mathematically required by the T-on-both-sides equation; no amount of cleverness removes it. What we can remove is a different loop — the one that repeated this whole solve cell-by-cell in slow Python. That's the Section 10 distinction, and it's the one that confused you.

9Newton's Method — Clever Guessing

Newton's method is just hot-and-cold guessing with a brain: instead of blindly halving, it uses the slope of the curve to leap almost straight to the answer.

The picture You're on a hillside in fog, trying to reach the valley floor (where the "wrongness" is zero). You can't see the bottom, but you can feel the slope under your feet. Newton's trick: look at the slope right where you stand, and step to where that straight slope would hit the floor. From there, feel the new slope, step again. For smooth curves you arrive in a handful of steps — the number of correct digits roughly doubles each step.

next guess = this guess − (how wrong it is) ÷ (the slope here)
"how wrong" = the residual f(T) (heat-in minus heat-out); "slope" = the derivative f′(T). Dividing by the slope is what turns a big error into the right-sized step.
Worked example — feel one Newton step (find √10 by hand)
Same maths, friendlier numbers. We want T with T² = 10, i.e. drive f(T)=T²−10 to zero. Slope f′(T)=2T.
Guess T = 3. Wrongness f = 3²−10 = −1 (a bit low). Slope = 2·3 = 6.
Next = 3 − (−1)/6 = 3 + 0.1667 = 3.1667.
Again: f = 3.1667²−10 = 0.0278; slope = 6.333; next = 3.1667 − 0.0278/6.333 = 3.1623.
Check: real √10 = 3.16228. Two steps from a rough guess of 3 and we're correct to 5 digits. That doubling-of-accuracy is why the thermal solver needs only a handful of steps, not hundreds.
The 2-node twist We have two unknowns (T₁, T₂), so "the slope" becomes a little 2×2 table of slopes — the Jacobian (just "the slopes of two equations with respect to two unknowns"). The code writes that 2×2 by hand and inverts it with the schoolbook formula. Same idea, two dimensions.
Ladder · why the slope, and why it's fast
Q1. Why use the slope at all — why not just halve the interval each time (hot/cold)?
The slope tells you not just which way you're wrong but roughly how far. Halving ignores the "how far" and creeps; the slope lets you leap close in one move.
Q2. In "step = wrongness ÷ slope", why divide by the slope?
A steep slope means a small move in T changes the wrongness a lot → take a small step. A shallow slope means you must move far to fix the same error → big step. Dividing by the slope sizes the step automatically.
Q3. "The correct digits roughly double each step." Starting from 1 correct digit, how many steps to ~8 correct digits?
1 → 2 → 4 → 8: about three steps. That's why "max 50 iterations" in the code is wild over-provision — it almost always finishes in 4–6.
Q4. When could Newton misbehave?
If the slope is near zero (a flat spot — you'd divide by almost nothing and leap wildly), or if the first guess is far off on a wiggly curve. Our heat curve is smooth and steep (thanks to T⁴), and we start from a sensible ~28 °C, so it behaves. The code still guards the divide just in case.
Practice 5 Use one Newton step to improve a guess for √20. Start at T = 4. (f = T²−20, slope = 2T.) Show wrongness, slope, and the next guess.

10"No Iteration" & Vectorising — the Part That Confused You

You wrote: "I can barely understand why you said there is no iteration, and why we vectorise." Totally fair — the notes used "iteration" for two different things. Let's pull them apart cleanly.

The trap — one word, two meanings Meaning A — Newton iteration (KEPT): repeating "guess → correct → guess" until the temperature stops changing. This is the necessary math from Section 8. It stays.
Meaning B — the per-cell Python loop (REMOVED): the old code wrapped that whole solve in a slow Python for-loop that walked the cells one at a time — solve cell 1, then cell 2, then cell 3… This is the "iteration" we deleted. When the notes say "no iteration", they mean this loop, not Newton's.
The picture 1000 exam papers to grade, each needing the same 3-step mark scheme. Old way (Python loop): one teacher picks up paper 1, does steps 1-2-3, puts it down, picks up paper 2… 1000 times. The 3 steps are necessary (that's Newton). The "one paper at a time by one slow teacher" is the waste. New way (vectorise): hand the whole stack to a machine that applies step 1 to all 1000 papers at once, then step 2 to all 1000, then step 3. Same 3 steps — but done across the entire stack simultaneously. We didn't remove the steps; we removed the one-at-a-time queue.
Vectorise Put all the cells' numbers into one array (a row of numbers), and apply each math step to the whole array at once using numpy — a library that does array arithmetic in fast compiled code. One instruction touches a thousand cells. The Python interpreter's slow per-item overhead is paid once, not a thousand times.
Why it's allowed here Vectorising works because the cells are independent: in this two-node model there's no sideways heat flow from one cell to its neighbour (only front↔rear within a cell). So solving cell 57 doesn't need cell 56's answer — they can all be solved in parallel. If cells were coupled to each other, we couldn't just blast them all at once.
Ladder · separate the two "iterations", then see the speed-up
Q1. Name the two different things the word "iteration" was used for.
(A) Newton's "guess-correct-repeat" until T settles — necessary, kept. (B) The Python for-loop that solved cells one-by-one — wasteful, removed. The notes' "no iteration" means (B).
Q2. Did vectorising change the physics or the answer?
Not at all. Same equations, same Newton steps, same temperatures. Only the bookkeeping changed — all cells processed together instead of in a queue. It's a speed change, not a physics change.
Q3. Why is the Python one-at-a-time loop slow in the first place?
Python checks types and manages each operation individually — fine once, but a huge tax when repeated a million times (N cells × many Newton steps). numpy hands the whole array to pre-compiled C that skips that per-item tax. Typical result: tens to hundreds of times faster.
Q4. Could we vectorise if each cell's temperature depended on its neighbours' temperatures?
Not as simply — they'd be tangled together like T₁ and T₂ were, and you'd need to solve a big linked system, not N independent little ones. We get the easy win precisely because the model has no cell-to-cell heat conduction, so the cells are independent. That independence is the gift that makes "solve them all at once" correct.
Q5 (top rung). Put it in one sentence: what did we remove and what did we keep?
We kept the mathematically required repeat-until-settled (Newton) and removed the only-an-artifact- of-slow-code cell-by-cell Python loop, by solving every cell's two-node balance simultaneously with numpy. "No iteration" = no slow per-cell loop, not "no Newton".

11Electro-Thermal Coupling — the Thermostat Loop

One more loop, and it's a different chicken-and-egg from Section 8. Here the electricity and the temperature each depend on the other, so we bounce between them until they agree.

The picture A thermostat-and-heater pair settling down. The heater's output depends on the temperature; the temperature depends on the heater's output. You can't know either first — so the room hunts: heat a bit, temperature rises, heater eases, temperature settles… After a few swings it lands on the one state where both agree. Our cell does the same dance between how much electrical heat it makes and how hot it is.
Why does electricity depend on temperature, and vice-versa?

A solar cell's voltage and current shift with temperature (hot cells push a little less). So the electrical heat it dumps depends on its temperature. But its temperature (Sections 6-7) depends on how much electrical heat it's dumping. Round and round. So we loop: guess a temperature → compute the electricity and its heat at that temperature → feed that heat into the thermal solver → get a new temperature → repeat until the temperature stops moving.

Fixed point The state that maps to itself: put it in, run the model, get the same state back out. The loop is searching for that self-consistent "everybody agrees" temperature.
Damping (the omega knob) Instead of jumping fully to each new guess (which can overshoot and oscillate), we move only part way — a blend of old and new. Like nudging a shower tap gently instead of slamming it hot-cold-hot. It trades a few extra loops for a calm, sure landing.
Ladder · the loop that makes two things agree
Q1. How is this loop different from the Newton loop in Section 9?
Newton solves one equation (the heat balance) for temperature. This outer loop makes two whole models — the electrical one and the thermal one — agree with each other. It wraps around the thermal solve.
Q2. Why can't we compute the electricity once and be done?
Because the electricity depends on temperature, and we don't know the final temperature until we know the electrical heat, which depends on temperature… the same both-sides knot as Section 8, now between two models. So we iterate them to agreement.
Q3. What does "the loop has converged" mean in plain words?
The temperature came back essentially unchanged after another round (it moved less than a tiny tolerance). Both models now agree; nothing more will move; that's the answer.
Q4. Why bother damping (moving only part-way each round)? Wouldn't full jumps be faster?
Full jumps can overshoot and bounce back and forth forever (hot, cold, hot, cold). A partial, blended move kills the bounce so it spirals calmly into the answer. A little slower per step, but it actually arrives — and reliably arriving beats fast-but-oscillating.

12Monte-Carlo — Answering With Dice

We want to know which failures are dangerous. But the number of possible failure combinations is astronomically large — we can't try them all. So we sample: try many random ones and look at the spread. That's Monte-Carlo.

The picture You want the average height of everyone in a huge city. Measuring all million is impossible. So you measure a random few thousand and average them — close enough, and the more you measure the closer you get. Monte-Carlo is "ask a random sample instead of everyone." The name is a nod to the casino: we're literally rolling dice to pick which cells fail.
Monte-Carlo method Estimate an answer about a huge set of possibilities by drawing many random samples from it and looking at the results, instead of enumerating every possibility. Here: randomly fail some cells, run the thermal model, record the worst temperature; repeat thousands of times; see which patterns tend to cook the panel.
Three ways to choose failures (the "three modes") Position sweep: fail each single cell in turn — "if exactly this one dies, how bad?" (maps the map). Count sweep: fail a fixed number of random cells (2, 3, 4…) — "how bad when k cells die?". Random sampling: each cell fails with some probability — realistic mixed scenarios. Together they answer "where", "how many", and "realistically".
The 1/√N rule The estimate's wobble shrinks like 1 over the square root of the number of runs N. Want half the wobble? Run four times as many. Want a tenth? A hundred times as many. Diminishing returns — which is exactly why we estimate how many runs we need rather than running forever.
Worked example — how many runs for a target accuracy
Suppose one batch of 100 runs gives a spread (standard error) of 8 °C, and we want it down to 2 °C.
Accuracy improves like 1/√N, so to cut the wobble by a factor of 4 (8→2), we need 4² = 16× more runs.
16 × 100 = 1600 runs. (This is exactly what the code's runs_needed returns.)
Check: 8/√1600 wobble vs 8/√100 = 8/40 vs 8/10 = 0.2 vs 0.8 — a 4× improvement. ✓
Ladder · from "why random" to "how many"
Q1. Why not just test every possible failure combination?
There are far too many. Even choosing which 3 of a few hundred cells fail is millions of combinations; realistic mixes explode beyond counting. Sampling a random few thousand is feasible and nearly as informative.
Q2. If we only do a handful of random runs, why might we be misled?
Bad luck — a small sample might miss the rare dangerous patterns, or over-represent them. Small samples are noisy. More runs average the luck out.
Q3. We did 100 runs and the answer still wobbles too much. We quadruple to 400. By how much does the wobble shrink?
By √4 = 2 — it halves. The 1/√N rule: 4× the runs → 2× tighter. To get 10× tighter you'd need 100× the runs.
Q4 (top rung). Given that diminishing return, how do we decide when to stop?
Pick a target accuracy, measure the wobble from a first batch, and use 1/√N to compute how many runs hit the target (the worked example: 1600). Run that many, then stop — more would cost a lot for a tiny extra sharpness. That's engineering the sample size instead of guessing it.

13Sun, Earthshine & Albedo — the Fluxes

The "tap" filling our heat-bathtub isn't only sunlight. In orbit, three different energy streams hit the panel. Each is a flux — energy per second per square metre.

The picture Stand outside near a big sunlit white wall. You feel three warmths: the sun directly on your face; sunlight bounced off the bright wall onto you; and the wall's own warmth glowing from having been heated. A satellite over Earth feels the same three: direct sun, sunlight reflected off Earth (albedo), and Earth's own infrared glow.
FluxEveryday versionWhat sets it
Direct sunSun on your face≈1367 W/m² at Earth's distance; zero in eclipse (Earth's shadow)
AlbedoSun bounced off a white wall≈0.3 × the sunlight hitting the Earth below you (0.3 = Earth's reflectivity)
Planetary IRA warm wall glowing at nightEarth's own heat-glow (~240 W/m²), present even in the dark
Albedo The fraction of incoming sunlight a surface reflects. Fresh snow ≈ 0.8, dark ocean ≈ 0.06, Earth overall ≈ 0.3. So roughly 30% of the sun hitting the Earth beneath the satellite bounces back up and adds a second, weaker "sun" from below.
Inverse-square & the tilt (cosine) rule Sunlight thins with distance as 1/distance² (twice as far → a quarter as strong). And a surface tilted away from the sun catches less: the catch scales with the cosine of the tilt angle — face-on (0°) catches all of it, edge-on (90°) catches none. Same reason the midday sun warms the ground more than the low evening sun.
Worked example — the albedo flux
Sunlight reaching the Earth below ≈ 1367 W/m². Earth reflects a fraction (albedo) ≈ 0.3 of it.
But the satellite only "sees" part of that bounce — a visibility factor, say 0.25 for its position.
Albedo flux ≈ 0.25 × (0.3 × 1367) ≈ 0.25 × 410 ≈ 103 W/m² arriving from below.
Check: it's a sizeable minority of direct sun — small enough to be a "second sun", big enough that ignoring it would mis-predict the temperature. That's why the model includes it.
Ladder · the three streams, from simplest up
Q1. What does "flux" mean, in kitchen English?
How much energy lands on each square metre each second. Bigger flux = more warming power per patch of surface.
Q2. The satellite passes into Earth's shadow (eclipse). What happens to the direct-sun flux?
It drops to zero — Earth blocks the sun. (The code literally sets sun = 0 in eclipse.) The panel can suddenly cool fast, which is its own stress.
Q3. In that same eclipse, are albedo and planetary-IR also zero?
Albedo yes (no sunlight to bounce). But planetary-IR no — Earth still glows with its own heat in the dark, so that stream keeps gently warming the panel even in shadow.
Q4. Why does a flat panel tilted 60° away from the sun catch only half the sunlight?
Because the catch scales with cos(tilt). cos(60°) = 0.5, so it intercepts half as much as face-on. A tilted surface spreads the same beam over more area, so each square metre gets less.
Q5 (top rung). Why bother with all three streams plus eclipse and tilt — why not just "use the sun"?
Because the cell's balance temperature depends on the total tap, and the extra streams are a real chunk of it. Over an orbit the tap swings — full sun + albedo + IR on the day side, only IR in eclipse — so the temperature swings too. Predicting hot-spots honestly means feeding the solver the true, time-varying tap, which is what the hapsira-driven flux code supplies.

Worked Solutions

Solution 1. (a) P = V × I = 2.0 × 0.50 = 1.0 W. (b) Both positive (push and flow same direction) → power positive → the cell is giving energy.
Solution 2. Series adds voltage, parallel adds current. (a) One line = 5 cells in series → 5 × 2.3 = 11.5 V; its current = one cell's = 0.48 A. (b) Module = 2 lines in parallel → same voltage as one line = 11.5 V. (c) Parallel adds current → 0.48 + 0.48 = 0.96 A.
Solution 3. (a) P = (−15) × 0.50 = −7.5 W. (b) Negative → absorbing. (c) The 7.5 W it absorbs has nowhere to go but heat → 7.5 W of heat.
Solution 4. Adding a heat source raises the tap rate, so heat-in temporarily exceeds heat-out and the temperature rises. But radiated heat-out grows steeply with temperature (T⁴), so as the cell warms it sheds heat faster, until heat-out again equals the new, larger heat-in. It settles at a new, higher balance temperature rather than climbing forever — the T⁴ drain is a self-limiting brake.
Solution 5. f = T²−20, slope = 2T. At T = 4: wrongness f = 16−20 = −4; slope = 2×4 = 8; next = 4 − (−4)/8 = 4 + 0.5 = 4.5. (Check: √20 ≈ 4.472 — one step already very close.)
Solution 6. Model answer: "We kept Newton's repeat-until-settled loop because the heat-balance equation has the temperature on both sides (inside T⁴), so there's no one-line formula — you must guess and correct until it stops moving.
