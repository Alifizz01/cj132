8 Monte Carlo: estimating the unknowable by rolling dice

Sometimes the honest answer to "what is this number?" is: nobody can compute it exactly. Either the calculation has no tidy formula, or there are simply too many possibilities to check them all in any lifetime. A solar array is exactly this kind of problem — Section 7 gave us a model that tells us, for one chosen set of cell failures, how hot the panel gets and how much power it loses. But an array has hundreds or thousands of cells, and the number of ways they could fail is astronomically large. We cannot test every case. This section introduces the escape hatch engineers reach for when a problem is too big to solve head-on: Monte Carlo — the art of estimating a quantity you cannot compute directly by running many random trials and averaging what you see. We build it from the ground up: what an average of random samples actually is, the law that guarantees it converges, the famous 1/√N rule that says exactly how slowly it converges, and then why this slow, humble method is nonetheless the right tool for an array with 2N possible failure states.

Key idea

Monte Carlo (MC) trades an impossible exact calculation for an easy approximate one: instead of working out the true answer, you run the experiment many times with random inputs and average the results. The average of many random trials drifts towards the true value you wanted — and the more trials you run, the closer it gets. You never reach the exact answer, but you get a controllably good estimate, plus an honest measure of how uncertain that estimate is. For a solar array we cannot simulate every possible pattern of cell failures, so we sample failure patterns at random, simulate each one with the Section 7 electro-thermal model, and build up a statistical picture of what happens.

8.1 What Monte Carlo is, and where the name comes from

Term — Monte Carlo (MC) method

A Monte Carlo method is any technique that estimates a hard quantity by running MANY trials with RANDOM inputs and AVERAGING the results. The three words carry the whole idea: many (one trial tells you almost nothing; thousands tell you a lot), random (each trial feeds in inputs drawn by chance), and average (the typical outcome across all trials is your estimate). The name is a joke that stuck: it is named after the Monte Carlo casino in Monaco, because the method runs on randomness — the same rolling-dice, spinning-wheel chance that drives a casino. Physicists working on the atomic bomb in the 1940s needed answers to problems with no formula, so they "played the odds" by simulating random events, and named the trick after the most famous gambling house they knew.

When do you reach for Monte Carlo?

Use MC when an exact calculation is impossible, or when there are too many combinations to check them all. Two flavours of "impossible":

  1. No closed form. The quantity is defined by something with no neat formula — a tangled integral, or the output of a simulation that has no shortcut. You cannot write down the answer; you can only measure it by running the thing.
  2. Too many cases. There is a way to compute each individual case, but the number of cases is so vast you could never finish. Checking them all is hopeless, so you check a random sample of them and infer the whole from the part.

The solar array sits squarely in the second flavour, as §8.4 will show — but the machinery is identical for both.

8.2 Expected value: the long-run average a random quantity is "aiming at"

To understand why averaging random trials works, we need one foundational idea — the thing the average is converging towards.

Term — expected value (the mean)

The expected value of a random quantity is its long-run average — the number you would get if you could repeat the random experiment forever and average all the outcomes. It is not necessarily a value you will ever actually see on a single trial; it is the centre of gravity of all possible outcomes, each weighted by how likely it is. We write it E[X] for a random quantity X, and often call it the mean of X.

Concrete: the expected value of one die roll
List the outcomes. A fair six-sided die shows 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 or 6, each with probability 1/6.
Average them, weighted by probability.   E[roll] = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6.
Add the top.   1+2+3+4+5+6 = 21.
Divide.   E[roll] = 21 / 6 = 3.5.
Note 3.5 is a value the die can never actually show — there is no "3.5" face. The expected value is the long-run average, not a possible single result. Roll a die five times and you might average 2.8 or 4.2; roll it ten thousand times and the average will hug 3.5 very closely. That drift towards 3.5 as the rolls pile up is the whole point of the next two sections. ✓

The bridge from this toy to Monte Carlo is short: if you cannot compute E[X] by listing outcomes and weighting them (because there are too many, or you do not know the weights), you can estimate it by drawing many samples of X at random and taking their ordinary average. That average is called the sample mean, and it is your Monte Carlo estimate of E[X].

Sample mean as an estimate of the expected value x̄ = (1/N) · (x1 + x2 + … + xN)   ≈   E[X]
x1…xN = the outcomes of N independent random trials; x̄ ("x-bar") = their sample mean; E[X] = the true expected value we are after. The approximation "≈" gets better as N grows — that is the next section.

8.3 The Law of Large Numbers — why averaging works at all

Why should the average of a handful of random, jittery outcomes settle down to a single trustworthy number? Because of a theorem so fundamental it underwrites all of statistics.

Term — Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

The Law of Large Numbers states: as the number of trials N grows, the sample average converges to the true expected value. The more times you repeat a random experiment and average, the closer that average gets to E[X]. Individual trials wobble all over the place; their running average steadily calms down and homes in on the mean. This is the licence that makes Monte Carlo legitimate — it is the guarantee that "average lots of random trials" actually delivers the quantity you wanted, given enough trials.

What the LLN does and does not promise

It promises the average will get and stay close to E[X] as N → ∞. It does not promise the average equals E[X] at any finite N, nor that it marches there smoothly — at small N the estimate jumps around (see the figure: wild on the left, calm on the right). The practical questions are therefore "how fast does it converge?" and "how do I know when I've done enough?" — and for those we need a number, not just a guarantee. That number is the 1/√N rule.

8.4 The 1/√N rule — Monte Carlo's blessing and its curse

The Law of Large Numbers says the error shrinks; the next result says how fast. It is the single most important practical fact about Monte Carlo, and it cuts both ways.

The error of an MC estimate shrinks like 1/√N EXAM-HOT

The typical error of a Monte Carlo estimate is proportional to 1/√N, where N is the number of trials. Read off the consequences, because they are not intuitive:

  • Quadrupling the trials HALVES the error. Since the error goes as 1/√N, multiplying N by 4 multiplies the error by 1/√4 = 1/2. Four times the work buys you only twice the accuracy.
  • To cut the error by 10× you need 100× the trials. To divide the error by 10 you must multiply √N by 10, i.e. multiply N by 10² = 100. Ten times more accuracy costs a hundred times more computing.

This slow convergence is MC's main weakness — accuracy is brutally expensive. But there is a redeeming twist that makes the whole method worthwhile.

The redeeming twist: 1/√N is DIMENSION-INDEPENDENT EXAM-HOT

That 1/√N rate does not care how many variables the problem has. Whether you are averaging over 1 random input or 10,000 of them, the error still falls as 1/√N — the dimension of the problem (the number of things varying at once) never enters the rate. This is extraordinary, because almost every other numerical method gets exponentially harder as you add variables (the "curse of dimensionality"): a method that needs 10 sample points per variable needs 102 for two variables, 103 for three, 10100 for a hundred — instantly hopeless. Monte Carlo sidesteps that entirely. This is precisely why MC wins for huge problems: a solar array with thousands of independently-failing cells is a thousand-dimensional problem, and MC is one of the very few methods whose cost does not explode as the dimension climbs.

Term — standard error (SE)

To turn "the error goes like 1/√N" into an actual number you can compute from your own run, use the standard error — an estimate of how far your sample mean is likely to be from the true mean:

SE ≈ s / √N
s = the sample standard deviation (how spread-out the individual trial outcomes are — big if the trials scatter widely, small if they cluster); N = number of trials. SE is the typical size of the gap between your estimate x̄ and the truth E[X].

The s in the numerator says noisier problems need more trials for the same accuracy; the √N in the denominator is the slow 1/√N convergence in person. Halving SE means quadrupling N — there it is again.

8.5 Why here: an array has 2N failure patterns

Now the motivating point — why a solar-array simulator needs Monte Carlo at all. It comes down to a count that gets out of hand astonishingly fast.

The combinatorial explosion: 2N failure states EXAM-HOT

Consider an array of N cells, where each cell is in one of two states — OK or failed. How many distinct failure patterns are there? Each cell independently doubles the count: 1 cell gives 2 patterns, 2 cells give 2×2 = 4, 3 cells give 8, and in general N cells give 2N combinations. This grows explosively. For just 100 cells that is 2100 ≈ 1.3 × 1030 patterns — a 1 with thirty zeros. There is no computer, and no amount of time, that can simulate 1030 cases. You cannot check them all.

The Monte Carlo response: sample, simulate, build a distribution

Since enumeration is hopeless, PowerPy does the Monte Carlo thing: it samples failure patterns at random (or sweeps them cleverly — §8.6), simulates each one with the Section 7 electro-thermal model, and assembles a statistical picture from the results. Rather than one impossible exact answer, you get distributions and probabilities:

  • the distribution of total power loss across many failure scenarios — how bad does the array's output typically get, and how bad in the worst cases?
  • the distribution of maximum cell temperature — how hot does the hottest spot tend to run?
  • the probability that a hot-spot exceeds the substrate's melt limit — the headline safety number: across realistic failures, how often does a cell get hot enough to destroy the panel?

None of these can be read off a single calculation. They are statistical outcomes, and Monte Carlo is how you earn them.

8.6 How a "run" works in PowerPy, and the three sampling modes

Concretely, what is one Monte Carlo trial here? It is one full pass through the simulator with a chosen set of failures. (The details of building and solving the circuit are Chapter 2's subject; here we just need the shape of a trial.)

One MC run, step by step season=0
  1. Pick which cells fail. In PowerPy a cell is marked failed by setting its season=0 — that flag tells the model this cell is dead. Choosing a failure pattern is choosing which cells get season=0.
  2. Build the circuit for that pattern of healthy and failed cells.
  3. Solve the coupled electro-thermal model (Section 7) — the iteration that finds each cell's self-consistent temperature and power.
  4. Record the outcomes: total power produced, maximum cell temperature, number of hot-spots, and whether any cell crosses the breakdown/melt limit.
  5. Repeat many times, with many different failure patterns, accumulating the outcomes into distributions.

PowerPy plans three sampling MODES — three different ways of choosing the failure patterns to feed in, each answering a different engineering question.

The three sampling modes
(1) POSITION sweep.  Fail each location in turn — kill cell 1 alone, then cell 2 alone, and so on across every position. Goal: find the worst spot — which single cell, if it fails, hurts the array most. This is a deterministic sweep, not random sampling, but it is part of the same machinery.
(2) COUNT sweep.  Fail k random cells for k = 1, 2, 3, … — one failure, then two, then three, increasing the number of dead cells. Goal: chart degradation versus number of failures — how does the array decline as more cells die?
(3) RANDOM sampling.  Throw many random failure patterns at the model. Goal: get the full distributions of §8.5 — the complete statistical picture of power loss, temperature and melt probability. This is Monte Carlo in its purest form.
All three feed the same per-run machinery (pick failures → build → solve → record); they differ only in how the failure patterns are chosen. The outputs become histograms (the shape of each distribution) and rankings — "most vs least damaging" failure locations, and "which layout is most robust" across designs. ✓

8.7 Reproducibility: seed the random number generator

Monte Carlo runs on randomness — but uncontrolled randomness is a debugging nightmare and makes fair comparison impossible. The fix is the seed.

Term — seed (of a random number generator)

A computer's "random" numbers are not truly random — they come from a pseudo-random number generator (PRNG), a deterministic algorithm that produces a stream of numbers that look random. The seed is the starting value that algorithm is launched from. Fixing the seed makes a run repeatable: the same seed always produces the same sequence of "random" numbers, so the whole Monte Carlo run replays identically.

Why fixing the seed matters

Two reasons, both essential:

  • Debugging. If a run crashes or produces a weird result, a fixed seed lets you reproduce the exact same trials and hunt the bug. With an unseeded run, every execution is different and the failure may never recur — you cannot debug what you cannot repeat.
  • Fair comparison of designs. To compare two array layouts honestly, you must subject them to the same random failures. Seed both runs identically and any difference in outcome is due to the design, not to luck of the draw. Different seeds would confound design differences with random noise.

8.8 Variance reduction: needing fewer runs (names only)

Smarter sampling, same accuracy, fewer runs

Because the 1/√N rule makes brute-force accuracy so expensive, a whole toolkit exists for getting the same accuracy from fewer runs by sampling more cleverly. These are called variance-reduction techniques. Two you will hear named:

  • Stratified sampling — deliberately spread the samples across distinct regions ("strata") instead of letting chance clump them, so no region is over- or under-sampled.
  • Importance sampling — concentrate samples where they matter most (e.g. on the rare-but-dangerous failures that drive the melt probability), then correct for the bias this introduces.

You do not need the mechanics here — just file them as "ways to need fewer runs" when plain Monte Carlo would be too slow.

8.9 Worked examples

Worked 8A — estimating π by throwing random darts

Goal: compute π using nothing but random points and counting — the canonical Monte Carlo demonstration. The idea: if a shape's area is hard to integrate, measure it by peppering a known region with random darts and seeing what fraction land inside.

Set up the geometry.  Throw points uniformly (every spot equally likely) into a 1×1 square. Inscribe a quarter-circle of radius 1 in it, centred at one corner. A point (x, y) is "inside" the quarter-circle when x² + y² ≤ 1.
Relate the fraction to areas.  Because the darts are uniform, the fraction landing inside the arc ≈ (area of quarter-circle) / (area of square).
Put in the areas.  Area of the square = 1×1 = 1. Area of a quarter-circle of radius 1 = (1/4)·π·1² = π/4. So the inside-fraction ≈ (π/4) / 1 = π/4.
Rearrange for π.  If inside/total ≈ π/4, then π ≈ 4 × (inside / total). We have turned "compute π" into "throw darts and count".
Do a tiny illustrative run.  Throw 1000 darts; suppose 786 land inside the arc. Then inside/total = 786/1000 = 0.786.
Estimate π.  π ≈ 4 × 0.786 = 3.144.
Sanity-check vs the true value.  The true π = 3.14159…, so 3.144 is off by about 0.002 — close, from just 1000 random darts and no calculus at all. The error is right in line with 1/√N: with N = 1000, 1/√N ≈ 0.032, and our estimate landed well within that ballpark of the truth. Throw a million darts and you would expect roughly another √1000 ≈ 32× tightening. ✓
Worked 8B — convergence arithmetic: how many runs for a target accuracy?

Goal: a concrete planning calculation using the 1/√N rule. Given: 100 runs gave a standard error of 8 W on the estimated power loss. Question: how many runs are needed to reach an SE of 2 W?

State the proportionality.  SE ∝ 1/√N, so SE shrinks only as the square root of the run count.
Find the required improvement factor.  We want to go from 8 W down to 2 W — that is cutting SE by a factor of 8/2 = 4×.
Translate to a run-count factor.  Since SE ∝ 1/√N, cutting SE by 4× means increasing √N by 4×, i.e. increasing N by 4² = 16×.
Apply the algebra explicitly.  N2 = N1 · (SE1 / SE2)² = 100 · (8 W / 2 W)² = 100 · 4² = 100 · 16.
Result.  N2 = 1600 runs.
Sanity: the units inside (SE1/SE2) cancel (W/W), as they must — a ratio of errors is dimensionless, leaving a pure run count. And the answer respects the rule: 4× better accuracy cost 16× the runs, exactly the "halve the error → quadruple the runs, squared" behaviour. This is why Monte Carlo budgets balloon when you demand precision. ✓
Worked 8C (optional) — a sample average only approaches the expected value slowly

Goal: see the Law of Large Numbers in miniature, reusing the die from §8.2 (true E[roll] = 3.5).

A few rolls.  Roll 4 times and get {2, 6, 3, 1}. Sample mean = (2+6+3+1)/4 = 12/4 = 3.0 — off the true 3.5 by 0.5.
More rolls.  Roll 10 times: {2,6,3,1,5,4,4,2,6,3}. Sum = 36, mean = 36/10 = 3.6 — now off by only 0.1.
Many rolls.  With thousands of rolls, the mean would sit on 3.5 to two or three decimals.
The estimate from a few rolls is unreliable (3.0), tightens with more (3.6), and only approaches 3.5 as the rolls grow — never snapping exactly onto it at any finite count. That gradual homing-in is the Law of Large Numbers, and the speed of the homing-in is the 1/√N rule. ✓

8.10 Socratic ladder — sampling failures, and knowing when to stop

Ladder — Why do we sample random failures instead of simulating every possible failure, and how do we know when we've run ENOUGH?
Q1. (Foundation) What does it mean to estimate the expected value of a quantity by sampling?
The expected value E[X] is the long-run average of a random quantity X. To estimate it by sampling, you draw many independent random outcomes x1, x2, …, xN and take their ordinary average x̄ = (1/N)Σxi. By the Law of Large Numbers that sample mean converges to E[X] as N grows. So "estimate by sampling" = "run the random experiment many times and average what comes out".
Q2. For an array of N cells, each either OK or failed, how many failure combinations are there?
2N. Each cell independently has 2 states, and the states multiply: 2 × 2 × … × 2 (N times) = 2N. For 100 cells that is 2100 ≈ 1.3 × 1030 distinct patterns.
Q3. Why can't we just enumerate them — simulate every single one of those 2N patterns?
Because 2N is astronomically large. Even at 100 cells there are ~1030 patterns, and each pattern requires a full electro-thermal solve (Section 7) that itself takes real compute. No computer could finish 1030 solves in any feasible time — the universe is far too young. Enumeration is simply impossible, so we must sample: simulate a random subset and infer the whole statistical picture from it.
Q4. (Why) If we only sample, how does the error of our estimate behave as we add more samples?
It shrinks as 1/√N — slowly. Quadrupling the samples only halves the error; getting 10× more accuracy costs 100× more samples. The good news is that this rate is dimension-independent: it does not get worse just because the array has thousands of cells, which is exactly why sampling is viable here at all. We quantify the current error with the standard error SE ≈ s/√N.
Q5. (Payoff) So what tells us we've run ENOUGH — when do we stop?
Two linked signals. First, the estimate stabilises: as you add runs, watch the running estimate (the convergence curve); once it stops drifting and just jitters within a narrow band, more runs are buying little. Second, the standard error is small enough for the decision at hand: compute SE ≈ s/√N and keep going until SE drops below your tolerance (as in Worked 8B — if you need SE ≤ 2 W and 100 runs give 8 W, you know to push to 1600 runs). "Enough" is not a fixed number; it is whenever the estimate has settled and its standard error meets your required accuracy.

8.11 Pitfall

Pitfall — the 1/√N cost, and unseeded runs aren't comparable EXAM-HOT

Two traps, both born of forgetting how Monte Carlo really behaves.

(1) Accuracy is punishingly expensive. Because the error falls only as 1/√N, wanting 10× more accuracy costs 100× more runs — not 10×. Engineers routinely under-budget Monte Carlo because they assume error falls linearly with effort; it does not. If 1000 runs give you ±10 W and you need ±1 W, brace for 100,000 runs, not 10,000. (This is also the cue to consider variance reduction, §8.8.)

(2) An unseeded run isn't reproducible. If you do not fix the seed, every execution uses a different random sequence, so a result can never be reproduced — fatal for debugging, and fatal for fair comparison. Comparing two designs on different random failures confounds the design difference with the luck of the draw; you cannot tell whether layout A beat layout B because A is better or because A happened to draw easier failures. Always seed when comparing designs or chasing a bug.

8.12 Practice

Practice 8-1 — runs needed for a target accuracy

A Monte Carlo run of 500 trials gives a standard error of 6 W on the estimated power loss. You need the standard error down to 1.5 W.  (a) By what factor must the standard error shrink?  (b) Using SE ∝ 1/√N, by what factor must the number of trials grow?  (c) Apply N2 = N1·(SE1/SE2)² to find the total number of trials needed.  (d) In one sentence, why is this "slow" — what does the 1/√N rule say about the price of accuracy?

Practice 8-2 — estimate π from a stated hit count

You throw 2000 random darts uniformly into a 1×1 square with an inscribed quarter-circle of radius 1, and 1562 land inside the arc.  (a) Write the formula linking π to the inside-fraction, and explain in one line where the factor of 4 comes from.  (b) Compute the Monte Carlo estimate of π.  (c) Compare it with the true value 3.14159 and state the size of the error.  (d) If you wanted to roughly halve that error, about how many darts should you throw, and why?

Practice 8-3 — why 2N enumeration is hopeless for 200 cells

An array has 200 cells, each either OK or failed.  (a) How many distinct failure patterns are there, as a power of 2?  (b) It works out to roughly 1.6 × 1060 — explain in two or three sentences why simulating every one is impossible, referring both to the count and to the cost of a single electro-thermal solve.  (c) What does PowerPy do instead, and which of the three sampling modes (position / count / random) would you use to obtain the full distribution of power loss?

Looking ahead: §8 set up why PowerPy is fundamentally a Monte Carlo simulator — the 2N failure states that make exhaustive analysis impossible, the sample-and-average escape hatch, the 1/√N rule that prices accuracy, and the three sampling modes (position, count, random) that turn many electro-thermal runs into histograms and rankings. But every one of those thousands of runs calls the per-cell, iterative electro-thermal solve of Section 7 — and that is exactly the bottleneck Chapter 2 attacks. Make each run fast enough, and the slow 1/√N convergence stops being a wall: you can simply afford the runs that Monte Carlo demands.
