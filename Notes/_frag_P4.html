4 Loss factors, seasons, pointing, and the voltage corrections

Section 3 produced four honest operating-point numbers for a cell at a given temperature and radiation dose. But the array still has not delivered a single watt to the spacecraft bus, because the real world keeps shaving the numbers down: a sheet of coverglass eats a little light, the wiring drops a little voltage, the calibration of the test lamp is never perfectly known, the Sun is a few percent brighter in January than in July, and the panel is almost never pointed dead-on. This section is the bookkeeping of all those small adjustments — how PowerPy represents each one, how it combines them, and the one genuinely subtle piece: why a brighter Sun nudges the voltage up by a logarithm.

Key idea Everything in this section is a multiplier on the bare cell numbers from Section 3 — a fraction near 1.0. A factor of 0.98 means "2 % of this quantity is lost." PowerPy keeps current losses and voltage losses in two separate piles, multiplies each pile down to a single number, and only then scales the operating point. Three different combination rules appear — simple product (for certain deductions), root-sum-square (for independent uncertainties), and a cosine (for pointing) — and knowing which to use when is the whole game.

4.1 Loss factors: a deduction expressed as a multiplier

Term — loss factor

A loss factor is a dimensionless multiplier, almost always just below 1.0, that represents a fractional reduction of a current or a voltage. The convention is "fraction surviving", not "fraction lost": 0.98 means 98 % gets through, i.e. 2 % is lost. A factor of exactly 1.0 is a no-op (no loss); the smaller the factor, the bigger the bite.

Why does a real array deliver less than the bare cell numbers? A whole catalogue of small effects, each modelled as one factor:

Where the losses come from
  • Coverglass — the thin transparent shield bonded over each cell to stop micrometeoroids and radiation reflects and absorbs a little of the incoming light before it reaches the junction.
  • Assembly — bonding, soldering and handling during manufacture cost a fraction of a percent.
  • Wiring — the harness carrying current off the panel has resistance, so a little voltage is dropped on the way to the bus.
  • Contamination — outgassed material and dust deposit on the glass over the mission and dim it slightly.
  • UV (ultraviolet) darkening — ultraviolet sunlight slowly discolours the adhesive and coverglass, costing a little transmission.
  • Calibration uncertainty — the numbers came from a test under a lamp whose absolute intensity is only known to within a percent or two; that uncertainty is itself booked as a loss factor.

PowerPy lets you attach these to a cell (and the same calls exist on a string, a section, or a whole panel, where they apply to every cell inside). Each loss is split into the quantity it actually attacks — current or voltage:

cell.addLossI(name, factor)
adds a named current loss (multiplies the current).
cell.addLossV(name, factor)
adds a named voltage loss (multiplies the voltage).
cell.removeLosses()
clears every loss back to none.
The name is just a label for readability. The factor is the surviving fraction (0.98 = 2 % lost). Adding a loss to a string/section/panel applies it to all the cells it contains.

4.2 Combining losses by product

If a cell carries several current losses, PowerPy combines them into one current-loss factor by multiplying them all together; likewise all the voltage losses multiply down to one voltage-loss factor. Multiplication — not addition — is the right model for losses that act in series, each one taking its cut of whatever the previous one left.

lossItotal = f1 · f2 · f3 · …     lossVtotal = g1 · g2 · g3 · …
Example: a 0.98 loss followed by a 0.97 loss gives 0.98 · 0.97 = 0.9506, i.e. about 4.94 % lost overall — slightly less than the 5 % you would get by naively adding "2 % + 3 %", because the second loss only acts on the 98 % the first one left.
Why multiply, not add

Picture light passing through two slightly dirty windows. The first lets 98 % through. The second lets 97 % of what reaches it through — and what reaches it is already only 98 % of the original. So the survivor is 0.98 · 0.97 of the original, never 0.98 + 0.97 − 1. Adding the percentages is an approximation that drifts as the losses grow; multiplying is exact for stacked, deterministic deductions.

4.3 RSS loss: combining independent uncertainties

Sometimes a "loss" is not a known, certain deduction but an uncertainty — a "±" you are not sure of the sign of. Calibration uncertainty is the classic case: you do not actually know the lamp was 1.5 % too bright; you know it was within ±1.5 %. When several such uncertainties are statistically independent, multiplying them treats them as if they all conspire to hit worst-case at once, which is needlessly pessimistic. The honest combination is root-sum-square (RSS) — adding them in quadrature.

Term — RSS (Root-Sum-Square)

To combine quantities in quadrature means to square them, add the squares, and take the square root: √(a² + b² + …). It is the same Pythagorean rule that gives the length of a vector from its components, and it is how independent random errors combine, because their cross-terms average to zero. The result is always smaller than the plain sum a + b + …

RSS_loss(list) = 1 − √( Σ (1 − lossk)² )
For each loss it takes the lost fraction (1 − lossk), squares it, sums over all losses, square-roots, and turns the combined lost-fraction back into a surviving factor by subtracting from 1. Input is the list of surviving-fraction factors; output is a single surviving-fraction factor.
Which rule, when EXAM-HOT
  • Deterministic, known deductions → MULTIPLY. Coverglass transmission, a wiring drop, an assembly factor: these are real, repeatable, always-present cuts. Stack them by product (§4.2).
  • Independent ± uncertainties → RSS. Calibration uncertainty, measurement scatter: these are error bars, equally likely either way, unlikely to all align. Combine in quadrature (§4.3).

RSS always gives a larger surviving factor (a smaller total loss) than multiplying the same numbers, because it refuses to assume every uncertainty hits the bad way at once. RSS is less pessimistic — appropriate precisely because independent errors partly cancel.

4.4 Worked example — the same three numbers, both ways

Worked 4A — product vs RSS on three losses

Given three loss factors: coverglass 0.99 (1 % lost), wiring 0.98 (2 % lost), calibration 0.995 (0.5 % lost). Combine them both ways and decide which is right.

Product (treat all as certain deductions):
0.99 · 0.98 · 0.995 = 0.9653  → about 3.47 % lost.
RSS (treat all as independent uncertainties): first the lost fractions are
1 − 0.99 = 0.010,  1 − 0.98 = 0.020,  1 − 0.995 = 0.005.
Square and sum:  0.010² + 0.020² + 0.005² = 0.0001 + 0.0004 + 0.000025 = 0.000525.
Square-root:  √0.000525 = 0.0229  (the combined lost fraction, ≈ 2.29 %).
Back to a surviving factor:  1 − 0.0229 = 0.9771  → about 2.29 % lost.
Compare and choose. Product = 0.965 (3.47 % lost); RSS = 0.977 (2.29 % lost). RSS is the gentler number — exactly as promised, because it does not assume the three losses all bite worst-case simultaneously. Which is appropriate? It depends on the nature of each loss, not on which answer you prefer. Coverglass and wiring are deterministic, ever-present deductions → they belong in the product. Calibration is a genuine ± uncertainty → it belongs in an RSS of the uncertain terms. A real budget often does both: multiply the deterministic losses, RSS the uncertainties, then multiply those two results together. Using RSS on a known deduction would flatter the budget; using product on pure uncertainties would over-design the array. ✓

4.5 Seasons: how strong the sunlight is

A spacecraft in Earth orbit does not see a constant Sun. The Earth's orbit is slightly elliptical, so the Earth–Sun distance changes over the year, and the intensity of sunlight changes with it by a few percent. PowerPy expresses the strength of the sunlight as a number relative to a fixed reference called AM0.

Term — AM0 (Air Mass Zero), 1 AU, B0

AM0 (Air Mass Zero) is the solar spectrum and intensity in space, above the atmosphere, at the Earth's mean distance from the Sun. "Air Mass Zero" literally means "no atmosphere in the way" — the spectrum a panel in orbit actually sees, unlike the ground-level AM1.5 used for terrestrial panels.

1 AU (one astronomical unit) is that mean Earth–Sun distance, about 1.496×108 km. At 1 AU the AM0 intensity is 1367 W/m² — the "solar constant," stored in PowerPy as B_0. Every season number is a multiplier on this baseline.

The method cell.setSeason(s) sets that multiplier. Two of the four named seasons also set a pointing angle (the solstices, when the Sun is farthest off the equatorial plane):

Pitfall — summer is the WEAKER Sun EXAM-HOT

Northern-hemisphere intuition screams that summer should be the strong Sun. It is not. Summer (in the north) happens when the Earth is farther from the Sun, so Summer Solstice is the fainter sunlight: 0.967 (about 3.3 % below AM0). Winter, with the Earth nearer the Sun, is the brighter Sun at 1.034. The seasons you feel are driven by axial tilt, not distance — the distance effect runs the opposite way. Examiners love this reversal; memorise summer faint, winter bright.

And setSeason(0) means DEAD: a zero multiplier kills all output. It is not a season at all but the way you model a failed cell, string, or panel — switch one to 0 and PowerPy reports what the rest of the array can still deliver.

4.6 Pointing and tilt: the cosine of incidence

The array is rarely square-on to the Sun. When sunlight strikes a panel at an angle, the panel intercepts less of it — the same beam is spread over a larger slice of panel, so the power per unit panel area falls by the cosine of the incidence angle. (At grazing incidence, cos → 0 and the panel catches almost nothing; dead-on, cos 0° = 1 and it catches everything.)

PowerPy describes the geometry with two tilt angles plus the season angle from §4.5:

tilt = cos(alpha + season_angle) · cos(beta)
alpha and beta are the two panel tilt angles, set by cell.setAngles(alpha, beta); season_angle is set by cell.setSeasonAngle(sa) (and is filled in automatically by setSeason("SS")/("WS") as +23.5°/−23.5°). When everything is zero, tilt = cos 0° · cos 0° = 1 · 1 = 1: a perfectly pointed panel loses nothing to geometry.
How tilt feeds the irradiance

The light a cell actually captures is the baseline scaled by both the season and the tilt: effective irradiance = season · tilt · B0. The season says how bright the Sun is; the tilt says how much of it the panel faces. Multiply both into the 1367 W/m² baseline and you have the irradiance the cell really sees — which is also what scales the photocurrent (more light → more current, near-linearly).

4.7 Worked example — effective irradiance with season and tilt

Worked 4B — VEX with a 30° tilt

Given: season "VEX" (multiplier 1.008), alpha = 30°, beta = 0°, season_angle = 0° (the vernal equinox sets no season angle). Find the effective irradiance.

Tilt:  tilt = cos(alpha + season_angle)·cos(beta) = cos(30° + 0°)·cos(0°) = cos 30° · 1 = 0.8660 · 1 = 0.866.
Effective irradiance:  season · tilt · B0 = 1.008 · 0.866 · 1367 W/m².
Step the arithmetic:  1.008 · 0.866 = 0.8729;  0.8729 · 1367 W/m² = 1193 W/m².
Sanity. 1193 W/m² is well below the 1367 W/m² baseline even though VEX (1.008) is slightly brighter than AM0 — because the 30° tilt costs about 13.4 % on its own, far outweighing the +0.8 % season gain. The combined ratio season·tilt = 0.873 < 1, so on balance the cell sees less than AM0. Hold on to that combined number 0.873 — it is exactly what drives the voltage correction in §4.8. ✓

4.8 The voltage illumination correction — a logarithm

One last subtlety, and it is the one students misread. A solar cell makes slightly more voltage in brighter light and slightly less in fainter light — but not in proportion. The dependence is logarithmic. This is not an arbitrary fudge: it falls straight out of the diode equation from Section 2, where the open-circuit voltage goes like the logarithm of the photocurrent, Voc ∝ ln(Ilight). Double the light and the voltage rises by a fixed step, not a factor.

PowerPy builds two small correction terms from the brightness ratio the cell actually sees — and that ratio is the combined season·tilt from §4.6, not the season alone. When the season is non-zero it computes:

vmp_corr = (1 + (isc − imp)/imp) · ln(season · tilt)
voc_corr = (1 + (vmp/voc)·(isc − imp)/imp) · ln(season · tilt)
Each is a small dimensionless term applied as a nudge to the corresponding voltage — Vmp → Vmp·(1 + vmp_corr), Voc → Voc·(1 + voc_corr) — not a factor you multiply the voltage by directly. The bracketed coefficient just sets how big the nudge is; the ln(season·tilt) sets its sign and size.
The intuition — read the ln, not the algebra

Do not get lost in the bracketed coefficients. The whole behaviour lives in ln(season·tilt):

  • Exactly at AM0 and square-on → season·tilt = 1 → ln(1) = 0 → no correction at all. The reference condition leaves the voltage untouched, which is the consistency guarantee (just like the temperature term vanished at t_ref in §3).
  • Brighter than AM0 (season·tilt > 1) → ln > 0 → a small voltage gain.
  • Fainter than AM0 (season·tilt < 1) → ln < 0 → a small voltage loss.

The logarithm compresses: it vanishes exactly at AM0 and squashes even large brightness swings into modest voltage moves — doubling the light adds a fixed step of voltage, not a factor. That compression is the signature of the diode's ln(Ilight) law.

Pitfall — it is season·tilt that decides the sign, not season

A natural mistake: "VEX is 1.008, brighter than AM0, so the voltage rises." Wrong, once the panel is tilted. In Worked 4B the season was 1.008 (brighter) but the 30° tilt pulled the combined ratio to season·tilt = 0.873 < 1, so ln(0.873) < 0 and the voltage correction is a small loss. Always feed the logarithm the combined brightness the cell really sees, never the season number on its own.

4.9 Socratic ladder — why multiply, and when to RSS

Ladder — Why are losses MULTIPLIED rather than added, and when should we use RSS instead?
Q1. (Foundation) What does a single loss factor of 0.98 actually mean?
It means 98 % of the quantity survives and 2 % is lost. It is a surviving fraction, a pure multiplier: take the number, multiply by 0.98, and you have the value after that one loss. "0.98" and "2 % lost" are two names for the same thing.
Q2. Now apply two losses in succession, 0.98 then 0.97. Why is the answer 0.9506 and not 0.95?
The second loss acts on what the first one left, not on the original. After the first, 98 % remains; the second keeps 97 % of that: 0.97 · 0.98 = 0.9506. Naively adding the losses ("2 % + 3 % = 5 % → 0.95") double-counts, because the 3 % would only ever be taken from the 98 % that survived, not the full 100 %. Stacked deductions compound, and compounding is multiplication. That is why PowerPy multiplies all current losses into one factor and all voltage losses into another.
Q3. So multiplication is exact for stacked deductions. When is multiplying the wrong thing to do?
When the "losses" are not certain deductions but independent uncertainties — error bars, equally likely to push either way. Multiplying them implicitly assumes they all land on the pessimistic side at the same time, which for independent errors is very unlikely. A loss is a certainty when it is always present and repeatable (coverglass transmission, a wiring drop); it is an uncertainty when it is a ± you cannot pin down (calibration of the test lamp, measurement scatter).
Q4. (Why) What does RSS do differently, and why is its answer larger (less pessimistic)?
RSS adds the lost fractions in quadrature: 1 − √(Σ(1−lossk)²). Squaring shrinks the small fractions before they are summed, and the square root brings the total back, but the net result of combining independent errors this way is always smaller than adding them linearly — because their random over- and under-estimates partly cancel rather than reinforce. In Worked 4A the same three numbers gave 0.977 by RSS versus 0.965 by product: a smaller total loss. RSS is the statistically honest combination for things that are genuinely independent and uncertain; product is the honest combination for things that are certain and always there. Use each for what it models — never pick the one that simply gives the prettier number.

4.10 The API you will actually call

PowerPy methods and helper for this section
  • cell.addLossI(name, factor) — add a current loss (surviving-fraction multiplier).
  • cell.addLossV(name, factor) — add a voltage loss.
  • cell.removeLosses() — clear all losses. (All three also exist on string/section/panel.)
  • cell.setSeason(s) — set the irradiance multiplier; "SS"/"WS" also set ±23.5° season angle; 0 = dead.
  • cell.setAngles(alpha, beta) — set the two panel tilt angles.
  • cell.setSeasonAngle(sa) — set the season pointing angle directly.
  • RSS_loss(list) — helper that combines a list of loss factors in quadrature: 1 − √(Σ(1−lossk)²).
