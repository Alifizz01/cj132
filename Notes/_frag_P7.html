7 How hot does it get? The space thermal environment and the 2-node model

A solar cell's behaviour is not fixed — it depends on how hot the cell is. Section 3 showed that temperature slides the whole IV curve (the current–voltage characteristic of a cell), so the power a panel makes is a function of its temperature. But the temperature is itself a function of the power: every watt the cell pulls out as electricity is a watt that no longer heats the panel. Power needs temperature; temperature needs power. The two are locked together, and you cannot solve one without the other. On top of that, Section 6's hot-spot was fundamentally a thermal failure — a melt. So PowerPy cannot ignore heat; it has to model it. This section builds the space thermal picture from first principles — why there is no air to carry heat away, how a panel gains and loses energy by radiation alone, the Stefan–Boltzmann law that governs the loss, and the 2-node energy-balance model PowerPy actually solves — then closes with the orbit and eclipse that make temperature a story unfolding in time.

Key idea

In space there is no air, so there is no convection — a panel can only exchange heat by radiation (to and from the Sun, the planet, and deep space) and by conduction through its own material. The temperature settles at thermal equilibrium: the one temperature where heat in = heat out. Because the panel has a hot front face and a cooler rear face, PowerPy treats it as two nodes — a front and a rear — writes one "energy in = energy out" equation for each, and solves the pair together.

7.1 Why temperature matters at all

It is worth being precise about why a power simulator must drag temperature into the story, because at first glance the two seem like separate subjects. They are not separable here, for two distinct reasons.

  • Temperature moves the IV curve. The term IV curve (current–voltage curve) is the graph of how much current a cell delivers at each voltage; it is the cell's entire electrical personality (Section 3). Heat shifts it — broadly, a hotter silicon-type cell loses a little voltage. So the same cell, in the same sunlight, makes different power at 0 °C and at 80 °C. You literally cannot read the power off the curve until you know which curve you are on, and that is set by temperature.
  • A hot-spot is a thermal failure. Section 6 showed a reverse-biased cell turning into a heater. Whether that heater melts the substrate is a question about temperature. To answer "does this failure cause a melt?" PowerPy must be able to compute how hot a patch of panel actually gets.
The chicken-and-egg, stated once

Temperature → sets the IV curve → sets the operating point → sets the electrical power extracted → which changes how much absorbed energy is left to heat the panel → which changes the temperature. It is a loop. PowerPy resolves it by iteration (Section 7.7): guess a temperature, work all the way round the loop, and repeat until the guess stops changing. Hold this loop in mind — it is the spine of the whole section.

7.2 Heat in a vacuum: radiation is the only game in town

On Earth a hot object sheds heat three ways: conduction (touching something cooler), convection (warm air or water flowing past and carrying heat off), and radiation (glowing — emitting electromagnetic waves). Step into orbit and one of those vanishes entirely.

No air → no convection

Space is very nearly a vacuum. With essentially no gas around the panel, there is nothing to convect heat away — the cooling breeze that keeps a laptop or a rooftop solar panel in check simply does not exist. A space panel can shed heat in only two ways:

  1. Radiation — it glows in the infrared, sending energy out as electromagnetic waves to whatever is around it (the planet, deep space). This also works in reverse: the Sun, the planet, and reflected sunlight radiate onto the panel and heat it.
  2. Conduction — heat flows through the panel's own solid material, e.g. from the hot front face through the board to the cooler rear face.

That is the entire toolkit. Every term in the model below is one of these two mechanisms. Let us inventory the inputs and the one big output.

7.3 The heat inputs — three ways the environment warms the panel

Three separate streams of energy arrive at the panel from outside. Define each carefully, because the model treats them differently depending on which face they hit and which surface property absorbs them.

Term — α (alpha), solar absorptivity

α is the fraction of incident sunlight a surface absorbs (a pure number between 0 and 1). α = 0.97 means the surface soaks up 97% of the sunlight that lands on it and reflects 3%. The front face's absorbed solar power is

Pin,sun = α · S · A · tilt
S = incident solar flux (W/m²); A = area (m²); tilt = a geometric factor (0–1) for how squarely the face meets the Sun — face-on is 1, edge-on is 0.
Term — albedo (PAlbedo)

Albedo is sunlight that bounces off the planet and lands on the panel — second-hand sunshine. It is a fraction of the direct sunlight, expressed by PowerPy as a flux PAlbedo (W/m²) hitting the facing side. Because albedo is reflected sunlight (short-wavelength), it is absorbed through the same solar property α as direct sun.

Term — planetary infrared, IR (PIR)

IR stands for infrared — light just beyond the red end of the visible band, the kind warm objects emit. The planet itself is warm, so it glows in the infrared; the panel's facing side absorbs some of this, a flux PIR (W/m²). Crucially, planetary IR is long-wavelength thermal radiation, so it is absorbed through the surface's emissivity ε, not through α.

Why albedo uses α but planetary IR uses ε EXAM-HOT

This split confuses everyone, and the reason is a real physical law (Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation): a surface absorbs and emits equally well at any given wavelength. So a surface has effectively two separate "absorb numbers": one for sunlight's short wavelengths (we call it α) and one for thermal long wavelengths (we call it ε, the same number that governs how well it emits). Albedo is reflected sunlight → short-wavelength → absorbed by α. Planetary IR is the warm planet's own glow → long-wavelength → absorbed by ε. Same photons-in, but different parts of the spectrum, so different surface property does the absorbing. This is exactly why spacecraft engineers obsess over the ratio α/ε: it sets whether a surface runs hot or cold (we prove this in Worked 7A).

7.4 The heat output — the Stefan–Boltzmann law

Now the dominant way a panel loses heat: it glows. Any surface above absolute zero radiates electromagnetic energy, and the amount is governed by one of the most important equations in this whole subject.

Prad = ε · σ · A · T4
ε (epsilon) = emissivity, 0–1, how good a radiator the surface is (1 = a perfect "black-body" emitter); σ (sigma) = the Stefan–Boltzmann constant = 5.670 × 10−8 W/m²K⁴; A = radiating area (m²); T = absolute temperature in KELVIN.
Term — emissivity ε

ε measures how efficiently a surface radiates heat compared with a perfect black-body at the same temperature. ε = 0.9 means the surface sheds 90% of the heat a perfect radiator would. A high-ε surface is a good radiator — it dumps heat to space easily and therefore runs cooler. (Note ε is also the property that absorbs incoming long-wavelength planetary IR — §7.3 — because, by Kirchhoff, a good emitter is an equally good absorber at that wavelength.)

Two facts to burn in: KELVIN, and the fourth power EXAM-HOT

Why Kelvin? The law is built on absolute temperature — the true amount of thermal energy, measured from absolute zero. Plugging in °C would be nonsense: 0 °C is not "no thermal radiation," it is a balmy 273 K of glow. PowerPy stores temperatures in °C and converts with a constant T0 = −273.15, so that TKelvin = T°C − T0 (which is just T°C + 273.15). Forgetting this conversion is the single most destructive mistake in the section (Pitfall 7.10).

Why the fourth power? Radiated power grows as T⁴, not T. That steepness matters enormously: a surface that gets a little hotter sheds a lot more heat. Double the absolute temperature and you radiate 2⁴ = 16× as much. This is precisely what makes equilibrium stable — if a panel overheats, the T⁴ term ramps up fast and claws the temperature back down.

Both faces radiate. The front and the rear each glow outward, each against the brutal cold of deep space — a background temperature of only TSpace = 2.7 K (the residual cold of the universe). Because 2.7 K is so close to absolute zero, space radiates almost nothing back, so each face sheds heat as ε·σ·A·(T⁴ − TSpace⁴) — essentially full-bore radiation outward with a tiny correction for what little comes back.

7.5 Two more energy flows: electrical extraction and front↔rear conduction

Two terms remain, and the first contains the most important physical insight in the section.

Electrical power leaves as electricity — so it does NOT heat the panel

When the cell delivers electrical power Pelec, that energy walks out down the wires. It is absorbed sunlight that became electricity instead of heat. So in the energy balance, Pelec is subtracted at the front node — it is one more exit door for energy, alongside radiation.

The crucial link to Section 6: a healthy cell has positive Pelec and so removes heat. But a reverse-biased, failed cell has negative Pelec — it absorbs electrical power rather than delivering it. A negative number being subtracted is a positive being added: the failed cell adds heat to the panel. That sign flip is the mathematical heart of the hot-spot.

Term — conduction coefficient C (front↔rear)

Heat also flows through the board, from the hotter face to the cooler one. PowerPy bundles the material into a single conduction coefficient

C = conductivity / thickness
conductivity (W/m·K) is how readily the substrate material passes heat; thickness (m) is the board's depth. C has units W/m²K.

The conductive power flowing into the front node from the rear is then C·A·(T2 − T1) — proportional to the temperature difference between the faces (T₂ = rear, T₁ = front). If the rear is hotter, heat flows forward (positive into the front); if the front is hotter, the same expression is negative and heat flows backward. One expression, both directions handled by its sign.

7.6 The 2-node model: two coupled equations

We now have every ingredient. PowerPy models the panel as two thermal nodes — a front node at temperature T1 and a rear node at temperature T2 (both in Kelvin) — and writes a single "everything in − everything out = 0" equation for each. At equilibrium each node is neither warming nor cooling, so each balance equals zero.

Front node (T1) αF·A·PSun·tilt  −  εF·A·σ·(T1⁴ − TSpace⁴)  −  Pelec  +  C·A·(T2 − T1)  =  0
Rear node (T2) (αR·PAlbedo + εR·PIR)·A·tilt  −  εR·A·σ·(T2⁴ − TSpace⁴)  −  C·A·(T2 − T1)  =  0

Read each term aloud — the equations are just the bookkeeping of §§7.3–7.5:

Every term in words
αF·A·PSun·tilt  (front, IN): direct sunlight absorbed by the front face — its solar absorptivity αF times the solar flux PSun, over area A, scaled by the tilt geometry.
− εF·A·σ·(T1⁴ − TSpace⁴)  (front, OUT): Stefan–Boltzmann radiation from the front face to deep space. Subtracted because it leaves.
− Pelec  (front, OUT): the electrical power extracted, leaving as electricity. Subtracted. Negative for a failed cell → becomes heat IN.
+ C·A·(T2 − T1)  (front, conduction): heat conducted from the rear into the front. Positive if the rear is hotter.
(αR·PAlbedo + εR·PIR)·A·tilt  (rear, IN): the rear's environment heat — reflected sunlight (albedo, via αR) plus the planet's IR glow (via εR).
− εR·A·σ·(T2⁴ − TSpace⁴)  (rear, OUT): Stefan–Boltzmann radiation from the rear face to deep space.
− C·A·(T2 − T1)  (rear, conduction): the same conductive flux, with the opposite sign — what leaves the rear is exactly what enters the front. Energy is conserved across the link.
Notice the conduction term appears with a + on the front and a − on the rear: it is one flow of heat, counted as a gain for whichever node receives it and a loss for the one that gives it up. The two signs must be opposite or energy would be created from nothing. ✓
Why this needs a numerical solver — and why it is the bottleneck

The two equations are coupled (T₁ appears in the rear equation's conduction term and T₂ in the front's, so you cannot solve one without the other) and nonlinear (the T⁴ terms — you cannot rearrange a T⁴ into a tidy closed-form solution). So PowerPy hands the pair to a numerical root-finder, scipy.fsolve scipy.fsolve, which hunts for the (T₁, T₂) that drive both balances to zero. Critically, it does this one panel/cell at a time, in a Python loop. EXAM-HOT That per-item loop is the performance bottleneck of the whole simulator: looping over thousands of cells, each with its own fsolve call, is slow. Chapter 2 replaces it with a single VECTORized solve that handles all cells at once.

7.7 Where the material properties come from: the substrate file

The numbers αF, εR, C and so on are not invented per run — they describe a real panel material and live in a data file.

The substrate JSON  getSubstrateData

Each material is a file data/substrates/NAME.json, loaded by getSubstrateData. Its keys are: name, alpha_front, alpha_rear, epsilon_front, epsilon_rear, conductivity, thickness. Then panel.prepareParameters() prepareParameters reads this file, builds two property dictionaries — front_side and rear_side — and computes the conduction coefficient C = conductivity / thickness.

Example substrate — "SRP MSRO Case 2" 
{
  "name":          "SRP MSRO Case 2",
  "alpha_front":    0.970,
  "alpha_rear":     0.930,
  "epsilon_front":  0.9,
  "epsilon_rear":   0.89,
  "conductivity":   10,      // W/m·K
  "thickness":      0.01     // m
}
Compute C:   C = conductivity / thickness = 10 W/m·K ÷ 0.01 m.
Result:   C = 1000 W/m²K. (Units: (W/m·K) ÷ m = W/m²K. ✓)
A high C (1000 W/m²K) means the board passes heat front-to-rear very readily, so the two faces will sit at nearly the same temperature. A thin, conductive board ties the nodes tightly together; a thick, insulating one lets them drift apart. ✓

7.8 Closing the loop: electro-thermal coupling

Now we can state precisely how PowerPy resolves the chicken-and-egg of §7.1. The coupling is a loop, and the loop is solved by iteration.

The electro-thermal loop  thermalEquilibrium

PowerPy's panel.thermalEquilibrium(voltage, season, P_Albedo, P_IR) drives this cycle:

  1. Guess a temperature T.
  2. Build the cells at T — i.e. fix the IV curve for that temperature (Section 3).
  3. Compute the electrical power at the requested voltage: Pelec = currentAtVoltage(V)·V — current drawn at V, times V.
  4. Solve the thermal balance (§7.6) with that Pelec to get a new temperature.
  5. Repeat until T stops changing.

The outer search for the self-consistent T uses scipy.minimize with the Nelder–Mead method — a derivative-free "amoeba" optimiser that nudges a guess around until it settles. EXAM-HOT Like the thermal solve itself, this iteration is slow — it re-builds the cells and re-solves on every pass, for every item. Chapter 2 vectorizes this too, removing the per-item iteration.

The loop in one sentence

Temperature sets the IV curve → the operating point sets Pelec → Pelec changes the heat balance → which sets a new temperature. Round and round until it converges. That self-consistent fixed point is the panel's true operating temperature and its true power, found together because neither can be known alone.

7.9 Worked examples

Worked 7A — one-node sunlit equilibrium (the α/ε result)

Goal: find the equilibrium temperature of a sunlit plate, stripped to its essence. Ignore albedo, IR, conduction and electrical extraction — just direct sun absorbed, balanced against radiation from one face. (Also take TSpace⁴ ≈ 0, since 2.7 K to the fourth is utterly negligible beside a panel near 400 K.)

Write the balance — heat in = heat out:   α·S·A = ε·σ·A·T⁴.
Area cancels.   A is on both sides (the plate catches and radiates over the same area), so divide it out:   α·S = ε·σ·T⁴. Equilibrium temperature does not depend on size.
Solve for T⁴:   T⁴ = α·S / (ε·σ).
Solve for T:   T = [ α·S / (ε·σ) ]1/4.
Plug in numbers — take α = 0.9,  S = 1367 W/m² (AM0, full sun at 1 AU — defined in §7.11),  ε = 0.85.
Numerator:   α·S = 0.9 × 1367 W/m² = 1230.3 W/m².
Denominator:   ε·σ = 0.85 × 5.67×10−8 W/m²K⁴ = 4.82×10−8 W/m²K⁴.
Divide → T⁴:   T⁴ = 1230.3 / 4.82×10−8 = 2.553×1010 K⁴.
Fourth root, done as two square roots (the step you cannot eyeball):   first √(2.553×1010 K⁴) = 1.598×105 K²;   then √(1.598×105 K²) = 399.8 K.
Convert to °C:   399.8 K + T0 = 399.8 − 273.15 ≈ 127 °C.
Sanity: a black-ish plate in full, unfiltered sunlight with no air to cool it sitting at ~127 °C is exactly the "frying pan in space" you'd expect — far hotter than anything in shade. The order of magnitude is right. ✓
The punchline — T depends on the RATIO α/ε  EXAM-HOT

Factor the result to make the physics jump out:

T = (S/σ)1/4 · (α/ε)1/4 = 394.1 K · (α/ε)1/4
(S/σ)1/4 = (1367 / 5.67×10−8)1/4 = (2.411×1010)1/4 ≈ 394.1 K is fixed by the sunlight alone. Everything about the surface enters only through the single ratio α/ε.

So T ∝ (α/ε)1/4. Only the ratio of solar absorptivity to emissivity matters, not either alone. A surface that drinks sunlight (high α) but radiates poorly (low ε) runs blazing hot; one that reflects sun (low α) yet radiates well (high ε) runs cold. Check our case: (0.9/0.85)1/4 = 1.0144, and 394.1 × 1.0144 = 399.8 K — matching the long calculation exactly. Raising ε lowers T: a better radiator sheds heat faster, so equilibrium settles cooler. This α/ε ratio is the master dial of spacecraft thermal design.

Worked 7B — extracting electrical power lowers the temperature

Goal: redo 7A but now let the cell pull electrical power out, and show the panel runs cooler. The point: energy that leaves as electricity is energy that no longer has to leave as heat, so less radiation — hence a lower T — is needed to balance the books.

Watch the units (this is the trap). In the front-node equation, every term carries the area A except Pelec, which is an absolute power in watts. So unlike 7A, area does NOT cancel once Pelec is present — we must keep A explicit. We use one §3/§6 cell: area A = 30 cm² = 0.0030 m², operating at 2.35 V × 0.48 A.

Balance with electrical term:   α·S·A − Pelec = ε·σ·A·T⁴.  (Absorbed sun, minus electricity removed, equals what must be radiated.)
Absorbed solar power (now an absolute watt figure):   α·S·A = 0.9 × 1367 W/m² × 0.0030 m² = 3.69 W.
Electrical power extracted:   Pelec = 2.35 V × 0.48 A = 1.128 W.
Power that must be radiated:   ε·σ·A·T⁴ = 3.69 W − 1.128 W = 2.563 W. (Less than 7A's 3.69 W — the electricity carried part of the load away.)
Radiative coefficient ε·σ·A:   0.85 × 5.67×10−8 × 0.0030 = 1.446×10−10 W/K⁴.
Solve for T⁴:   T⁴ = 2.563 / 1.446×10−10 = 1.773×1010 K⁴.
Fourth root (two square roots):   √(1.773×1010) = 1.331×105;   √(1.331×105) = ≈ 365 K  (≈ 92 °C).
Compare: 365 K with extraction vs 399.8 K without — about 35 K cooler. The argument holds: roughly a third of the absorbed sunlight (1.128 of 3.69 W) departed as electricity, so the panel needs less T⁴ radiation to balance, and a lower T⁴ means a lower T. Pulling power out of a panel genuinely cools it. ✓  (And the §6 sign warning: had this been a failed cell with Pelec = −1.128 W, the minus-a-negative would ADD 1.128 W, pushing T up instead.)

7.10 Socratic ladder — the ratio α/ε and the fourth power

Ladder — Why does equilibrium temperature depend on the RATIO α/ε, and why is radiated heat proportional to T⁴ rather than T?
Q1. In the vacuum of space, with no air, how can a sunlit panel lose heat at all?
Only by radiation — by glowing, emitting electromagnetic energy out to deep space (and, through the board, by conduction to its other face, but to leave the panel entirely it must ultimately radiate). There is no air, so no convection; there is nothing touching it to conduct into. If it could not radiate, an illuminated panel would heat up without limit.
Q2. What law sets how much it radiates, and on what does that depend?
The Stefan–Boltzmann law, Prad = ε·σ·A·T⁴. The radiated power depends on the emissivity ε (how good a radiator the surface is), the area A, the constant σ, and — steeply — the absolute temperature T raised to the fourth power.
Q3. As the panel heats up, what happens to heat-IN versus heat-OUT, and why does that force a stable equilibrium?
Heat-IN (absorbed sunlight, α·S·A) barely changes with temperature — the Sun delivers the same flux regardless of how warm the panel is. But heat-OUT (ε·σ·A·T⁴) rises sharply as T climbs, because of the fourth power. So if the panel is too cold, OUT < IN and it warms; if too hot, OUT > IN and it cools. There is exactly one temperature where they match — and the T⁴ steepness makes the system snap back to it firmly. The fourth power is what makes equilibrium stable.
Q4. Now set IN = OUT and solve — where does the ratio α/ε appear, and what does it govern?
Setting α·S·A = ε·σ·A·T⁴, the area cancels, leaving T⁴ = (α/ε)·(S/σ), i.e. T = (S/σ)1/4·(α/ε)1/4. The two surface properties never appear alone — only as the ratio α/ε, and even that under a fourth root. Physically: α controls how much sunlight comes IN, ε controls how readily heat goes OUT, so it is their balance that sets the temperature. Want a cooler surface? Lower α/ε — reflect more sun or radiate better. That single ratio is the spacecraft thermal designer's master control, and Worked 7A is its proof.

7.11 Orbit and eclipse — why temperature is really a time series

Everything so far found one equilibrium temperature for one set of conditions. But a satellite orbits, and its surroundings change continuously as it goes — so the real answer is not a single number but a curve in time.

The environment changes around the orbit

In sunlight the array is hot (full solar input) and making power. Then the satellite swings behind the planet into eclipse — the planet blocks the Sun — and abruptly there is no solar input: the array cools fast and makes no power. Albedo and IR also vary with where the satellite is over the planet (above bright cloud vs dark ocean, day side vs night side). So temperature and power are genuinely time series traced out over each orbit, not constants.

Solar intensity falls with distance from the Sun

The Sun's light spreads over an ever-bigger sphere as it travels outward, so the flux dilutes with distance:

flux = LSun / (4π·d²)
LSun = the Sun's luminosity (total power output, W); d = distance from the Sun (m); 4π·d² = the surface area of a sphere of radius d.

The term AM0 ("Air Mass Zero") is the solar spectrum in space with no atmosphere in the way — its intensity is 1367 W/m² at 1 AU (one AU, Astronomical Unit, = the Earth–Sun distance, ≈1.496×1011 m). The 1/d² law is exactly why this value differs elsewhere: at Mars (≈1.52 AU) the flux is only 1367 / 1.52² ≈ 591 W/m² — under half of Earth's — which is why a Mars array must be correspondingly larger.

Term — umbra and penumbra

The planet's shadow has two parts. The umbra is the region of full shadow — the Sun is completely blocked, total eclipse. The penumbra is the partial shadow ringing it, where the planet blocks only part of the Sun's disc, so a fraction of sunlight still gets through. A satellite passing through experiences a brief penumbral dimming, then full umbral darkness, then dimming again on the way out.

How PowerPy finds eclipses  eclipse.py

eclipse.py propagates (steps forward in time) the satellite's orbit and the Sun/planet positions, then works out the intervals when the satellite is inside the umbra — i.e. when it is eclipsed. It currently does this with the poliastro/astropy astronomy libraries. EXAM-HOT Chapter 2 replaces this with the maintained hapsira library (poliastro is no longer maintained), which additionally lets PowerPy compute the albedo and IR fluxes properly rather than approximating them.

7.12 Pitfalls

Pitfall — forgetting to convert °C to KELVIN before the T⁴ term EXAM-HOT

The Stefan–Boltzmann law demands absolute temperature. If you leave T in °C, the error is colossal — not a few percent but orders of magnitude. A panel at 27 °C radiates as 300 K, i.e. ∝ 300⁴ = 8.1×109; plug in "27" and you get 27⁴ = 5.3×105 — wrong by a factor of about 15,000. And near 0 °C it is catastrophic: 0 °C is 273 K (huge radiation), but "0"⁴ = 0 (claims the panel radiates nothing). Always apply TKelvin = T°C − T0 (= T°C + 273.15) before raising to the fourth power. This is why PowerPy stores °C but converts to Kelvin inside the balance.

Pitfall — forgetting that a failed cell's negative Pelec ADDS heat EXAM-HOT

In the front-node equation Pelec is subtracted, which for a healthy cell correctly removes heat. But a reverse-biased failed cell (Section 6) has negative Pelec. Subtracting a negative adds: the term flips from cooling to heating, and the failed cell becomes a heat source in the balance. Forget this sign and you will predict a failed cell cooling the panel, when it is in fact the hot-spot melting it. The same equation describes both regimes — only the sign of Pelec changes.
