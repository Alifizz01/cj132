6 Reverse bias, hot-spots, and why a failed cell can melt the panel

Here is the most counter-intuitive fact in the whole study of solar arrays: when one cell in a string dies, the danger is not that it stops working — it is that the circuit forces it to keep carrying current it can no longer make. To do that, the dead cell turns itself into a tiny electric heater, and the energy for that heater is donated by every healthy cell around it. A single failed cell can become the hottest point on the entire panel — hot enough, unprotected, to scorch the silicon and even soften the aluminium it is glued to. This section builds that failure from first principles, shows the one component that tames it (the bypass diode), and explains why predicting "which failures melt, and which layouts survive" is precisely the job PowerPy's new failure-sweep machinery exists to do.

Key idea In a series string, every cell is forced to pass the same current (Section 5's rule). If one cell can no longer generate that current, it does not politely drop out — the others push the current through it anyway. The only way a non-generating cell can carry current is to go to negative voltage: it flips from being a battery to being a resistor-like load, and the electrical power it now absorbs comes out as heat. That heat is the failure we are trying to predict.

6.1 Setup recap — the iron rule of a series string

Section 5 established the rule we lean on for the entire section, so hold it firmly in mind: in a series string the current is common to every cell. You cannot have 0.48 A flowing in one cell of a string and 0.20 A in its neighbour — they are wired end-to-end, so the same charge per second must pass through all of them, like cars on a single-lane road. Each cell is free to sit at its own voltage, and the string's total voltage is the sum of the cell voltages; but the current is shared and identical. (Forward reference: Section 5 derived this from how series connections add. We take it as given here.)

That one rule is the seed of the entire failure mechanism. Everything below is just following it to its uncomfortable conclusion.

6.2 The failure, one step at a time

Let us walk a single cell from "healthy" to "dangerous," one link in the chain at a time. Picture a string of healthy cells with one cell in the middle that has just cracked, been shadowed, or otherwise failed.

The mechanism — step by step
1. A cell stops generating. One cell is shadowed, micro-cracked, or simply degraded past use. It can no longer photo-generate the current the string is running at. On its own it would like to pass only a trickle.
2. But the current still has to flow through it. The other healthy cells in the string — and any parallel strings sharing the same bus — keep generating and keep pushing their current. Because the string is series (the rule of §6.1), that current has nowhere else to go: it must pass through the bad cell.
3. So the bad cell is driven to NEGATIVE voltage. To carry a current it cannot produce from light, the cell is forced to the only state that lets the current through: its voltage goes below zero. It stops acting as a generator (which would push current out) and starts acting as a load (which absorbs energy). This back-to-front operating state is called reverse bias — the cell is said to be back-biased.
4. Pushed hard enough, it reaches reverse breakdown. A cell can only hold off so much reverse voltage. Past a certain point — its reverse breakdown voltage, around −20 V for these cells (the figure on the design sketch) — the junction gives way and conducts heavily in reverse.
5. Negative voltage × current = power turned to HEAT. Electrical power is P = V·I. When V is negative, the product is the power the cell absorbs rather than delivers — it dissipates |V|·I as heat. A small region forced to swallow the electrical output of many other cells is a hot-spot.
6. The heat raises the local temperature — possibly past what the panel can survive. All that power is dumped into one ~30 cm² patch. Its temperature climbs. If it exceeds the limit of the panel substrate — here an aluminium honeycomb core — it can permanently break down the cell or even soften and melt the aluminium. That destructive outcome is the event we want to predict and design against.
Read the chain as one sentence: series forces equal current → the dead cell must carry it → carrying it without generating means going negative → negative voltage times current is heat → concentrated heat can melt the substrate. Each arrow is forced by the one before it; nowhere did the cell "get a choice." ✓
Term — reverse bias (back-biased)

Reverse bias means a component is operated with its voltage the "wrong way round" compared to how it normally delivers power. A solar cell normally sits at positive voltage and pushes current out (it is a source). A reverse-biased cell sits at negative voltage while current is pushed into it (it is a sink). Plainly: a back-biased cell has stopped being a battery and become a heater. The deeper the negative voltage, the more power it absorbs.

Term — hot-spot EXAM-HOT

A hot-spot is a small region of a panel that is forced to absorb the electrical power of many other cells and dissipate it as heat, so its local temperature rises far above the rest of the array. It is created when a back-biased cell (or a small shaded patch) becomes the load for a whole string — or worse, for several parallel strings at once.

Term — substrate / aluminium honeycomb core

The substrate is the structural panel the cells are mounted on. On a spacecraft it is typically an aluminium honeycomb core: a lightweight sandwich of two thin aluminium (or composite) face-sheets bonded either side of a hexagonal-cell aluminium "honeycomb," like a stiff, ultra-light cardboard. It is wonderfully strong-for-its-weight but it is still aluminium — it has a temperature limit, and a hot-spot that exceeds it can soften or melt the core, wrecking the panel's structure as well as the cell.

6.3 Where does the energy come from? (The part people miss)

It is tempting to think the heat in a hot-spot somehow comes from "inside" the failed cell. It does not — a dead cell has no power source of its own. The dissipated heat is supplied entirely by all the OTHER cells and strings that are forcing the current through it.

The donor picture

Every healthy cell in the string is still a little generator. Normally each spends its energy delivering useful power to the load. When one cell goes reverse-biased, the healthy cells' energy is instead spent pushing current through that one bad cell — and the bad cell, sitting at negative voltage, turns that pushed-in energy into heat. Add parallel strings sharing the bus and the situation is worse still: now several strings' worth of current can be crammed through the single failed cell. One tiny cell is being asked to swallow the combined output of many. That asymmetry — many sources, one sink — is exactly why a single failure can be destructive out of all proportion to its size.

6.4 The protection — the bypass diode

The cure was introduced in Section 5: a bypass diode wired across a cell or a group of cells, oriented so it does nothing during normal operation but switches on the instant a cell tries to go strongly negative.

Term — bypass diode (vs blocking diode)

A bypass diode is connected in parallel across a cell or cell-group, the "wrong" way for normal current, so it stays off while the cells generate. If a cell is driven into reverse, the diode becomes forward-biased and turns on at about −0.7 V, offering the string current an easy detour around the bad cell. (Contrast the blocking diode of §6.6, which sits in series with a whole string to stop current flowing backwards into it from the bus. Bypass goes around; blocking sits in line.)

The bypass diode does two things at once, and the second is the one that saves the panel:

  • It carries the string current around the bad cell. The healthy cells' current flows through the diode instead of being forced through the failed cell, so the string keeps delivering (minus that one cell's contribution).
  • It CLAMPS the reverse voltage. Because the diode conducts at about 0.7 V, the voltage across the bypassed path can never run away to −20 V — it is pinned near −0.7 V. The failed cell sees a small reverse voltage instead of a catastrophic one.
Why the clamp is everything

Heat in the failed cell is |V|·I. The bypass diode does not change the current much, but it slashes |V| from ~20 V down to ~0.7 V — a factor of about 28×. Same current, a tiny fraction of the voltage, so a tiny fraction of the heat. That is how a clamp at three-quarters of a volt prevents a melt.

But here is the subtlety that makes the whole design trade-off make sense. A bypass diode is rarely fitted across every single cell (that would be a lot of diodes). It is usually fitted across a group of K cells at once — a bypass group. When the diode fires, it clamps the whole group to about −0.7 V. But inside that group the other (K−1) healthy cells are still generating, each adding its own forward voltage. Those healthy in-group voltages stack up and push the failed cell more negative than the −0.7 V seen across the group as a whole:

|Vfail|  ≈  0.7 V  +  (K − 1)·Vcell   (capped at the reverse-breakdown voltage, ≈ 20 V)
K = cells per bypass group; Vcell = a healthy cell's operating voltage (≈ 2.35 V for the §3 cell). With K = 1 (a diode per cell) the pile-on term vanishes and |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 V — the best case. As K grows, the failed cell is driven harder, until the stack reaches the −20 V breakdown and the protection is effectively overwhelmed.
The trade-off, stated cleanly

Fewer cells per bypass group (more diodes) = safer but heavier. Shrinking K reduces the (K−1)·Vcell pile-on, so a failed cell sits closer to the gentle −0.7 V clamp and dissipates less heat. The price is more diodes: more mass, more solder joints, more complexity, more things that can themselves fail. Choosing K is a genuine engineering decision — protection bought with grams — and it is exactly the kind of decision PowerPy is built to evaluate.

6.5 Why PowerPy and this project care

Whether a given failure is a shrug or a melt depends on three design choices: how many cells share each bypass diode (the K above), where the blocking diodes sit, and the overall layout of cells, strings and buses. Change those and you change which single-cell failures stay harmless and which become destructive hot-spots. Quantifying that — which failures cause a melt, and which layouts are robust — is not a side-task; it is the headline job of the new tooling.

How PowerPy models this
  • Circuit (customizable) — the new Chapter 2 customizable Circuit class lets you describe an arbitrary layout: how many cells per bypass group, how strings are paralleled, where blocking diodes go. The layout under test is no longer hard-coded.
  • Monte-Carlo failure sweep — a Chapter 2 sweep that randomly fails cells across many trials and asks, statistically, how often a single failure drives a hot-spot past the danger line. This is how you compare "robust" against "fragile" layouts with numbers, not hand-waving.
  • DD_Shunt — PowerPy already represents the cell's reverse path (the conduction route a back-biased cell takes) via its DD_Shunt element, so the negative-voltage behaviour at the heart of this section is in the model, not bolted on.
  • season = 0 — to simulate a failure, PowerPy can drive a cell to season = 0 (a "dead" cell that generates nothing), then watch what the surrounding circuit forces upon it. That is the on-switch for the whole failure analysis.

6.6 A note on the blocking diode

One related part, so the vocabulary does not blur. A blocking diode sits in series with an entire string, between the string and the shared bus. Its job is not hot-spot protection of a single cell but to stop current flowing backwards into a weak or shaded string from the stronger strings on the bus — which would otherwise be another way to back-bias cells. Bypass diodes go around cells; blocking diodes sit in line with strings. Both shape how bad a failure gets, which is why the customizable Circuit class lets you place both.

6.7 Worked examples

Now the numbers — the contrast that makes the whole section concrete. We use the §3 cell's max-power current, I = 0.48 A, and its ~30 cm² area throughout.

Worked 6A — PROTECTED: bypass diode clamps the reverse voltage

Given: a failed cell in a string carrying I = 0.48 A. A bypass diode (here one per cell, the best case K = 1) clamps the cell's reverse voltage to V = −0.7 V. Find the heat dissipated in the failed cell.

Identify the dissipation:   power turned to heat in the cell = |V|·I.
Magnitude of the reverse voltage:   |V| = |−0.7 V| = 0.7 V.
Multiply by the current:   P = 0.7 V × 0.48 A = 0.336 W ≈ 0.34 W.
Is that a lot? No — about a third of a watt spread over ~30 cm². That is a barely-warm patch, comparable to a small indicator LED. The cell survives, the panel survives. The clamp did its job. ✓
Worked 6B — UNPROTECTED: the cell is driven to reverse breakdown

Given: the same current I = 0.48 A, but now no effective bypass — the cell is pushed all the way to its reverse-breakdown voltage V = −20 V. Find the heat, the heat flux, and compare to sunlight.

Heat dissipated:   P = |V|·I = 20 V × 0.48 A = 9.6 W — concentrated in one ~30 cm² cell.
Compare to the protected case:   9.6 W ÷ 0.336 W ≈ 28× more power in the very same cell, for the very same current. The only thing that changed was the voltage the cell was allowed to reach.
Heat flux (power per unit area):   first convert area, 30 cm² = 30 cm² × (1 m / 100 cm)² = 30 × 10−4 m² = 0.0030 m².
Flux:   9.6 W ÷ 30 cm² = 0.32 W/cm²; in SI, 9.6 W ÷ 0.0030 m² = 3200 W/m².
Reference it to sunlight:   full sunlight in space (the solar constant) is ≈ 1361 W/m². So 3200 W/m² is about 2× (roughly 2.3×) the intensity of full sun — but unlike sunlight, which warms the whole panel, this is dumped into one tiny spot.
Verdict. Concentrating twice the power of full sunlight into a single ~30 cm² patch, with no clamp to relieve it, is comfortably enough to scorch the cell and threaten the aluminium core beneath it. The protected cell (Worked 6A) ran at one twenty-eighth of this. That 28× gap, bought by a 0.7 V clamp, is the entire reason bypass diodes exist. ✓
Worked 6C (optional) — how the bypass GROUP size K changes the worst case

Setup: use |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (K−1)·Vcell with healthy cells at Vcell ≈ 2.35 V (the §3 cell), still at I = 0.48 A, breakdown capped at 20 V. Compare one big group versus halving it.

K = 1 (a diode per cell):   |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 0 = 0.7 V → P ≈ 0.7 × 0.48 = 0.34 W (the Worked 6A bookend).
When does the stack hit breakdown?   set 0.7 + (K−1)·2.35 = 20 → (K−1) = 19.3 / 2.35 ≈ 8.2 → K ≈ 9. So a group of about 9 or more cells can drive the failed cell all the way to the −20 V breakdown (the Worked 6B bookend), P ≈ 9.6 W.
Halving a large group, e.g. K = 8 → K = 4:   at K = 4, |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 3×2.35 = 0.7 + 7.05 = 7.75 V → P ≈ 7.75 × 0.48 ≈ 3.7 W, versus K = 8 giving |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 7×2.35 = 17.15 V → P ≈ 8.2 W. (both below the 20 V cap, so the formula applies directly)
The lesson in one line. Halving the group slides the failed cell down the line from the breakdown bookend toward the clamp bookend — here from ~8.2 W to ~3.7 W. More diodes, less heat. This is the trade-off of §6.4 made quantitative, and it is exactly the quantity the Monte-Carlo sweep estimates across a whole array. ✓

6.8 Socratic ladder — why the dead cell is the hottest point

Ladder — Why does a single failed cell get HOTTER than any healthy cell ever does, and what sets whether it melts the substrate?
Q1. In a series string, what is forced to be the same for every cell?
The current. Series cells are wired end-to-end, so the same charge-per-second flows through all of them (§6.1). A cell can sit at its own voltage, but it cannot opt out of the shared current. This is the constraint everything else is forced to obey.
Q2. If a cell can no longer generate that shared current, what must it do to let the current pass through it?
It must go to negative voltage — it must reverse-bias. A generating cell sits at positive voltage and pushes current out; a cell that cannot generate but is forced to conduct can only do so by becoming a load, i.e. by dropping below zero volts. It has no other way to satisfy "the current must pass through me."
Q3. Once it sits at negative voltage carrying current, where does its heat energy come from?
From all the other cells and strings pushing the current (§6.3). The failed cell has no source of its own; it is a sink. Power |V|·I flows into it, donated by the healthy generators around it, and comes out as heat. A healthy cell, by contrast, delivers power outward and is gently warmed only by sunlight — so it can never reach the temperature of a cell absorbing the output of many neighbours. That asymmetry (many donors, one sink) is the whole reason the failed cell is uniquely hot.
Q4. So what finally decides whether that hot-spot melts the substrate?
A race between two limits. On one side, how negative the voltage is allowed to get: with a bypass diode the voltage is clamped near −0.7 V (small heat, Worked 6A); without one it runs to the −20 V breakdown (≈28× the heat, Worked 6B). On the other side, the substrate's temperature limit: the aluminium-honeycomb core can only take so much before it softens. Melt happens when the dissipated heat drives the local temperature past the substrate limit. So the answer is: the bypass clamp sets how much heat is generated; the substrate limit sets how much heat is survivable; whichever side wins decides between "warm patch" and "melted panel." Tightening the bypass groups (smaller K) is how the designer keeps the first number safely under the second.

6.9 Pitfall

Pitfall — "a dead cell just goes open-circuit" EXAM-HOT

The instinct is to assume a failed cell simply stops contributing — an open circuit, a quiet gap in the string. In a series string the opposite is the dangerous case: the surrounding cells force current through the failed cell, driving it into reverse bias, and it becomes the hottest, most dangerous point on the panel — the exact place a melt starts. "Dead" does not mean "inert." Without a bypass diode, "dead" can mean "on fire." Always ask not "did the cell stop?" but "what is the rest of the circuit now forcing upon it?"
