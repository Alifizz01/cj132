The Bounce,

How do you solve a problem where two things each depend on the other — and you know neither of them? This is the idea that's been hard. So here it is taken as slowly as it goes: one small step per page, the same example repeated until it's boring, nothing clever held back for later.

You asked the exact right question: "electricity and temperature each depend on the other, and both are missing — how do we even solve that?" It feels impossible. It isn't. There's a single trick, and once you see it you'll wonder why it looked hard. We'll get there in tiny steps. If any one page feels too fast, that's a page to re-read — there's no prize for rushing.

Start at zero — why a satellite even cares

Before any maths, let's stand on Earth and follow the whole story, step by step, so you can see why we end up needing this at all.

1 · you build a satellite

On Earth, in a clean room, you build a satellite. It has computers, radios, cameras — all of which need electricity to run. There are no power sockets in space.

2 · so you give it solar panels

You bolt on big wings covered in solar cells — thousands of little tiles that turn sunlight into electricity. These panels are the satellite's only power source. If they fail, the whole satellite goes dark.

3 · you launch it; it circles the Earth

A rocket carries it up. Now it loops around the Earth, over and over. On the sunny side the panels soak up sunlight and make power; then it swings behind the Earth into shadow (an eclipse), goes cold and dark, then back into the sun. Round and round, hot–cold, hot–cold.

4 · one day, a cell fails

After years of radiation and tiny impacts, one solar cell dies. Now its still-working neighbours shove electricity backwards through the dead one — and instead of making power, it turns into a tiny heater stuck on the panel. That hot spot can get hot enough to melt the panel underneath.

5 · here's the catch: you can't fix it

There is no repair truck in orbit. Whatever you launched is what you live with. So the only safe moment to deal with this is before launch, back on Earth — by predicting, on a computer, which cell failures would be dangerous and which would be harmless.

So what must we predict? "Dangerous" means "gets too hot." So for any failure we imagine, we must answer one question: what temperature does that cell reach? Below the melt limit → safe. Above it → trouble. Everything in this handbook exists to compute that one temperature number, for a satellite that hasn't flown yet.
And that is where today's hard idea comes from

To get that temperature, we hit a snag: a cell's temperature depends on its electricity, and its electricity depends on its temperature. Each needs the other first. That is the knot this chapter is about — and the rest of these pages is the simple trick that unties it.

First — what exactly is hard

Two things. Call them temperature and electricity.

Here is the knot:

To know the electricity, you first need the temperature.
To know the temperature, you first need the electricity.

So you sit down to start… and you can't. To begin with electricity, you'd need the temperature — which you don't have. To begin with temperature, you'd need the electricity — which you don't have either.

Neither one will go first. That's the whole difficulty. It's not the maths. It's that there seems to be no place to start.

A picture for the knot Two polite people at a door. Each says: "After you." The other says: "No — after you." Neither steps through. They could stand there all day. Nothing is broken; they're just both waiting for the other to go first.

The trick — and it's the whole secret

Watch how the two people actually get through the door in real life. One of them just… goes. Picks a moment and steps through. Not because they were told to — they just break the standstill by moving.

We do the same. We can't get temperature or electricity to go first… so we make one up. We pick a temperature. Any temperature. We don't know if it's right — that's fine, it's a placeholder.

Pretend you know the temperature.

And here is the magic that makes it all work:

The one sentence to remember The moment you pretend you know the temperature, the electricity stops being a mystery — because the only reason electricity was unknown was that temperature was unknown.

Read that again. Electricity wasn't unknowable. It was just waiting for temperature. Hand it a temperature — even a fake one — and it can be worked out like any normal sum.

Why a fake number is allowed Think of a recipe that says "add salt to taste." You can't compute "to taste" in advance. So you just guess a pinch, taste it, and adjust. The guess isn't cheating — it's how you get unstuck. You fix it after.

One word that trips everyone: "unknown"

"Unknown" sounds like "we can never know it." That's not what it means here.

What "unknown" really means here It means "I can't work this out yet — because it's waiting on the other thing." Not "impossible." Just "not until I know its partner."

So both were "unknown" only because they were waiting on each other. Break the waiting — feed one of them a number — and the other becomes a plain, ordinary calculation. No mystery left in it.

So far, in three short lines

1. The hard part is only that neither will go first.

2. So we make one up (pretend a temperature).

3. With that, the other one (electricity) is easy to work out.

That's already most of the idea. The only thing left is: our made-up number was probably wrong. What do we do about that? Next page.

Fixing the fake — the bounce

We made up a temperature. It was a guess, so it's probably wrong. Here's the beautiful part: we use it to get a better one, then throw the old one away. Over and over. Each one better than the last.

Let's do it with real numbers, one step at a time. Don't skim — read each box fully before the next.

Step 1 · make one up

Pretend the cell is at 28 °C (just room temperature — a placeholder, nothing special).

Step 2 · now the rest is easy

Because the temperature is now "known" (28 °C), we can work out the electricity, and from that the heat, and from that the real temperature. Out comes 65 °C.

Step 3 · compare

We pretended 28 °C, but it actually came out 65 °C. Those don't match. So our pretend was wrong. No problem — we just learned a better number.

Step 4 · do it again with the better number

Now pretend 65 °C instead, and run the same easy steps again. This time it comes back… 65 °C.

Step 5 · it matched — stop

We put in 65 and got out 65. The number stopped changing. That means temperature and electricity finally agree with each other. That is the answer. We're done.

The same thing, as a picture

Each round, the number you put in and the number you get out come closer together, until they're the same. Like walking down stairs that get shallower until the floor is flat.

The whole method in one breath

Make one up → work out the rest → replace your made-up number with the better one → repeat until the number stops changing.

The three questions people get stuck on

If something still itches, it's almost always one of these three. Each is answered on its own, slowly.

A · "Why does picking a temperature make the electricity workable?"
The short answer

Because electricity was only stuck waiting for the temperature. A cell at a fixed, known temperature has a definite electrical behaviour you can just calculate — the same way a kettle at a known setting draws a definite power. Fix the temperature (even a fake one) and the "waiting" ends; the sum becomes ordinary.

B · "Why redo it? Why not trust the first answer?"
The short answer

Because the first answer was built on a made-up temperature (28 °C). The cell isn't really at 28 °C, so the electricity we computed was slightly off, so the temperature it gave (65 °C) is better but still not final. We redo it with 65 °C to remove the leftover error. Each redo uses a truer starting number, so each answer is truer.

C · "Why does it ever settle? Why not bounce forever?"
The short answer

Because each round the mistake shrinks. 28 was off by a lot; 70 was off by less; 66 by less still; 65.2 by almost nothing. The corrections get tinier and tinier until they're too small to matter — the number stops moving. When putting a number in gives the same number out, there's nothing left to fix. That's the signal to stop.

One more everyday version, to lock it in

Guessing the shower temperature You step toward a shower with an unfamiliar tap. You can't calculate the right tap position — it depends on the water, which depends on the tap… the same knot. So what do you actually do? You pick a position (guess), feel the water (work out the result), and nudge (replace your guess with a better one). Too cold → nudge warmer. A bit too warm → nudge back a touch. Each nudge is smaller. Within a few tries it's just right and you stop nudging. You just ran the exact method — guess, check, nudge, repeat until it stops changing — without anyone calling it maths.

That shower is the whole chapter. The cell does the same dance between its temperature and its electricity; the computer just nudges with numbers instead of your hand on the tap.

The one page to keep

If you remember nothing else

The knot: two things each need the other first, so neither will start.

The fix: make one up. Pretending one is known turns the other into an easy sum.

The cleanup: your made-up number was wrong, so replace it with the better one you just got — and do it again.

The finish: when the number stops changing (same in, same out), the two things finally agree — that's the answer.
