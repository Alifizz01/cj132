The setup — the numbers we use everywhere

One cell, one panel material. These exact values come from the real substrate file msro_case2.json and the test case. Keep this page open as a reference for all the worked cases.

Kelvin vs Celsius — read this first All the physics is done in kelvin (K), which is just Celsius shifted: K = °C + 273.15. Why? Because the glow law uses temperature to the 4th power, and that only works from absolute zero (−273.15 °C = 0 K), the coldest anything can be. So we compute in kelvin, then subtract 273.15 at the very end to report °C.
The one mental model Every case below is a bathtub: heat pours in (sunlight, and for a failed cell, reverse-bias heat), heat drains out (the cell glowing to space). The temperature is the water level, and it settles where the tap rate equals the drain rate. All we ever do is find that level.

1The master equation: in = out

At steady state nothing is changing, so heat in must equal heat out. Writing that down is the whole solver. Let's build it in plain pieces.

Heat IN — what the cell absorbs

Qin = αF · A · Psun  (+ any electrical heat if the cell is failed)
front soaks up a fraction αF of the sunlight falling on its area A.

Heat OUT — what the cell glows away

Qout = ε · A · σ · T⁴
a warm surface radiates at a rate set by its emissivity ε, its area A, the constant σ, and its temperature to the 4th power. (Section 6 unpacks the T⁴.)
A useful simplification — and why it's allowed The real model tracks the front and rear faces separately, joined by conduction C. But that conduction term is huge here (C·A = 1000 × 0.003 = 3 W per degree), so the two faces are dragged to almost the same temperature — they end up only ~0.66 °C apart. So for hand-work we may treat the cell as one temperature that glows from both faces at once. We add the two emissivities:
Qout ≈ (εF + εR) · A · σ · T⁴ = k · T⁴,   with  k = (εF+εR)·A·σ
This "lumped" answer lands within about 1 °C of the exact two-node code — close enough to follow by hand, and Section 8 then shows where the last degree comes from.
Compute the constant k once, reuse it forever
k = (εF + εR) · A · σ
= (0.90 + 0.89) × 0.003 × 5.670367×10⁻⁸
= 1.79 × 0.003 × 5.670367×10⁻⁸
= 1.79 × 1.7011×10⁻¹⁰
k = 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰  (units: W/K⁴)
Check: it's a tiny number because σ is tiny — that's fine, it gets multiplied by T⁴ which is huge (T⁴ ≈ 10¹⁰). Tiny × huge = a sensible number of watts.
The master equation we'll solve, three times Qin = k · T⁴  →  T = (Qin / k)1/4. The only thing that changes between cases is Qin. That's it.

2Case A — the idle sunlit cell → 65 °C

A healthy cell in full sun that is not being asked for power yet. The only heat in is the sunlight.

Worked, A to Z
Step 1 — heat in. Qin = αF·A·Psun = 0.970 × 0.003 × 1367.
First 0.003 × 1367 = 4.101. Then 0.970 × 4.101 = 3.978 W. The cell soaks up 3.978 joules every second.
Step 2 — set in = out. 3.978 = k · T⁴ = 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ · T⁴.
Step 3 — solve for T⁴. T⁴ = 3.978 ÷ 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ = 1.3064×10¹⁰.
Step 4 — take the 4th root (that's two square roots in a row).
√(1.3064×10¹⁰) = 1.14298×10⁵. Then √(1.14298×10⁵) = 338.08. So T = 338.08 K.
Step 5 — back to Celsius. 338.08 − 273.15 = 64.93 °C.
Code's exact two-node answer: front 65.26 °C, rear 64.60 °C code ✓. Our hand value 64.93 sits right between them — the lumped model gives the average of the two faces, as expected.
Why 65 °C and not boiling? 3.978 W on a thumbnail-sized cell sounds small, and it is — so the cell only needs to be modestly warm to glow it all back out. The T⁴ drain is generous: by ~65 °C the glow already carries off all 3.978 W, so it stops warming there.
Practice 1 Redo Step 1 if the sunlight were only 1000 W/m² (a dimmer orbit). What is Qin? (You don't need to finish the temperature.)

3Case B — a cell making power → 39 °C

Now the same cell is actually delivering electricity to the satellite — it extracts 1.1 W. Energy that leaves as electricity is energy that does not stay as heat, so less is left to glow off, so the cell runs cooler.

Worked, A to Z
Step 1 — heat in, minus what leaves as electricity. The sun still puts in 3.978 W, but 1.1 W walks out as electrical power. Net heat to glow off = 3.978 − 1.1 = 2.878 W.
Step 2 — in = out. 2.878 = 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ · T⁴.
Step 3. T⁴ = 2.878 ÷ 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ = 9.452×10⁹.
Step 4. √(9.452×10⁹) = 97 221. √(97 221) = 311.80 K.
Step 5. 311.80 − 273.15 = 38.65 °C.
Code exact: 38.89 °C code ✓. And it's cooler than the idle 65 °C — exactly as expected, because power that left as electricity isn't there to heat the cell.
A surprising-but-true point Pulling out just 1.1 W dropped the temperature from 65 °C to 39 °C — a big fall for a small power. Why so dramatic? Because of the T⁴ drain again: near 65 °C the cell is glowing hard, so removing even a little incoming heat lets the temperature drop a fair amount before the (weaker, cooler) glow re-balances. Small change in heat, noticeable change in temperature.
Practice 2 Using the steps above, find the temperature if the cell extracts 2.0 W instead of 1.1 W. (Net heat = 3.978 − 2.0.) Carry it to °C.

4Case C — the reverse-bias hot-spot → 187 °C

The dangerous one. A failed cell is driven backwards by its neighbours and, instead of giving power, it absorbs 9.6 W of electrical power — all of which becomes heat, on top of the sunlight.

Worked, A to Z
Step 1 — heat in, now bigger. Sun puts in 3.978 W, and the reverse-bias adds another 9.6 W of heat (extracted power is −9.6 W, and subtracting a negative adds): 3.978 − (−9.6) = 3.978 + 9.6 = 13.578 W.
Step 2 — in = out. 13.578 = 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ · T⁴.
Step 3. T⁴ = 13.578 ÷ 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ = 4.459×10¹⁰.
Step 4. √(4.459×10¹⁰) = 211 164. √(211 164) = 459.53 K.
Step 5. 459.53 − 273.15 = 186.38 °C.
Code exact: 187.48 °C code ✓. This is the hot-spot the whole project worries about — hot enough to threaten the glue and the aluminium honeycomb underneath.
Why this melts and the others don't Idle 65 °C, working 39 °C, failed 187 °C — same little cell, same sunlight. The difference is entirely the 9.6 W of reverse heat dumped into a thumbnail of area. Big heat, tiny spot, no air to carry it away → a temperature that can cook the panel.
Three cases, one equation The only thing that changed across A, B, C was the heat in: 3.978 W (idle) → 2.878 W (working) → 13.578 W (failed). Feed each into T = (Qin/k)1/4 and out come 65, 39, 187 °C.

6The T⁴ law, explained slowly

We've leaned on "glow ∝ T⁴" three times. It's worth understanding, because it's why the temperatures settle where they do instead of running away.

Stefan–Boltzmann law Every warm object radiates heat as invisible infrared light. The rate it radiates grows with the fourth power of its absolute temperature (in kelvin): double the kelvin temperature and the glow goes up 2⁴ = 16 times.
Why "to the 4th power" makes the cell safe Because the drain ramps up so steeply, a cell can shed a lot more heat with only a modest rise in temperature. When the reverse-bias dumped an extra 9.6 W, the cell didn't climb to thousands of degrees — it climbed to 187 °C, where the (now much stronger) glow already matches the bigger heat-in. The T⁴ is a self-tightening brake: the hotter it gets, the much-faster it sheds, so it always finds a finite balance.
Everyday version A trickling drain (cool object) barely empties the tub. But this drain widens fast as the water rises — by the time the level is high, it's a gushing torrent. So the level can't run away; a little extra height unleashes a lot more draining. That "widens fast with height" is the T⁴.
Ladder · feel the 4th power
Q1. Glow ∝ T⁴. If kelvin temperature doubles, the glow multiplies by what?
2⁴ = 16. Four factors of 2 multiplied together.
Q2. Our idle cell was 338 K, the hot-spot 460 K. Ratio of temperatures ≈ 1.36. Roughly how much more does the hot-spot glow?
1.36⁴ ≈ 3.4 times more glow. So the hot-spot sheds about 3.4× the heat per second the idle cell does — which it must, because it's being fed about 3.4× the heat (13.6 W vs 3.98 W). It checks out.
Q3. Why must we use kelvin, not Celsius, in T⁴?
Because the law counts heat from the true zero of temperature (absolute zero). Celsius has its zero at freezing water, an arbitrary point — raising 0 °C to the 4th power would give 0 glow, which is nonsense. Kelvin fixes that.

7Newton's method — the full hand trace

Taking a 4th root with a calculator is easy. But the real equation (two faces, conduction) can't be rearranged into "T = …" at all, so the code doesn't take a root — it searches with Newton's method. Let's watch it solve the reverse-bias case from a cold start, every step by hand.

We hunt for the T where the net heat is zero:

f(T) = 13.578 − k·T⁴  (net heat, want = 0)   |   f′(T) = −4·k·T³ (its slope)
Newton's rule: next T = T − f(T) ÷ f′(T). Start at 28 °C = 301.15 K.
Iteration 0 — start cold at 301.15 K (28 °C)
T⁴ = 301.15⁴ = 8.225×10⁹. So f = 13.578 − 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ × 8.225×10⁹ = 13.578 − 2.505 = +11.07 W (way too much heat — too cold).
T³ = 301.15³ = 2.732×10⁷. Slope f′ = −4 × 3.0450×10⁻¹⁰ × 2.732×10⁷ = −0.0333.
next T = 301.15 − (11.07 ÷ −0.0333) = 301.15 + 332.8 = 634.0 K = 360.9 °C.
It overshot way past the answer! That's expected here — read the note below.
Why the first jump overshoots (a real, honest detail) At a cold 28 °C the glow curve is nearly flat (the slope f′ is tiny, −0.033). Newton follows that shallow slope a long way before it predicts a zero-crossing, so it leaps past. No problem — from the next point the curve is steeper and it homes in fast. This is exactly why the code starts from a sensible 28 °C and still always converges within a few more steps.

8Two temperatures — front vs rear, and the 2×2

Our hand method used one temperature. The code uses two — a front and a rear — because the sunlit front really is a touch hotter than the shaded rear. Here's where that last fraction of a degree lives.

f1 = Qfront − εF·A·σ·(T₁⁴ − Tsp⁴) − Pelec + C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0
front balance: sun in, front glow out, electricity out, and heat conducted to the rear.
f2 = Qrear − εR·A·σ·(T₂⁴ − Tsp⁴) − C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0
rear balance: (here no rear sun) rear glow out, and the same conducted heat arriving from the front.
Why the front ends up hotter — and only just All the sunlight lands on the front, so the front would love to be hotter. But the conduction term C·A·(T₂−T₁), with C·A = 3 W per degree, is a fat pipe shovelling that heat to the rear almost as fast as it arrives. The result: front 65.26 °C, rear 64.60 °C — a gap of only 0.66 °C. The fat pipe nearly ties them together, which is exactly why our one-temperature hand method (64.93 °C, the average) was so close.
What the "2×2 Jacobian" is, in plain words With two unknowns (T₁, T₂) and two equations (f₁, f₂), Newton needs the slopes — but now there are four of them: how f₁ changes with T₁, with T₂, and how f₂ changes with T₁, with T₂. Arrange those four slopes in a little 2-by-2 box and you have the Jacobian — just "the slope, but for two equations at once." The code inverts that tiny box with the schoolbook formula for a 2×2 and takes the Newton step. Same idea as Section 7, one dimension richer.
Jacobian = [ a  b ; c  d ],  a = −4εFAσ T₁³ − CA,  b = c = +CA,  d = −4εRAσ T₂³ − CA
a, d are each face's own glow-slope plus the shared conduction; b, c are the conduction link between them. Invert, step, repeat — converged in 5–7 steps.
Picture the 2×2 Two bathtubs (front, rear) joined by a fat pipe. To predict both levels you can't treat one tub alone — pushing the front level changes the rear through the pipe. The 2×2 box is just the bookkeeping of "if I nudge this tub, how do both tubs respond," which is what you need to correct both guesses together.

9The electro-thermal bounce — worked rounds

Everything so far assumed we already knew the heat in. But a cell's electricity depends on its temperature, and its temperature depends on its electricity — the chicken-and-egg from Chapter 5. Here we work the loop with numbers. Take a partly-shaded cell whose dissipated heat grows a little as it warms: Pelec(T) = −(8 + 0.02·(T − 50)) W (negative = heat in).

Round 0 — start at 28 °C
Electricity at 28 °C: Pelec = −(8 + 0.02·(28 − 50)) = −(8 − 0.44) = −7.56 W.
Thermal solve with that heat: net in = 3.978 + 7.56 = 11.538 W → T⁴ = 11.538/k = 3.789×10¹⁰ → T = 441.2 K = 168.0 °C.
Damped step (ω = 0.5): don't jump all the way; move halfway. new T = 28 + 0.5·(168.0 − 28) = 28 + 70 = 98.0 °C.
Round 1 — now at 98.5 °C
Electricity at 98.5 °C: Pelec = −(8 + 0.02·(98.5 − 50)) = −(8 + 0.97) = −8.97 W.
Thermal solve: net in = 3.978 + 8.97 = 12.95 W → T = 454.1 K = 181.0 °C.
Damped step: new T = 98.5 + 0.5·(181.0 − 98.5) = 139.8 °C.
Keep going and it climbs 28 → 98 → 140 → 165 → … and settles at 198.8 °C code ✓, where the temperature you put in is the temperature you get out — the two models finally agree.
What "damping" (ω) buys you Each round we step only a fraction ω of the way to the new temperature. With ω = 0.5 we move halfway. It's like nudging a stiff shower tap gently instead of slamming it: a few more nudges, but no wild hot–cold swings. In easy cases full jumps also work; the gentle step is insurance for the hard ones.

10The breakdown (melt) check

Once we have a temperature, one last easy check decides "safe" or "destroyed". Two rules; if either trips, the cell is flagged dead.

Rule 1 (heat):  Tfront ≥ Tlimit   |   Rule 2 (electrical):  V < 0 and |V·I| ≥ Prev-limit
Use Tlimit = 150 °C and Prev-limit = 2 W for this example.
Cell 1 — the idle cell (65.26 °C, V = +2.3 V, I = 0.48 A)
Rule 1: 65.26 ≥ 150? No.
Rule 2: V = +2.3 is not negative, so reverse power = 0 W; 0 ≥ 2? No.
Verdict: SURVIVES.
Cell 2 — the hot-spot (187.48 °C, V = −20 V, I = 0.48 A)
Rule 1: 187.48 ≥ 150? Yes. (already destroyed on heat alone)
Rule 2: V = −20 is negative; |V·I| = |−20 × 0.48| = 9.6 W; 9.6 ≥ 2? Yes.
Verdict: DESTROYED — and both rules agree. code ✓
Why two rules, not one Heat catches the slow cooking; reverse-power catches a violent electrical stress that could crack a cell even before it fully heats. Checking both means a failure that one rule misses, the other still catches.

11The orbit fluxes — sun, albedo, IR, tilt

In orbit the "heat in" isn't only direct sun. Three streams arrive, and the panel's angle scales them. All four are one-line calculations.

Direct sun — the inverse-square law
At Earth (1 AU): Psun = 1367 W/m² code ✓.
At Mars (1.52 AU), sunlight thins as 1/distance²: 1367 ÷ 1.52² = 1367 ÷ 2.3104 = 591.7 W/m² code ✓.
Farther from the sun → weaker sun. Mars gets under half of Earth's sunlight.
Albedo — sunlight bounced off the planet
albedo flux = (bond albedo) × (sun at planet) × (view factor) = 0.25 × 591 × 0.3.
= 0.25 × 591 = 147.75; × 0.3 = 44.33 W/m² code ✓.
Planetary infrared — the planet's own glow
IR = ε · σ · Tplanet⁴ · (view factor), with Earth ≈ 255 K, ε = 1, view = 0.3.
255⁴ = 4.228×10⁹. × 5.670367×10⁻⁸ = 239.8 W/m². × 0.3 = 71.93 W/m² code ✓.
Notice this uses the same σT⁴ glow law from Section 6 — the planet is just a big warm body glowing at us.
Tilt — the cosine rule
A panel tilted 30° from facing the sun catches cos(30°) of the beam = 0.866 code ✓.
Face-on (0°) → cos 0 = 1 (full). Edge-on (90°) → cos 90 = 0 (nothing). 30° keeps about 87%.

12Monte-Carlo — the run-count maths

We can't test every possible failure, so we sample many random ones. Two small calculations tell us how trustworthy a batch of runs is, and how many we need.

Standard error — the "wobble" of an average
Take two results, 0 and 2. Their average = 1.
Spread (sample variance) = [(0−1)² + (2−1)²] ÷ (2−1) = (1 + 1) ÷ 1 = 2. So standard deviation = √2 ≈ 1.414.
Standard error = std ÷ √N = 1.414 ÷ √2 = 1.0 code ✓.
Standard error is "how much the average would wobble if you reran the whole batch." More runs → smaller wobble.
How many runs do we need?
Suppose 100 runs give a wobble of 8 °C, and we want it down to 2 °C.
Accuracy improves like 1/√N, so to get 4× tighter (8 → 2) we need 4² = 16× the runs.
16 × 100 = 1600 runs code ✓.
Everyday version Polling a city: ask 100 people and your estimate jitters; ask 1600 and it steadies. But to keep halving the jitter you must keep quadrupling the sample. At some point "good enough" beats "perfect but endless."

The one equation behind all of it

Everything you worked, in one line

Find the temperature where heat in = heat out: Qin = k·T⁴.

• Change Qin (sun, ± electricity, reverse-bias) → get 65, 39, 187 °C.

• Can't take a clean root (two faces) → Newton searches it (Section 7).

• Electricity and temperature each need the other → bounce until they agree (Section 9).

• Then a melt check, the orbit fluxes feeding Qin, and Monte-Carlo to repeat it for many failures.

Why the hand answers were a touch off The lumped one-temperature method gave 64.93 / 38.65 / 186.38; the code's two-node solver gave 65.26 / 38.89 / 187.48. The ~1 °C gap is the small front–rear split (Section 8). The physics and the method are identical — the hand version just averages the two faces into one.

Glossary

Absorptivity (α) Fraction of incoming sunlight a surface soaks up (0–1).
Albedo flux Sunlight reflected off the planet onto the array = albedo × sun × view-factor.
Conduction (C) Heat seeping through the panel = conductivity ÷ thickness; here C·A = 3 W/°C.
Emissivity (ε) How good a surface is at glowing heat away (0–1).
Flux Energy per second per square metre arriving on a surface (W/m²).
Jacobian (2×2) The four slopes of two equations in two unknowns — Newton's "slope" in two dimensions.
Kelvin (K) Absolute temperature = °C + 273.15; required for the T⁴ law.
Lumped model Treating the cell as one temperature (front+rear averaged); the hand shortcut.
Newton–Raphson Root-finder: next T = T − f(T)/f′(T); follows the slope to where net heat = 0.
Qin Total heat into the cell per second (sun ± electrical).
Reverse bias A failed cell driven backwards; absorbs electrical power as heat (Pelec < 0).
Standard error How much a Monte-Carlo average would wobble; = std ÷ √N.
Stefan–Boltzmann (σ) Constant in the glow law; σ = 5.670367×10⁻⁸ W/m²K⁴.
T⁴ law Radiated heat rises with the 4th power of absolute temperature.
Under-relaxation (ω) Stepping only a fraction ω toward each new guess so the loop settles calmly.
Vectorised Solving all cells' equations at once with numpy instead of a per-cell loop.
