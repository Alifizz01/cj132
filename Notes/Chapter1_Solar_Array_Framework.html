Before the topics, here are the core words in plain language. Don't memorise them — just meet them once, so they aren't fog when we use them. Each returns, fully explained, in its own section, and all of them are collected in the Glossary at the back.

Photovoltaic (PV) effect Light knocks electrons loose in a semiconductor and drives them as a current. A solar cell is one such light-to-electricity device.
The four numbers Isc (short-circuit current, at 0 V), Voc (open-circuit voltage, at 0 A), and the MPP (Maximum Power Point, the voltage/current pair Vmp, Imp where power V·I is greatest). They summarise a cell.

AM0 — Air Mass Zero The strength of sunlight in space at the Earth's distance: 1367 W/m². PowerPy's reference irradiance.

Monte Carlo Estimating a hard quantity by running many random trials and averaging — here, random cell-failure patterns to find which are most damaging.

1Why a Solar Array — and the Four Numbers of a Cell

A satellite is a machine that must keep itself alive in one of the most hostile places we know — the vacuum of space — for years at a time, with no fuel line, no power cable, and no repair crew. Almost everything it does, from firing its radio back to Earth to keeping its own electronics from freezing, costs electrical power. That power has to come from somewhere, and for the overwhelming majority of spacecraft it comes from sunlight, captured by a solar array. This section builds your mental model of a solar array from the single building block — one tiny solar cell — and introduces the four measured numbers an engineer uses to describe it. Everything later in PowerPy is built on these four numbers, so we will go slowly and define every word the moment it appears.

1.1 What a solar array is for, and what PowerPy does

Let us start with the mission, because the numbers only make sense once you know what they are protecting. A spacecraft in orbit lives through conditions that would destroy ordinary electronics:

  • Vacuum. There is no air. That sounds harmless, but it means there is no convection — no breeze, no air to carry heat away. The only way a satellite can shed heat is by radiating it as infrared light. This is why temperature is such a central concern, as we will see.
  • Radiation. High-energy particles from the Sun and from deep space steadily damage the semiconductor crystal in each solar cell. Over years this degradation slowly lowers the power the array can produce.
  • Thermal cycling. Each orbit the spacecraft swings from full sunlight (hot) into the Earth's shadow (cold) and back, sometimes dozens of times a day. The array expands and contracts with every cycle.
  • Eclipses. When the spacecraft passes behind the Earth, the Sun is blocked and the array produces nothing. The satellite must run on batteries until it comes back into the light.

The engineer's job is to design an array that is big enough to power the spacecraft and robust enough to survive all of the above for the whole mission. To do that without launching a real satellite to find out, we simulate it. That is what PowerPy is.

Term PowerPy — a software tool that simulates a satellite solar array. Crucially, it predicts two linked things at once: the array's electrical output (how many volts and amps, hence how many watts, it delivers) and its temperature. Both matter, because — as we will learn — a solar cell's electrical behaviour depends strongly on how hot it is. PowerPy lets an engineer size an array (choose how many cells, and how to wire them) and verify that it keeps working even when something goes wrong (a "fault").
Note A solar cell is fundamentally a heat engine living in a fridge that has no door: sunlight pours energy in, only ~30 % leaves as electricity, and the rest becomes heat that can only escape by radiating to cold space. So an electrical model that ignores temperature is only half a model. Keep this dual nature in mind — it is the reason PowerPy computes both.

A real array is not one cell. It is a hierarchy, assembled from the bottom up. You will meet each of these objects by name later in the notes; for now just see the ladder so the vocabulary is not a surprise:

Term cell → string → section → panel — the four levels of a solar array, smallest to largest. A cell is one photovoltaic device (our subject this section). Cells wired end-to-end form a string. Strings grouped together form a section. Sections mounted on a physical wing form a panel. (Each is taught in its own later section — here we only name them so you have the map.)
Key idea Master the single cell first. A string is just cells in a row, a section is strings grouped, a panel is sections mounted — so every higher level is a combination of the level below it. If you truly understand one cell's four numbers, the rest of the array is bookkeeping built on top of them.

1.2 How a solar cell makes electricity — the photovoltaic effect

Before the numbers, the physics — in plain words. The word photovoltaic literally splits into photo (light) and voltaic (electricity, after Volta): "light → electricity." A solar cell turns light directly into electrical current with no moving parts. Here is the mechanism, one step at a time.

A solar cell is made of semiconductor material — a crystal (classically silicon; in space, usually gallium arsenide) whose electrons are almost free to move but need a small kick of energy to break loose. The cell is built as two layers pressed together:

Term p–n junction — the boundary where a layer of p-type semiconductor (doped so it has a shortage of electrons, i.e. spare positive "holes") meets a layer of n-type semiconductor (doped so it has spare electrons). At this boundary an internal electric field forms, like a one-way ramp. "Junction" simply means this boundary. ("Doped" means a tiny, deliberate amount of another element has been added to the crystal to give it extra electrons or extra holes.)

Now the effect itself, in four micro-steps:

  1. A particle of light — a photon — strikes the cell. A photon is a single packet of light energy; sunlight is a torrent of them.
  2. If the photon carries enough energy, it knocks an electron loose from the crystal, leaving behind a hole. Now you have one free negative charge (the electron) and one free positive charge (the hole).
  3. The internal field at the p–n junction sweeps the electron one way and the hole the other — the "one-way ramp" separates them so they cannot simply recombine.
  4. Connect a wire between the cell's two terminals and the separated electrons flow through it to get back to the holes. That flow of charge is an electric current. Put a load (say, a radio) in that wire and the current does useful work.
Key idea Light in, current out, no moving parts: a photon frees an electron, the junction's built-in field pushes it around an external circuit. More light striking the cell means more electrons freed per second, which means more current. Hold on to that last sentence — it is exactly why the first of our four numbers depends on sunlight.

Space cells are special. Sunlight contains many colours (energies) of photon, and any single semiconductor only efficiently catches a narrow band of them. To catch more of the spectrum, space cells stack three sub-cells:

Term triple-junction GaAs cell — a high-performance space solar cell made of gallium arsenide (chemical symbol GaAs) built as three stacked sub-cells, one on top of another. Each sub-cell is tuned to absorb a different part of the sunlight's colour spectrum (roughly: top catches blue, middle catches green, bottom catches red). Stacking three means much less sunlight is wasted, which is why these cells reach an efficiency of about 30 % — far higher than the ~15–20 % of an ordinary silicon panel on a house roof.
Note "Efficiency ~30 %" is a given property of this cell type — it means about 30 % of the sunlight energy landing on the cell leaves as electricity. We will not calculate it here, because that needs the strength of sunlight as a separate input number, which we introduce later. Treat 30 % as a stated fact for now.

1.3 The four numbers that describe a cell EXAM-HOT

An engineer does not describe a solar cell with a wall of physics. They describe it with four measured numbers, plus one special point those numbers define. Get these four straight and keep them distinct — confusing them is the single most common beginner mistake. We will define each precisely, with its defining condition.

Number 1 — Short-circuit current, Isc

Term Short-circuit current (Isc) — the current that flows when you connect the two terminals of the cell directly together with a plain wire, so the voltage across the cell is zero (V = 0). This is the most current the cell will ever push. Its value is set by how much sunlight is hitting the cell and how big the cell is — more light or more area means more electrons freed per second, hence more current. For our cell, Isc ≈ 0.50 A.
Note "Short circuit" sounds like a fault, but for a solar cell it is harmless and is a standard test condition: a solar cell is a current source, so shorting it just lets its natural current flow with nothing held back. It will not burn out from being shorted the way a battery would.

Number 2 — Open-circuit voltage, Voc

Term Open-circuit voltage (Voc) — the voltage measured across the cell's terminals when nothing is connected (the circuit is "open"), so no current flows (I = 0). This is the most voltage the cell will ever show. Its value is set by the physics of the p–n junction and — very importantly — by temperature: a hotter cell has a lower Voc. For our cell, Voc ≈ 2.67 V.
Key idea The two "ends" of a solar cell's behaviour are opposites: Isc is "all current, no voltage" (V = 0); Voc is "all voltage, no current" (I = 0). Sunlight mainly drives Isc; temperature mainly drives Voc. This split is worth memorising — it explains most of what an array does in orbit.

Numbers 3 & 4 — Max-power voltage and current, Vmp and Imp

Between the two extremes lies the operating point an engineer actually cares about. A cell can be operated anywhere along its curve — you choose, by choosing the load. At one special place the product of voltage and current (which is power) is as large as it gets. The voltage and current at that place are our third and fourth numbers.

Term Max-power voltage and current (Vmp, Imp) — the voltage and the current the cell delivers when it is operating at the single best point on its curve, where output power is greatest. For our cell, Vmp ≈ 2.35 V and Imp ≈ 0.48 A. Notice each is a little below its extreme: Vmp < Voc and Imp < Isc. That "give a little of each" is the whole secret, as we will see.
Key idea — the count of four The four numbers are exactly: Isc, Voc, Vmp, Imp. That is all four. The MPP, Pmp and the Fill Factor we meet next are not extra members of the four — they are derived from these four. Keep the headcount honest: four measured numbers, everything else computed from them.

The point those last two numbers define — the MPP

Term Maximum Power Point (MPP) — the single point on the cell's curve, located at the coordinates (Vmp, Imp), where the output power P = V · I reaches its largest value. The power there is called the maximum power, Pmp = Vmp · Imp. "MPP" is the point (a location on the curve); "Pmp" is the number of watts at that point.
Pitfall Do not confuse the point with the power. The MPP is a place (a voltage paired with a current). Pmp is a single number of watts. Saying "the MPP is 1.13 W" is sloppy; the MPP is at (2.35 V, 0.48 A), and the power there is Pmp ≈ 1.13 W. Examiners love this distinction.

1.4 The IV curve and the power curve

The four numbers are really four landmarks on one continuous picture: the cell's IV curve. This single graph is the heart of solar-cell engineering, so we will read it carefully.

Term IV curve (current– voltage curve) — a graph that plots the cell's current I (vertical axis) against the voltage V (horizontal axis) for every possible operating point. You read it as: "if the cell is held at this voltage, it will deliver that current." The curve runs from the point (0, Isc) on the left — zero volts, full current — across to (Voc, 0) on the right — full volts, zero current.

The shape between those two endpoints is the cell's signature. Reading left to right:

  • Flat stretch. Starting at the left, as voltage rises from zero the current barely drops — the curve runs almost horizontally near Isc. Across most of its working range the cell behaves like a near-constant current source.
  • The knee. At a certain voltage the curve bends sharply downward — like the knee of a bent leg. This bend sits right around Vmp.
  • The steep drop. Past the knee the current falls fast, plunging to zero at Voc.

Now overlay a second curve: power. For every point on the IV curve we can compute P = V · I and plot it. This power curve is not flat — it is a hump that starts at zero on the left, rises to a peak, and falls back to zero on the right. The peak of the hump sits exactly above the knee of the IV curve, at the MPP.

Why is the power curve a hump that touches zero at both ends? Because power is the product of two things, and at each end one of them is zero:

P = V · I
where P is power in watts (W), V is voltage in volts (V), and I is current in amperes (A). One volt times one amp equals one watt.
Key idea — why P = 0 at both ends At the left end (short circuit) the voltage is zero, so P = 0 · Isc = 0 W: plenty of current, but no voltage to push it through, so no power. At the right end (open circuit) the current is zero, so P = Voc · 0 = 0 W: plenty of voltage, but no current flowing, so no power. A product is zero whenever either factor is zero. Real power only appears between the ends, where both V and I are sizeable at once — and it is biggest where their product peaks, the MPP.
Think it through — climbing to "why not at the ends?"
Q1. What does the IV curve plot, in one sentence?
The current the cell delivers (vertical axis) at each possible voltage you could hold it at (horizontal axis), from (0, Isc) on the left to (Voc, 0) on the right.
Q2. How do we turn a point on the IV curve into power?
Multiply that point's voltage by its current: P = V · I. Every point on the IV curve has a power, and plotting those powers gives the power curve.
Q3. What is the power at the far left end of the curve (V = 0)?
Zero. P = 0 · Isc = 0 W. The current is at its maximum, but with zero voltage there is no power. All flow, no push.
Q4. And at the far right end (I = 0, at Voc)?
Also zero. P = Voc · 0 = 0 W. The voltage is at its maximum, but with zero current there is no power. All push, no flow.
Q5. So why is the maximum power NOT at Voc or Isc, but somewhere in between?
Because power needs both a healthy voltage and a healthy current at the same time — it is their product. At each extreme one factor collapses to zero and drags the product to zero. As you move inward from the left, voltage climbs while current stays high, so the product grows; keep going and eventually current starts dropping faster than voltage rises, so the product shrinks again. The best trade — the largest product — sits at the knee, in between. That balance point is the MPP, at (Vmp, Imp). You sacrifice a little voltage off Voc and a little current off Isc to keep both high together.

1.5 Fill Factor — how "square" the curve is EXAM-HOT

We now have everything we need for the most important derived figure of merit in solar engineering: the Fill Factor. It answers a natural question: an ideal cell would deliver its full Isc right up until its full Voc — a perfect rectangle of power, worth Voc · Isc. Real cells round off at the knee and fall short of that rectangle. How close does a real cell get? That ratio is the Fill Factor.

Term Fill Factor (FF) — a pure number between 0 and 1 that measures how "square" the IV curve is, i.e. how close the cell's actual maximum power comes to the ideal rectangle Voc · Isc. It is the ratio of the real maximum power to that ideal rectangle. Good space cells score about 0.8 (sometimes higher). A higher FF means a squarer curve and a better cell.
FF = (Vmp · Imp) / (Voc · Isc) = Pmp / (Voc · Isc)
where Pmp = Vmp · Imp is the real maximum power (W), and Voc · Isc is the "ideal rectangle" power (W). The watts cancel, so FF has no units — it is a bare ratio.
Note Picture two rectangles drawn under the IV curve from the origin. The big one has corners at Voc and Isc — the ideal. The smaller one has its far corner at the MPP, (Vmp, Imp) — the real maximum power. FF is simply the area of the inner (MPP) rectangle divided by the area of the outer (ideal) rectangle. A perfectly square curve would make them equal and FF = 1.
Worked example (a) Compute Pmp and the Fill Factor for our reference cell: Isc = 0.50 A, Voc = 2.67 V, Vmp = 2.35 V, Imp = 0.48 A.
Step 1 — maximum power. The MPP is at (2.35 V, 0.48 A). Multiply: Pmp = Vmp · Imp = 2.35 V × 0.48 A = 1.128 W. (Volts × amps = watts.)
Step 2 — the ideal rectangle. Voc · Isc = 2.67 V × 0.50 A = 1.335 W. This is the power the cell would give if its curve were a perfect rectangle.
Step 3 — divide. FF = Pmp / (Voc · Isc) = 1.128 W / 1.335 W = 0.845 (≈ 0.84). The watts cancel, so the answer is a bare number with no units, as it must be.
Check: FF came out 0.845, comfortably inside the legal range 0–1, and right around the "~0.8" we expect for a good space cell. Both sanity tests pass. A value above 1, or with units left on it, would mean an arithmetic slip.
Worked example (b) Power away from the MPP. Suppose we read the IV table and find that at V = 2.0 V the cell delivers I ≈ 0.49 A. How much power is that, and how does it compare with Pmp?
Step 1 — power at this point. P = V · I = 2.0 V × 0.49 A = 0.98 W.
Step 2 — compare with the maximum. From example (a), Pmp = 1.128 W. So here we get 0.98 W ÷ 1.128 W ≈ 0.87, i.e. about 87 % of the maximum.
Step 3 — the surprising part. Look closely: the current here (0.49 A) is actually higher than the current at the MPP (Imp = 0.48 A). Yet the power is lower. Why? Because we dropped the voltage from 2.35 V to 2.0 V — a loss of 0.35 V — to gain only 0.01 A of current. The voltage fell by much more (in proportion) than the current rose, so the product V·I went down.
Check: 0.98 W < 1.128 W, so operating off the MPP gives less power than the MPP — exactly as it must, since the MPP is by definition the maximum. Running at higher current did not help: power is the product, and we lost more on voltage than we gained on current. This is the IV curve's central lesson in one number.

1.6 Why the MPP matters in practice

Why obsess over a single point? Because every watt counts on a spacecraft. A solar array is heavy and expensive to launch, so engineers want the most power from the fewest cells. That means operating the array as close to its MPP as possible.

  • Some spacecraft use a dedicated electronic circuit, a maximum power point tracker, that continuously adjusts the load to sit the array right at its MPP — squeezing out the most watts at every moment.
  • Others use a simpler scheme: they hold the array at a fixed bus voltage chosen to be near Vmp under typical conditions. Cheaper, slightly less optimal.
Pitfall The MPP is not nailed down — it moves. Recall that temperature strongly affects Voc (a hotter cell gives less voltage), and that radiation slowly degrades the cell over years. Both shift the whole IV curve, and with it the MPP. An array sized for a cold, brand-new cell can fall well short when the cell is hot and aged. PowerPy exists precisely to predict these shifts; we study how temperature and degradation move the curve in later sections. For now, just hold the warning: the MPP you design for is not the MPP you will always have.
Key idea Operate near the MPP to get the most power per cell — but design with margin, because the MPP drifts with temperature and age. "Size for the worst case (hot, old), not the best case (cold, new)" is an engineer's habit you will use again and again.

1.7 Meeting these ideas in PowerPy — the cell API

Everything above maps onto a handful of PowerPy commands. You do not need to run anything now; just connect each command to the concept it computes, so the code feels familiar when you reach it.

Term API (Application Programming Interface) — the set of named commands a piece of software gives you to ask it to do things. PowerPy's cell API is just the list of instructions you type to build a cell and ask about it.
  • cell("3G30") — create a cell object. The string "3G30" names the cell type to model (a triple-junction, ~30 cm² space cell — the very cell whose numbers we have been using).
  • cell.calcQuad() — returns the four numbers plus the maximum power, as a bundle: {isc, voc, vmp, imp, pmp}. (Our four landmarks, with Pmp handed to you for free.)
  • cell.calcMP() — returns the Maximum Power Point: the (Vmp, Imp) where power peaks.
  • cell.calcIVCurve(step) — returns the IV table: a list of (V, I) points marching along the curve, where step sets how finely spaced the voltage samples are (a smaller step gives a denser, smoother table).
  • cell.IVCurve() — plots that table as the picture you saw above: current versus voltage.
Note Two methods sound similar but differ: cell.calcQuad() hands you the summary numbers (the landmarks), whereas cell.calcIVCurve(step) hands you the whole curve as a table of many points. Use the first when you only need Isc/Voc/MPP/FF; use the second when you need the full shape — for example to find the power at an arbitrary voltage, as in worked example (b).

2The Equivalent Circuit, Model Fitting, and SPICE

2   The single-diode equivalent circuit, how PowerPy fits it, and SPICE

A solar cell is a slab of semiconductor, not a wire and a resistor. Yet to simulate a cell on a computer we need to describe it with components a circuit simulator understands. The trick that makes all of PowerPy work is this: a solar cell behaves almost exactly like a small network of ideal electrical parts, so we replace the physics with that network and solve the network instead. This section builds that network one part at a time, shows how it bends into the current–voltage curve you actually measure, then shows the two ways PowerPy turns four measured numbers into the network's component values — and finally how it hands the whole thing to a real circuit simulator (SPICE / ngspice).

The one idea. Model, don't measure-everything. If a cell behaves like (current source) + (diode) + (two resistors), then once we know those four part-values we can predict the cell's current at any voltage, temperature or illumination — without ever building it. PowerPy's job is to find those part-values from a datasheet's four numbers and then let a simulator do the predicting.

2.1   Building the model one part at a time

Light falling on the junction frees charge carriers and drives a current. So the heart of the model is an ideal current source.

Ideal current source. A two-terminal element that pushes a fixed current through itself no matter what voltage appears across it. In the model it represents the light-generated current (the photocurrent), and its value is approximately the short-circuit current, Isc — the current you measure when you connect the cell's two terminals straight together (zero volts across them).

But a real cell is not only a current source: it is built on a p–n junction, the same structure as a diode. That junction is always present, sitting across the cell, so we add a diode in parallel with the current source.

Diode. A one-way valve for current. It conducts easily when its anode is pushed positive relative to its cathode (forward bias) and blocks almost completely the other way. In the cell model it is the p–n junction itself — and crucially it is what bends the IV curve over near the top voltage.

Real junctions also leak: a tiny bit of current sneaks across the junction edge or through defects even when the diode "should" block it. We capture that leak with a resistor straight across the junction.

Shunt resistance, Rsh. A resistor in parallel with the diode/current-source, representing every unwanted leakage path across the junction. "Shunt" means a side-path that current can divert through. A good cell has a very large Rsh (little leakage); a poor or shaded cell has a smaller Rsh.

Finally, the current has to travel through the metal: the front contacts, the grid fingers, the interconnect tabs between cells. Metal is not perfectly conducting, so we add a resistor in series with the output.

Series resistance, Rs. A resistor in series between the junction and the output terminals, representing the resistance of contacts and interconnects. A good cell has a very small Rs. Every milliamp that flows must pay a voltage toll I·Rs across it.
Why "single-diode". Because the model uses exactly one diode to represent the junction. There is a more elaborate "two-diode" model (a second diode captures recombination in the depletion region) but PowerPy uses the simpler single-diode form, which is accurate enough for array-level energy studies and far cheaper to fit and simulate.

2.2   The diode's behaviour: the Shockley equation EXAM-HOT

Everything interesting about the curve's shape comes from how the diode conducts. That conduction is described by the Shockley diode equation:

Idiode = I0 · ( exp( V / (n · Vt) ) − 1 )
where
• Idiode = current through the diode (A)
• V = voltage across the diode (V)
• I0 = saturation current (A): the minuscule reverse leakage of the junction. It is the thing that sets the open-circuit voltage. Typical values for a cell are tiny — 10−12 A or smaller.
• Vt = thermal voltage = kB·T / q ≈ 0.02585 V at T = 300 K
• n = ideality factor (dimensionless, ≈ 1–2 for a single junction): how far the junction departs from a perfect textbook diode. n = 1 is ideal; defects and recombination push it toward 2 (and higher for stacked junctions — see §2.5).
Thermal voltage, Vt = kBT/q. The natural voltage scale of a junction at temperature T. • kB = Boltzmann constant ≈ 1.381×10−23 J/K (converts temperature into energy).
• T = absolute temperature in kelvin (K). 300 K ≈ 27 °C, a convenient "room/spec" temperature.
• q = elementary charge ≈ 1.602×10−19 C (the charge on one electron).
Plug them in: Vt = (1.381e−23 × 300) / 1.602e−19 ≈ 0.02585 V ≈ 25.85 mV. Because Vt ∝ T, a hotter cell has a larger thermal voltage.
Read the exponential. The term exp(V/(n·Vt)) is the whole story of the curve's shape. For small V it is barely above 1, so (exp(…)−1) ≈ 0 and the diode carries almost nothing — the curve sits flat. Once V climbs to a few times n·Vt, the exponential explodes and the diode suddenly gulps current — this is the knee. The knee is not a separate component; it is just the exponential turning on.

2.3   How current source + diode make the IV curve

Now combine them. The current source constantly pushes Isc upward. Where does that current go? It splits between (i) the diode, (ii) the shunt resistor, and (iii) the output terminals. Kirchhoff's current law says the three shares always add up to Isc. Walk the voltage from zero up to open-circuit:

  • At V = 0 (short circuit). The diode sees ~0 V, so by the Shockley equation it carries ~0. The shunt also sees ~0 V, so it carries ~0. With nothing diverted internally, almost all the photocurrent leaves through the terminals: I ≈ Isc. This is why the source value is ≈ Isc.
  • Climbing toward Voc. As the output voltage rises, the diode finally reaches its knee and its exponential turns on. The diode now "steals" the photocurrent internally — current that used to leave the terminals is instead consumed by the diode. The output current falls.
  • At V = Voc (open circuit). The diode steals everything: all of Isc is recirculating internally through the diode (and shunt), so zero leaves the terminals. The voltage at which this happens is the open-circuit voltage, Voc.
Voc (open-circuit voltage) = terminal voltage when no current is drawn (open terminals). Vmp, Imp (voltage/current at maximum power) = the operating point where the product V·I is largest — the "knee" you want to sit on. Pmp = Vmp·Imp, the peak power.

The two resistors fine-tune this picture:

  • Rs rounds the knee. Series resistance subtracts I·Rs from the terminal voltage. Near the knee, current is still high, so the voltage toll is real and the sharp corner gets rounded and pulled left — lowering Vmp and the peak power.
  • Rsh tilts the flat part. A finite shunt bleeds a little current that grows with voltage (Ohm's law through Rsh), so the "flat" plateau is not perfectly flat — it droops with a slope set by 1/Rsh. A large Rsh keeps it nearly flat.
Q·a (foundation). At exactly V = 0, why is the output current equal to Isc and not zero?
Because at 0 V the diode and shunt both have 0 V across them, so each carries ~0 A (Shockley gives exp(0)−1 = 0; Ohm gives 0/Rsh = 0). Nothing is diverted internally, so the full source current Isc must exit through the terminals.
Q·b (why). As V rises toward Voc, the source still pushes a constant Isc. So why does the output current drop?
The source current is fixed, but its destination changes. As V grows the diode's exponential turns on and the diode siphons an ever-larger share internally. Output current = Isc − Idiode − Ishunt, so as the diode's share grows, the terminals' share shrinks.
Q·c (deep). At Voc the cell delivers zero current yet the light is still shining and Isc is still being generated. Where did all that current go — and is the cell doing any useful work?
It is all recirculating internally: every amp from the source is consumed by the diode (plus a trickle through Rsh), dissipated as heat in the junction. None reaches an external load, so the delivered power V·I = Voc·0 = 0. Open circuit is the highest voltage but zero power point — useful work happens between 0 and Voc, peaking at the knee (Vmp, Imp).

2.4   From four measured numbers to circuit parameters

A datasheet gives you four numbers per cell: isc, imp, vmp, voc. PowerPy turns these into the model's component values. It offers two strategies.

First, a name that will trip you. PowerPy calls one quantity vt_cell, but it is not the bare thermal voltage kBT/q. It is the lumped product n·Vt — an effective thermal voltage that already has the ideality factor baked in. Look back at the Shockley exponent V/(n·Vt): vt_cell fills the whole n·Vt slot. That is exactly why, later, we recover the ideality factor by dividing it back out: n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice, where V_T_Spice is the bare Vt. Keep effective (vt_cell = n·Vt) and bare (V_T_Spice ≈ 0.02585 V) firmly apart.
RSeriesModel — the default RSeriesModel

This model throws away the shunt (sets it astronomically large) and puts all the non-ideality into a closed-form series resistance. No equation-solving is needed — every value is a direct formula. That is why it is the default: fast and robust.

vt_cell = vmp · (isc − imp) / imp
The effective thermal voltage n·Vt, fixed by the spacing between imp and isc. Intuition: the closer imp sits to isc (a "boxy", high-fill-factor curve), the smaller (isc−imp) and so the smaller vt_cell — a sharper knee. Units: V × (A/A) = V. ✓
i0_cell = isc / ( exp( voc / vt_cell ) − 1 )
The saturation current, obtained by demanding that at V = voc the diode carries the whole isc. Rearranged Shockley: the huge exponential in the denominator makes i0_cell tiny. Units: A / (dimensionless) = A. ✓
r_cell_shunt = 1×109 Ω
A deliberately enormous shunt — effectively "no shunt at all". With 109 Ω across the cell, the leakage current at a couple of volts is ~10−9 A, utterly negligible next to an amp of photocurrent. So RSeriesModel ignores shunt leakage by construction.
r_cell_high = (vt_cell / imp) · ln( (isc − imp) / i0_cell ) − vmp / imp
The series resistance (the variable is named r_cell_high in the code). It is found by forcing the model to pass exactly through the measured maximum-power point (vmp, imp): solve the cell equation at that point for Rs. The ln term is the junction voltage the diode would need to carry (isc−imp); subtracting vmp/imp converts the leftover into a resistance per amp. Units: (V/A)·(dimensionless) − (V/A) = Ω. ✓
What each formula is really doing. Two of the four model constraints come from the curve's endpoints and one from its knee: i0_cell nails the curve at Voc (top), the source value isc nails it at V = 0 (bottom), and r_cell_high nails it at the maximum-power knee (vmp, imp). The shunt is simply removed. Four measurements → four placements → a curve pinned through all the points that matter.
Worked example (a) — full parameter extraction. Given a triple-junction-style cell: isc = 0.50 A, imp = 0.48 A, vmp = 2.35 V, voc = 2.67 V. Find vt_cell, i0_cell, r_cell_high.
Step 1 — vt_cell. isc − imp = 0.50 − 0.48 = 0.02 A.
vt_cell = vmp · (isc−imp)/imp = 2.35 V × (0.02 A / 0.48 A) = 2.35 × 0.041667 = 0.0979 V.
Units: the (A/A) ratio is dimensionless, so the answer is in volts. ✓
Step 2 — the exponent first. Before the saturation current, evaluate the exponent it needs: voc / vt_cell = 2.67 V / 0.0979 V = 27.3 (dimensionless — volts cancel). ✓
Step 3 — i0_cell. exp(27.3) ≈ 7.2×1011. Subtract 1 (negligible): denominator ≈ 7.2×1011.
i0_cell = isc / (exp(27.3) − 1) = 0.50 A / 7.2×1011 ≈ 6.9×10−13 A.
Sanity: a sub-picoamp saturation current is exactly the "tiny leakage" the theory promised. ✓
Step 4 — the log term for r_cell_high. (isc−imp)/i0_cell = 0.02 A / 6.9×10−13 A = 2.9×1010 (dimensionless).
ln(2.9×1010) ≈ 24.1.
Step 5 — assemble r_cell_high. First term: (vt_cell/imp)·ln(…) = (0.0979 V / 0.48 A) × 24.1 = 0.2040 (V/A) × 24.1 = 4.91 Ω.
Second term: vmp/imp = 2.35 V / 0.48 A = 4.90 Ω.
r_cell_high = 4.91 − 4.90 = ≈ 0.01 Ω (a small fraction of an ohm).
Sanity check. A good cell should have a small series resistance — a few hundredths of an ohm here. ✓ Notice the danger: r_cell_high is the difference of two nearly equal numbers (4.91 − 4.90). That fragility is the whole point of the pitfall below — a slightly off input can make the two terms cross over and the result go negative.
RShuntModel — the alternative RShuntModel

Sometimes the leakage matters — a cell with real shunt paths, partial shading, or a low fill factor. Then you do not want to throw the shunt away. RShuntModel keeps a finite shunt resistance. The price: the equations no longer rearrange into a neat closed form. The model must solve a transcendental equation — an equation where the unknown appears both inside and outside an exponential/log, so no algebra isolates it.

Transcendental equation. One that cannot be solved by rearrangement (e.g. x = e−x). You must find the root numerically: guess, check, refine, repeat until the two sides match.
Root-finder / Brent's method (brentq). An algorithm that finds where a function crosses zero. Brent's method is a robust bracketing root-finder (combining bisection with faster interpolation) exposed in SciPy as brentq. PowerPy hands it the cell's residual equation; it returns the vt_cell that makes the model pass through (vmp, imp) with the finite shunt present. From that vt_cell the model then derives i0_cell and the finite r_cell_shunt.
Which to use. Use RSeriesModel (default) for clean, high-quality cells — it is faster and never fails to converge. Switch to RShuntModel when shunt/leakage behaviour matters. You select it on a cell object with cell.setModel("RShuntModel").

2.5   The ideality factor and triple-junction cells

Once vt_cell (= n·Vt) is known, recovering the ideality factor is one division — divide out the bare thermal voltage:

n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice
V_T_Spice = kB·300 / q ≈ 0.02585 V is the bare thermal voltage at 300 K (the same Vt from §2.2). Dividing the lumped vt_cell by it strips out Vt and leaves n alone. Dimensionless.
Worked example (b) — ideality factor. Using vt_cell = 0.0979 V from (a):
Step 1. n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice = 0.0979 V / 0.02585 V = 3.79 (volts cancel → dimensionless). ✓
Reconcile with "n ≈ 1–2". Back in §2.2 we said a single junction has n ≈ 1–2, yet here n ≈ 3.79. No contradiction: this is a triple-junction cell — three p–n junctions stacked in series. Their junction voltages add (which is why voc ≈ 2.67 V, not ~0.7 V), and so do their effective idealities, giving roughly 3× a single junction's value. An n near 3×(1–2) ≈ 3–4 is exactly what a healthy triple-junction stack should show. ✓
Pitfall — negative series resistance is a data smell. r_cell_high is built as a difference of two nearly equal terms (in example (a): 4.91 − 4.90). If the four measured numbers are not mutually self-consistent — e.g. a vmp that is too high for the given imp/isc/voc — the second term can exceed the first and r_cell_high comes out slightly negative. A negative physical resistance is impossible, so treat it as a red flag that isc, imp, vmp, voc don't describe one real curve. Don't "fix" it by clamping to zero — recheck the inputs. (This is the same fragility flagged in the sanity check of example (a).)

2.6   Handing the model to SPICE / ngspice EXAM-HOT

Having the four part-values is not the same as solving the circuit — the diode's exponential makes it nonlinear. PowerPy therefore exports the model to a genuine circuit simulator and lets it do the numerical heavy lifting.

SPICE. The decades-old standard family of analog circuit simulators ("Simulation Program with Integrated Circuit Emphasis"). You describe a circuit as a text netlist (a list of components and which nodes they connect) and SPICE solves the node voltages and branch currents.
ngspice / NgSpiceShared. ngspice is the open-source SPICE engine PowerPy uses. NgSpiceShared is the in-process interface that lets Python feed it a netlist and read results back without spawning an external program.
Subcircuit (.subckt). A reusable named block in a netlist — here, one solar cell — with named external terminals. PowerPy's builder routine cellBuilder emits a .subckt with two terminals named high and low, then stamps the model components between them.

Between high and low, cellBuilder stamps these elements:

Netlist nameComponentValue
II_Cellcurrent source (the photocurrent)= isc
DD_Celljunction diode, model DcIS = i0_cell, n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice
RR_CHseries resistor= r_cell_high
RR_Cell_Shuntshunt resistor= r_cell_shunt
RR_Iconninterconnect resistor= r_interconnect (from the cell file)
DD_Shuntbypass / shunt diode, model Dsreverse-protection (detailed in a later section)
Notice where the ideality factor lands. SPICE's diode model takes its own n. PowerPy sets the diode's n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice — the very division from §2.5. So the "effective thermal voltage" we fitted is communicated to SPICE precisely by handing the diode that ideality factor on top of the bare thermal voltage SPICE already assumes. The two halves (bare Vt inside SPICE, ideality n from us) multiply back to the n·Vt we measured.

With the subcircuit defined, two API calls drive everything:

  • currentAtVoltage(V) — cell.currentAtVoltage(V) builds the subckt, attaches a test voltage source set to V across high/low, runs an operating-point analysis (.op — SPICE solves the single steady DC state), and reads back the branch current. One voltage in, one current out.
  • calcIVCurve() — cell.calcIVCurve() performs a DC sweep (.dc) of the test source from V = 0 up to Voc, collecting the current at each step, to produce the entire IV curve in one run.
Operating-point (.op) vs DC sweep (.dc). A .op solves the circuit at one fixed set of sources — a single (V, I) point. A .dc repeats that solve while stepping a source across a range — many points, i.e. a curve. So currentAtVoltage is one .op; calcIVCurve is a .dc built from many such solves.
The full pipeline in one breath. Four datasheet numbers → a model (RSeriesModel or RShuntModel) turns them into vt_cell, i0_cell, r_cell_shunt, r_cell_high → cellBuilder writes a .subckt with II_Cell / DD_Cell / RR_CH / RR_Cell_Shunt / RR_Iconn / DD_Shunt → ngspice solves it via .op (one point) or .dc (whole curve) → out comes current vs voltage. Every later capability in PowerPy — strings, shading, temperature — is built on this single-cell pipeline.
Q·d (foundation). What does the ideal current source in the model actually do?
It forces a fixed current (≈ Isc, the light-generated current) through itself regardless of the voltage across it. It is the model's stand-in for photons liberating charge carriers.
Q·e (why). What does the diode do near Voc, and why does that matter?
Near Voc its exponential turns on and it diverts (steals) the photocurrent internally, dropping the output current to zero. This is what creates the knee and caps the cell's voltage — without it the output would never bend over.
Q·f (deep why). So why model the cell as a current source with a diode across it rather than as a battery?
A battery holds a roughly constant voltage and will source whatever current a load demands — the wrong behaviour entirely. A real cell instead holds a roughly constant current (set by light) up to a knee, then collapses in voltage; under shading its current drops, and under heavy load its voltage falls away. Only the current-source-plus-diode pair reproduces that: the source fixes the current floor, the diode bends the voltage ceiling, and Rs/Rsh shape the corner. A battery model would mispredict every operating point off open-circuit — exactly the points where a solar array actually delivers power.
Section recap. A solar cell ≈ current source ∥ diode ∥ shunt, then a series resistor to the terminals. The Shockley exponential bends the curve; i0_cell sets Voc, the source sets Isc, Rs rounds the knee, Rsh tilts the plateau. RSeriesModel fits all of this in closed form (and discards the shunt); RShuntModel keeps a finite shunt and solves a transcendental equation with brentq. Watch the vt_cell = n·Vt naming, and remember a negative r_cell_high means inconsistent inputs. PowerPy then writes a .subckt and lets ngspice solve it — one point with .op, the whole curve with .dc.

3The Cell Over Its Life: Temperature and Radiation

3 How a cell changes over its life: temperature and radiation, and the prepareModel pipeline

In Section 2 we treated a solar cell as four fixed numbers feeding an equivalent circuit. That was a convenient lie. The moment you put a cell in orbit, those four numbers start to move — minute by minute as the cell heats and cools, and year by year as space radiation slowly wrecks the crystal. This section is the story of where the four numbers come from at any given instant, and of the small assembly line — PowerPy calls it prepareModel — that builds them from a fixed data file, a temperature, and a radiation dose.

Key idea A space solar cell's four headline numbers — Isc, Imp, Vmp, Voc — are not constants. They shift with temperature (cold makes a cell push out more voltage; hot makes it push out less) and they degrade as the cell soaks up radiation damage over the mission. PowerPy keeps, per cell type, a JSON file holding the room-temperature Beginning-of-Life values plus lookup curves that describe both shifts.

3.1 A one-line reminder of the four numbers

Before we move them around, recall what they are (Section 2 derived them from the IV curve):

Recap — the four operating-point numbers
  • Isc — short-circuit current (A). The current when the cell's terminals are shorted; the top-left of the IV curve.
  • Voc — open-circuit voltage (V). The voltage with no load drawn; the bottom-right of the IV curve.
  • Imp, Vmp — the current and voltage at the maximum-power point (the "knee"), where I·V is largest.

Everything in this section is about computing these four values for a specific temperature T and a specific accumulated radiation dose, then handing them to the circuit model.

3.2 Beginning of Life and End of Life

Term — BOL / EOL

BOL (Beginning Of Life) — the cell as it leaves the factory: fresh, undamaged, zero accumulated radiation. The numbers printed on a datasheet are BOL numbers.

EOL (End Of Life) — the same cell after it has absorbed the entire mission's radiation dose. Same silicon, same area, but the crystal is now peppered with defects, so it produces less current and less voltage. A mission is sized so that even the weakest, end-of-life array still meets the power budget — so EOL, not BOL, is usually the number that decides whether a satellite survives.

The gap between BOL and EOL is the whole reason this section exists. If cells never aged, you would size a solar array once and forget it. Because they do age, the simulator must know how far down each number has drifted by the time the cell reaches the dose your orbit delivers.

3.3 The cell configuration: what the JSON file holds

Each cell type ships as a small data file. It records the fixed, measured BOL properties of that cell plus the housekeeping PowerPy needs. The keys are:

{
  "name":           " exact part name of the cell ",
  "reference":      " datasheet / source the numbers came from ",
  "alpha":          0.91,      // solar absorptivity  (thermal: how much sunlight is absorbed)
  "epsilon":        0.85,      // IR emissivity       (thermal: how readily it radiates heat away)
  "isc":            0.50,      // short-circuit current        [A]
  "imp":            0.48,      // current at max-power point   [A]
  "vmp":            2.35,      // voltage at max-power point   [V]
  "voc":            2.67,      // open-circuit voltage         [V]
  "r_interconnect": 0.030,     // series resistance of the cell interconnect [ohm]
  "t_ref":          28,        // reference temperature the numbers were measured at [degC]
  "area":           30.18      // cell area [cm^2]
}
Reading the keys
  • alpha (solar absorptivity) and epsilon (IR emissivity) are thermal, not electrical. They decide how hot the cell gets: alpha is the fraction of incoming sunlight the cell absorbs (heats it up), epsilon is how efficiently it re-radiates that heat to space (cools it down). A cell's equilibrium temperature is set by the balance of the two. We only need them here to remember that T is itself a computed quantity, not a free knob.
  • t_ref (reference temperature) is the temperature at which isc, imp, vmp, voc were measured — typically 28 °C. Every temperature correction in this section is measured relative to t_ref: the correction is zero exactly at T = t_ref.
  • r_interconnect is the small series resistance of the metal tab joining cells; it feeds the series-resistance term of the Section-2 circuit.
  • area lets PowerPy scale a cell up or down (a half-size cell delivers half the current). The base isc/imp belong to this reference area.

3.4 Radiation dose, and why it is one number

Space is full of trapped electrons and protons — charged particles held by the planet's magnetic field (the Van Allen belts are the famous example). When one of these particles slams into the cell, it can knock an atom out of place in the crystal lattice. Each displacement is a new defect that traps charge carriers before they reach the contacts, so a little current and a little voltage are lost. Over years, the damage accumulates.

The awkward part: the particles arrive at a huge range of energies, and a 2 MeV proton does not do the same damage as a 0.5 MeV electron. Tracking the whole spectrum cell-by-cell would be hopeless. So the field uses a standard trick:

Term — equivalent 1 MeV electron fluence

Fluence is the total number of particles that have passed through one square centimetre over the whole mission (units: particles per cm²). The equivalent 1 MeV electron fluence collapses the entire messy spectrum of electrons and protons of all energies into one single number: "the dose that would have done the same damage if it had all been 1 MeV electrons." It is the universal currency of radiation damage — a single scalar standing in for the whole spectrum.

PowerPy lets you set this dose, and it does something subtle: it tracks the dose affecting current and the dose affecting voltage separately, because current and voltage degrade at different rates under the same radiation.

cell.setDose(dose_i, dose_v)
sets the current-degrading dose dose_i and the voltage-degrading dose dose_v.
Inputs are in units of 1×1014 e/cm². The code multiplies what you pass by 1e14 internally. So to model a fluence of 1×1015 e/cm² you pass 10, not 1e15.
Pitfall — the units-of-1e14 trap EXAM-HOT

The dose argument is pre-scaled: PowerPy multiplies it by 1×1014 for you. If you read a real fluence of 1e15 e/cm² off a radiation report and type cell.setDose(1e15, 1e15), you have just asked for 1×1015 × 1×1014 = 1×1029 e/cm² — a dose that would vaporise the solar system. The crystal "dies" instantly and every remaining factor collapses.

Right way: divide the real fluence by 1e14 first. 1×1015 e/cm² → pass 10. 5×1014 → pass 5. 3×1013 → pass 0.3.

3.5 Regressors: turning dose into a number

How does the simulator know that a dose of, say, 5×1014 e/cm² leaves 92 % of the current intact? It looks it up. For each cell type the JSON file carries a set of curves — PowerPy calls them regressors — and each is just an interpolation lookup: hand it a dose, it returns a number.

Term — regressor

A regressor here is a stored curve, evaluated by interpolation, that maps a radiation dose to a coefficient. Two families exist, and they answer two different questions.

Family 1 — remaining factors  risc, rimp, rvmp, rvoc. Each is the fraction of the BOL value still surviving after a given dose. At zero dose every remaining factor is exactly 1.0 (nothing has degraded yet); as dose climbs they fall, reaching roughly 0.8–0.9 at end of life. You multiply the BOL value by its remaining factor to get the radiation-degraded value.

Family 2 — temperature coefficients  isc_dt, imp_dt, vmp_dt, voc_dt. Each is a slope: how much the parameter changes per degree Celsius, d(param)/dT. Crucially, these slopes themselves depend on dose — a radiation-damaged cell responds to temperature slightly differently — which is why they are stored as regressors too, not as plain constants.

Signs you must memorise
  • isc_dt is small and POSITIVE. Heating a cell raises its current a little — a hot cell absorbs photons slightly more readily (the band gap narrows). Magnitude is tiny, order 10−4 A/°C.
  • voc_dt and vmp_dt are NEGATIVE and larger in magnitude. Heating a cell drops its voltage, and this is the effect that dominates. A typical figure is voc_dt ≈ −6 mV/°C per junction (a multi-junction cell stacks several, so the cell-level number scales up — treat −6 mV/°C as an illustrative per-junction scale, not the whole-cell value).

One sentence to carry the whole section: cold helps voltage, heat hurts it, and current barely cares.

3.6 The prepareModel pipeline

Now we can assemble the operating-point numbers. prepareModel is the routine that, given the cell's fixed JSON values, a temperature T (in °C), and the two doses, produces the four numbers at this instant. Read it as an assembly line — each step takes the running value and adjusts it.

Two inputs arrive from elsewhere in the simulator and we simply carry them through:

Pipeline — steps 1–2 (pointing & losses, taught in other sections)
1. Tilt (pointing).   tilt = cos(angle_alpha + season_angle) · cos(angle_beta). This is how squarely the sun strikes the panel; 1.0 means dead-on. (Geometry section.)
2. Combine loss factors.   lossI = product of all current-loss multipliers (including an inherited tilt and season factor);   lossV = product of all voltage-loss multipliers. (Losses section.) These are held aside and applied last.

Steps 3–7 are the heart of this section — the temperature-and-dose correction of each of the four numbers. Notice carefully which dose keys which parameter: Isc and Imp use dose_i; Vmp and Voc use dose_v.

Pipeline — steps 3–7 (the dose + temperature correction)
3. Look up the slopes.   imp_dt = imp_dt(dose_i),   isc_dt = isc_dt(dose_i). (When T < 0 °C a special low-temperature blend of the slope is used instead, because the response goes slightly non-linear in the deep cold — mentioned for completeness; we will not dwell on it.)
4. Short-circuit current:   isc = isc·risc(dose_i) + isc_dt(dose_i)·(T − t_ref)
5. Max-power current:   imp = imp·rimp(dose_i) + imp_dt·(T − t_ref)
6. Max-power voltage:   vmp = vmp·rvmp(dose_v) + vmp_dt(dose_v)·(T − t_ref)
7. Open-circuit voltage:   voc = voc·rvoc(dose_v) + voc_dt(dose_v)·(T − t_ref)
Every line has the same shape: (BOL value × remaining factor) + (slope × temperature offset from t_ref). The first term is the radiation hit; the second term is the temperature shift. At T = t_ref the second term vanishes, and at zero dose every remaining factor is 1.0 — so a fresh cell at its reference temperature returns exactly the datasheet number. ✓
Pipeline — step 8 (apply losses, taught in the losses section)
8. Apply the illumination/season voltage corrections and the loss factors held from step 2:
isc ·= lossI;   imp ·= lossI;   vmp ·= lossV·vmp_corr;   voc ·= lossV·voc_corr
The result — (isc, imp, vmp, voc) at this temperature and this dose — is exactly the four-number set that the Section-2 equivalent-circuit model consumes to draw the IV curve. The pipeline's whole job is to feed that model honest, life-stage-correct numbers. ✓
Mental model

Steps 4–7 move each number to the right place for the cell's age and temperature. Step 8 then shaves off the optical and wiring losses that the rest of the system imposes. The worked examples that follow stop at the end of step 7 — at the bare dose-and-temperature operating point — because the loss factors are a separate downstream multiply taught elsewhere. 3.7 Worked examples

These walk steps 4–7 with real numbers and units. They stop before step 8 (no loss factors applied).

Worked 3A — Voc of a cold cell

Given: base voc = 2.67 V at t_ref = 28 °C;   voc_dt = −6.0×10−3 V/°C;   rvoc(dose) = 0.95;   the cell is cold at T = −80 °C. Apply step 7.

Temperature offset:   ΔT = T − t_ref = (−80) − (28) = −108 °C.  (the cell is 108 degrees colder than the reference)

That is exactly right: going cold RAISES the voltage, and at −80 °C the temperature gain (+0.648 V) easily beats the radiation loss (−0.13 V). If you had gotten a number below 2.5365 V you would have flipped a sign somewhere. ✓
Worked 3B — Isc of a hot cell at EOL

Given: base isc = 0.50 A;   risc(dose) = 0.97 (end-of-life dose);   isc_dt = +0.30×10−3 A/°C;   the cell is hot at T = +70 °C. Apply step 4.

Read the reference point carefully. The current barely moves, and it moves up — but "up" here means relative to the radiation-degraded 0.485 A, not the BOL 0.50 A. The full story: radiation drops it from 0.50 A to 0.485 A, then the +70 °C heat nudges it back up to 0.498 A — still slightly under the BOL value. Current is almost temperature-insensitive (the slope is tiny and positive), which is exactly why isc_dt is the small positive coefficient. ✓
Worked 3C — the formula collapses to the BOL number

Claim: at T = t_ref and zero dose, every step 4–7 line returns the original datasheet value. Take Voc with base voc = 2.67 V.

Sum:   voc = 2.67 V + 0 V = 2.67 V — the BOL datasheet value, unchanged.
This is the consistency guarantee: the same one formula that corrects for cold, heat, and radiation reduces to "do nothing" at the conditions where the numbers were measured. A correction that did not vanish at its own reference point would be a bug. ✓

3.8 Why colder means more voltage — a Socratic ladder

Ladder — Why does a solar cell make MORE voltage when it is COLDER?
Q1. What is a "temperature coefficient" in the first place?
It is a slope: how much a quantity changes for each 1 °C change in temperature, d(param)/dT. For voltage we wrote it voc_dt, in volts per °C. Multiply it by how far the temperature is from t_ref and you get the voltage shift. It is the bridge between "temperature changed" and "the number changed."
Q2. What is the SIGN of that coefficient for voltage, and what does the sign mean physically?
It is negative (e.g. voc_dt ≈ −6 mV/°C per junction). A negative slope means: as T goes up, voltage goes down; and as T goes down, voltage goes up. So cold raises voltage follows directly from the minus sign — exactly what Worked 3A showed numerically (ΔT went negative, and −×− gave a positive bump).
Q3. But WHY is the sign negative? What is actually happening inside the cell?
The cell is a diode in the light. Its open-circuit voltage is set by a tug-of-war between the light-generated current and the diode's own saturation current I0 — the tiny leakage current that flows backwards through the junction. I0 depends extremely strongly on temperature: it is governed by how many charge carriers have enough thermal energy to cross the band gap, which is an exponential function of temperature. When the cell gets colder, far fewer carriers have that energy, so I0 plummets. A smaller I0 means the diode leaks less, so the light current can hold the junction at a higher voltage before the two balance — Voc rises. Heat does the reverse: I0 climbs, the junction leaks more, Voc sags.
Q4. Tie it back to Section 2 — where did we already meet this I0?
It is the i0_cell term in the Section-2 equivalent-circuit model — the diode's reverse saturation current that sets where the IV curve bends down to meet the voltage axis. Temperature does not appear as a separate knob in that circuit; instead, prepareModel bakes the temperature effect into the value of Voc it hands the circuit (step 7). So the "cold → more voltage" physics and the circuit's i0_cell are two views of one mechanism: cold suppresses i0_cell, which raises Voc.
Q5. (Trap-spotter) A classmate says: "voltage comes from kT/q, and that term gets smaller when it's cold, so surely cold should LOWER the voltage." Where is the error?
The kT/q prefactor does shrink in the cold, taken alone — but it is not the dominant term. Voc ≈ (kT/q)·ln(Ilight/I0 + 1), and I0 sits inside the logarithm with an exponential temperature dependence. As the cell cools, I0 falls so steeply that the logarithm grows faster than the kT/q prefactor shrinks. The exponential collapse of I0 wins decisively, so Voc rises overall. The classmate fixated on the small prefactor and missed the big exponential — the classic student trap.

4Losses, Seasons, Pointing, and the Voltage Corrections

4 Loss factors, seasons, pointing, and the voltage corrections

Section 3 produced four honest operating-point numbers for a cell at a given temperature and radiation dose. But the array still has not delivered a single watt to the spacecraft bus, because the real world keeps shaving the numbers down: a sheet of coverglass eats a little light, the wiring drops a little voltage, the calibration of the test lamp is never perfectly known, the Sun is a few percent brighter in January than in July, and the panel is almost never pointed dead-on. This section is the bookkeeping of all those small adjustments — how PowerPy represents each one, how it combines them, and the one genuinely subtle piece: why a brighter Sun nudges the voltage up by a logarithm.

Key idea Everything in this section is a multiplier on the bare cell numbers from Section 3 — a fraction near 1.0. A factor of 0.98 means "2 % of this quantity is lost." PowerPy keeps current losses and voltage losses in two separate piles, multiplies each pile down to a single number, and only then scales the operating point. Three different combination rules appear — simple product (for certain deductions), root-sum-square (for independent uncertainties), and a cosine (for pointing) — and knowing which to use when is the whole game.

4.1 Loss factors: a deduction expressed as a multiplier

Term — loss factor

A loss factor is a dimensionless multiplier, almost always just below 1.0, that represents a fractional reduction of a current or a voltage. The convention is "fraction surviving", not "fraction lost": 0.98 means 98 % gets through, i.e. 2 % is lost. A factor of exactly 1.0 is a no-op (no loss); the smaller the factor, the bigger the bite.

Why does a real array deliver less than the bare cell numbers? A whole catalogue of small effects, each modelled as one factor:

Where the losses come from
  • Coverglass — the thin transparent shield bonded over each cell to stop micrometeoroids and radiation reflects and absorbs a little of the incoming light before it reaches the junction.
  • Assembly — bonding, soldering and handling during manufacture cost a fraction of a percent.
  • Wiring — the harness carrying current off the panel has resistance, so a little voltage is dropped on the way to the bus.
  • Contamination — outgassed material and dust deposit on the glass over the mission and dim it slightly.
  • UV (ultraviolet) darkening — ultraviolet sunlight slowly discolours the adhesive and coverglass, costing a little transmission.
  • Calibration uncertainty — the numbers came from a test under a lamp whose absolute intensity is only known to within a percent or two; that uncertainty is itself booked as a loss factor.

PowerPy lets you attach these to a cell (and the same calls exist on a string, a section, or a whole panel, where they apply to every cell inside). Each loss is split into the quantity it actually attacks — current or voltage:

cell.addLossI(name, factor)
adds a named current loss (multiplies the current).
cell.addLossV(name, factor)
adds a named voltage loss (multiplies the voltage).
cell.removeLosses()
clears every loss back to none.
The name is just a label for readability. The factor is the surviving fraction (0.98 = 2 % lost). Adding a loss to a string/section/panel applies it to all the cells it contains.

4.2 Combining losses by product

If a cell carries several current losses, PowerPy combines them into one current-loss factor by multiplying them all together; likewise all the voltage losses multiply down to one voltage-loss factor. Multiplication — not addition — is the right model for losses that act in series, each one taking its cut of whatever the previous one left.

lossItotal = f1 · f2 · f3 · …     lossVtotal = g1 · g2 · g3 · …
Example: a 0.98 loss followed by a 0.97 loss gives 0.98 · 0.97 = 0.9506, i.e. about 4.94 % lost overall — slightly less than the 5 % you would get by naively adding "2 % + 3 %", because the second loss only acts on the 98 % the first one left.
Why multiply, not add

Picture light passing through two slightly dirty windows. The first lets 98 % through. The second lets 97 % of what reaches it through — and what reaches it is already only 98 % of the original. So the survivor is 0.98 · 0.97 of the original, never 0.98 + 0.97 − 1. Adding the percentages is an approximation that drifts as the losses grow; multiplying is exact for stacked, deterministic deductions.

4.3 RSS loss: combining independent uncertainties

Sometimes a "loss" is not a known, certain deduction but an uncertainty — a "±" you are not sure of the sign of. Calibration uncertainty is the classic case: you do not actually know the lamp was 1.5 % too bright; you know it was within ±1.5 %. When several such uncertainties are statistically independent, multiplying them treats them as if they all conspire to hit worst-case at once, which is needlessly pessimistic. The honest combination is root-sum-square (RSS) — adding them in quadrature.

Term — RSS (Root-Sum-Square)

To combine quantities in quadrature means to square them, add the squares, and take the square root: √(a² + b² + …). It is the same Pythagorean rule that gives the length of a vector from its components, and it is how independent random errors combine, because their cross-terms average to zero. The result is always smaller than the plain sum a + b + …

RSS_loss(list) = 1 − √( Σ (1 − lossk)² )
For each loss it takes the lost fraction (1 − lossk), squares it, sums over all losses, square-roots, and turns the combined lost-fraction back into a surviving factor by subtracting from 1. Input is the list of surviving-fraction factors; output is a single surviving-fraction factor.
Which rule, when EXAM-HOT
  • Deterministic, known deductions → MULTIPLY. Coverglass transmission, a wiring drop, an assembly factor: these are real, repeatable, always-present cuts. Stack them by product (§4.2).
  • Independent ± uncertainties → RSS. Calibration uncertainty, measurement scatter: these are error bars, equally likely either way, unlikely to all align. Combine in quadrature (§4.3).

RSS always gives a larger surviving factor (a smaller total loss) than multiplying the same numbers, because it refuses to assume every uncertainty hits the bad way at once. RSS is less pessimistic — appropriate precisely because independent errors partly cancel.

4.4 Worked example — the same three numbers, both ways

Worked 4A — product vs RSS on three losses

Given three loss factors: coverglass 0.99 (1 % lost), wiring 0.98 (2 % lost), calibration 0.995 (0.5 % lost). Combine them both ways and decide which is right.

Product (treat all as certain deductions):
0.99 · 0.98 · 0.995 = 0.9653  → about 3.47 % lost.
RSS (treat all as independent uncertainties): first the lost fractions are
1 − 0.99 = 0.010,  1 − 0.98 = 0.020,  1 − 0.995 = 0.005.
Square and sum:  0.010² + 0.020² + 0.005² = 0.0001 + 0.0004 + 0.000025 = 0.000525.
Square-root:  √0.000525 = 0.0229  (the combined lost fraction, ≈ 2.29 %).
Back to a surviving factor:  1 − 0.0229 = 0.9771  → about 2.29 % lost.
Compare and choose. Product = 0.965 (3.47 % lost); RSS = 0.977 (2.29 % lost). RSS is the gentler number — exactly as promised, because it does not assume the three losses all bite worst-case simultaneously. Which is appropriate? It depends on the nature of each loss, not on which answer you prefer. Coverglass and wiring are deterministic, ever-present deductions → they belong in the product. Calibration is a genuine ± uncertainty → it belongs in an RSS of the uncertain terms. A real budget often does both: multiply the deterministic losses, RSS the uncertainties, then multiply those two results together. Using RSS on a known deduction would flatter the budget; using product on pure uncertainties would over-design the array. ✓

4.5 Seasons: how strong the sunlight is

A spacecraft in Earth orbit does not see a constant Sun. The Earth's orbit is slightly elliptical, so the Earth–Sun distance changes over the year, and the intensity of sunlight changes with it by a few percent. PowerPy expresses the strength of the sunlight as a number relative to a fixed reference called AM0.

Term — AM0 (Air Mass Zero), 1 AU, B0

AM0 (Air Mass Zero) is the solar spectrum and intensity in space, above the atmosphere, at the Earth's mean distance from the Sun. "Air Mass Zero" literally means "no atmosphere in the way" — the spectrum a panel in orbit actually sees, unlike the ground-level AM1.5 used for terrestrial panels.

1 AU (one astronomical unit) is that mean Earth–Sun distance, about 1.496×108 km. At 1 AU the AM0 intensity is 1367 W/m² — the "solar constant," stored in PowerPy as B_0. Every season number is a multiplier on this baseline.

The method cell.setSeason(s) sets that multiplier. Two of the four named seasons also set a pointing angle (the solstices, when the Sun is farthest off the equatorial plane):

Northern-hemisphere intuition screams that summer should be the strong Sun. It is not. Summer (in the north) happens when the Earth is farther from the Sun, so Summer Solstice is the fainter sunlight: 0.967 (about 3.3 % below AM0). Winter, with the Earth nearer the Sun, is the brighter Sun at 1.034. The seasons you feel are driven by axial tilt, not distance — the distance effect runs the opposite way. Examiners love this reversal; memorise summer faint, winter bright.

And setSeason(0) means DEAD: a zero multiplier kills all output. It is not a season at all but the way you model a failed cell, string, or panel — switch one to 0 and PowerPy reports what the rest of the array can still deliver.

4.6 Pointing and tilt: the cosine of incidence

The array is rarely square-on to the Sun. When sunlight strikes a panel at an angle, the panel intercepts less of it — the same beam is spread over a larger slice of panel, so the power per unit panel area falls by the cosine of the incidence angle. (At grazing incidence, cos → 0 and the panel catches almost nothing; dead-on, cos 0° = 1 and it catches everything.)

PowerPy describes the geometry with two tilt angles plus the season angle from §4.5:

tilt = cos(alpha + season_angle) · cos(beta)
alpha and beta are the two panel tilt angles, set by cell.setAngles(alpha, beta); season_angle is set by cell.setSeasonAngle(sa) (and is filled in automatically by setSeason("SS")/("WS") as +23.5°/−23.5°). When everything is zero, tilt = cos 0° · cos 0° = 1 · 1 = 1: a perfectly pointed panel loses nothing to geometry.
How tilt feeds the irradiance

The light a cell actually captures is the baseline scaled by both the season and the tilt: effective irradiance = season · tilt · B0. The season says how bright the Sun is; the tilt says how much of it the panel faces. Multiply both into the 1367 W/m² baseline and you have the irradiance the cell really sees — which is also what scales the photocurrent (more light → more current, near-linearly).

4.7 Worked example — effective irradiance with season and tilt

Worked 4B — VEX with a 30° tilt

Given: season "VEX" (multiplier 1.008), alpha = 30°, beta = 0°, season_angle = 0° (the vernal equinox sets no season angle). Find the effective irradiance.

Tilt:  tilt = cos(alpha + season_angle)·cos(beta) = cos(30° + 0°)·cos(0°) = cos 30° · 1 = 0.8660 · 1 = 0.866.
Effective irradiance:  season · tilt · B0 = 1.008 · 0.866 · 1367 W/m².
Step the arithmetic:  1.008 · 0.866 = 0.8729;  0.8729 · 1367 W/m² = 1193 W/m².
Sanity. 1193 W/m² is well below the 1367 W/m² baseline even though VEX (1.008) is slightly brighter than AM0 — because the 30° tilt costs about 13.4 % on its own, far outweighing the +0.8 % season gain. The combined ratio season·tilt = 0.873 < 1, so on balance the cell sees less than AM0. Hold on to that combined number 0.873 — it is exactly what drives the voltage correction in §4.8. ✓

4.8 The voltage illumination correction — a logarithm

One last subtlety, and it is the one students misread. A solar cell makes slightly more voltage in brighter light and slightly less in fainter light — but not in proportion. The dependence is logarithmic. This is not an arbitrary fudge: it falls straight out of the diode equation from Section 2, where the open-circuit voltage goes like the logarithm of the photocurrent, Voc ∝ ln(Ilight). Double the light and the voltage rises by a fixed step, not a factor.

PowerPy builds two small correction terms from the brightness ratio the cell actually sees — and that ratio is the combined season·tilt from §4.6, not the season alone. When the season is non-zero it computes:

vmp_corr = (1 + (isc − imp)/imp) · ln(season · tilt)
voc_corr = (1 + (vmp/voc)·(isc − imp)/imp) · ln(season · tilt)
Each is a small dimensionless term applied as a nudge to the corresponding voltage — Vmp → Vmp·(1 + vmp_corr), Voc → Voc·(1 + voc_corr) — not a factor you multiply the voltage by directly. The bracketed coefficient just sets how big the nudge is; the ln(season·tilt) sets its sign and size.
The intuition — read the ln, not the algebra

Do not get lost in the bracketed coefficients. The whole behaviour lives in ln(season·tilt):

  • Exactly at AM0 and square-on → season·tilt = 1 → ln(1) = 0 → no correction at all. The reference condition leaves the voltage untouched, which is the consistency guarantee (just like the temperature term vanished at t_ref in §3).
  • Brighter than AM0 (season·tilt > 1) → ln > 0 → a small voltage gain.
  • Fainter than AM0 (season·tilt < 1) → ln < 0 → a small voltage loss.

The logarithm compresses: it vanishes exactly at AM0 and squashes even large brightness swings into modest voltage moves — doubling the light adds a fixed step of voltage, not a factor. That compression is the signature of the diode's ln(Ilight) law.

Pitfall — it is season·tilt that decides the sign, not season

A natural mistake: "VEX is 1.008, brighter than AM0, so the voltage rises." Wrong, once the panel is tilted. In Worked 4B the season was 1.008 (brighter) but the 30° tilt pulled the combined ratio to season·tilt = 0.873 < 1, so ln(0.873) < 0 and the voltage correction is a small loss. Always feed the logarithm the combined brightness the cell really sees, never the season number on its own.

4.9 Socratic ladder — why multiply, and when to RSS

Ladder — Why are losses MULTIPLIED rather than added, and when should we use RSS instead?
Q1. (Foundation) What does a single loss factor of 0.98 actually mean?
It means 98 % of the quantity survives and 2 % is lost. It is a surviving fraction, a pure multiplier: take the number, multiply by 0.98, and you have the value after that one loss. "0.98" and "2 % lost" are two names for the same thing.
Q2. Now apply two losses in succession, 0.98 then 0.97. Why is the answer 0.9506 and not 0.95?
The second loss acts on what the first one left, not on the original. After the first, 98 % remains; the second keeps 97 % of that: 0.97 · 0.98 = 0.9506. Naively adding the losses ("2 % + 3 % = 5 % → 0.95") double-counts, because the 3 % would only ever be taken from the 98 % that survived, not the full 100 %. Stacked deductions compound, and compounding is multiplication. That is why PowerPy multiplies all current losses into one factor and all voltage losses into another.
Q3. So multiplication is exact for stacked deductions. When is multiplying the wrong thing to do?
When the "losses" are not certain deductions but independent uncertainties — error bars, equally likely to push either way. Multiplying them implicitly assumes they all land on the pessimistic side at the same time, which for independent errors is very unlikely. A loss is a certainty when it is always present and repeatable (coverglass transmission, a wiring drop); it is an uncertainty when it is a ± you cannot pin down (calibration of the test lamp, measurement scatter).
Q4. (Why) What does RSS do differently, and why is its answer larger (less pessimistic)?
RSS adds the lost fractions in quadrature: 1 − √(Σ(1−lossk)²). Squaring shrinks the small fractions before they are summed, and the square root brings the total back, but the net result of combining independent errors this way is always smaller than adding them linearly — because their random over- and under-estimates partly cancel rather than reinforce. In Worked 4A the same three numbers gave 0.977 by RSS versus 0.965 by product: a smaller total loss. RSS is the statistically honest combination for things that are genuinely independent and uncertain; product is the honest combination for things that are certain and always there. Use each for what it models — never pick the one that simply gives the prettier number.

5From Cell to Array: SCA, the Hierarchy, and the Two Diodes

5 From one cell to a whole array: the SCA hierarchy, and the two kinds of diode

A single space solar cell is almost uselessly small: about 2.6 V and half an amp. A satellite, meanwhile, wants a bus at perhaps 50–100 V drawing many amps. The entire gap between those two facts is bridged by wiring — putting cells end-to-end to build voltage, and side-by-side to build current. This section teaches that wiring from the ground up: the one electrical rule that governs all of it, the four-level object hierarchy PowerPy uses to model it (string → section → panel → array), one subtlety that catches everyone (the deep-copy "reference break"), and finally the single most-confused topic in the whole subject — the two different diodes, which look similar, sit in different places, and solve completely different problems.

Key idea

One cell ≈ 2.6 V, ≈ 0.5 A — far too little. You reach a useful bus by combining cells two ways: series adds voltage, parallel adds current. PowerPy models the build-up as a nested stack of Python objects — a string (cells in series), a section (strings in parallel), and a panel (sections on a physical substrate) — and a command sent to any level cascades down to every cell inside it.

5.1 The basic unit: the SCA

Before any wiring, fix what the "atom" of an array actually is. You do not lay a bare wafer onto a satellite — a raw cell would be cracked by launch vibration and stripped of charge by the first proton that hit it. The manufactured, flight-ready unit is the SCA.

Term — SCA (Solar Cell Assembly)

An SCA is the basic manufactured building block: a solar cell together with its protective coverglass (a thin glass layer bonded on top that stops radiation and micrometeoroids) and its welded interconnects (the metal tabs that carry current in and out and let you weld one cell to the next). It arrives ready to lay straight onto the panel.

You will very often see it written CIC — Cell-Interconnect-Coverglass — which spells out the same three ingredients. For our purposes SCA and CIC mean the same thing: the cell-plus-coverglass-plus-tabs unit you actually wire together.

So when the rest of this section says "cell," picture an SCA: a single small voltage source (≈2.6 V) with two welded tabs ready to join to its neighbours. Everything from here is about how those tabs get joined.

5.2 The one rule that underpins everything: series vs parallel

If you remember nothing else from this section, remember the next box. Every voltage, current and power figure for a string, a section, a panel or an array is just this rule applied over and over. We will teach it slowly because almost every mistake later traces back to mixing up its two halves.

The fundamental rule EXAM-HOT

SERIES (cells end-to-end, like links in a chain):

  • The voltages ADD. Ten 2.6 V cells in series make ≈26 V. Stacking voltage sources nose-to-tail sums their voltages.
  • The current is COMMON to every cell — the same current flows through all of them, because there is only one path. And that shared current can be no larger than the weakest cell can supply. A chain carries only as much current as its weakest link allows; one shaded or failed cell throttles the entire string. (This single fact is the seed of the whole bypass-diode story in §5.7.)

PARALLEL (cells side-by-side, all tops joined, all bottoms joined):

  • The currents ADD. Twenty 0.48 A strings in parallel deliver ≈9.6 A. Each branch contributes its own current into the shared rails.
  • The voltage is COMMON to every branch — they all sit between the same two rails, so they are all held at the same voltage.
series   Vstring = V1 + V2 + … + Vn   (≈ n · Vcell)   |   Istring = I1 = I2 = … (common, set by the weakest cell)
parallel   Isection = I1 + I2 + … + Im   (≈ m · Istring)   |   Vsection = V1 = V2 = … (common)
A handy mnemonic: "series sums volts, parallel sums amps." The quantity that is not summing is the one that is shared (common) across all members. Power is always P = V·I at whatever level you have just computed V and I.
Why "common" matters more than it looks

The word common does the heavy lifting. In a series string the current is common, so a problem with current (a weak cell) is a problem for the whole string. In a parallel section the voltage is common, so every string is forced to the same voltage whether it likes it or not — which is precisely how a healthy string can end up driving current backwards through a weak neighbour (the seed of the blocking-diode story in §5.7). Keep asking, at each level: which quantity is common here?

5.3 The PowerPy hierarchy: four nested objects

PowerPy mirrors the physical build-up with a stack of Python objects. Each level is a real class; you construct a level from the level below it. Read the stack bottom-up — it is just the series/parallel rule made into software.

The four levels at a glance
  • cell — one SCA. ≈2.6 V, ≈0.5 A. (Built and aged in Sections 2–3.)
  • string — cells wired in SERIES. Builds voltage. Also carries an intra-string resistance and one or more blocking diodes.
  • section — strings wired in PARALLEL. Builds current. Carries a section resistance.
  • panel — one or more sections mounted on a substrate (the physical board) with a total area. Owns the thermal behaviour (Section 7).
  • array — combines panels (the top of the tree).

5.3.1 string — cells in series

A string is cells joined end-to-end. Because they are in series, the string's voltage is roughly the cell voltage times the number of cells, and the string's current is just one cell's current (the common current). PowerPy gives you two constructors:

string.fromSingleCell(cell, n_series, resistance, diodeType, n_diodes_series, n_diodes_parallel)
Takes one prototype cell and clones it n_series times to make a string of identical cells. resistance is the intra-string wiring resistance (ohms). diodeType chooses the blocking-diode model; n_diodes_series / n_diodes_parallel set how many blocking diodes sit at the string output and how they are arranged. Resulting voltage ≈ n_series × cell voltage.
string.fromCells(list_of_cells)
Builds a string from an explicit list of cell objects, in series, in the order given. Use this when the cells are not all identical (e.g. you want to model one deliberately weaker cell).

5.3.2 section — strings in parallel

A section places strings side-by-side in parallel. The currents add; the voltage is common. Three constructors, including a convenient shortcut that builds the whole section straight from a single cell:

section.fromSingleString(string, n_parallel, resistance)
Clones one prototype string n_parallel times and wires the clones in parallel. resistance is the section-level wiring resistance. Resulting current ≈ n_parallel × string current.
section.fromStrings(list)
Builds a section from an explicit list of (possibly different) string objects, in parallel.
section.fromSingleCell(cell, n_parallel, n_serial, resistance, diodeType)
The one-shot shortcut: from a single prototype cell it builds n_serial cells per string and n_parallel strings in parallel in a single call — i.e. it does §5.3.1 and the parallel step together. Handy for a uniform array; reach for the longer route when you need non-uniform parts.

5.3.3 panel — sections on a substrate

panel.fromSections(list, substrate, area)
list is the sections mounted on this panel; substrate is the physical board the cells are bonded to (it carries the thermal properties — how the panel heats up in sun and cools in shadow); area is the total panel area. The panel is the level that owns temperature, which is why Section 7 (thermal) operates here.
Term — substrate

The substrate is the rigid (or flexible) board onto which the SCAs are bonded. Electrically it is just a carrier, but thermally it is central: its absorptivity, emissivity and heat capacity set how hot the cells get, and cell temperature feeds straight back into the four operating-point numbers (Section 3). That is why the substrate lives at the panel level — the level that computes temperature.

Above the panel sits the array, which combines panels into the complete wing or body-mounted surface. The same rule still applies at every join: are these panels effectively adding voltage (series) or current (parallel)?

5.4 Cascading commands: one call reaches every cell

Because the objects nest, PowerPy makes a command on a higher object forward automatically to all its children. You set a condition once, at the level it applies, and it propagates down to every cell inside. This is what makes a whole-panel simulation a one-liner.

Cascading (forwarding) commands

Examples — each call below reaches every cell beneath the object it is called on:

  • string.setTemperature(T) — set temperature on every cell in the string.
  • section.addLossI(name, factor) — add a named current-loss factor to every cell in every string of the section.
  • panel.setSeason(s) — set the season (sun-distance/illumination) for the whole panel.
  • panel.setDose(di, dv) — apply the current/voltage radiation dose to every cell on the panel.

The full forwarding set: setTemperature, setDose, setSeason, addLossI/addLossV, removeLosses, setAngles, setModel, setDiode, setCell. Call any of these on a string, section, panel or array and it walks the tree down to the leaves.

5.5 The reference break: PowerPy DEEP-COPIES your prototype

Here is the subtlety that trips up almost everyone the first time. When you build a string or section from a prototype (a single cell, or a single string), PowerPy does not store a reference to your prototype — it stores an independent deep copy. The clones inside the container are their own objects, disconnected from the original.

Term — deep copy vs reference

A reference is a second name for the same object: change it through either name and both see the change. A deep copy is a brand-new, fully independent duplicate: changing the original afterwards has no effect on the copy, and vice versa. PowerPy's fromSingleCell/fromSingleString constructors make deep copies — so the cells inside the string are not the cell you handed in.

Pitfall — editing the prototype after the build does nothing EXAM-HOT

This is a silent failure: no error is raised, the numbers are just quietly wrong. Set properties at the right level, or before you build. Tweaking the prototype cell after building the string changes nothing inside the string — those cells were copied at construction time and have moved on independently. To change cells already inside a string, either (a) set the property on the prototype before calling the constructor, or (b) call a cascading command (§5.4) on the string so it reaches the copies.

# WRONG — modifying the prototype AFTER the build does nothing to the string
cell = Cell(); cell.setTemperature(28)
s = string.fromSingleCell(cell, 10, 0.03, diodeType, 1, 1)  # 10 deep copies taken HERE
cell.setTemperature(-80)        # changes only the lonely prototype...
# ...the 10 cells inside s are STILL at 28 degC. No effect.

# RIGHT (a) — set it on the prototype BEFORE building
cell = Cell(); cell.setTemperature(-80)
s = string.fromSingleCell(cell, 10, 0.03, diodeType, 1, 1)  # copies inherit -80

# RIGHT (b) — set it on the STRING, so the cascade reaches every copy
s.setTemperature(-80)           # forwards to all 10 internal cells (see 5.4)
Why this is a feature, not a bug

Deep copying buys independence. Once cells are inside a string they can be aged, heated or shaded individually without your edits leaking back to some shared template — exactly what you need to model one shadowed string among twenty healthy ones. The price is that you must aim your edits at the right object. When a change "doesn't take," your first suspicion should be: did I edit the prototype after the deep copy was made?

5.6 How it is wired inside: a netlist sketch

PowerPy ultimately turns the hierarchy into a circuit netlist (SPICE-style). You do not need the literal text, but a mental picture of the nodes makes the diodes in §5.7 obvious.

Node picture (conceptual)
  • Inside a string the cells chain node to node: the top of XCell_0 is the bottom of XCell_1, and so on (XCell_0, XCell_1, XCell_2, …). One continuous ladder from the string bottom to the string top — the physical picture of "series."
  • A section gives every string a shared top rail and bottom rail and ties all the string tops to the top rail and all the string bottoms to the bottom rail — the physical picture of "parallel."
  • The blocking diode sits between each string's top and the top rail: current can leave the string into the rail, but the diode stops the rail pushing current back down into the string.

5.7 The two diodes — taught slowly, then contrasted EXAM-HOT

This is the part students confuse most, so we go one diode at a time and only compare them at the end. First, a one-line reminder of what any diode does: a diode is a one-way valve for current — it conducts when forward-biased past its turn-on voltage (≈0.7 V for silicon) and blocks when reverse-biased. The two diodes below differ only in where they sit and which disaster they prevent.

5.7.1 The BYPASS diode (a.k.a. shunt diode)

Term — bypass diode (shunt diode)

A bypass diode is wired in reverse-parallel across each cell (or each small group of cells) — i.e. it sits across the cell, pointing the "wrong" way so that in normal operation it is reverse-biased and OFF (it blocks, and has no effect on the healthy cell). It is there purely as an emergency relief path.

Walk through the emergency, because it is exactly the series rule biting. Suppose one cell in a long series string gets shaded, cracked or fails, so it can no longer produce the current its healthy neighbours are producing. But the string current is common — the good cells insist on pushing their full current through every cell, including the weak one. The weak cell cannot supply that much, so it is forced into reverse bias: the string drives current backwards through it, and the cell starts dropping a large negative voltage and dissipating power as heat (a "hotspot" that can crack the cell or scorch the panel).

Now the bypass diode earns its keep. As the weak cell's reverse voltage grows, it eventually reaches the diode's turn-on (≈0.7 V in the reverse direction across the cell). The diode switches ON and carries the string's current around the bad cell. This does two things at once: the string keeps flowing (you lose that cell's contribution but not the whole string), and the bad cell's reverse voltage is clamped to about −0.7 V, capping the power it can dissipate and saving it from thermal runaway.

In PowerPy

The bypass diode is the DD_Shunt element inside each cell's subcircuit, and is available standalone as the shuntdiode element. (The name shunt reflects that it shunts — diverts — current around the cell.)

5.7.2 The BLOCKING diode

Term — blocking diode

A blocking diode is wired in series at the END (output) of each string. It points "with" the string's normal current, so it lets current flow out of the string into the bus — but it blocks any current trying to flow back into the string from outside.

Why would current ever try to flow backwards into a string? Two classic situations, both flowing straight from "voltage is common in parallel" (§5.2):

  • A weak neighbour on the same bus. Parallel strings share rails, so they are all forced to the same voltage. A shadowed or unusually cold string may have a lower (or mismatched) operating point than its bright neighbours, and the brighter strings can then drive current down into the weak one — wasting power and stressing it. The blocking diode at the weak string's output refuses that reverse current.
  • The battery at night. In eclipse the array makes no power, but the spacecraft battery is still sitting on the bus at a real voltage. Without protection it would push current back into the dark array (dissipating the battery into the cells). The blocking diode stops this back-feed cold.
In PowerPy

Blocking diodes are configured on the string via n_diodes_series and n_diodes_parallel (with diodeType choosing the model) — the same arguments you saw in string.fromSingleCell(...) in §5.3.1. They live at the string's output, between the string top and the section's top rail (§5.6).

5.8 Worked examples (with units)

These apply the §5.2 rule level by level. Watch which quantity adds and which is common at each step — that is the whole exercise.

Worked 5A — a 10-cell string, then subtract the blocking diode

Given: 10 identical cells in series, each at its max-power point Vmp = 2.35 V, Imp = 0.48 A. One blocking diode at the output with a forward drop of ≈0.7 V.

1. String voltage (series ⇒ volts ADD):   Vstring = 10 × 2.35 V = 23.5 V.
2. String current (series ⇒ current COMMON):   Istring = Imp of one cell = 0.48 A. (it does NOT multiply by 10 — there is one path)
3. String power:   P = V·I = 23.5 V × 0.48 A = 11.28 W ≈ 11.3 W.
4. Subtract the blocking-diode drop:   the diode is in series at the output, so its ≈0.7 V comes off the voltage:   Vout = 23.5 V − 0.7 V = 22.8 V. The current is unchanged at 0.48 A (a series element shares the common current).
Units and rule both check out: ten 2.35 V cells gave 23.5 V (volts summed), the current stayed at one cell's 0.48 A (common in series), and the one series diode shaved 0.7 V off the voltage only. Output: 22.8 V at 0.48 A. ✓
Worked 5B — 20 such strings in parallel make a section

Given: 20 copies of the Worked-5A string (each 22.8 V output at 0.48 A) wired in parallel into a section. Ignore the small section resistance.

1. Section voltage (parallel ⇒ voltage COMMON):   Vsection ≈ 22.8 V. (it stays at one string's voltage — it does NOT multiply by 20)
2. Section current (parallel ⇒ currents ADD):   Isection = 20 × 0.48 A = 9.6 A.
3. Section power:   P = V·I = 22.8 V × 9.6 A = 218.88 W ≈ 219 W.
The mirror of 5A. Going to parallel flips which quantity does what: in series we summed volts and kept current common; in parallel we sum amps and keep voltage common. So the section is ≈22.8 V (not 20×22.8) at 9.6 A (= 20×0.48). The most common slip is multiplying the voltage by 20 — that would violate "voltage is common in parallel." ✓
Worked 5C — sizing for a target bus voltage

Question: using the same 2.35 V cells, how many in series do you need to reach a 70 V bus?

Series ⇒ volts add, so n ≈ target ÷ cell voltage:   n = 70 V ÷ 2.35 V ≈ 29.8.
Round up (you cannot have a fraction of a cell, and rounding down would fall short):   n = 30 cells.

5.9 Why one weak cell drags the whole string — a Socratic ladder

Ladder — If one cell in a long series string is weak, why does it drag down the WHOLE string, and what stops it overheating?
Q1. In a series string, which quantity is common to every cell — the voltage or the current?
The current. There is a single path through the string, so the same current flows through every cell. (The voltages, by contrast, add up — §5.2.) This one fact is the root of everything that follows.
Q2. The healthy cells are producing, say, 0.48 A. What is the weak (shaded) cell now required to do?
It is required to pass that same 0.48 A, because the current is common — the string can only carry one current, and the strong cells are driving it. But a shaded cell can only generate a much smaller current on its own. It is being asked to carry more current than it can produce.
Q3. What does being forced to pass more current than it can generate do to the weak cell's voltage?
It pushes the cell into reverse bias. Instead of adding voltage like its healthy neighbours, the weak cell now drops a negative voltage — the rest of the string is effectively driving current backwards through it. And current × a large reverse voltage = real power dissipated as heat inside that one cell (the dreaded hotspot). This is also why the string's overall output sags: the weak cell is now subtracting voltage instead of contributing it.
Q4. So what does the bypass diode do at that moment, and what does it protect?
As the weak cell's reverse voltage climbs to ≈0.7 V, the bypass diode across it turns ON and carries the string's 0.48 A around the bad cell. That clamps the bad cell's reverse voltage to ≈−0.7 V, capping the heat it can dissipate (protecting it from the hotspot), and keeps the rest of the string delivering current. You lose that one cell's contribution, but you save the cell and keep the string alive — instead of losing the whole string and possibly cracking a cell. (Note which diode this is: the bypass, because the danger is internal reverse bias — contrast §5.7.3.)

6Reverse Bias, Hot-Spots, and Substrate Damage

6 Reverse bias, hot-spots, and why a failed cell can melt the panel

Here is the most counter-intuitive fact in the whole study of solar arrays: when one cell in a string dies, the danger is not that it stops working — it is that the circuit forces it to keep carrying current it can no longer make. To do that, the dead cell turns itself into a tiny electric heater, and the energy for that heater is donated by every healthy cell around it. A single failed cell can become the hottest point on the entire panel — hot enough, unprotected, to scorch the silicon and even soften the aluminium it is glued to. This section builds that failure from first principles, shows the one component that tames it (the bypass diode), and explains why predicting "which failures melt, and which layouts survive" is precisely the job PowerPy's new failure-sweep machinery exists to do.

Key idea In a series string, every cell is forced to pass the same current (Section 5's rule). If one cell can no longer generate that current, it does not politely drop out — the others push the current through it anyway. The only way a non-generating cell can carry current is to go to negative voltage: it flips from being a battery to being a resistor-like load, and the electrical power it now absorbs comes out as heat. That heat is the failure we are trying to predict.

6.1 Setup recap — the iron rule of a series string

Section 5 established the rule we lean on for the entire section, so hold it firmly in mind: in a series string the current is common to every cell. You cannot have 0.48 A flowing in one cell of a string and 0.20 A in its neighbour — they are wired end-to-end, so the same charge per second must pass through all of them, like cars on a single-lane road. Each cell is free to sit at its own voltage, and the string's total voltage is the sum of the cell voltages; but the current is shared and identical. (Forward reference: Section 5 derived this from how series connections add. We take it as given here.)

That one rule is the seed of the entire failure mechanism. Everything below is just following it to its uncomfortable conclusion.

6.2 The failure, one step at a time

Let us walk a single cell from "healthy" to "dangerous," one link in the chain at a time. Picture a string of healthy cells with one cell in the middle that has just cracked, been shadowed, or otherwise failed.

The mechanism — step by step
1. A cell stops generating. One cell is shadowed, micro-cracked, or simply degraded past use. It can no longer photo-generate the current the string is running at. On its own it would like to pass only a trickle.
2. But the current still has to flow through it. The other healthy cells in the string — and any parallel strings sharing the same bus — keep generating and keep pushing their current. Because the string is series (the rule of §6.1), that current has nowhere else to go: it must pass through the bad cell.
3. So the bad cell is driven to NEGATIVE voltage. To carry a current it cannot produce from light, the cell is forced to the only state that lets the current through: its voltage goes below zero. It stops acting as a generator (which would push current out) and starts acting as a load (which absorbs energy). This back-to-front operating state is called reverse bias — the cell is said to be back-biased.
4. Pushed hard enough, it reaches reverse breakdown. A cell can only hold off so much reverse voltage. Past a certain point — its reverse breakdown voltage, around −20 V for these cells (the figure on the design sketch) — the junction gives way and conducts heavily in reverse.
5. Negative voltage × current = power turned to HEAT. Electrical power is P = V·I. When V is negative, the product is the power the cell absorbs rather than delivers — it dissipates |V|·I as heat. A small region forced to swallow the electrical output of many other cells is a hot-spot.
6. The heat raises the local temperature — possibly past what the panel can survive. All that power is dumped into one ~30 cm² patch. Its temperature climbs. If it exceeds the limit of the panel substrate — here an aluminium honeycomb core — it can permanently break down the cell or even soften and melt the aluminium. That destructive outcome is the event we want to predict and design against.
Read the chain as one sentence: series forces equal current → the dead cell must carry it → carrying it without generating means going negative → negative voltage times current is heat → concentrated heat can melt the substrate. Each arrow is forced by the one before it; nowhere did the cell "get a choice." ✓
Term — reverse bias (back-biased)

Reverse bias means a component is operated with its voltage the "wrong way round" compared to how it normally delivers power. A solar cell normally sits at positive voltage and pushes current out (it is a source). A reverse-biased cell sits at negative voltage while current is pushed into it (it is a sink). Plainly: a back-biased cell has stopped being a battery and become a heater. The deeper the negative voltage, the more power it absorbs.

Term — hot-spot EXAM-HOT

A hot-spot is a small region of a panel that is forced to absorb the electrical power of many other cells and dissipate it as heat, so its local temperature rises far above the rest of the array. It is created when a back-biased cell (or a small shaded patch) becomes the load for a whole string — or worse, for several parallel strings at once.

Term — substrate / aluminium honeycomb core

The substrate is the structural panel the cells are mounted on. On a spacecraft it is typically an aluminium honeycomb core: a lightweight sandwich of two thin aluminium (or composite) face-sheets bonded either side of a hexagonal-cell aluminium "honeycomb," like a stiff, ultra-light cardboard. It is wonderfully strong-for-its-weight but it is still aluminium — it has a temperature limit, and a hot-spot that exceeds it can soften or melt the core, wrecking the panel's structure as well as the cell.

6.3 Where does the energy come from? (The part people miss)

It is tempting to think the heat in a hot-spot somehow comes from "inside" the failed cell. It does not — a dead cell has no power source of its own. The dissipated heat is supplied entirely by all the OTHER cells and strings that are forcing the current through it.

The donor picture

Every healthy cell in the string is still a little generator. Normally each spends its energy delivering useful power to the load. When one cell goes reverse-biased, the healthy cells' energy is instead spent pushing current through that one bad cell — and the bad cell, sitting at negative voltage, turns that pushed-in energy into heat. Add parallel strings sharing the bus and the situation is worse still: now several strings' worth of current can be crammed through the single failed cell. One tiny cell is being asked to swallow the combined output of many. That asymmetry — many sources, one sink — is exactly why a single failure can be destructive out of all proportion to its size.

6.4 The protection — the bypass diode

The cure was introduced in Section 5: a bypass diode wired across a cell or a group of cells, oriented so it does nothing during normal operation but switches on the instant a cell tries to go strongly negative.

Term — bypass diode (vs blocking diode)

A bypass diode is connected in parallel across a cell or cell-group, the "wrong" way for normal current, so it stays off while the cells generate. If a cell is driven into reverse, the diode becomes forward-biased and turns on at about −0.7 V, offering the string current an easy detour around the bad cell. (Contrast the blocking diode of §6.6, which sits in series with a whole string to stop current flowing backwards into it from the bus. Bypass goes around; blocking sits in line.)

The bypass diode does two things at once, and the second is the one that saves the panel:

  • It carries the string current around the bad cell. The healthy cells' current flows through the diode instead of being forced through the failed cell, so the string keeps delivering (minus that one cell's contribution).
  • It CLAMPS the reverse voltage. Because the diode conducts at about 0.7 V, the voltage across the bypassed path can never run away to −20 V — it is pinned near −0.7 V. The failed cell sees a small reverse voltage instead of a catastrophic one.
Why the clamp is everything

Heat in the failed cell is |V|·I. The bypass diode does not change the current much, but it slashes |V| from ~20 V down to ~0.7 V — a factor of about 28×. Same current, a tiny fraction of the voltage, so a tiny fraction of the heat. That is how a clamp at three-quarters of a volt prevents a melt.

But here is the subtlety that makes the whole design trade-off make sense. A bypass diode is rarely fitted across every single cell (that would be a lot of diodes). It is usually fitted across a group of K cells at once — a bypass group. When the diode fires, it clamps the whole group to about −0.7 V. But inside that group the other (K−1) healthy cells are still generating, each adding its own forward voltage. Those healthy in-group voltages stack up and push the failed cell more negative than the −0.7 V seen across the group as a whole:

|Vfail|  ≈  0.7 V  +  (K − 1)·Vcell   (capped at the reverse-breakdown voltage, ≈ 20 V)
K = cells per bypass group; Vcell = a healthy cell's operating voltage (≈ 2.35 V for the §3 cell). With K = 1 (a diode per cell) the pile-on term vanishes and |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 V — the best case. As K grows, the failed cell is driven harder, until the stack reaches the −20 V breakdown and the protection is effectively overwhelmed.
The trade-off, stated cleanly

Fewer cells per bypass group (more diodes) = safer but heavier. Shrinking K reduces the (K−1)·Vcell pile-on, so a failed cell sits closer to the gentle −0.7 V clamp and dissipates less heat. The price is more diodes: more mass, more solder joints, more complexity, more things that can themselves fail. Choosing K is a genuine engineering decision — protection bought with grams — and it is exactly the kind of decision PowerPy is built to evaluate.

6.5 Why PowerPy and this project care

Whether a given failure is a shrug or a melt depends on three design choices: how many cells share each bypass diode (the K above), where the blocking diodes sit, and the overall layout of cells, strings and buses. Change those and you change which single-cell failures stay harmless and which become destructive hot-spots. Quantifying that — which failures cause a melt, and which layouts are robust — is not a side-task; it is the headline job of the new tooling.

How PowerPy models this
  • Circuit (customizable) — the new Chapter 2 customizable Circuit class lets you describe an arbitrary layout: how many cells per bypass group, how strings are paralleled, where blocking diodes go. The layout under test is no longer hard-coded.
  • Monte-Carlo failure sweep — a Chapter 2 sweep that randomly fails cells across many trials and asks, statistically, how often a single failure drives a hot-spot past the danger line. This is how you compare "robust" against "fragile" layouts with numbers, not hand-waving.
  • DD_Shunt — PowerPy already represents the cell's reverse path (the conduction route a back-biased cell takes) via its DD_Shunt element, so the negative-voltage behaviour at the heart of this section is in the model, not bolted on.
  • season = 0 — to simulate a failure, PowerPy can drive a cell to season = 0 (a "dead" cell that generates nothing), then watch what the surrounding circuit forces upon it. That is the on-switch for the whole failure analysis.

6.6 A note on the blocking diode

One related part, so the vocabulary does not blur. A blocking diode sits in series with an entire string, between the string and the shared bus. Its job is not hot-spot protection of a single cell but to stop current flowing backwards into a weak or shaded string from the stronger strings on the bus — which would otherwise be another way to back-bias cells. Bypass diodes go around cells; blocking diodes sit in line with strings. Both shape how bad a failure gets, which is why the customizable Circuit class lets you place both.

6.7 Worked examples

Now the numbers — the contrast that makes the whole section concrete. We use the §3 cell's max-power current, I = 0.48 A, and its ~30 cm² area throughout.

Worked 6A — PROTECTED: bypass diode clamps the reverse voltage

Given: a failed cell in a string carrying I = 0.48 A. A bypass diode (here one per cell, the best case K = 1) clamps the cell's reverse voltage to V = −0.7 V. Find the heat dissipated in the failed cell.

Identify the dissipation:   power turned to heat in the cell = |V|·I.
Magnitude of the reverse voltage:   |V| = |−0.7 V| = 0.7 V.
Multiply by the current:   P = 0.7 V × 0.48 A = 0.336 W ≈ 0.34 W.
Is that a lot? No — about a third of a watt spread over ~30 cm². That is a barely-warm patch, comparable to a small indicator LED. The cell survives, the panel survives. The clamp did its job. ✓
Worked 6B — UNPROTECTED: the cell is driven to reverse breakdown

Given: the same current I = 0.48 A, but now no effective bypass — the cell is pushed all the way to its reverse-breakdown voltage V = −20 V. Find the heat, the heat flux, and compare to sunlight.

Heat dissipated:   P = |V|·I = 20 V × 0.48 A = 9.6 W — concentrated in one ~30 cm² cell.
Compare to the protected case:   9.6 W ÷ 0.336 W ≈ 28× more power in the very same cell, for the very same current. The only thing that changed was the voltage the cell was allowed to reach.
Heat flux (power per unit area):   first convert area, 30 cm² = 30 cm² × (1 m / 100 cm)² = 30 × 10−4 m² = 0.0030 m².
Flux:   9.6 W ÷ 30 cm² = 0.32 W/cm²; in SI, 9.6 W ÷ 0.0030 m² = 3200 W/m².
Reference it to sunlight:   full sunlight in space (the solar constant) is ≈ 1361 W/m². So 3200 W/m² is about 2× (roughly 2.3×) the intensity of full sun — but unlike sunlight, which warms the whole panel, this is dumped into one tiny spot.
Verdict. Concentrating twice the power of full sunlight into a single ~30 cm² patch, with no clamp to relieve it, is comfortably enough to scorch the cell and threaten the aluminium core beneath it. The protected cell (Worked 6A) ran at one twenty-eighth of this. That 28× gap, bought by a 0.7 V clamp, is the entire reason bypass diodes exist. ✓
Worked 6C (optional) — how the bypass GROUP size K changes the worst case

Setup: use |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (K−1)·Vcell with healthy cells at Vcell ≈ 2.35 V (the §3 cell), still at I = 0.48 A, breakdown capped at 20 V. Compare one big group versus halving it.

K = 1 (a diode per cell):   |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 0 = 0.7 V → P ≈ 0.7 × 0.48 = 0.34 W (the Worked 6A bookend).
When does the stack hit breakdown?   set 0.7 + (K−1)·2.35 = 20 → (K−1) = 19.3 / 2.35 ≈ 8.2 → K ≈ 9. So a group of about 9 or more cells can drive the failed cell all the way to the −20 V breakdown (the Worked 6B bookend), P ≈ 9.6 W.
Halving a large group, e.g. K = 8 → K = 4:   at K = 4, |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 3×2.35 = 0.7 + 7.05 = 7.75 V → P ≈ 7.75 × 0.48 ≈ 3.7 W, versus K = 8 giving |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + 7×2.35 = 17.15 V → P ≈ 8.2 W. (both below the 20 V cap, so the formula applies directly)
The lesson in one line. Halving the group slides the failed cell down the line from the breakdown bookend toward the clamp bookend — here from ~8.2 W to ~3.7 W. More diodes, less heat. This is the trade-off of §6.4 made quantitative, and it is exactly the quantity the Monte-Carlo sweep estimates across a whole array. ✓

6.8 Socratic ladder — why the dead cell is the hottest point

Ladder — Why does a single failed cell get HOTTER than any healthy cell ever does, and what sets whether it melts the substrate?
Q1. In a series string, what is forced to be the same for every cell?
The current. Series cells are wired end-to-end, so the same charge-per-second flows through all of them (§6.1). A cell can sit at its own voltage, but it cannot opt out of the shared current. This is the constraint everything else is forced to obey.
Q2. If a cell can no longer generate that shared current, what must it do to let the current pass through it?
It must go to negative voltage — it must reverse-bias. A generating cell sits at positive voltage and pushes current out; a cell that cannot generate but is forced to conduct can only do so by becoming a load, i.e. by dropping below zero volts. It has no other way to satisfy "the current must pass through me."
Q3. Once it sits at negative voltage carrying current, where does its heat energy come from?
From all the other cells and strings pushing the current (§6.3). The failed cell has no source of its own; it is a sink. Power |V|·I flows into it, donated by the healthy generators around it, and comes out as heat. A healthy cell, by contrast, delivers power outward and is gently warmed only by sunlight — so it can never reach the temperature of a cell absorbing the output of many neighbours. That asymmetry (many donors, one sink) is the whole reason the failed cell is uniquely hot.
Q4. So what finally decides whether that hot-spot melts the substrate?
A race between two limits. On one side, how negative the voltage is allowed to get: with a bypass diode the voltage is clamped near −0.7 V (small heat, Worked 6A); without one it runs to the −20 V breakdown (≈28× the heat, Worked 6B). On the other side, the substrate's temperature limit: the aluminium-honeycomb core can only take so much before it softens. Melt happens when the dissipated heat drives the local temperature past the substrate limit. So the answer is: the bypass clamp sets how much heat is generated; the substrate limit sets how much heat is survivable; whichever side wins decides between "warm patch" and "melted panel." Tightening the bypass groups (smaller K) is how the designer keeps the first number safely under the second.

6.9 Pitfall

Pitfall — "a dead cell just goes open-circuit" EXAM-HOT

The instinct is to assume a failed cell simply stops contributing — an open circuit, a quiet gap in the string. In a series string the opposite is the dangerous case: the surrounding cells force current through the failed cell, driving it into reverse bias, and it becomes the hottest, most dangerous point on the panel — the exact place a melt starts. "Dead" does not mean "inert." Without a bypass diode, "dead" can mean "on fire." Always ask not "did the cell stop?" but "what is the rest of the circuit now forcing upon it?"

7The Space Thermal Environment and the 2-Node Model

7 How hot does it get? The space thermal environment and the 2-node model

A solar cell's behaviour is not fixed — it depends on how hot the cell is. Section 3 showed that temperature slides the whole IV curve (the current–voltage characteristic of a cell), so the power a panel makes is a function of its temperature. But the temperature is itself a function of the power: every watt the cell pulls out as electricity is a watt that no longer heats the panel. Power needs temperature; temperature needs power. The two are locked together, and you cannot solve one without the other. On top of that, Section 6's hot-spot was fundamentally a thermal failure — a melt. So PowerPy cannot ignore heat; it has to model it. This section builds the space thermal picture from first principles — why there is no air to carry heat away, how a panel gains and loses energy by radiation alone, the Stefan–Boltzmann law that governs the loss, and the 2-node energy-balance model PowerPy actually solves — then closes with the orbit and eclipse that make temperature a story unfolding in time.

Key idea

In space there is no air, so there is no convection — a panel can only exchange heat by radiation (to and from the Sun, the planet, and deep space) and by conduction through its own material. The temperature settles at thermal equilibrium: the one temperature where heat in = heat out. Because the panel has a hot front face and a cooler rear face, PowerPy treats it as two nodes — a front and a rear — writes one "energy in = energy out" equation for each, and solves the pair together.

7.1 Why temperature matters at all

It is worth being precise about why a power simulator must drag temperature into the story, because at first glance the two seem like separate subjects. They are not separable here, for two distinct reasons.

  • Temperature moves the IV curve. The term IV curve (current–voltage curve) is the graph of how much current a cell delivers at each voltage; it is the cell's entire electrical personality (Section 3). Heat shifts it — broadly, a hotter silicon-type cell loses a little voltage. So the same cell, in the same sunlight, makes different power at 0 °C and at 80 °C. You literally cannot read the power off the curve until you know which curve you are on, and that is set by temperature.
  • A hot-spot is a thermal failure. Section 6 showed a reverse-biased cell turning into a heater. Whether that heater melts the substrate is a question about temperature. To answer "does this failure cause a melt?" PowerPy must be able to compute how hot a patch of panel actually gets.
The chicken-and-egg, stated once

Temperature → sets the IV curve → sets the operating point → sets the electrical power extracted → which changes how much absorbed energy is left to heat the panel → which changes the temperature. It is a loop. PowerPy resolves it by iteration (Section 7.7): guess a temperature, work all the way round the loop, and repeat until the guess stops changing. Hold this loop in mind — it is the spine of the whole section.

7.2 Heat in a vacuum: radiation is the only game in town

On Earth a hot object sheds heat three ways: conduction (touching something cooler), convection (warm air or water flowing past and carrying heat off), and radiation (glowing — emitting electromagnetic waves). Step into orbit and one of those vanishes entirely.

No air → no convection

Space is very nearly a vacuum. With essentially no gas around the panel, there is nothing to convect heat away — the cooling breeze that keeps a laptop or a rooftop solar panel in check simply does not exist. A space panel can shed heat in only two ways:

  1. Radiation — it glows in the infrared, sending energy out as electromagnetic waves to whatever is around it (the planet, deep space). This also works in reverse: the Sun, the planet, and reflected sunlight radiate onto the panel and heat it.
  2. Conduction — heat flows through the panel's own solid material, e.g. from the hot front face through the board to the cooler rear face.

That is the entire toolkit. Every term in the model below is one of these two mechanisms. Let us inventory the inputs and the one big output.

7.3 The heat inputs — three ways the environment warms the panel

Three separate streams of energy arrive at the panel from outside. Define each carefully, because the model treats them differently depending on which face they hit and which surface property absorbs them.

Term — α (alpha), solar absorptivity

α is the fraction of incident sunlight a surface absorbs (a pure number between 0 and 1). α = 0.97 means the surface soaks up 97% of the sunlight that lands on it and reflects 3%. The front face's absorbed solar power is

Pin,sun = α · S · A · tilt
S = incident solar flux (W/m²); A = area (m²); tilt = a geometric factor (0–1) for how squarely the face meets the Sun — face-on is 1, edge-on is 0.
Term — albedo (PAlbedo)

Albedo is sunlight that bounces off the planet and lands on the panel — second-hand sunshine. It is a fraction of the direct sunlight, expressed by PowerPy as a flux PAlbedo (W/m²) hitting the facing side. Because albedo is reflected sunlight (short-wavelength), it is absorbed through the same solar property α as direct sun.

Term — planetary infrared, IR (PIR)

IR stands for infrared — light just beyond the red end of the visible band, the kind warm objects emit. The planet itself is warm, so it glows in the infrared; the panel's facing side absorbs some of this, a flux PIR (W/m²). Crucially, planetary IR is long-wavelength thermal radiation, so it is absorbed through the surface's emissivity ε, not through α.

Why albedo uses α but planetary IR uses ε EXAM-HOT

This split confuses everyone, and the reason is a real physical law (Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation): a surface absorbs and emits equally well at any given wavelength. So a surface has effectively two separate "absorb numbers": one for sunlight's short wavelengths (we call it α) and one for thermal long wavelengths (we call it ε, the same number that governs how well it emits). Albedo is reflected sunlight → short-wavelength → absorbed by α. Planetary IR is the warm planet's own glow → long-wavelength → absorbed by ε. Same photons-in, but different parts of the spectrum, so different surface property does the absorbing. This is exactly why spacecraft engineers obsess over the ratio α/ε: it sets whether a surface runs hot or cold (we prove this in Worked 7A).

7.4 The heat output — the Stefan–Boltzmann law

Now the dominant way a panel loses heat: it glows. Any surface above absolute zero radiates electromagnetic energy, and the amount is governed by one of the most important equations in this whole subject.

Prad = ε · σ · A · T4
ε (epsilon) = emissivity, 0–1, how good a radiator the surface is (1 = a perfect "black-body" emitter); σ (sigma) = the Stefan–Boltzmann constant = 5.670 × 10−8 W/m²K⁴; A = radiating area (m²); T = absolute temperature in KELVIN.
Term — emissivity ε

ε measures how efficiently a surface radiates heat compared with a perfect black-body at the same temperature. ε = 0.9 means the surface sheds 90% of the heat a perfect radiator would. A high-ε surface is a good radiator — it dumps heat to space easily and therefore runs cooler. (Note ε is also the property that absorbs incoming long-wavelength planetary IR — §7.3 — because, by Kirchhoff, a good emitter is an equally good absorber at that wavelength.)

Two facts to burn in: KELVIN, and the fourth power EXAM-HOT

Why Kelvin? The law is built on absolute temperature — the true amount of thermal energy, measured from absolute zero. Plugging in °C would be nonsense: 0 °C is not "no thermal radiation," it is a balmy 273 K of glow. PowerPy stores temperatures in °C and converts with a constant T0 = −273.15, so that TKelvin = T°C − T0 (which is just T°C + 273.15). Forgetting this conversion is the single most destructive mistake in the section (Pitfall 7.10).

Why the fourth power? Radiated power grows as T⁴, not T. That steepness matters enormously: a surface that gets a little hotter sheds a lot more heat. Double the absolute temperature and you radiate 2⁴ = 16× as much. This is precisely what makes equilibrium stable — if a panel overheats, the T⁴ term ramps up fast and claws the temperature back down.

Both faces radiate. The front and the rear each glow outward, each against the brutal cold of deep space — a background temperature of only TSpace = 2.7 K (the residual cold of the universe). Because 2.7 K is so close to absolute zero, space radiates almost nothing back, so each face sheds heat as ε·σ·A·(T⁴ − TSpace⁴) — essentially full-bore radiation outward with a tiny correction for what little comes back.

7.5 Two more energy flows: electrical extraction and front↔rear conduction

Two terms remain, and the first contains the most important physical insight in the section.

Electrical power leaves as electricity — so it does NOT heat the panel

When the cell delivers electrical power Pelec, that energy walks out down the wires. It is absorbed sunlight that became electricity instead of heat. So in the energy balance, Pelec is subtracted at the front node — it is one more exit door for energy, alongside radiation.

The crucial link to Section 6: a healthy cell has positive Pelec and so removes heat. But a reverse-biased, failed cell has negative Pelec — it absorbs electrical power rather than delivering it. A negative number being subtracted is a positive being added: the failed cell adds heat to the panel. That sign flip is the mathematical heart of the hot-spot.

Term — conduction coefficient C (front↔rear)

Heat also flows through the board, from the hotter face to the cooler one. PowerPy bundles the material into a single conduction coefficient

C = conductivity / thickness
conductivity (W/m·K) is how readily the substrate material passes heat; thickness (m) is the board's depth. C has units W/m²K.

The conductive power flowing into the front node from the rear is then C·A·(T2 − T1) — proportional to the temperature difference between the faces (T₂ = rear, T₁ = front). If the rear is hotter, heat flows forward (positive into the front); if the front is hotter, the same expression is negative and heat flows backward. One expression, both directions handled by its sign.

7.6 The 2-node model: two coupled equations

We now have every ingredient. PowerPy models the panel as two thermal nodes — a front node at temperature T1 and a rear node at temperature T2 (both in Kelvin) — and writes a single "everything in − everything out = 0" equation for each. At equilibrium each node is neither warming nor cooling, so each balance equals zero.

Front node (T1) αF·A·PSun·tilt  −  εF·A·σ·(T1⁴ − TSpace⁴)  −  Pelec  +  C·A·(T2 − T1)  =  0
Rear node (T2) (αR·PAlbedo + εR·PIR)·A·tilt  −  εR·A·σ·(T2⁴ − TSpace⁴)  −  C·A·(T2 − T1)  =  0

Read each term aloud — the equations are just the bookkeeping of §§7.3–7.5:

Every term in words
αF·A·PSun·tilt  (front, IN): direct sunlight absorbed by the front face — its solar absorptivity αF times the solar flux PSun, over area A, scaled by the tilt geometry.
− εF·A·σ·(T1⁴ − TSpace⁴)  (front, OUT): Stefan–Boltzmann radiation from the front face to deep space. Subtracted because it leaves.
− Pelec  (front, OUT): the electrical power extracted, leaving as electricity. Subtracted. Negative for a failed cell → becomes heat IN.
+ C·A·(T2 − T1)  (front, conduction): heat conducted from the rear into the front. Positive if the rear is hotter.
(αR·PAlbedo + εR·PIR)·A·tilt  (rear, IN): the rear's environment heat — reflected sunlight (albedo, via αR) plus the planet's IR glow (via εR).
− εR·A·σ·(T2⁴ − TSpace⁴)  (rear, OUT): Stefan–Boltzmann radiation from the rear face to deep space.
− C·A·(T2 − T1)  (rear, conduction): the same conductive flux, with the opposite sign — what leaves the rear is exactly what enters the front. Energy is conserved across the link.
Notice the conduction term appears with a + on the front and a − on the rear: it is one flow of heat, counted as a gain for whichever node receives it and a loss for the one that gives it up. The two signs must be opposite or energy would be created from nothing. ✓
Why this needs a numerical solver — and why it is the bottleneck

The two equations are coupled (T₁ appears in the rear equation's conduction term and T₂ in the front's, so you cannot solve one without the other) and nonlinear (the T⁴ terms — you cannot rearrange a T⁴ into a tidy closed-form solution). So PowerPy hands the pair to a numerical root-finder, scipy.fsolve scipy.fsolve, which hunts for the (T₁, T₂) that drive both balances to zero. Critically, it does this one panel/cell at a time, in a Python loop. EXAM-HOT That per-item loop is the performance bottleneck of the whole simulator: looping over thousands of cells, each with its own fsolve call, is slow. Chapter 2 replaces it with a single VECTORized solve that handles all cells at once.

7.7 Where the material properties come from: the substrate file

The numbers αF, εR, C and so on are not invented per run — they describe a real panel material and live in a data file.

The substrate JSON  getSubstrateData

Each material is a file data/substrates/NAME.json, loaded by getSubstrateData. Its keys are: name, alpha_front, alpha_rear, epsilon_front, epsilon_rear, conductivity, thickness. Then panel.prepareParameters() prepareParameters reads this file, builds two property dictionaries — front_side and rear_side — and computes the conduction coefficient C = conductivity / thickness.

Example substrate — "SRP MSRO Case 2" 
{
  "name":          "SRP MSRO Case 2",
  "alpha_front":    0.970,
  "alpha_rear":     0.930,
  "epsilon_front":  0.9,
  "epsilon_rear":   0.89,
  "conductivity":   10,      // W/m·K
  "thickness":      0.01     // m
}
Compute C:   C = conductivity / thickness = 10 W/m·K ÷ 0.01 m.
Result:   C = 1000 W/m²K. (Units: (W/m·K) ÷ m = W/m²K. ✓)
A high C (1000 W/m²K) means the board passes heat front-to-rear very readily, so the two faces will sit at nearly the same temperature. A thin, conductive board ties the nodes tightly together; a thick, insulating one lets them drift apart. ✓

7.8 Closing the loop: electro-thermal coupling

Now we can state precisely how PowerPy resolves the chicken-and-egg of §7.1. The coupling is a loop, and the loop is solved by iteration.

The electro-thermal loop  thermalEquilibrium

PowerPy's panel.thermalEquilibrium(voltage, season, P_Albedo, P_IR) drives this cycle:

  1. Guess a temperature T.
  2. Build the cells at T — i.e. fix the IV curve for that temperature (Section 3).
  3. Compute the electrical power at the requested voltage: Pelec = currentAtVoltage(V)·V — current drawn at V, times V.
  4. Solve the thermal balance (§7.6) with that Pelec to get a new temperature.
  5. Repeat until T stops changing.

The outer search for the self-consistent T uses scipy.minimize with the Nelder–Mead method — a derivative-free "amoeba" optimiser that nudges a guess around until it settles. EXAM-HOT Like the thermal solve itself, this iteration is slow — it re-builds the cells and re-solves on every pass, for every item. Chapter 2 vectorizes this too, removing the per-item iteration.

The loop in one sentence

Temperature sets the IV curve → the operating point sets Pelec → Pelec changes the heat balance → which sets a new temperature. Round and round until it converges. That self-consistent fixed point is the panel's true operating temperature and its true power, found together because neither can be known alone.

7.9 Worked examples

Worked 7A — one-node sunlit equilibrium (the α/ε result)

Goal: find the equilibrium temperature of a sunlit plate, stripped to its essence. Ignore albedo, IR, conduction and electrical extraction — just direct sun absorbed, balanced against radiation from one face. (Also take TSpace⁴ ≈ 0, since 2.7 K to the fourth is utterly negligible beside a panel near 400 K.)

Write the balance — heat in = heat out:   α·S·A = ε·σ·A·T⁴.
Area cancels.   A is on both sides (the plate catches and radiates over the same area), so divide it out:   α·S = ε·σ·T⁴. Equilibrium temperature does not depend on size.
Solve for T⁴:   T⁴ = α·S / (ε·σ).
Solve for T:   T = [ α·S / (ε·σ) ]1/4.
Plug in numbers — take α = 0.9,  S = 1367 W/m² (AM0, full sun at 1 AU — defined in §7.11),  ε = 0.85.
Numerator:   α·S = 0.9 × 1367 W/m² = 1230.3 W/m².
Denominator:   ε·σ = 0.85 × 5.67×10−8 W/m²K⁴ = 4.82×10−8 W/m²K⁴.
Divide → T⁴:   T⁴ = 1230.3 / 4.82×10−8 = 2.553×1010 K⁴.
Fourth root, done as two square roots (the step you cannot eyeball):   first √(2.553×1010 K⁴) = 1.598×105 K²;   then √(1.598×105 K²) = 399.8 K.
Convert to °C:   399.8 K + T0 = 399.8 − 273.15 ≈ 127 °C.
Sanity: a black-ish plate in full, unfiltered sunlight with no air to cool it sitting at ~127 °C is exactly the "frying pan in space" you'd expect — far hotter than anything in shade. The order of magnitude is right. ✓
The punchline — T depends on the RATIO α/ε  EXAM-HOT

Factor the result to make the physics jump out:

T = (S/σ)1/4 · (α/ε)1/4 = 394.1 K · (α/ε)1/4
(S/σ)1/4 = (1367 / 5.67×10−8)1/4 = (2.411×1010)1/4 ≈ 394.1 K is fixed by the sunlight alone. Everything about the surface enters only through the single ratio α/ε.

So T ∝ (α/ε)1/4. Only the ratio of solar absorptivity to emissivity matters, not either alone. A surface that drinks sunlight (high α) but radiates poorly (low ε) runs blazing hot; one that reflects sun (low α) yet radiates well (high ε) runs cold. Check our case: (0.9/0.85)1/4 = 1.0144, and 394.1 × 1.0144 = 399.8 K — matching the long calculation exactly. Raising ε lowers T: a better radiator sheds heat faster, so equilibrium settles cooler. This α/ε ratio is the master dial of spacecraft thermal design.

Worked 7B — extracting electrical power lowers the temperature

Goal: redo 7A but now let the cell pull electrical power out, and show the panel runs cooler. The point: energy that leaves as electricity is energy that no longer has to leave as heat, so less radiation — hence a lower T — is needed to balance the books.

Watch the units (this is the trap). In the front-node equation, every term carries the area A except Pelec, which is an absolute power in watts. So unlike 7A, area does NOT cancel once Pelec is present — we must keep A explicit. We use one §3/§6 cell: area A = 30 cm² = 0.0030 m², operating at 2.35 V × 0.48 A.

Balance with electrical term:   α·S·A − Pelec = ε·σ·A·T⁴.  (Absorbed sun, minus electricity removed, equals what must be radiated.)
Absorbed solar power (now an absolute watt figure):   α·S·A = 0.9 × 1367 W/m² × 0.0030 m² = 3.69 W.
Electrical power extracted:   Pelec = 2.35 V × 0.48 A = 1.128 W.
Power that must be radiated:   ε·σ·A·T⁴ = 3.69 W − 1.128 W = 2.563 W. (Less than 7A's 3.69 W — the electricity carried part of the load away.)
Radiative coefficient ε·σ·A:   0.85 × 5.67×10−8 × 0.0030 = 1.446×10−10 W/K⁴.
Solve for T⁴:   T⁴ = 2.563 / 1.446×10−10 = 1.773×1010 K⁴.
Fourth root (two square roots):   √(1.773×1010) = 1.331×105;   √(1.331×105) = ≈ 365 K  (≈ 92 °C).
Compare: 365 K with extraction vs 399.8 K without — about 35 K cooler. The argument holds: roughly a third of the absorbed sunlight (1.128 of 3.69 W) departed as electricity, so the panel needs less T⁴ radiation to balance, and a lower T⁴ means a lower T. Pulling power out of a panel genuinely cools it. ✓  (And the §6 sign warning: had this been a failed cell with Pelec = −1.128 W, the minus-a-negative would ADD 1.128 W, pushing T up instead.)

7.10 Socratic ladder — the ratio α/ε and the fourth power

Ladder — Why does equilibrium temperature depend on the RATIO α/ε, and why is radiated heat proportional to T⁴ rather than T?
Q1. In the vacuum of space, with no air, how can a sunlit panel lose heat at all?
Only by radiation — by glowing, emitting electromagnetic energy out to deep space (and, through the board, by conduction to its other face, but to leave the panel entirely it must ultimately radiate). There is no air, so no convection; there is nothing touching it to conduct into. If it could not radiate, an illuminated panel would heat up without limit.
Q2. What law sets how much it radiates, and on what does that depend?
The Stefan–Boltzmann law, Prad = ε·σ·A·T⁴. The radiated power depends on the emissivity ε (how good a radiator the surface is), the area A, the constant σ, and — steeply — the absolute temperature T raised to the fourth power.
Q3. As the panel heats up, what happens to heat-IN versus heat-OUT, and why does that force a stable equilibrium?
Heat-IN (absorbed sunlight, α·S·A) barely changes with temperature — the Sun delivers the same flux regardless of how warm the panel is. But heat-OUT (ε·σ·A·T⁴) rises sharply as T climbs, because of the fourth power. So if the panel is too cold, OUT < IN and it warms; if too hot, OUT > IN and it cools. There is exactly one temperature where they match — and the T⁴ steepness makes the system snap back to it firmly. The fourth power is what makes equilibrium stable.
Q4. Now set IN = OUT and solve — where does the ratio α/ε appear, and what does it govern?
Setting α·S·A = ε·σ·A·T⁴, the area cancels, leaving T⁴ = (α/ε)·(S/σ), i.e. T = (S/σ)1/4·(α/ε)1/4. The two surface properties never appear alone — only as the ratio α/ε, and even that under a fourth root. Physically: α controls how much sunlight comes IN, ε controls how readily heat goes OUT, so it is their balance that sets the temperature. Want a cooler surface? Lower α/ε — reflect more sun or radiate better. That single ratio is the spacecraft thermal designer's master control, and Worked 7A is its proof.

7.11 Orbit and eclipse — why temperature is really a time series

Everything so far found one equilibrium temperature for one set of conditions. But a satellite orbits, and its surroundings change continuously as it goes — so the real answer is not a single number but a curve in time.

The environment changes around the orbit

In sunlight the array is hot (full solar input) and making power. Then the satellite swings behind the planet into eclipse — the planet blocks the Sun — and abruptly there is no solar input: the array cools fast and makes no power. Albedo and IR also vary with where the satellite is over the planet (above bright cloud vs dark ocean, day side vs night side). So temperature and power are genuinely time series traced out over each orbit, not constants.

Solar intensity falls with distance from the Sun

The Sun's light spreads over an ever-bigger sphere as it travels outward, so the flux dilutes with distance:

flux = LSun / (4π·d²)
LSun = the Sun's luminosity (total power output, W); d = distance from the Sun (m); 4π·d² = the surface area of a sphere of radius d.

The term AM0 ("Air Mass Zero") is the solar spectrum in space with no atmosphere in the way — its intensity is 1367 W/m² at 1 AU (one AU, Astronomical Unit, = the Earth–Sun distance, ≈1.496×1011 m). The 1/d² law is exactly why this value differs elsewhere: at Mars (≈1.52 AU) the flux is only 1367 / 1.52² ≈ 591 W/m² — under half of Earth's — which is why a Mars array must be correspondingly larger.

Term — umbra and penumbra

The planet's shadow has two parts. The umbra is the region of full shadow — the Sun is completely blocked, total eclipse. The penumbra is the partial shadow ringing it, where the planet blocks only part of the Sun's disc, so a fraction of sunlight still gets through. A satellite passing through experiences a brief penumbral dimming, then full umbral darkness, then dimming again on the way out.

How PowerPy finds eclipses  eclipse.py

eclipse.py propagates (steps forward in time) the satellite's orbit and the Sun/planet positions, then works out the intervals when the satellite is inside the umbra — i.e. when it is eclipsed. It currently does this with the poliastro/astropy astronomy libraries. EXAM-HOT Chapter 2 replaces this with the maintained hapsira library (poliastro is no longer maintained), which additionally lets PowerPy compute the albedo and IR fluxes properly rather than approximating them.

7.12 Pitfalls

Pitfall — forgetting to convert °C to KELVIN before the T⁴ term EXAM-HOT

The Stefan–Boltzmann law demands absolute temperature. If you leave T in °C, the error is colossal — not a few percent but orders of magnitude. A panel at 27 °C radiates as 300 K, i.e. ∝ 300⁴ = 8.1×109; plug in "27" and you get 27⁴ = 5.3×105 — wrong by a factor of about 15,000. And near 0 °C it is catastrophic: 0 °C is 273 K (huge radiation), but "0"⁴ = 0 (claims the panel radiates nothing). Always apply TKelvin = T°C − T0 (= T°C + 273.15) before raising to the fourth power. This is why PowerPy stores °C but converts to Kelvin inside the balance.

Pitfall — forgetting that a failed cell's negative Pelec ADDS heat EXAM-HOT

In the front-node equation Pelec is subtracted, which for a healthy cell correctly removes heat. But a reverse-biased failed cell (Section 6) has negative Pelec. Subtracting a negative adds: the term flips from cooling to heating, and the failed cell becomes a heat source in the balance. Forget this sign and you will predict a failed cell cooling the panel, when it is in fact the hot-spot melting it. The same equation describes both regimes — only the sign of Pelec changes.

8Monte Carlo: Estimating the Unknowable

8 Monte Carlo: estimating the unknowable by rolling dice

Sometimes the honest answer to "what is this number?" is: nobody can compute it exactly. Either the calculation has no tidy formula, or there are simply too many possibilities to check them all in any lifetime. A solar array is exactly this kind of problem — Section 7 gave us a model that tells us, for one chosen set of cell failures, how hot the panel gets and how much power it loses. But an array has hundreds or thousands of cells, and the number of ways they could fail is astronomically large. We cannot test every case. This section introduces the escape hatch engineers reach for when a problem is too big to solve head-on: Monte Carlo — the art of estimating a quantity you cannot compute directly by running many random trials and averaging what you see. We build it from the ground up: what an average of random samples actually is, the law that guarantees it converges, the famous 1/√N rule that says exactly how slowly it converges, and then why this slow, humble method is nonetheless the right tool for an array with 2N possible failure states.

Key idea

Monte Carlo (MC) trades an impossible exact calculation for an easy approximate one: instead of working out the true answer, you run the experiment many times with random inputs and average the results. The average of many random trials drifts towards the true value you wanted — and the more trials you run, the closer it gets. You never reach the exact answer, but you get a controllably good estimate, plus an honest measure of how uncertain that estimate is. For a solar array we cannot simulate every possible pattern of cell failures, so we sample failure patterns at random, simulate each one with the Section 7 electro-thermal model, and build up a statistical picture of what happens.

8.1 What Monte Carlo is, and where the name comes from

Term — Monte Carlo (MC) method

A Monte Carlo method is any technique that estimates a hard quantity by running MANY trials with RANDOM inputs and AVERAGING the results. The three words carry the whole idea: many (one trial tells you almost nothing; thousands tell you a lot), random (each trial feeds in inputs drawn by chance), and average (the typical outcome across all trials is your estimate). The name is a joke that stuck: it is named after the Monte Carlo casino in Monaco, because the method runs on randomness — the same rolling-dice, spinning-wheel chance that drives a casino. Physicists working on the atomic bomb in the 1940s needed answers to problems with no formula, so they "played the odds" by simulating random events, and named the trick after the most famous gambling house they knew.

When do you reach for Monte Carlo?

Use MC when an exact calculation is impossible, or when there are too many combinations to check them all. Two flavours of "impossible":

  1. No closed form. The quantity is defined by something with no neat formula — a tangled integral, or the output of a simulation that has no shortcut. You cannot write down the answer; you can only measure it by running the thing.
  2. Too many cases. There is a way to compute each individual case, but the number of cases is so vast you could never finish. Checking them all is hopeless, so you check a random sample of them and infer the whole from the part.

The solar array sits squarely in the second flavour, as §8.4 will show — but the machinery is identical for both.

8.2 Expected value: the long-run average a random quantity is "aiming at"

To understand why averaging random trials works, we need one foundational idea — the thing the average is converging towards.

Term — expected value (the mean)

The expected value of a random quantity is its long-run average — the number you would get if you could repeat the random experiment forever and average all the outcomes. It is not necessarily a value you will ever actually see on a single trial; it is the centre of gravity of all possible outcomes, each weighted by how likely it is. We write it E[X] for a random quantity X, and often call it the mean of X.

Concrete: the expected value of one die roll
List the outcomes. A fair six-sided die shows 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 or 6, each with probability 1/6.
Average them, weighted by probability.   E[roll] = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6.
Add the top.   1+2+3+4+5+6 = 21.
Divide.   E[roll] = 21 / 6 = 3.5.
Note 3.5 is a value the die can never actually show — there is no "3.5" face. The expected value is the long-run average, not a possible single result. Roll a die five times and you might average 2.8 or 4.2; roll it ten thousand times and the average will hug 3.5 very closely. That drift towards 3.5 as the rolls pile up is the whole point of the next two sections. ✓

The bridge from this toy to Monte Carlo is short: if you cannot compute E[X] by listing outcomes and weighting them (because there are too many, or you do not know the weights), you can estimate it by drawing many samples of X at random and taking their ordinary average. That average is called the sample mean, and it is your Monte Carlo estimate of E[X].

Sample mean as an estimate of the expected value x̄ = (1/N) · (x1 + x2 + … + xN)   ≈   E[X]
x1…xN = the outcomes of N independent random trials; x̄ ("x-bar") = their sample mean; E[X] = the true expected value we are after. The approximation "≈" gets better as N grows — that is the next section.

8.3 The Law of Large Numbers — why averaging works at all

Why should the average of a handful of random, jittery outcomes settle down to a single trustworthy number? Because of a theorem so fundamental it underwrites all of statistics.

Term — Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

The Law of Large Numbers states: as the number of trials N grows, the sample average converges to the true expected value. The more times you repeat a random experiment and average, the closer that average gets to E[X]. Individual trials wobble all over the place; their running average steadily calms down and homes in on the mean. This is the licence that makes Monte Carlo legitimate — it is the guarantee that "average lots of random trials" actually delivers the quantity you wanted, given enough trials.

What the LLN does and does not promise

It promises the average will get and stay close to E[X] as N → ∞. It does not promise the average equals E[X] at any finite N, nor that it marches there smoothly — at small N the estimate jumps around (see the figure: wild on the left, calm on the right). The practical questions are therefore "how fast does it converge?" and "how do I know when I've done enough?" — and for those we need a number, not just a guarantee. That number is the 1/√N rule.

8.4 The 1/√N rule — Monte Carlo's blessing and its curse

The Law of Large Numbers says the error shrinks; the next result says how fast. It is the single most important practical fact about Monte Carlo, and it cuts both ways.

The error of an MC estimate shrinks like 1/√N EXAM-HOT

The typical error of a Monte Carlo estimate is proportional to 1/√N, where N is the number of trials. Read off the consequences, because they are not intuitive:

  • Quadrupling the trials HALVES the error. Since the error goes as 1/√N, multiplying N by 4 multiplies the error by 1/√4 = 1/2. Four times the work buys you only twice the accuracy.
  • To cut the error by 10× you need 100× the trials. To divide the error by 10 you must multiply √N by 10, i.e. multiply N by 10² = 100. Ten times more accuracy costs a hundred times more computing.

This slow convergence is MC's main weakness — accuracy is brutally expensive. But there is a redeeming twist that makes the whole method worthwhile.

The redeeming twist: 1/√N is DIMENSION-INDEPENDENT EXAM-HOT

That 1/√N rate does not care how many variables the problem has. Whether you are averaging over 1 random input or 10,000 of them, the error still falls as 1/√N — the dimension of the problem (the number of things varying at once) never enters the rate. This is extraordinary, because almost every other numerical method gets exponentially harder as you add variables (the "curse of dimensionality"): a method that needs 10 sample points per variable needs 102 for two variables, 103 for three, 10100 for a hundred — instantly hopeless. Monte Carlo sidesteps that entirely. This is precisely why MC wins for huge problems: a solar array with thousands of independently-failing cells is a thousand-dimensional problem, and MC is one of the very few methods whose cost does not explode as the dimension climbs.

Term — standard error (SE)

To turn "the error goes like 1/√N" into an actual number you can compute from your own run, use the standard error — an estimate of how far your sample mean is likely to be from the true mean:

SE ≈ s / √N
s = the sample standard deviation (how spread-out the individual trial outcomes are — big if the trials scatter widely, small if they cluster); N = number of trials. SE is the typical size of the gap between your estimate x̄ and the truth E[X].

The s in the numerator says noisier problems need more trials for the same accuracy; the √N in the denominator is the slow 1/√N convergence in person. Halving SE means quadrupling N — there it is again.

8.5 Why here: an array has 2N failure patterns

Now the motivating point — why a solar-array simulator needs Monte Carlo at all. It comes down to a count that gets out of hand astonishingly fast.

The combinatorial explosion: 2N failure states EXAM-HOT

Consider an array of N cells, where each cell is in one of two states — OK or failed. How many distinct failure patterns are there? Each cell independently doubles the count: 1 cell gives 2 patterns, 2 cells give 2×2 = 4, 3 cells give 8, and in general N cells give 2N combinations. This grows explosively. For just 100 cells that is 2100 ≈ 1.3 × 1030 patterns — a 1 with thirty zeros. There is no computer, and no amount of time, that can simulate 1030 cases. You cannot check them all.

The Monte Carlo response: sample, simulate, build a distribution

Since enumeration is hopeless, PowerPy does the Monte Carlo thing: it samples failure patterns at random (or sweeps them cleverly — §8.6), simulates each one with the Section 7 electro-thermal model, and assembles a statistical picture from the results. Rather than one impossible exact answer, you get distributions and probabilities:

  • the distribution of total power loss across many failure scenarios — how bad does the array's output typically get, and how bad in the worst cases?
  • the distribution of maximum cell temperature — how hot does the hottest spot tend to run?
  • the probability that a hot-spot exceeds the substrate's melt limit — the headline safety number: across realistic failures, how often does a cell get hot enough to destroy the panel?

None of these can be read off a single calculation. They are statistical outcomes, and Monte Carlo is how you earn them.

8.6 How a "run" works in PowerPy, and the three sampling modes

Concretely, what is one Monte Carlo trial here? It is one full pass through the simulator with a chosen set of failures. (The details of building and solving the circuit are Chapter 2's subject; here we just need the shape of a trial.)

One MC run, step by step season=0
  1. Pick which cells fail. In PowerPy a cell is marked failed by setting its season=0 — that flag tells the model this cell is dead. Choosing a failure pattern is choosing which cells get season=0.
  2. Build the circuit for that pattern of healthy and failed cells.
  3. Solve the coupled electro-thermal model (Section 7) — the iteration that finds each cell's self-consistent temperature and power.
  4. Record the outcomes: total power produced, maximum cell temperature, number of hot-spots, and whether any cell crosses the breakdown/melt limit.
  5. Repeat many times, with many different failure patterns, accumulating the outcomes into distributions.

PowerPy plans three sampling MODES — three different ways of choosing the failure patterns to feed in, each answering a different engineering question.

The three sampling modes
(1) POSITION sweep.  Fail each location in turn — kill cell 1 alone, then cell 2 alone, and so on across every position. Goal: find the worst spot — which single cell, if it fails, hurts the array most. This is a deterministic sweep, not random sampling, but it is part of the same machinery.
(2) COUNT sweep.  Fail k random cells for k = 1, 2, 3, … — one failure, then two, then three, increasing the number of dead cells. Goal: chart degradation versus number of failures — how does the array decline as more cells die?
(3) RANDOM sampling.  Throw many random failure patterns at the model. Goal: get the full distributions of §8.5 — the complete statistical picture of power loss, temperature and melt probability. This is Monte Carlo in its purest form.
All three feed the same per-run machinery (pick failures → build → solve → record); they differ only in how the failure patterns are chosen. The outputs become histograms (the shape of each distribution) and rankings — "most vs least damaging" failure locations, and "which layout is most robust" across designs. ✓

8.7 Reproducibility: seed the random number generator

Monte Carlo runs on randomness — but uncontrolled randomness is a debugging nightmare and makes fair comparison impossible. The fix is the seed.

Term — seed (of a random number generator)

A computer's "random" numbers are not truly random — they come from a pseudo-random number generator (PRNG), a deterministic algorithm that produces a stream of numbers that look random. The seed is the starting value that algorithm is launched from. Fixing the seed makes a run repeatable: the same seed always produces the same sequence of "random" numbers, so the whole Monte Carlo run replays identically.

Why fixing the seed matters

Two reasons, both essential:

  • Debugging. If a run crashes or produces a weird result, a fixed seed lets you reproduce the exact same trials and hunt the bug. With an unseeded run, every execution is different and the failure may never recur — you cannot debug what you cannot repeat.
  • Fair comparison of designs. To compare two array layouts honestly, you must subject them to the same random failures. Seed both runs identically and any difference in outcome is due to the design, not to luck of the draw. Different seeds would confound design differences with random noise.

8.8 Variance reduction: needing fewer runs (names only)

Smarter sampling, same accuracy, fewer runs

Because the 1/√N rule makes brute-force accuracy so expensive, a whole toolkit exists for getting the same accuracy from fewer runs by sampling more cleverly. These are called variance-reduction techniques. Two you will hear named:

  • Stratified sampling — deliberately spread the samples across distinct regions ("strata") instead of letting chance clump them, so no region is over- or under-sampled.
  • Importance sampling — concentrate samples where they matter most (e.g. on the rare-but-dangerous failures that drive the melt probability), then correct for the bias this introduces.

You do not need the mechanics here — just file them as "ways to need fewer runs" when plain Monte Carlo would be too slow.

8.9 Worked examples

Worked 8A — estimating π by throwing random darts

Goal: compute π using nothing but random points and counting — the canonical Monte Carlo demonstration. The idea: if a shape's area is hard to integrate, measure it by peppering a known region with random darts and seeing what fraction land inside.

Set up the geometry.  Throw points uniformly (every spot equally likely) into a 1×1 square. Inscribe a quarter-circle of radius 1 in it, centred at one corner. A point (x, y) is "inside" the quarter-circle when x² + y² ≤ 1.
Relate the fraction to areas.  Because the darts are uniform, the fraction landing inside the arc ≈ (area of quarter-circle) / (area of square).
Put in the areas.  Area of the square = 1×1 = 1. Area of a quarter-circle of radius 1 = (1/4)·π·1² = π/4. So the inside-fraction ≈ (π/4) / 1 = π/4.
Rearrange for π.  If inside/total ≈ π/4, then π ≈ 4 × (inside / total). We have turned "compute π" into "throw darts and count".
Do a tiny illustrative run.  Throw 1000 darts; suppose 786 land inside the arc. Then inside/total = 786/1000 = 0.786.
Estimate π.  π ≈ 4 × 0.786 = 3.144.
Sanity-check vs the true value.  The true π = 3.14159…, so 3.144 is off by about 0.002 — close, from just 1000 random darts and no calculus at all. The error is right in line with 1/√N: with N = 1000, 1/√N ≈ 0.032, and our estimate landed well within that ballpark of the truth. Throw a million darts and you would expect roughly another √1000 ≈ 32× tightening. ✓
Worked 8B — convergence arithmetic: how many runs for a target accuracy?

Goal: a concrete planning calculation using the 1/√N rule. Given: 100 runs gave a standard error of 8 W on the estimated power loss. Question: how many runs are needed to reach an SE of 2 W?

State the proportionality.  SE ∝ 1/√N, so SE shrinks only as the square root of the run count.
Find the required improvement factor.  We want to go from 8 W down to 2 W — that is cutting SE by a factor of 8/2 = 4×.
Translate to a run-count factor.  Since SE ∝ 1/√N, cutting SE by 4× means increasing √N by 4×, i.e. increasing N by 4² = 16×.
Apply the algebra explicitly.  N2 = N1 · (SE1 / SE2)² = 100 · (8 W / 2 W)² = 100 · 4² = 100 · 16.
Result.  N2 = 1600 runs.
Sanity: the units inside (SE1/SE2) cancel (W/W), as they must — a ratio of errors is dimensionless, leaving a pure run count. And the answer respects the rule: 4× better accuracy cost 16× the runs, exactly the "halve the error → quadruple the runs, squared" behaviour. This is why Monte Carlo budgets balloon when you demand precision. ✓
Worked 8C (optional) — a sample average only approaches the expected value slowly

Goal: see the Law of Large Numbers in miniature, reusing the die from §8.2 (true E[roll] = 3.5).

A few rolls.  Roll 4 times and get {2, 6, 3, 1}. Sample mean = (2+6+3+1)/4 = 12/4 = 3.0 — off the true 3.5 by 0.5.
More rolls.  Roll 10 times: {2,6,3,1,5,4,4,2,6,3}. Sum = 36, mean = 36/10 = 3.6 — now off by only 0.1.
Many rolls.  With thousands of rolls, the mean would sit on 3.5 to two or three decimals.
The estimate from a few rolls is unreliable (3.0), tightens with more (3.6), and only approaches 3.5 as the rolls grow — never snapping exactly onto it at any finite count. That gradual homing-in is the Law of Large Numbers, and the speed of the homing-in is the 1/√N rule. ✓

8.10 Socratic ladder — sampling failures, and knowing when to stop

Ladder — Why do we sample random failures instead of simulating every possible failure, and how do we know when we've run ENOUGH?
Q1. (Foundation) What does it mean to estimate the expected value of a quantity by sampling?
The expected value E[X] is the long-run average of a random quantity X. To estimate it by sampling, you draw many independent random outcomes x1, x2, …, xN and take their ordinary average x̄ = (1/N)Σxi. By the Law of Large Numbers that sample mean converges to E[X] as N grows. So "estimate by sampling" = "run the random experiment many times and average what comes out".
Q2. For an array of N cells, each either OK or failed, how many failure combinations are there?
2N. Each cell independently has 2 states, and the states multiply: 2 × 2 × … × 2 (N times) = 2N. For 100 cells that is 2100 ≈ 1.3 × 1030 distinct patterns.
Q3. Why can't we just enumerate them — simulate every single one of those 2N patterns?
Because 2N is astronomically large. Even at 100 cells there are ~1030 patterns, and each pattern requires a full electro-thermal solve (Section 7) that itself takes real compute. No computer could finish 1030 solves in any feasible time — the universe is far too young. Enumeration is simply impossible, so we must sample: simulate a random subset and infer the whole statistical picture from it.
Q4. (Why) If we only sample, how does the error of our estimate behave as we add more samples?
It shrinks as 1/√N — slowly. Quadrupling the samples only halves the error; getting 10× more accuracy costs 100× more samples. The good news is that this rate is dimension-independent: it does not get worse just because the array has thousands of cells, which is exactly why sampling is viable here at all. We quantify the current error with the standard error SE ≈ s/√N.
Q5. (Payoff) So what tells us we've run ENOUGH — when do we stop?
Two linked signals. First, the estimate stabilises: as you add runs, watch the running estimate (the convergence curve); once it stops drifting and just jitters within a narrow band, more runs are buying little. Second, the standard error is small enough for the decision at hand: compute SE ≈ s/√N and keep going until SE drops below your tolerance (as in Worked 8B — if you need SE ≤ 2 W and 100 runs give 8 W, you know to push to 1600 runs). "Enough" is not a fixed number; it is whenever the estimate has settled and its standard error meets your required accuracy.

8.11 Pitfall

Pitfall — the 1/√N cost, and unseeded runs aren't comparable EXAM-HOT

Two traps, both born of forgetting how Monte Carlo really behaves.

(1) Accuracy is punishingly expensive. Because the error falls only as 1/√N, wanting 10× more accuracy costs 100× more runs — not 10×. Engineers routinely under-budget Monte Carlo because they assume error falls linearly with effort; it does not. If 1000 runs give you ±10 W and you need ±1 W, brace for 100,000 runs, not 10,000. (This is also the cue to consider variance reduction, §8.8.)

(2) An unseeded run isn't reproducible. If you do not fix the seed, every execution uses a different random sequence, so a result can never be reproduced — fatal for debugging, and fatal for fair comparison. Comparing two designs on different random failures confounds the design difference with the luck of the draw; you cannot tell whether layout A beat layout B because A is better or because A happened to draw easier failures. Always seed when comparing designs or chasing a bug.

Worked Solutions

Section 1 — Why a Solar Array — and the Four Numbers of a Cell

P1-1. Cell quoted as Isc = 0.52 A, Voc = 2.70 V, Vmp = 2.30 V, Imp = 0.50 A.

(a) Coordinates of the MPP. The Maximum Power Point is, by definition, located at (Vmp, Imp). So the MPP is at (2.30 V, 0.50 A). (Remember the MPP is a point — a voltage paired with a current — not a number of watts.)

(b) Maximum power. Pmp = Vmp · Imp = 2.30 V × 0.50 A = 1.15 W (volts × amps = watts).
Sanity check: the ideal rectangle here is Voc·Isc = 2.70 × 0.52 = 1.404 W, and 1.15 W is a bit below that, as the real maximum power always must be — consistent.

(c) Defining conditions. Isc is the current measured when the terminals are shorted together so the voltage is zero (V = 0). Voc is the voltage measured when nothing is connected so that no current flows (I = 0). (One is "all current, no voltage"; the other is "all voltage, no current".)
P1-2. Same reference cell, but a flaw rounds the knee to a new operating point Vmp = 2.20 V, Imp = 0.45 A, with Voc = 2.67 V and Isc = 0.50 A unchanged.

(a) New maximum power. Pmp = Vmp · Imp = 2.20 V × 0.45 A = 0.99 W. That is down from the healthy cell's 1.128 W — the rounded knee costs about 0.14 W per cell.

(b) New Fill Factor. The ideal rectangle is unchanged because Voc and Isc are unchanged: Voc·Isc = 2.67 × 0.50 = 1.335 W. So
FF = Pmp / (Voc·Isc) = 0.99 W / 1.335 W = 0.742 (≈ 0.74).
Sanity check: still between 0 and 1, unit-less, and lower than the healthy 0.845 — exactly what a more rounded (less square) curve should give.

(c) Why a higher Fill Factor is better (one line). A higher FF means the cell's actual power sits closer to the ideal Voc·Isc rectangle, so you get more watts out of the same Voc and Isc — more power per cell, hence fewer/lighter cells to launch for the required power.
P1-3. cell.calcQuad() returned {isc: 0.50, voc: 2.67, vmp: 2.35, imp: 0.48, pmp: 1.13}.

(a) Which are the four numbers, which is derived? The four measured numbers are isc, voc, vmp and imp (= Isc, Voc, Vmp, Imp). The fifth entry, pmp, is derived: it is just Vmp · Imp = 2.35 × 0.48 ≈ 1.13 W, handed to you for convenience. (The MPP and FF are likewise derived, not measured.)

(b) Fill Factor from the bundle.
Pmp = 1.13 W (given, = 2.35 × 0.48).
Ideal rectangle = Voc · Isc = 2.67 × 0.50 = 1.335 W.
FF = 1.13 / 1.335 = 0.846 (≈ 0.85, matching the 0.845 we found from the raw values — the tiny difference is just the rounding of Pmp to 1.13). Unit-less and inside 0–1: good.

(c) Current at exactly V = 1.5 V — which call? Reach for cell.calcIVCurve(step). calcQuad() only gives the summary landmarks (the four numbers + Pmp); it does not tell you the current at an arbitrary voltage like 1.5 V. The IV table from calcIVCurve(step) lists (V, I) points all along the curve, so you can read off (or interpolate) the current at 1.5 V. (Choosing a smaller step gives points spaced more finely, so a value near 1.5 V is easier to read directly.)

Section 2 — The Equivalent Circuit, Model Fitting, and SPICE

P2-1. Extract vt_cell then n for isc = 0.52 A, imp = 0.49 A, vmp = 2.30 V, voc = 2.62 V.
Step 1 — vt_cell. isc − imp = 0.52 − 0.49 = 0.03 A. Then vt_cell = vmp · (isc−imp)/imp = 2.30 V × (0.03 A / 0.49 A) = 2.30 × 0.061224 = 0.1408 V. The (A/A) ratio is dimensionless, so the result is in volts. ✓
Step 2 — ideality factor. n = vt_cell / V_T_Spice = 0.1408 V / 0.02585 V = 5.45 (volts cancel → dimensionless).
Comment. n ≈ 5.4 is higher than the ≈3.79 of the worked example because this curve is "softer" — imp sits further below isc (gap 0.03 A vs 0.02 A), which enlarges vt_cell and hence n. For a triple-junction stack n in the 3–5 range is plausible; a value drifting well above that hints at a low-fill-factor or leaky cell where RShuntModel would be the better fit.
P2-2. Why reporting vmp = 2.40 V (instead of 2.35 V) pushes r_cell_high toward or past zero.
Recall r_cell_high = (vt_cell/imp)·ln((isc−imp)/i0_cell) − vmp/imp, and in example (a) this was 4.91 − 4.90 ≈ 0.01 Ω. The second term, vmp/imp, is directly proportional to vmp, so raising vmp raises it the most: 2.40 V / 0.48 A = 5.00 Ω (up from 4.90 Ω). The first term moves far less: vt_cell shifts only slightly (vt_cell ∝ vmp gives ~0.0999 V) and it sits inside a logarithm, which barely budges — the first term stays near 4.9–5.0 Ω. So the second term now roughly equals or exceeds the first, and r_cell_high ≈ (~5.0) − 5.00 ≤ 0, i.e. essentially zero or slightly negative. What it tells you: a (near-)negative series resistance is physically impossible, so the four numbers no longer describe one self-consistent curve — the inflated vmp is the data error to recheck, not something to clamp away.
P2-3. Effect of raising Rs.
(i) The knee. Series resistance subtracts a voltage toll I·Rs from the terminal voltage, and that toll is largest where the current is still high — right at the knee. So a larger Rs rounds off the sharp corner and pulls the high-current part of the curve to the left (lower voltage). The plateau-to-knee transition becomes a gentler diagonal instead of a crisp bend.
(ii) Maximum power. Pmp = Vmp·Imp falls, because the knee voltage drops while the current there is essentially unchanged. Which number moves most: vmp — the maximum-power voltage — is the one pulled down by the I·Rs drop; imp is barely affected since near the knee the current is still close to the light-limited value. (Physically: this is why minimising contact/interconnect resistance is worth real effort — series resistance is a direct power leak at the operating point.)

Section 3 — The Cell Over Its Life: Temperature and Radiation

P3-1. Vmp at end of life, hot. Apply pipeline step 6,  vmp = vmp·rvmp(dose_v) + vmp_dt(dose_v)·(T − t_ref), then stop (no loss factors).
Radiation term:  2.35 V × 0.93 = 2.1855 V.
Temperature offset:  ΔT = 65 − 28 = +37 °C.
Slope term:  (−5.5×10−3 V/°C) × (+37 °C) = −0.2035 V. The slope is negative and ΔT is positive, so the term subtracts — being hotter than t_ref pulls the voltage down, as expected.
Sum:  Vmp = 2.1855 V − 0.2035 V = 1.98 V (1.982 V).
Sanity: both effects push the same way for Vmp here — radiation lowers it (2.35→2.19 V) and heat lowers it further (→1.98 V). Voltage at a hot end-of-life cell is well below the datasheet value; this is why the worst-case voltage check is usually "hot & EOL."
P3-2. Read a remaining-factor curve, then apply it.
(a) The dose argument is in units of 1×1014 e/cm², so divide the real fluence by 1e14:  5×1014 ÷ 1×1014 = 5. You would call cell.setDose(5, …) for the current dose.
(b) With ΔT = 0 the temperature term drops out, leaving only the radiation term of step 5:  Imp = imp·rimp(dose_i) = 0.48 A × 0.90 = 0.432 A.
(c) The temperature term is isc_dt-style slope × (T − t_ref); the cell is held at exactly t_ref, so (T − t_ref) = 0 and the entire slope term is zero regardless of its value. Only the radiation factor remains.
P3-3. Spot the units error. The setDose argument is pre-scaled: PowerPy multiplies whatever you pass by 1×1014 internally. By passing the raw fluence 2e15, the engineer actually requested 2×1015 × 1×1014 = 2×1029 e/cm² — an absurd dose far past anything physical, so every remaining-factor regressor saturates to ~0 (the modelled cell is "destroyed").
Fix: divide the real fluence by 1e14 before passing it:  2×1015 ÷ 1×1014 = 20. The correct call is cell.setDose(20, 20) (20 for current, 20 for voltage, since the report gives the same fluence to both).

Section 4 — Losses, Seasons, Pointing, and the Voltage Corrections

P4-1. Combine four losses, both ways. Factors: 0.99, 0.985, 0.97, 0.995.
(a) Product:  0.99 · 0.985 · 0.97 · 0.995 = 0.9412 (≈ 5.88 % lost).
(b) RSS via RSS_loss. Lost fractions: 1−0.99 = 0.010,  1−0.985 = 0.015,  1−0.97 = 0.030,  1−0.995 = 0.005.
Square and sum:  0.010² + 0.015² + 0.030² + 0.005² = 0.0001 + 0.000225 + 0.0009 + 0.000025 = 0.00125.
Square-root:  √0.00125 = 0.0354 (combined lost fraction ≈ 3.54 %). Back to a surviving factor:  1 − 0.0354 = 0.9646.
(c) The RSS result (0.9646) is larger than the product (0.9412) — RSS always gives the smaller total loss because it does not assume every loss hits worst-case at once. Which to trust: since coverglass, assembly and wiring are deterministic, ever-present deductions, they belong in the product; only the calibration is a genuine ± uncertainty. The defensible budget multiplies the three deterministic losses and RSS-combines the uncertain one(s) separately, then multiplies the two results — so neither the pure product (over-pessimistic) nor the pure RSS (over-optimistic on the certain losses) is right by itself.
P4-2. Effective irradiance at Winter Solstice. setSeason("WS") sets multiplier = 1.034 and season_angle = −23.5°; alpha = beta = 0°.
(a) Tilt:  tilt = cos(alpha + season_angle)·cos(beta) = cos(0° + (−23.5°))·cos(0°) = cos(−23.5°)·1 = 0.9171. (Note it is not 1, because the solstice season angle tilts the geometry even with alpha = beta = 0.)
(b) Effective irradiance:  season·tilt·B0 = 1.034 · 0.9171 · 1367 W/m². Step it: 1.034 · 0.9171 = 0.9483;  0.9483 · 1367 = 1296 W/m².
(c) The combined ratio season·tilt = 0.9483 is below 1, so ln(season·tilt) < 0 and the §4.8 voltage correction is a small loss — even though Winter Solstice is the brighter Sun (1.034 > 1), the −23.5° season tilt more than cancels the brightness gain. As always, it is the combined season·tilt, not the season alone, that sets the sign.
P4-3. What is setSeason(0) for?
(a) A season multiplier of 0 scales that string's irradiance — and therefore its current and output — to zero. The string is modelled as completely dead: it produces nothing.
(b) A failed string (open circuit, shattered cells, a disconnected harness) contributes no power, which is exactly what a 0 multiplier reproduces. By setting one string to 0 while the rest stay at "VEX", the engineer asks the simulator: "If this string dies, how much power can the surviving array still deliver — does the spacecraft still meet its budget?" It is a single-point-failure / degraded-mode study.
(c) setSeason(1) is the opposite extreme — it sets the irradiance to exactly AM0 (full nominal output, no season scaling), whereas setSeason(0) sets it to nothing (a dead element used for failure modelling).

Section 5 — From Cell to Array: SCA, the Hierarchy, and the Two Diodes

P5-1. Compute a section's bus V / I / P. Apply the §5.2 rule at each level — series sums volts and keeps current common; parallel sums amps and keeps voltage common.
(a) One string (12 cells in SERIES):  voltages add ⇒ V = 12 × 2.30 V = 27.6 V; subtract the one series blocking diode (≈0.7 V comes off the voltage) ⇒ 26.9 V. Current is common ⇒ I = one cell's 0.50 A = 0.50 A (unchanged by the series diode).
(b) Section voltage (16 strings in PARALLEL):  voltage is common across parallel branches ⇒ Vsection ≈ 26.9 V (it does not multiply by 16).
(c) Section current:  currents add ⇒ Isection = 16 × 0.50 A = 8.0 A.
(d) Section power:  P = V·I = 26.9 V × 8.0 A = 215.2 W ≈ 215 W.
Summary of which operation applied: step (a) voltage = added (series), step (a) current = common (series); step (b) voltage = common (parallel); step (c) current = added (parallel). The single most common slip is multiplying the voltage by 16 in (b) — that would break "voltage is common in parallel."
P5-2. Which diode protects, and why. Tell them apart by placement: blocking = in series with a string, stops external back-feed; bypass = across a cell, stops internal reverse-bias/hotspot.
(a) Battery pushing current back into the dark array at night → BLOCKING diode. It is in series at each string's output and lets current out but blocks current trying to flow back into the array, so the battery cannot dump current into the cells in eclipse. (External back-feed.)
(b) One shaded cell partway up a string overheating → BYPASS (shunt) diode. The common string current forces the shaded cell into reverse bias; the bypass diode across it turns on at ≈0.7 V, carries the string current around the bad cell and clamps it to ≈−0.7 V, capping the hotspot. (Internal reverse bias.)
(c) Brighter parallel neighbours driving current down into a cold mismatched string → BLOCKING diode. Because voltage is common on the shared rails, the bright strings can back-feed the weak one; the blocking diode at the weak string's output refuses that reverse current. (External back-feed, between strings.)
Pattern: (a) and (c) are both external current trying to enter a string → blocking; (b) is an internal cell being reverse-biased → bypass.
P5-3. Sizing, plus a reference-break trap.
(a) Series ⇒ volts add, so n ≈ target ÷ cell voltage = 50 V ÷ 2.30 V ≈ 21.7. Round up to 22 cells (you cannot fit a fractional cell, and rounding down to 21 gives 21 × 2.30 = 48.3 V, which falls short of 50 V). Check: 22 × 2.30 V = 50.6 V, just clearing 50 V before the blocking-diode drop and any margin for the hot/end-of-life worst case.
(b) What went wrong: string.fromSingleCell(...) takes a deep copy of the prototype cell at the moment it is called (it clones the cell 12 times). The later cell.setDose(20,20) modifies only the original prototype, which is now independent of the 12 copies inside the string — so the string is still made of zero-dose ("brand-new") cells. This is the reference break (§5.5).
Fix: either set the dose on the prototype before building (cell.setDose(20,20) first, then string.fromSingleCell(...)), or set it on the string so the cascade reaches every copy (s.setDose(20,20) — a forwarding command, §5.4). Both give all 12 internal cells the intended dose.

Section 6 — Reverse Bias, Hot-Spots, and Substrate Damage

P6-1. Dissipation with and without a bypass diode. Heat in the failed cell is |V|·I.
(a) Protected:  |V| = 0.7 V,  P = 0.7 V × 0.50 A = 0.35 W. A barely-warm patch.
(b) Unprotected (driven to breakdown):  |V| = 20 V,  P = 20 V × 0.50 A = 10.0 W concentrated in one ~30 cm² cell — easily enough to scorch.
(c) Reduction factor:  10.0 W ÷ 0.35 W ≈ 28×. The factor is the same as in Worked 6A/6B because it is just the ratio of the two voltages, 20 V ÷ 0.7 V ≈ 28.6: the current appears in both numerator and denominator and cancels, so the reduction depends only on how far the clamp holds the voltage below the breakdown level, not on the value of the current.
P6-2. Worst case from the layout. Use  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (K−1)·Vcell,  Vcell = 2.35 V,  I = 0.48 A, cap at 20 V.
(a) K = 6:  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (6−1)×2.35 = 0.7 + 11.75 = 12.45 V. This is below the 20 V breakdown cap, so the formula applies directly (the diode group does not quite drive the cell to breakdown). The N = 24 figure does not enter the worst single-cell case — only the group size K does, because the pile-on comes from the (K−1) healthy cells inside the same bypass group.
(b) Worst-case heat:  P = |Vfail|·I = 12.45 V × 0.48 A = 5.98 W ≈ 6.0 W. Roughly 18× the protected (K = 1) bookend of 0.34 W — uncomfortably hot, though short of the full 9.6 W breakdown case.
(c) Re-wire to K = 2:  |Vfail| ≈ 0.7 + (2−1)×2.35 = 3.05 V;  P = 3.05 V × 0.48 A = 1.46 W ≈ 1.5 W. What the designer bought: the worst-case hot-spot dropped from ~6.0 W to ~1.5 W (about 4× cooler), sliding the failed cell toward the gentle −0.7 V clamp. What it cost: three times as many bypass diodes — 12 groups instead of 4 — i.e. more mass, more solder joints, more complexity. This is the §6.4 trade-off in numbers.
P6-3. Why parallel strings make it worse. In §6.3's picture the failed cell is the single sink and every healthy generator is a donor. In scenario A the only donors are the healthy cells of the failed cell's own string, so the current forced through the bad cell is limited to roughly one string's worth. In scenario B the three extra parallel strings share the same bus, and their current can be pushed backwards across the bus into the weak string and through the failed cell on top of its own string's contribution. More donors feeding one sink means a larger current I forced through the bad cell, and since its heat is |V|·I, a larger I means more dissipation for the same reverse voltage — the hot-spot runs hotter. The component that limits this: the blocking diode (§6.6), placed in series with each string, which stops current from the bus flowing backwards into a weak or shaded string and thus caps the extra push that parallel strings would otherwise add.

Section 7 — The Space Thermal Environment and the 2-Node Model

P7-1. Equilibrium T for a different α/ε. Use T = (S/σ)1/4·(α/ε)1/4 with (S/σ)1/4 = 394.1 K,  α = 0.30,  ε = 0.80.
(a) Compute the ratio and its fourth root:  α/ε = 0.30 / 0.80 = 0.375.  Fourth root as two square roots: √0.375 = 0.6124, then √0.6124 = 0.7826.  So T = 394.1 K × 0.7826 = 308.4 K.  In °C: 308.4 − 273.15 ≈ 35.3 °C.
(b) Hotter or cooler than Worked 7A?  Much cooler — 308 K versus 399.8 K, about 91 K cooler. The reason is the α/ε ratio: 7A had α/ε = 0.9/0.85 ≈ 1.06, whereas here α/ε = 0.375, roughly 2.8× smaller. A smaller α/ε means the surface absorbs proportionally less sunlight (low α) relative to how well it radiates heat away (high ε), so equilibrium settles at a lower temperature. Since T ∝ (α/ε)1/4, the temperatures scale as (0.375/1.06)1/4 ≈ 0.77, and 399.8 × 0.77 ≈ 308 K — consistent.
(c) What coating, and why on a radiator?  A sun-reflecting coating gives low α/ε: it has a low α (it reflects most sunlight rather than absorbing it) while keeping a high ε (it still radiates infrared heat efficiently) — e.g. a white paint or an optical solar reflector. You put exactly this on a radiator panel because its job is to dump internally generated heat to space, not to soak up sunlight: low α stops the Sun from loading it up, and high ε lets it radiate the spacecraft's waste heat away, so it runs as cold as possible.
P7-2. Compute C from a substrate file. The conduction coefficient is C = conductivity / thickness.
(a) Compute C:  C = 25 W/m·K ÷ 0.005 m = 5000 W/m²K.  (Units check: (W/m·K) ÷ m = W/m²K. ✓)
(b) Tighter or looser coupling than SRP MSRO Case 2?  This board has C = 5000 W/m²K versus the SRP board's 1000 W/m²K — five times larger. A larger C means heat passes front-to-rear far more readily for a given temperature difference (the conductive flux is C·A·(T₂−T₁)), so this board ties its two faces more tightly together: their temperatures T₁ and T₂ will sit even closer to each other. (Physically sensible — it is both more conductive, 25 vs 10 W/m·K, and thinner, 0.005 vs 0.01 m, and both of those raise C.)
(c) Which file and functions?  The numbers come from the substrate JSON file data/substrates/NAME.json, loaded by getSubstrateData; panel.prepareParameters() then builds the front_side/rear_side property dictionaries and computes C = conductivity/thickness.
P7-3. What happens to T entering eclipse.
(a) Which term vanishes?  The direct-sunlight input αF·A·PSun·tilt goes to zero the moment the satellite is fully inside the umbra, because the planet blocks the Sun entirely (PSun → 0). The largest heat input to the front node disappears, so the balance is thrown out of equilibrium with heat-out now exceeding heat-in. (Albedo also vanishes, since albedo is reflected sunlight; only the planet's own IR glow remains as a small input.)
(b) Why the temperature falls.  The Stefan–Boltzmann term ε·σ·A·(T⁴ − TSpace⁴) does not switch off — the panel keeps glowing to deep space (2.7 K) regardless of whether the Sun is shining, because any surface above absolute zero radiates. With the solar input gone but radiation continuing, heat-OUT now exceeds heat-IN, so the panel loses net energy and its temperature drops. It cools quickly at first (T⁴ is large while it is still hot) and the cooling eases as T falls.
(c) Why a time series, and electrical output.  Because the inputs themselves change as the satellite moves around its orbit — full sun, then penumbra, then umbra, then back — the equilibrium temperature is different at every point, so T traces a curve over time rather than holding one value; PowerPy must solve the balance repeatedly along the orbit. During eclipse the array's electrical output falls to zero: with no sunlight there is no photo-generated current, so the cells make no power (and the spacecraft must run from its battery until it returns to sunlight).

Section 8 — Monte Carlo: Estimating the Unknowable

P8-1. Runs needed for a target accuracy. The governing rule is SE ∝ 1/√N, so the standard error falls only as the square root of the trial count. Given N1 = 500 trials at SE1 = 6 W, target SE2 = 1.5 W.
(a) Required shrink in SE:  SE1/SE2 = 6 W / 1.5 W = 4× (the W units cancel — a ratio of errors is dimensionless).
(b) Required growth in N:  Since SE ∝ 1/√N, cutting SE by 4× means raising √N by 4×, i.e. raising N by 4² = 16×.
(c) Total trials:  N2 = N1·(SE1/SE2)² = 500 · (6/1.5)² = 500 · 16 = 8000 trials.
(d) Why "slow":  Because error falls as 1/√N, accuracy is bought at a squared price — here a mere 4× improvement in error demanded 16× the work, and in general 10× more accuracy costs 100× more runs. That punishing exchange rate is Monte Carlo's defining weakness.
P8-2. Estimate π from a stated hit count. Darts uniform in a 1×1 square with an inscribed quarter-circle of radius 1; 1562 of 2000 land inside the arc.
(a) Formula and the factor of 4:  π ≈ 4 × (inside / total). The 4 appears because the inside-fraction approximates (area of quarter-circle)/(area of square) = (π/4)/1 = π/4; solving that for π multiplies by 4. (A quarter-circle is one-quarter of a full circle, hence the π/4, hence the ×4 to undo it.)
(b) Compute the estimate:  inside/total = 1562/2000 = 0.781, so π ≈ 4 × 0.781 = 3.124.
(c) Compare with the truth:  true π = 3.14159, so the error is |3.124 − 3.14159| ≈ 0.018 — close, from 2000 random darts and no calculus. (For N = 2000, 1/√N ≈ 0.022, and the error sits comfortably within that scale.)
(d) Halving the error:  Since the error scales as 1/√N, to roughly halve it you must quadruple the darts (√4 = 2): throw about 4 × 2000 = 8000 darts. Each halving of error costs four times the work — the 1/√N rule again.
P8-3. Why 2N enumeration is hopeless for 200 cells.
(a) Number of patterns:  Each of the 200 cells is independently OK or failed (2 states), and the states multiply, giving 2200 distinct failure patterns.
(b) Why simulating them all is impossible:  2200 ≈ 1.6 × 1060 — a 1 followed by sixty zeros, vastly more than the number of atoms in the Earth. And each single pattern is not free: it requires a full coupled electro-thermal solve (Section 7), the iterative per-cell temperature/power calculation, which costs real compute time. Multiplying ~1060 patterns by the cost of one solve gives a total that no computer could finish in any feasible time — the age of the universe is roughly 1017 seconds, so even a trillion solves per second would not make a dent. Enumeration is therefore flatly impossible.
(c) What PowerPy does instead, and which mode:  It uses Monte Carlo — sample a manageable subset of failure patterns at random, simulate each, and infer the statistical picture from the sample rather than the full 2200. To obtain the full distribution of power loss you would use the RANDOM sampling mode (mode 3): many random failure patterns, accumulated into a histogram of total power loss. (The position sweep finds the single worst spot; the count sweep charts decline versus number of failures; only random sampling delivers the full distribution.)
