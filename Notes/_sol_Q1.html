Q1-1. A line with cells_per_line = 4, cells of Vmp = 2.35 V and Imp = 0.48 A; then a module of lines_parallel = 2 such lines.

(a) Series or parallel? A line is a series group — cells_per_line cells wired end-to-end. (Series is the line's default combine; its job is to build voltage.)

(b) The line's max-power voltage and current. In series, voltages add and the current is common to all cells in the chain.
Vmp,line = 4 × 2.35 V = 9.4 V.   Imp,line = 0.48 A (unchanged — putting cells in series does not increase current; the same current flows through every cell in the chain).

(c) The module (2 lines in parallel). A module is a parallel group, so its children (the lines) all sit between the same two rails: voltage stays that of one line and currents add.
Vmp,module = 9.4 V (same as one line).   Imp,module = 2 × 0.48 A = 0.96 A.

(d) What adds where. Voltage adds in series; current adds in parallel. (And the partner quantity is the one held common: current is common down a series chain, voltage is common across a parallel rail pair.)
Sanity check: power tracks correctly either way — one line is 9.4 V × 0.48 A = 4.512 W, two such lines in parallel is 9.4 V × 0.96 A = 9.024 W = exactly twice one line, as two identical lines must give. ✓
Q1-2. Target: nominal bus ≈ 23.5 V and exactly 3 parallel current paths, with a 2.35 V (Vmp) cell and default combines.

(a) Cells in series. Voltage adds only along series links, so the number of series cells is
Nseries = Vbus / Vmp = 23.5 V / 2.35 V = 10 cells in series.

(b) cells_per_line and modules_per_block. The series levels are the line and the block, and they multiply: cells_per_line × modules_per_block = 10. Any factor pair works; the cleanest is
cells_per_line = 10, modules_per_block = 1  (10 × 1 = 10).   (Equally valid: cells_per_line = 5, modules_per_block = 2, since 5 × 2 = 10.)

(c) lines_parallel and n_blocks. The parallel levels are the module and the circuit, and they multiply to the number of paths: lines_parallel × n_blocks = 3. So
lines_parallel = 1, n_blocks = 3  (1 × 3 = 3).   (Equally valid: lines_parallel = 3, n_blocks = 1.)

(d) Total cells. Multiply all four counts:
With (10, 1, 1, 3): cells_per_line × lines_parallel × modules_per_block × n_blocks = 10 × 1 × 1 × 3 = 30 cells.
Cross-check: series-depth × parallel-width = 10 × 3 = 30 = total cells. The two routes agree, so the choice is consistent. (If you picked 5×2 and 3×1 instead, that is 5×2×3×1 = 30 as well — same total, as it must be: 10 series × 3 parallel = 30 either way.) ✓
Q1-3. (a) Hierarchical id of cell 5, line 3, module 1, block 12. The id is the path from the root down to the cell, segment by segment — block, module, line, cell — joined with dots:
B12.M1.L3.C5.
(Note the order is always block → module → line → cell, regardless of the order the question names them in. Here block 12 → B12, module 1 → M1, line 3 → L3, cell 5 → C5.)

(b) Modules wired side by side — which parameter, to what value? "Side by side, currents add, block voltage stays that of one module" is the definition of a parallel combination. The block's combine rule is a parameter, so change the block level's combine from its default "series" to "parallel" (passed via combine_levels on Circuit.fromPrototype). Nothing else changes.

(c) Why one value here but a rewrite in Chapter 1. In Circuit the series/parallel choice at every level is a stored parameter (combine), so re-reading the wiring is a single value flip; in the fixed Chapter-1 hierarchy the rules "string = series, section = parallel" are hard-coded into the classes, so changing one would mean rewriting the code, not editing a setting.
Consequence check: after the flip, a block's voltage drops to one module's voltage while its current multiplies by modules_per_block — so the same hardware now reads as a lower-voltage, higher-current bus, exactly the alternative interpretation the review proposed. ✓
