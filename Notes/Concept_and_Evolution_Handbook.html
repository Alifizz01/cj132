Part I

The Problem, the Framework, the Pipeline

Why a satellite cares about one failed solar cell, and how the framework turns that worry into numbers.

1.1The mission, in one paragraph

A satellite runs on electricity it makes from sunlight with thousands of solar cells on rigid panels. Those panels are an aluminium honeycomb sandwich — light and stiff. Over years in orbit a cell can fail (radiation, micro-impacts, a cracked interconnect). When it does, the still-healthy cells wired around it drive current backwards through the dead one. Instead of making power, that cell now absorbs power and turns it into heat, concentrated in a thumbnail-sized spot, with no air to carry it away. The spot can get hot enough to soften the adhesive and the honeycomb — a hot-spot. There is no repair in orbit, so the only place to deal with it is on the ground, before launch, by predicting which failures and which panel layouts are dangerous. That prediction is what this framework computes.

The one question the framework answers For a given panel layout and a given failure, what temperature does each cell reach, and does any cell exceed the melt limit? Everything else — the circuit model, the thermal solver, the coupling loop, the Monte-Carlo sweep, the report — exists to answer that question accurately and quickly.

1.2The pipeline (the shape of the whole calculation)

The calculation flows through five conceptual layers. Each consumes the previous layer's output; this is also exactly how the code is organised (Part XII).

In words: CONFIG says what the panel is made of and where the cells sit; MODEL builds the electrical circuit and the orbit heat-loads; SOLVE finds the self-consistent temperatures; ANALYSIS decides what is destroyed and runs many failure scenarios; REPORT turns the result into a colour heat-map a human reads and a data file a machine re-reads.

1.3Three numbers that anchor everything

The same cell, same sunlight, three states — the whole project lives between these:

Working (delivering power)+1.1 W out39 °Cexporting energy cools it

Previous
The legacy code answered "what is the array's power?" for healthy panels — an IV/power tool. Failure heating was not its purpose; a hot-spot was at best a side note.
Now

Flaw: the old purpose couldn't tell you whether a given failure/layout would melt the panel — the actual engineering risk.
Benefit: the new purpose predicts that risk directly, before launch, for any layout.
Part II

Electrical Foundations & the Circuit Model

Volts, amps, watts; series and parallel; reverse bias; and how the framework builds an arbitrary array as a tree of cells.

2.1The three quantities

Electricity is described by three numbers. Voltage V (volts) is the push — like water pressure. Current I (amps) is the flow — litres per second past a point. Power P (watts) is work per second, and it is simply the product:

P = V · I
P in watts (W); V in volts (V); I in amps (A). 1 W = 1 joule of energy per second.

The sign of P is the whole story of this project. With our convention that a cell delivering power has positive P, a cell forced to absorb power has negative P — and absorbed electrical energy that is neither stored nor radiated as light becomes heat.

Worked — giver vs heater
Healthy: V = +2.3 V, I = 0.48 A → P = +1.10 W. Positive ⇒ the cell gives 1.1 W.
Reverse-biased: the line forces V = −20 V across it while 0.48 A still flows → P = −9.6 W.
Negative ⇒ the cell absorbs 9.6 W. Energy in with nowhere to go ⇒ heat. This is the hot-spot source.

2.2Series and parallel

One cell (~2.3–2.6 V) is too weak, so cells are combined two ways. Series: cells in a line; the same current flows through all and their voltages add. Parallel: cells side-by-side between the same two rails; the voltage is shared and their currents add.

Series:  Vtotal = Σ Vi,  I common   |   Parallel:  Itotal = Σ Ii,  V common

2.3Why one dead cell takes the whole line's voltage

In a series line of, say, ten cells there is only one current path, so one current flows through every cell. If cell #7 dies and stops pushing, the nine live cells still drive that current through #7 — the wrong way. With the dead cell as the only "gap", almost the entire stacked push of the live cells (≈9 × 2.3 ≈ 21 V) lands across it, as a negative (reverse) voltage of about −20 V. Times the current still flowing, that is the −9.6 W of §2.1. More cells in a series line ⇒ more reverse voltage available ⇒ a worse hot-spot — a design lever the framework can explore.

Ladder · from "same current" to "−20 V"
Q1. In a series line, how does the current through cell #1 compare with cell #7?
Identical — one path, one current.
Q2. #7 dies but the other nine still push. Where does their current go?
Through #7, backwards — it is the only path. #7 is now reverse-biased.
Q3. Roughly what voltage lands across #7?
Almost the whole live stack, ≈ −20 V — a small cell with the whole line's push dumped across it the wrong way.

2.4The Circuit model — an array as a tree

Real arrays nest four levels: cells in a line (series), lines in a module (parallel), modules in a block (series), blocks in the array (parallel). The framework represents this as a tree of groups, each group tagged series or parallel, with cells at the leaves. Every cell gets a stable id B{b}.M{m}.L{l}.C{c} and is reachable in O(1). Because series/parallel is a parameter at every level, a different reading of the wiring is a one-value change, not a rewrite.

From tree to SPICE netlist A recursive walk assigns nodes (series chains head-to-tail; parallel children between the same rails) and emits each cell's subcircuit once, producing an ngspice netlist. A second method, build_probed_netlist, additionally inserts a 0-V ammeter in series with every cell and returns a map {id: (node+, node−, ammeter)} — so after a SPICE solve each cell's V and I (hence P = V·I) can be read back. That is what wires the electrical solve into the thermal coupling (Part VI).
Previous
Topology was effectively hard-wired in procedural code (panel/section/string objects); changing the wiring or injecting a single-cell fault meant editing logic, and cells were not individually addressable for a failure study.
Now
One parametric Circuit tree: counts and series/parallel are arguments; every cell has an id; fail(id, mode) injects a local fault; netlist build is automatic.
Flaw: you couldn't sweep layouts or fail an arbitrary cell without code changes — fatal for a Monte-Carlo failure study.
Benefit: layouts and faults are data, so thousands of scenarios run with no code edits.
Part III

The Thermal Model: the Two-Node Energy Balance

Heat in equals heat out. The Stefan–Boltzmann T⁴ glow, conduction between the two faces, and the equations whose solution is the cell temperature.

3.1Steady state = a balanced bathtub

A cell in orbit is a bathtub: heat pours in (sunlight, plus reverse-bias heat if it has failed), heat drains out (the cell glows infrared to space). The temperature is the water level; it rises or falls until the tap rate equals the drain rate, then holds. That holding value — where heat-in = heat-out — is the balance temperature we want.

Why "node" A node is just a point we track one temperature at. The model uses two: the sunlit front (T₁) and the shaded rear (T₂), joined by conduction through the cell. Two is the simplest model richer than a single lump and captures the real front-warmer-than-rear effect.

3.2Heat in

Qfront = αF · A · Psun · tilt   (− Pelec if the cell carries electrical power)
αF = front absorptivity (fraction of sun soaked up); A = cell area; Psun = solar flux (≈1367 W/m² at Earth); tilt = cosine pointing factor. Pelec > 0 removes heat (export); Pelec < 0 adds heat (reverse bias).
Qrear = (αR · Palbedo + εR · PIR) · A · tilt
the rear faces the planet: it absorbs reflected sunlight (albedo) and the planet's own infrared glow. Part VIII derives these fluxes.

3.3Heat out — the Stefan–Boltzmann T⁴ law

A warm surface radiates heat as infrared light at a rate proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature:

Qout = ε · A · σ · (T⁴ − Tspace⁴)
ε = emissivity (how good a glower the surface is, 0–1); σ = 5.670367×10⁻⁸ W/m²K⁴ (a constant of nature); T in kelvin (K = °C + 273.15); Tspace = 2.7 K (deep space, usually negligible).
Why kelvin, and why the 4th power matters The law counts heat from absolute zero, so it must use kelvin (Celsius' zero is arbitrary). The 4th power means doubling the absolute temperature multiplies the glow 16-fold. Practically: the drain ramps up so fast with temperature that heat-in is always matched at a finite temperature — the system can't run away.

3.4The two coupled balance equations

Writing "in = out" at each face, with a conduction term C·A·(T₂−T₁) carrying heat from the hotter face to the cooler one (C = conductivity ÷ thickness):

f₁ = Qfront − εF·A·σ·(T₁⁴ − Tsp⁴) − Pelec + C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0
f₂ = Qrear − εR·A·σ·(T₂⁴ − Tsp⁴) − C·A·(T₂ − T₁) = 0

These are two equations in two unknowns (T₁, T₂), tangled together by the shared conduction term and each carrying a T⁴. There is no algebraic "T = …" — they must be solved numerically (Part IV).

3.5The lumped shortcut (and why it is close)

For hand-work, note C·A is large here (1000 × 0.003 = 3 W/K), so the two faces are dragged to nearly the same temperature (they differ by only ~0.66 °C). Treating the cell as one temperature glowing from both faces:

Qin = k · T⁴,   k = (εF + εR)·A·σ  ⇒  T = (Qin / k)1/4
k = (0.90+0.89)×0.003×5.670367×10⁻⁸ = 3.045×10⁻¹⁰ W/K⁴.
Worked — the three anchor temperatures, by hand
Sun absorbed: Q = αF·A·Psun = 0.97×0.003×1367 = 3.978 W.
Idle (Pelec=0): T⁴ = 3.978/k = 1.306×10¹⁰ → T = 338.1 K = 64.9 °C (code two-node: 65.26).
Working (+1.1 W out): T⁴ = (3.978−1.1)/k → T = 311.8 K = 38.7 °C (code: 38.89).
Failed (−9.6 W): T⁴ = (3.978+9.6)/k → T = 459.5 K = 186.4 °C (code: 187.48).
The lumped values sit within ~1 °C of the exact two-node solver — the gap is precisely the small front/rear split.
Previous
The legacy thermal routine (thermal.py) solved this two-node balance for a single cell with scipy.fsolve, and an OCR slip in the rear-emission term (a stray T-index) had been flagged.
Now
The same physics, re-derived and re-implemented cleanly in solve/thermal.py, with the rear face correctly emitting at T₂, verified against a hand-worked oracle (65.26 / 64.60 °C) in the test suite.
Flaw: a single-cell, fsolve-per-call routine with a transcription error — slow at scale and unverified.
Benefit: one audited solver, oracle-tested, and (Parts IV–VII) vectorised over the whole array.
Part IV

Solving It: Newton–Raphson

When T sits on both sides of the equation you can't isolate it — you search. Newton uses the slope to leap to the answer in a handful of steps.

4.1Why a search is unavoidable

The balance is Qin = k·T⁴ in the lumped case (root-able), but the real two-node form has T in two places — inside T⁴ and inside the linear conduction term, tangled with the other unknown. A 4th-root undoes a pure T⁴; it cannot undo a·T⁴ + b·T. With no way to isolate T, we search: guess, measure how wrong, correct, repeat.

4.2The method

Define the residual (the "wrongness") and its slope:

f(T) = heat-in − heat-out  (want 0)   |   next T = T − f(T) / f′(T)
f′ is the derivative (slope). Dividing the error by the slope sizes the step: steep slope → small step, shallow slope → big step.

For our glow balance, f(T) = Qin − k·T⁴ and f′(T) = −4·k·T³ (the steepness of T⁴ is 4T³ — the exponent drops to the front, the power falls by one). The imbalance f is a compass: positive ⇒ too cold (heat-in wins) ⇒ push warmer; negative ⇒ too hot ⇒ push cooler; zero ⇒ balanced ⇒ the answer.

4.3Two unknowns: the 2×2 Jacobian

With two unknowns (T₁, T₂) there are two residuals (f₁, f₂) and the single slope becomes four — each residual can change when you move either temperature. That 2×2 table of slopes is the Jacobian:

J = [ a  b ; c  d ],   a = −4εFAσ T₁³ − CA,   b = c = +CA,   d = −4εRAσ T₂³ − CA
a, d = each face's own glow-slope plus conduction; b, c = the conduction link tying the faces. The off-diagonals exist only because the faces are coupled.

The Newton step solves this little system. For one cell it inverts in closed form (det = ad − bc):

det = a·d − b·c;   ΔT₁ = −(d·f₁ − b·f₂)/det;   ΔT₂ = −(−c·f₁ + a·f₂)/det
Worked — one real two-node Newton step (idle cell from 28 °C)
f₁ = +2.719 W, f₂ = −1.245 W; a = −3.017, b = c = 3.0, d = −3.017; det = 9.100 − 9.000 = 0.100.
ΔT₁ = −[(−3.017)(2.719) − (3.0)(−1.245)]/0.100 = +44.6; ΔT₂ = +44.0.
From 28 °C the faces jump to ~72.6/72.0 °C, then a few more identical steps settle to 65.26/64.60 °C.

Now

Benefit: one vectorised Newton over all cells; ~5–7 iterations regardless of N.
Part V

Vectorisation: Why & How the Solver Is Fast

Doing the same arithmetic on the whole array at once, instead of one cell at a time in a Python loop — same answers, far faster.

5.1What "vector" means

A vector is a row of numbers handled as one object. Vectorising is applying the same operation to the whole row in a single compiled step instead of looping element by element:

[10, 20, 30] + [1, 2, 3] = [11, 22, 33]   — one step, not three

In the solver every per-cell quantity is a row: front temperatures T₁ = [t₁, t₂, …], heat-in, the residuals, the corrections. One line T1 = T1 + dT1 updates a thousand cells at once.

5.2Why it is faster (it is not the arithmetic)

A Python loop pays a large hidden tax per element: type checks, object boxing, bytecode dispatch, bounds checks. That bookkeeping dwarfs the actual add and is paid N times. A vectorised numpy operation hands the whole row to pre-compiled C that pays the bookkeeping once; on top of that the CPU's SIMD units add a small batch (≈8 numbers) in one instruction. The dominant win is the removed overhead.

Why independence permits it (the block-diagonal Jacobian) With no lateral conduction, each cell's two equations involve only that cell's two temperatures, so the global Jacobian is block-diagonal — N independent 2×2 blocks. Each block is inverted by the closed-form det-formula (§4.3), and because the blocks don't interact we compute "all the a's", "all the b's", … as rows and invert them together. Independence is exactly what turns the per-cell 2×2 into vectorised row arithmetic.

5.3The one Newton round, vectorised

Per iteration the whole array is processed in a few row operations (a, b, c, d, det, ΔT₁, ΔT₂ are all length-N), then T1 += dT1; T2 += dT2. No Python loop over cells, ever.

Previous
A Python for-loop walked the cells one at a time, each calling scipy.fsolve — N slow solver invocations per thermal evaluation, and that nested inside the Monte-Carlo loop.
Now
One vectorised Newton over numpy arrays solves all cells simultaneously; the closed-form 2×2 inverse is applied to the whole array in a handful of iterations.
Flaw: O(N) Python-level solver calls per evaluation made large arrays × many MC runs impractically slow.
Benefit: the per-cell loop is gone; the arithmetic is identical, the wall-clock is 10–100× lower.
Part VI

Electro-Thermal Coupling: the Bounce

A cell's electricity depends on its temperature and vice-versa. Neither is known first, so we iterate them to agreement.

6.1The chicken-and-egg

A solar cell's IV curve shifts with temperature (a hot cell converts a little less), so the electrical power it extracts/dissipates depends on its temperature — which depends on that power. Two unknowns, each waiting on the other. We break the deadlock by assuming one: pick a temperature, compute the electricity (now an ordinary calculation), feed the resulting heat into the thermal solve, get a new temperature, and repeat until the number stops moving — a fixed point where both agree.

guess T → power_fn(T) → Pelec → thermal solve → Tnew;   T ← T + ω·(Tnew − T)  until |ΔT| < tol
ω = under-relaxation (damping) factor, 0<ω≤1; moving only part-way each round prevents overshoot/oscillation in stiff cases.
Worked — the bounce climbing to a self-consistent answer
Start 28 °C → at 28 °C the dissipation gives a heat that solves to ~168 °C → damped half-step → 98 °C.
98 °C → ~181 °C → damped → 140 °C → … → settles at 198.8 °C (put-in equals come-out).
When the temperature returns unchanged, electricity and temperature finally agree — that is the answer.
Previous
Coupling lived implicitly inside the panel's equilibrium routine; the electrical operating point and the temperature were entangled in one procedure, hard to test or reuse.
Now
An explicit damped fixed-point loop, couple(power_fn, …), with the circuit solve injected as power_fn(T) — pure, testable, and reusable. The real ngspice power_fn is wired (Part II) and validated with a mock.
Flaw: the coupling was not isolated, so its convergence/damping couldn't be tested or swapped independently.
Benefit: a tested loop with explicit damping; swapping mock→real ngspice is a one-argument change.
Part VII

Lateral Conduction & the Sparse Jacobian

Letting heat spread sideways to neighbours turns N independent problems into one coupled system — solved with a sparse Newton whose constant structure is built once.

7.1From sealed islands to a connected sheet

The base model gives each cell front/rear conduction but no sideways path — sealed islands. Real panels conduct heat in-plane through the facesheets, so a hot cell bleeds heat to its neighbours. Adding a lateral conductance glat between adjacent tiles couples them: the cells are no longer independent.

f₁,ᵢ = … + Σj∈neighbours(i) glat·(T₁,ⱼ − T₁,ᵢ)  (and likewise for the rear)
glat = kfacesheet·t·(edge/pitch) [W/K]; the neighbour set comes straight from the grid layout (Part X).

7.2One big sparse system instead of N little ones

Coupling fills in the previously-empty off-diagonal blocks: the global Jacobian goes from block-diagonal (separate 2×2s) to a sparse 2N×2N matrix where each tile links to its ~4 neighbours. There is no tiny closed-form inverse anymore; we assemble the sparse matrix and solve it (scipy.sparse.linalg.spsolve) each Newton step. Crucially, glat=0 reproduces the independent solver exactly — the lateral model is a strict superset (verified: 65.26 °C).

7.3The vectorised constant-pattern Jacobian (the efficiency win)

Across Newton iterations only the radiative diagonal (−4εAσT³) changes; the conduction and lateral terms are constant. So the constant structure — the front↔rear blocks plus glat·(graph-Laplacian) — is assembled once, and each iteration merely adds the radiative diagonal. The lateral flux itself is a single sparse mat-vec glat·(Lap·T), not a Python loop over neighbour pairs.

J = Jconst + diag(−4εAσT³)  (rebuilt structure: never; diagonal: each step)
Measured A 40×40 = 1600-tile panel (3200 unknowns) with lateral conduction now solves in 0.046 s / 5 iterations. The earlier version rebuilt the whole sparse matrix from Python lists every iteration (~6,400 appends per step) — the new way pays that structural cost once.
Previous
(a) No lateral conduction at all → hot-spots piled up unrealistically (over-conservative). (b) When first added, the sparse Jacobian was rebuilt with Python loops every Newton iteration.
Now
Optional lateral conduction (glat=0 = baseline), with the constant structure assembled once and only the radiative diagonal updated per step; flux via a Laplacian mat-vec.
Flaw: sealed-island physics overstated peaks; the first lateral implementation re-assembled the matrix wastefully each step.
Benefit: realistic heat-spreading (187→~87 °C) and a fast, loop-free assembly (1600 tiles in 0.046 s).
Part VIII

The Orbit Environment: Sun, Eclipse, Albedo, IR

The "heat in" is not only direct sun. Three flux streams arrive, scaled by pointing angle, and they vary around the orbit.

8.1The three streams

A panel in orbit feels: direct sun on the front; albedo — sunlight reflected off the planet — on the rear; and planetary infrared — the planet's own warm glow — also on the rear.

Direct sun: Psun = AM0 / d²,   0 in eclipse  (AM0 = 1367 W/m² at 1 AU; d in AU)
Albedo: Palb = a · Psun@planet · view  (a = bond albedo, Earth ≈ 0.3)
Planetary IR: PIR = ε · σ · Tplanet⁴ · view  (the same σT⁴ glow law, now of the planet)
Pointing: tilt = cos(α)·cos(β)  (face-on = 1; edge-on = 0)
Worked — the fluxes
Sun at Mars (1.52 AU): 1367/1.52² = 591.7 W/m².
Albedo: 0.25 × 591 × 0.3 → 44.3 W/m² (the "0.25" is a position view factor).
Earth IR at 255 K: 1×σ×255⁴×0.3 = 71.9 W/m². Tilt 30°: cos30 = 0.866.
Eclipse Passing into the planet's shadow, direct sun and albedo drop to zero, but planetary IR keeps glowing — so a cell cools fast (thermal cycling) yet never to absolute cold. Over an orbit the heat-in swings, so the temperatures swing; feeding the solver this time-varying load is the orbit path.
Previous
eclipse.py computed fluxes via poliastro/astropy — not importable in this environment — and its albedo function was flagged erroneous.
Now
model/environment.py: pure, tested flux math (inverse-square sun, view-factor albedo/IR, cosine tilt), with the orbit propagation behind a guarded hapsira seam (the maintained poliastro fork).
Flaw: a broken dependency chain and a flagged-wrong albedo term made the flux path unusable.
Benefit: the flux equations are standalone and tested; only the concrete orbit remains to be plugged in.
Part IX

Failure Analysis: Breakdown & Monte-Carlo

Deciding what counts as destroyed, and sampling thousands of failures to find the dangerous ones.

9.1Breakdown criteria

A cell is flagged destroyed if either of two thresholds trips — temperature OR reverse power:

destroyed = (Tfront ≥ Tlimit)  OR  (V < 0 AND |V·I| ≥ Prev-limit)
temperature captures the steady thermal outcome; reverse power captures violent electrical stress that can crack a cell before it fully heats. ORing them catches failures either alone would miss.
Worked
Idle cell (65.26 °C, V=+2.3, I=0.48): 65 ≥ 150? no. V>0 → reverse power 0. Survives.
Hot-spot (187.48 °C, V=−20, I=0.48): 187 ≥ 150? yes. |−20·0.48|=9.6 ≥ 2? yes. Destroyed (both).

9.2Monte-Carlo over failures

The number of possible failure combinations is astronomically large, so we sample: draw many random failure patterns, solve each, record the worst temperature, and rank. Three modes — position (fail each single cell in turn), count (fail k random cells), and random (each cell fails with a probability) — answer "where", "how many", and "realistically".

standard error ∝ 1/√N  ⇒  runs needed = (σ·z / target)²  (4× tighter ⇒ 16× the runs)
e.g. an 8 °C wobble from 100 runs → to reach 2 °C needs 1600 runs.

9.3The grid failure study (with spreading)

Tying it together: panel_study sets a per-tile power map (failed cells driven into reverse bias, healthy cells exporting, bare tiles zero), solves the panel with lateral conduction, records peak temperature and tiles-over-limit, and ranks the patterns. Because spreading is included, it answers the real question — which failure positions are most damaging, and which neighbours get cooked.

Previous
No failure analysis: no explicit breakdown criteria and no mechanism to sweep failures — the legacy tool wasn't built to ask "which failure is worst?".
Now
Explicit dual breakdown criteria; a three-mode Monte-Carlo with run-count math; and a grid study that ranks failure positions accounting for lateral spreading.
Flaw: the central engineering question (which failures/layouts are dangerous) simply couldn't be posed.
Benefit: dangerous failures and layouts are found and ranked automatically, with quantified statistical confidence.
Part X

The Layout Convention

A panel as a grid of tiles keyed to a palette — asymmetry, empty regions and the conduction adjacency, all in readable text.

10.1Grid-map + palette

A panel is a 2-D grid of single-character tiles; each character is defined once in a palette carrying that tile's properties (is it a cell, its optical α/ε, which electrical string, the pitch). The grid is the picture; the palette is the legend.

palette: A = cell (α=.97, ε=.90, string S1) . = bare substrate D = bypass diode layout: A A A A A A . . A A A A A A . . A A . . A A A A pitch 55 mm A A . . A A A A D A A A A A A D . . A A A A . .

10.2Why bare regions run hotter

A working cell converts ~28% of absorbed sun to electricity that leaves, so it runs cooler. A bare tile (no cell) converts 0% — all absorbed sun stays as heat, and it carries no electrical power (forced Pelec=0). So bare patches balance hotter (≈61 °C vs ≈39 °C for powered cells in the demo), and with lateral conduction they warm their neighbours. The grid makes this first-class and gives the 4-neighbour adjacency the lateral solver needs for free.

Appendix A

Worked Numerical Walkthroughs

A.0How to read this appendix

This appendix exists for one reason: to prove that nothing the solver prints is magic. Every temperature, every survival verdict, every Monte-Carlo run count that appears elsewhere in this handbook can be reproduced with a pencil, a calculator, and patience. We do exactly that here. Each section takes one result, states the equation it comes from, substitutes the verified constants for the msro_case2 substrate, and grinds through the arithmetic one line at a time. At the end of each derivation we compare our by-hand answer against the number the two-node code actually prints, so you can see precisely how much accuracy the hand-simplification costs.

A word on the modelling choice that runs through everything. A real solar-array cell has two faces — a front face that sees the Sun and a rear face that radiates to deep space — and the two faces are at slightly different temperatures because heat has to conduct through the substrate to get from one to the other. The full code therefore solves a two-node problem: one temperature for the front, one for the rear, coupled by conduction. But for hand work that is awkward, so most of our walkthroughs use a lumped single-temperature approximation in which we pretend the whole cell sits at one temperature and radiates from both faces at once. The lumped answer is always a little hotter on the front and a little cooler on the rear than the true two-node split, and the gap is typically a fraction of a degree. Seeing that gap shrink is half the point.

Constants used throughout Substrate msro_case2: front absorptivity αfront=0.97, rear absorptivity αrear=0.93, front emissivity εfront=0.90, rear emissivity εrear=0.89, conductivity kc=10 W/mK, thickness t=0.01 m. Cell area A=0.003 m². Stefan–Boltzmann σ=5.670367e-8 W/m²K⁴. Deep-space temperature Tspace=2.7 K. Solar constant at 1 AU Psun=1367 W/m². Kelvin = °C + 273.15.

A.1The master equation and computing the radiation constant k

Everything starts from an energy balance. In steady state, whatever heat goes into the cell must come back out, otherwise the temperature would keep changing. Heat comes in from the absorbed sunlight and possibly from electrical dissipation; heat leaves only by thermal radiation, because in orbit there is no air to carry it away by convection and the only conductive path (the mounting) we fold into the substrate model. So the balance, lumped to a single temperature T, reads:

Qin = k · (T⁴ − Tspace⁴)
Qin = net heat into the cell (W); k = lumped radiation constant (W/K⁴); T = cell temperature (K); Tspace = 2.7 K deep-space sink

Because Tspace = 2.7 K, the term Tspace⁴ = 2.7⁴ ≈ 53.1 K⁴ is utterly negligible next to a cell at hundreds of kelvin (whose T⁴ is in the billions), so for hand work we drop it and write the simpler form that we will solve over and over:

T = ( Qin / k )1/4
the lumped-by-hand temperature; solve for T once Qin and k are known

Now we need k. Radiation leaves from both faces, each with its own emissivity, over the same cell area A, scaled by σ. So the lumped radiation constant is the sum of the two faces' emissivities times A times σ:

klump = (εfront + εrear) · A · σ
εfront=0.90, εrear=0.89, A=0.003 m², σ=5.670367e-8 W/m²K⁴
Worked — the radiation constant k_lump
Step 1 — add the emissivities: εfront + εrear = 0.90 + 0.89 = 1.79.
Step 2 — multiply by area: 1.79 × 0.003 = 0.00537 m².
Step 3 — multiply by σ: 0.00537 × 5.670367e-8. First 5.37e-3 × 5.670367e-8 = (5.37 × 5.670367) × 10-11 = 30.450 × 10-11 = 3.0450e-10 W/K⁴.
Check: klump = 3.0450e-10 W/K⁴. This single number sets the entire temperature scale for the cell — every lumped temperature in this appendix is (Q/3.0450e-10)1/4.

We also need the conductive coupling between the two faces, for the two-node work later. Conductance per unit area is conductivity divided by thickness, then times area:

ccond = kc / t   CA = ccond · A
kc=10 W/mK, t=0.01 m, A=0.003 m²
Worked — conduction coupling CA
Step 1 — per-area conductance: ccond = 10 / 0.01 = 1000 W/m²K.
Step 2 — multiply by area: CA = 1000 × 0.003 = 3.0 W/K.
Check: CA = 3.0 W/K. So every 1 K of front-to-rear temperature difference drives 3.0 W of conducted heat between the faces. We will see this exact 3.0 sitting on the off-diagonal of the two-node Jacobian in A.6.

Finally, the absorbed sunlight. The front face absorbs a fraction αfront of the incident solar flux over the cell area:

qfront = αfront · A · Psun
αfront=0.97, A=0.003 m², Psun=1367 W/m²
Worked — absorbed solar power
Step 1 — flux on the cell: A · Psun = 0.003 × 1367 = 4.101 W.
Step 2 — absorbed fraction: 0.97 × 4.101 = 3.978 W (0.97 × 4.101 = 4.101 − 0.03×4.101 = 4.101 − 0.12303 = 3.97797 ≈ 3.978).
Check: qfront = 3.978 W. This is the heat input for an idle cell — the engine that drives the whole thermal problem.

A.2The idle cell — no electrical load

The simplest case. The cell sits in sunlight doing no electrical work, so all the absorbed sunlight must be radiated away. The net heat input is just the absorbed solar power computed in A.1, Qin = qfront = 3.978 W, with no electrical term. We solve the master equation for T.

Worked — idle cell temperature
Step 1 — heat input: Qin = 3.978 W (nothing extracted, nothing dissipated).
Step 2 — divide by k: T⁴ = Qin / klump = 3.978 / 3.0450e-10. Compute 3.978 / 3.0450 = 1.3064, so T⁴ = 1.3064e10 K⁴ (≈ 1.306e10).
Step 3 — fourth root. Take the square root twice. √(1.306e10) = √1.306 × √1e10 = 1.1428 × 1e5 = 1.1428e5. Then √(1.1428e5) = √1.1428 × √1e5 = 1.0690 × 316.23 = 338.1. So T = 338.1 K.
Step 4 — to Celsius: 338.1 − 273.15 = 64.9 °C.
Check vs code: the two-node solver prints 65.26 °C front / 64.60 °C rear. Our lumped 64.9 °C lands neatly between the two faces, as expected — the lump is an average of front and rear. Agreement is within about a third of a degree on either side. The lumped model is excellent for the idle case.

Why does the lump sit between the two faces? Because conduction is very strong here (CA = 3.0 W/K) compared with the tiny radiative imbalance, so the front and rear stay within about 0.66 K of each other. When the two faces are nearly equal, their average is a faithful single-temperature stand-in. Cases where the faces split further apart — like the reverse-biased hot-spot — are where the lump starts to drift, and we will watch for that.

A.3The working cell — extracting electrical power (efficiency ≈ 28%)

When the cell actually does its job and feeds current into the bus, it converts some of the absorbed sunlight into electrical energy that leaves as wires-out rather than as heat. That extracted power is subtracted from the heat budget, so the cell runs cooler than idle. For this substrate the array extracts about 1.1 W at its operating point. Note the efficiency implied: 1.1 W extracted from 3.978 W absorbed is 1.1 / 3.978 = 0.2765, i.e. roughly 28% — a believable triple-junction cell number, which is a good sanity check that the +1.1 W figure is physically reasonable.

Qin = qfront − Pelec
Pelec = +1.1 W extracted (heat leaving as electricity)
Worked — working cell temperature
Step 1 — heat input: Qin = 3.978 − 1.1 = 2.878 W.
Step 2 — divide by k: T⁴ = 2.878 / 3.0450e-10. Compute 2.878 / 3.0450 = 0.94517, so T⁴ = 9.4517e9 K⁴.
Step 3 — fourth root. √(9.4517e9) = √9.4517 × √1e9 = 3.0744 × 31623 = 9.7213e4. Then √(9.7213e4) = √9.7213 × √1e4 = 3.1179 × 100 = 311.8 K.
Step 4 — to Celsius: 311.8 − 273.15 = 38.7 °C.
Check vs code: the two-node solver prints 38.89 °C. Our lumped 38.7 °C is within 0.2 °C. Extracting 1.1 W has dropped the cell from ≈65 °C (idle) to ≈39 °C — a 26 °C cooling — which makes intuitive sense: removing energy means less has to be radiated, and since radiated power scales as T⁴, even a modest cut in heat input pulls the temperature down noticeably.

For completeness, consider a more aggressive extraction of +2.0 W (a different operating point, e.g. nearer maximum power). Then Qin = 3.978 − 2.0 = 1.978 W, T⁴ = 1.978 / 3.0450e-10 = 6.496e9, and the fourth root gives 283.9 K = 10.7 °C. The code prints 10.91 °C for this case — again within about 0.2 °C. The pattern holds: the more electrical power you pull out, the colder the cell, and the lumped hand answer tracks the code faithfully whenever the two faces stay close together.

A.4The reverse-biased hot-spot — when a cell becomes a heater

Now the dangerous case. If a cell in a series string is shaded or fails, the healthy cells around it force current backwards through it. Instead of generating power, the bad cell dissipates it — it becomes a resistor heater buried in the array. Here the electrical term is negative (Pelec = −9.6 W), meaning electrical power is being dumped into the cell as heat. That heat adds to the absorbed sunlight, so the cell runs alarmingly hot.

Worked — reverse-biased hot-spot temperature
Step 1 — heat input: Qin = qfront − Pelec = 3.978 − (−9.6) = 3.978 + 9.6 = 13.578 W.
Step 2 — divide by k: T⁴ = 13.578 / 3.0450e-10. Compute 13.578 / 3.0450 = 4.4592, so T⁴ = 4.459e10 K⁴.
Step 3 — fourth root. √(4.459e10) = √4.459 × √1e10 = 2.1117 × 1e5 = 2.1117e5. Then √(2.1117e5) = √2.1117 × √1e5 = 1.4532 × 316.23 = 459.5 K.
Step 4 — to Celsius: 459.5 − 273.15 = 186.4 °C.
Check vs code: the two-node solver prints 187.48 °C. Our lumped 186.4 °C is about 1.1 °C low — a slightly bigger gap than the idle/working cases. That is exactly the expected behaviour: with 13.578 W to shed, the front face has to run hotter than the rear to push the extra heat through the substrate, so the two faces split further apart and the single-lump average underestimates the hottest face. Even so, the hand answer is close enough to flag the obvious problem: 186 °C is far above any safe operating limit.

Where does the −9.6 V / −9.6 W come from physically? In a series string each healthy cell produces about 2.3 V. When roughly nine live cells are connected in series and forced to drive current backward through one failed cell, the failed cell sees their combined reverse voltage: about 9 × 2.3 V ≈ −20 V. At the string current of about 0.48 A that is |−20 × 0.48| = 9.6 W of dissipation — which is precisely the +9.6 W of heat we added above. We will use that −20 V again in the breakdown check of A.9.

A.5The full Newton trace — solving T⁴ by hand, step by step

In A.2–A.4 we took fourth roots directly because the lumped equation is simple enough. But the code does not take fourth roots; it uses Newton's method, iterating from a guess toward the root of a residual function. It is worth tracing one full Newton solve by hand, both to demystify what the solver does internally and to show how an iterative root-finder behaves when it starts from a cold guess. We solve the lumped reverse-biased case (Qin = 13.578 W, true answer 186.4 °C).

Define the residual — how far the heat balance is from zero — and its derivative:

f(T) = 13.578 − k · T⁴   f′(T) = −4 · k · T³
k = klump = 3.0450e-10 W/K⁴; f = heat surplus (W); when f = 0 the cell is in balance

Newton's update is Tnext = T − f(T)/f′(T). We start cold, at 28 °C = 301.15 K, and watch it work. The very first step illustrates a hazard: when the cell is cold, T³ is small, so f′ is nearly flat, so dividing by it produces a huge step that wildly overshoots.

Worked — Newton step 0, from 28 °C (301.15 K)
Step 1 — T⁴ at 301.15 K: 301.15⁴. 301.15² = 90691, and 90691² = 8.225e9. So T⁴ = 8.225e9 K⁴.
Step 2 — heat radiated out: k · T⁴ = 3.0450e-10 × 8.225e9 = 2.505 W.
Step 3 — residual: f = 13.578 − 2.505 = +11.07 W. Positive means far too little is being radiated; the cell must heat up a lot.
Step 4 — derivative: f′ = −4 · k · T³. T³ = 301.15³ = 90691 × 301.15 = 2.732e7. So f′ = −4 × 3.0450e-10 × 2.732e7 = −4 × 8.32e-3 = −0.0333 W/K. Very small slope — the cold cell barely changes its radiation per kelvin.
Step 5 — Newton step: ΔT = −f/f′ = −(+11.07)/(−0.0333) = +332.8 K. A giant jump.
Step 6 — next guess: 301.15 + 332.8 = 634.0 K = 360.9 °C. Massive overshoot past the true 186 °C, because the flat cold slope fooled Newton into thinking it needed an enormous correction.

From there the iteration recovers and homes in fast. Once the cell is hot, T³ is large, the slope steepens, and each step roughly doubles the number of correct digits — the hallmark of Newton's quadratic convergence. Here is the whole trace:

Read the f column: it goes +11.07 → −35.63 → −8.56 → −1.16 → −0.034 → ≈0. After the initial overshoot, the magnitude of the residual shrinks roughly geometrically and then super-geometrically near the end (1.16 → 0.034 is a factor of ~34, far more than the earlier factor-of-7 steps) — that accelerating collapse is the quadratic convergence. The final 186.38 °C matches our direct fourth-root result of 186.4 °C from A.4, confirming both methods solve the same equation. The lesson for the code: a cold start can overshoot violently, which is exactly why the coupled solver in A.7 uses damping.

A.6The two-node 2×2 Newton step — solving front and rear together

The real model does not lump. It carries two unknowns, the front temperature T1 and the rear temperature T2, governed by two coupled residual equations. Front: absorbed sun, minus front radiation, minus conduction to the rear. Rear: minus rear radiation, plus conduction received from the front. Solving two coupled nonlinear equations needs a 2×2 Newton step: we build a Jacobian matrix of partial derivatives, invert it, and apply the correction. Let us do exactly one such step for the idle cell, starting both nodes at 301.15 K.

At the starting point the two residuals come out to f1 = +2.719 W (front has surplus heat — it absorbed sun and has not warmed enough to shed it) and f2 = −1.245 W (rear is slightly over-radiating relative to what it receives). The Jacobian entries are the partial derivatives of each residual with respect to each temperature: the diagonal terms combine radiative slope and conduction, the off-diagonal terms are pure conduction (the CA = 3.0 W/K we found in A.1).

J = [ a  b ; c  d ]   ΔT = −J⁻¹ · f
a = ∂f1/∂T1, b = ∂f1/∂T2, c = ∂f2/∂T1, d = ∂f2/∂T2; off-diagonals b = c = +CA = +3.0
Worked — one 2×2 Newton step, idle cell, start 301.15/301.15 K
Step 1 — assemble the Jacobian. a = d = −3.017 (each diagonal is minus the conduction coupling 3.0 minus the small radiative slope ≈0.017, giving −3.017). Off-diagonals b = c = +3.0 (the conduction term). So J = [ −3.017, 3.0 ; 3.0, −3.017 ].
Step 2 — determinant: det = a·d − b·c = (−3.017)(−3.017) − (3.0)(3.0) = 9.100 − 9.000 = 0.100. The determinant is small because the two diagonal terms nearly cancel the off-diagonals — strong conduction makes the system stiff, which is why a small det shows up.
Step 3 — solve for ΔT1. By Cramer/inverse, ΔT1 = −(d·f1 − b·f2)/det = −[(−3.017)(+2.719) − (3.0)(−1.245)] / 0.100 = −[ −8.203 + 3.735 ] / 0.100 = −(−4.468)/0.100 = +44.68 → +44.6 K.
Step 4 — solve for ΔT2. ΔT2 = −(−c·f1 + a·f2)/det = −[ −(3.0)(+2.719) + (−3.017)(−1.245) ] / 0.100 = −[ −8.157 + 3.756 ] / 0.100 = −(−4.401)/0.100 = +44.01 → +44.0 K.
Step 5 — apply the corrections. T1 = 301.15 + 44.6 = 345.75 K = 72.6 °C; T2 = 301.15 + 44.0 = 345.15 K = 72.0 °C.
Check: after this single step the front/rear sit at 72.6 / 72.0 °C — overshooting the true idle answer of 65.26 / 64.60 °C, just as the lumped Newton overshot in A.5 (a cold start over-corrects). A few more 2×2 steps settle it to 65.26 / 64.60 °C, matching A.2. Note that the front ends up 0.66 K above the rear in the converged answer (65.26 − 64.60) — that small split is the front-to-rear gradient driving conducted heat through the substrate, and it is exactly what the lump in A.2 averages over.

A.7The coupling bounce — iterating a dissipating cell to equilibrium

When a cell both dissipates electrical power and is thermally coupled to its neighbours, the simulator does not solve the whole array at once; it iterates round-robin, updating each cell's temperature, recomputing the dissipation that depends on temperature, and going again. This can bounce: a temperature jumps up, which changes the electrical operating point, which changes the heat, which moves the temperature again. To stop the bounce from oscillating or overshooting (recall the Newton overshoot of A.5), the solver applies a damping factor ω: instead of jumping the full distance to the freshly computed temperature, it moves only a fraction ω of the way. Here we trace a dissipating cell that finally settles at 198.8 °C, comparing under-relaxed damping (ω = 0.5, half-steps) against full jumps (ω = 1).

Tnext = Tnow + ω · ( Tsolved − Tnow )
ω = damping (relaxation) factor; Tsolved = the temperature the dissipation equation wants given the current state
Worked — damped rounds, ω = 0.5, start 28 °C
Round 0 — at 28 °C the dissipation balance solves to roughly 168 °C. A damped half-step moves only halfway: 28 + 0.5·(168 − 28) = 28 + 0.5·140 = 28 + 70 = 98.5 °C.
Round 1 — now at 98.5 °C the balance solves to about 181 °C; half-step: 98.5 + 0.5·(181 − 98.5) ≈ 98.5 + 41.7 ≈ 140.2 °C.
Round 2 — the next half-step lands near 164.7 °C, and the rounds keep closing the remaining gap by half each time.
Continuing — 28 → 98.5 → 140.2 → 164.7 → … each round eats roughly half the distance left, asymptotically approaching the fixed point after about 22 rounds.
Check: the damped sequence settles at 198.8 °C with no overshoot — safe and monotonic, but slow (≈22 rounds).
Worked — full jump, ω = 1, start 28 °C
Round 0 — take the whole step: 28 → 169 °C (the full solved value from the cold start).
Round 1 — recompute and jump fully: 169 → 194 °C.
Round 2 onward — 194 → 198.8 °C, converged.
Check: the full-jump sequence 28 → 169 → 194 → 198.8 reaches the same 198.8 °C in only about 5 rounds — roughly four times faster than the damped version. The trade-off is the whole point of the damping factor: ω = 1 is fast but risks the violent overshoot we saw in A.5 if the problem is stiff; ω = 0.5 is slow but bullet-proof. Both converge to the same physical equilibrium because damping changes only the path, never the destination.

A.8The orbit fluxes — sunlight, albedo, and planetary infrared

Everything so far assumed Earth-orbit sunlight at 1 AU, Psun = 1367 W/m². But the same array might fly to Mars, or tilt away from the Sun, or catch reflected light and planetary heat. This section computes the environmental fluxes the simulator can apply. The headline rule for distance is the inverse-square law: flux falls as one over the distance squared.

P(d) = Psun / d²
d = distance from Sun in AU; Psun = 1367 W/m² at d = 1
Worked — solar flux at different distances
At 1 AU (Earth): P = 1367 / 1² = 1367 W/m².
At 1.52 AU (Mars): d² = 1.52² = 2.3104, so P = 1367 / 2.3104 = 591.7 W/m². Mars gets well under half of Earth's sunlight.
At 2 AU: d² = 4, so P = 1367 / 4 = 341.75 W/m². Exactly a quarter of the 1-AU value, as the inverse-square law demands when distance doubles.
Check: doubling distance (1 → 2 AU) quartered the flux (1367 → 341.75). Inverse-square confirmed.

Near a planet the array also catches two extra inputs. Albedo is sunlight reflected off the planet's surface — a fraction (here 0.25) of the local solar flux, further reduced by a geometric view factor (≈0.3). Planetary infrared is the heat the planet itself radiates as a warm body at about 255 K, again seen through the view factor.

Worked — albedo flux near Mars
Albedo = 0.25 × (local solar flux 591) × (view factor 0.3) = 0.25 × 591 × 0.3. First 0.25 × 591 = 147.75; then × 0.3 = 44.325 W/m².
Check: albedo ≈ 44.3 W/m² — a small but non-negligible bonus heat input on the planet-facing side.
Worked — planetary infrared at 255 K
Step 1 — blackbody flux: σ · 255⁴. 255² = 65025; 65025² = 4.228e9. So 255⁴ = 4.228e9 K⁴.
Step 2 — times σ: 5.670367e-8 × 4.228e9 = 5.670367 × 4.228 × 10¹ = 23.97 × 10 = 239.7 W/m² (the full blackbody emission of a 255 K surface).
Step 3 — apply view factor 0.3: 239.7 × 0.3 = 71.93 W/m².
Check: planetary IR ≈ 71.9 W/m², the largest of the secondary inputs. So a Mars-orbit cell sees ~591.7 direct sun + ~44.3 albedo + ~71.9 IR on its various faces — the simulator sums these onto the appropriate face before solving the same balance as A.1.

Finally, when the array is tilted off the Sun line, the absorbed solar flux scales by the cosine of the tilt angle, because only the component of the flux perpendicular to the panel is collected. The verified cosines are: cos 30° = 0.866, cos 45° = 0.707, cos 60° = 0.5. So a panel tilted 60° off-Sun collects only half its head-on sunlight; at 45° about 71%; at 30° about 87%. These multiply directly onto Psun before the absorptivity step of A.1.

A.9The breakdown checks — survive or destroyed

Once the simulator has a converged temperature and electrical state for each cell, it applies two destruction criteria. The cell is destroyed if it gets too hot, or if it is forced to dissipate too much reverse power. The verified limits are a temperature limit tlimit = 150 °C and a reverse-power limit prev_limit = 2 W. A cell flagged as destroyed by either is removed from the array. Let us run both criteria against two cells from the earlier sections: the healthy idle cell and the reverse-biased hot-spot.

destroyed = ( T ≥ tlimit ) OR ( reverse_power ≥ prev_limit )
tlimit = 150 °C; prev_limit = 2 W; reverse_power = |V·I| only when V < 0, else 0
Worked — idle cell (65.26 °C, V = +2.3 V, I = 0.48 A)

A.10The Monte-Carlo run count — how many trials to trust an answer

The failure-sweep runs many randomised trials and reports an average survival temperature (or failure fraction). Because each trial is random, the reported average has a statistical uncertainty — the standard error — that shrinks as you run more trials. The rule is that the standard error of a mean is the population standard deviation divided by the square root of the number of trials.

SE = σ / √N
SE = standard error of the mean; σ = standard deviation of one trial; N = number of trials
Worked — standard error of a uniform [0, 2] sample
Step 1 — the mean of a uniform spread of values between 0 and 2 is the midpoint, 1.
Step 2 — the (sample) variance of this two-point spread is 2, so the standard deviation is σ = √2 ≈ 1.414.
Step 3 — with N = 2 samples, SE = σ/√N = √2 / √2 = 1.0.
Check: SE = 1.0. The mean is 1 ± 1 — i.e. with so few samples the uncertainty is as large as the answer itself. This is why Monte-Carlo needs many runs.

Now the practical question the sweep actually asks: I ran 100 trials and my standard error came out at about 8 °C, but I need it down to 2 °C — how many trials do I need? Because SE scales as 1/√N, halving the error needs four times the runs, and in general the run count scales as the square of the error-reduction ratio:

Nneeded = ( SEnow / SEtarget )² · Nnow
SEnow = 8 °C at Nnow = 100 runs; SEtarget = 2 °C
Worked — runs needed to reach 2 °C error
Step 1 — error-reduction ratio: SEnow/SEtarget = 8/2 = 4.
Step 2 — square it: 4² = 16 (because error falls as 1/√N, the runs must rise as the square).
Step 3 — scale the run count: Nneeded = 16 × 100 = 1600 runs.
Check: to cut the standard error from 8 °C to 2 °C — a factor of four — you need sixteen times the trials, so 1600 runs in total (1500 more than the 100 you already did). This square-law cost is the fundamental tax of Monte-Carlo: each extra digit of precision is dramatically more expensive than the last.

B.1Electricity — push, flow, and power

Before any thermal physics, the simulator needs to know how much electrical power each cell turns into something. Power is born from two simpler quantities, and getting their meanings firmly in hand makes everything downstream — heating, failure, the minus sign on a dead cell — fall out almost for free.

LADDER · what voltage and current actually are
Q1. If electricity were water in a pipe, what would voltage be?
The push — the pressure shoving the water along. A tall water tower pushes hard; a puddle pushes not at all. Voltage is the electrical equivalent of that pressure. A healthy cell here pushes with 2.3 V. Nothing has flowed yet; this is only the urge to flow.
Q2. And in the same picture, what is current?
The flow — how much water is actually moving past a point each second. You can have huge pressure and zero flow (a capped pipe), or gentle pressure and a steady stream (a wide open hose). Our cell carries 0.48 A: that is the rate of charge sliding through it, the stream itself rather than the urge.
Q3. Why do we need both numbers to describe what a cell is doing?
Because one alone tells you nothing useful. Pressure with no flow does no work — the water just sits there pushing on a cap. Flow with no pressure cannot happen on its own. Energy gets delivered only when something is both pushed and moving. So the cell's "doing-ness" lives in the pair (V, I) together, never in either alone.
Q4. So how do you combine push and flow into a single rate of energy?
You multiply them. Power = voltage × current, watts = V · I. With 2.3 V × 0.48 A you get about 1.1 W. That is the cell's whole electrical output as one number: roughly one joule of energy handed over every second.
Q5. Deep rung — why does multiplying, specifically, give energy per second?
Think of the units carrying the meaning. A volt is joules per coulomb (energy per unit of charge). An amp is coulombs per second (charge per unit of time). Multiply them and the coulombs cancel: (J/C) · (C/s) = J/s = watts. So the multiplication is not a trick — it is the literal statement that "energy per charge" times "charge per second" must equal "energy per second". The arithmetic and the physics are the same sentence written two ways.
LADDER · the sign of power and what negative means
Q1. When our healthy cell makes 1.1 W, is that number positive or negative?
Positive. We adopt the convention that power flowing out of a source — energy the cell delivers to the rest of the circuit — counts as positive. A cell doing its job is a positive number: +1.1 W handed out.
Q2. What single thing in V · I could you flip to make the product negative?
The sign of the voltage (or, equivalently, the direction of current relative to it). If the push reverses while the flow stays the same direction, the product V · I goes negative. The flow magnitude need not change at all — only its relationship to the push.
Q3. Physically, what is happening when a cell shows negative power?
The cell has stopped being a source and become a sink. Instead of pushing energy out into the circuit, it is having energy shoved into it by the rest of the string. The arrows of push and flow now disagree, so the cell absorbs rather than delivers. Negative power means "this part is eating energy, not making it".
Q4. Deep rung — where does that absorbed energy go, and why should an engineer fear it?
It has nowhere to go but into heat. A source converts chemical or photovoltaic energy into electrical output; a sink does the reverse, dumping incoming electrical energy as warmth in its own body. So a negative-power cell is, by definition, a heater whose wattage you did not plan for. Later (B.3) we will see one cell forced to −9.6 W — nearly nine times its healthy output, all of it heat, all of it concentrated in a part the size of a thumbnail. The minus sign is the first warning of a hot-spot.
Section recap Voltage is push, current is flow, power is their product (and the units prove why). Positive power = energy delivered; negative power = energy absorbed and turned to heat. Hold these and B.2–B.4 are almost automatic.

B.2Series & parallel — and the cruel arithmetic of a dead cell

Cells are not wired alone; they are chained. How they are chained decides what the others do when one fails. The most counter-intuitive fact in the whole simulator — that a dead cell is forced to about minus twenty volts — comes straight out of one simple rule about series chains.

LADDER · the one rule of a series chain
Q1. In a single pipe with no branches, can more water flow at the start than at the end?
No. Whatever enters one end must leave the other; the water has nowhere else to go. The flow rate is the same at every point along the pipe.
Q2. Cells wired in series are exactly that single unbranched pipe. So what must be equal for every cell in a series string?
The current. Series means one path, so the same amps pass through every cell — 0.48 A through the first, the same 0.48 A through the last. They have no choice; there is only one road.
Q3. If currents are forced equal, what is free to differ from cell to cell?
The voltage across each one. Series fixes the flow but lets each cell take its own share of the push. Add the shares and you get the string's total voltage. Current: shared and locked. Voltage: divided up and variable. (Parallel is the mirror image — voltage shared, current divided — but the array of interest here is the series string.)
Q4. Deep rung — a healthy cell makes +2.3 V. Why is a dead cell in the same string forced to roughly −20 V?
Here is the cruelty. The string's healthy neighbours keep on pushing their 0.48 A through the single path — and that path runs straight through the dead cell, which can no longer generate. The dead cell cannot stop the current (series gives it no off-switch), so the current is rammed backwards through it. To pass current the wrong way through a cell that does not want it, a large reverse voltage must appear across it — measured at roughly −20 V. It is not the dead cell choosing this; the surviving cells impose it. The series rule that the flow must be equal everywhere is precisely what dooms the casualty: unable to break the chain, it is back-driven into deep reverse bias.
Picture this A line of people passing buckets hand to hand (series — same buckets reach everyone). One person faints. The line behind keeps shoving buckets forward at the same rate, and the only place for them to pile up is on the fainted person. They are crushed under a load they never generated — the −20 V is that pile-up made electrical.
Section recap Series = one path = equal current for all, but voltage shared out. A failed series cell cannot interrupt the current, so its healthy neighbours back-drive it to a large negative voltage (~−20 V). That negative voltage is the seed of everything in B.3.

B.3Reverse bias & hot-spots — why a corpse runs hottest

Intuition says a broken cell should go cold and quiet. The simulator says the opposite: the failed cell is the hottest thing on the array, hotter than any healthy one. This section climbs from "what is reverse bias" to the brutal 9.6 W packed into a thumbnail.

LADDER · from reverse voltage to concentrated heat
Q1. From B.2, a dead cell sits at about −20 V while still carrying 0.48 A. What power is that?
Multiply, with the sign: −20 V × 0.48 A ≈ −9.6 W. The minus sign (from B.1) tells us this cell is absorbing, not delivering — it is a sink swallowing about nine and a half watts.
Q2. Compare that to the cell's healthy output. What stands out?
Healthy it gave out +1.1 W; failed it swallows −9.6 W. So the failure does not merely switch the cell off — it flips a 1.1 W producer into a 9.6 W consumer, nearly nine times its old output in magnitude and with the opposite sign. The energy budget swings by almost 11 W around this one tiny part.
Q3. The absorbed 9.6 W cannot vanish. Where does it have to go?
Into heat, inside the cell's own body. There is no chemical store to refill, no useful work to do — back-driven electrical energy in a reverse-biased junction comes out as warmth, deposited right where the current is forced through.
Q4. Why does dumping 9.6 W into a thumbnail-sized cell get so dangerous, when a 9.6 W lamp across a room feels mild?
Because heat damage is about concentration, not just total wattage. The same 9.6 W spread over a desk lamp's surface is gentle; squeezed into the area of a thumbnail it is an intense flux per unit area. A small body has little surface to shed heat from and little mass to soak it up, so its temperature climbs steeply. The watts are the same number; the square-centimetres they land on are tiny. That ratio is what melts things.
Q5. Deep rung — so why is the failed cell the hottest on the array, when we instinctively expect a broken thing to be inert?
Because failure here does not mean "switched off" — series wiring forbids that (B.2). It means "converted from a 1.1 W source into a 9.6 W concentrated heater". Healthy cells turn most of their absorbed sunlight into electricity that leaves down the wires; only a little stays as heat. The failed cell does the reverse: it receives electrical energy from its neighbours and keeps all of it as heat in a tiny volume. The corpse runs hottest precisely because it has become the array's only dedicated heating element, fed by the very cells trying to keep working around it.
Picture this A magnifying glass over paper. The sun's power was harmless spread out; focused to a dot it burns. The failed cell is that focal dot — the neighbours' watts, normally spread across the array, get funnelled into one small spot.
Section recap Reverse bias (−20 V) × surviving current (0.48 A) = −9.6 W absorbed, all turned to heat, all concentrated in a thumbnail. Concentration — not total power — is why it melts, and why the dead cell, far from inert, is the array's hottest point.

B.4The T⁴ law — why glow rises so violently, and why it self-limits

A hot body radiates heat away. The startling part is how the radiated power grows with temperature: not in step, but as the fourth power. This single fact both explains why hot-spots glow so fiercely and — happily — why they do not run away to infinity but settle at a finite, if alarming, 187 °C.

LADDER · the fourth power and why it is brutal
Q1. Does a warm body and a hot body radiate the same amount of heat?
No — the hotter body radiates far more. Radiated power climbs with temperature. The only question is how steeply, and the answer is steeper than almost anyone guesses.
Q2. The Stefan–Boltzmann law says radiated power goes as T⁴. If you double the temperature, by what factor does the glow rise?
By 2⁴ = 16×. Not double, not quadruple — sixteen times. The fourth power means small temperature rises buy enormous increases in heat shed.
Q3. Our idle plate sits at 338 K and a hot-spot reaches 460 K — a ratio of only about 1.36. How much more does the hot-spot radiate per unit area?
Raise the ratio to the fourth: 1.36⁴ ≈ 3.4×. A mere 36% rise in absolute temperature makes the surface throw off about three and a half times as much radiant heat. That is the fourth power working in our favour, as we will see in Q5.
Q4. Why must we use kelvin (338 K, 460 K) and never Celsius in the T⁴ law?
Because the law counts radiation from absolute zero — the true bottom of temperature, where thermal motion stops. Kelvin starts there; Celsius starts at the freezing point of water, an arbitrary place. Raising a number to the fourth power is meaningless unless zero means "no thermal energy at all". Use Celsius and 0° would falsely claim zero radiation from ice, while doubling 1 °C to 2 °C would pretend the glow rose 16× over a trivial change. Only kelvin makes the ratios physical. The constant tying it together is σ = 5.670367e-8 W·m⁻²·K⁻⁴ — its very units (per K⁴) demand an absolute scale.
Q5. Deep rung — the failed cell is fed a fixed ~9.6 W. Why does its temperature stop at a finite 187 °C instead of climbing forever?
Because the heat in is fixed (9.6 W) but the heat out grows as T⁴ — and a quantity that grows as the fourth power catches up with a constant astonishingly fast. As the cell warms, its radiated power rockets upward (16× for a doubling, 3.4× for our modest ratio). The temperature rises only until radiated-out exactly equals fed-in. Past that point any extra degree would radiate away more than the 9.6 W supplied, which is impossible, so it cannot get hotter. The fourth power is a fierce thermostat: it makes the "out" curve so steep that it slams into the fixed "in" line at a single finite crossing — here 187 °C. The same brutality that makes hot things glow so hard is exactly what saves the cell from melting to infinity.
Picture this Pouring water (heat in) into a bucket whose drain hole widens explosively as the water rises (radiation out, ∝ T⁴). At first it fills fast, but the drain grows so quickly that the level stops at the exact height where the widened drain leaks out as fast as you pour. It never overflows — the runaway drain is the T⁴ law.
Section recap Radiation ∝ T⁴, in kelvin (σ = 5.670367e-8). Double T → 16× glow; our 1.36 ratio → 3.4×. The steep "out" curve meets the fixed 9.6 W "in" at one finite point, 187 °C — the fourth power is both why hot-spots blaze and why they self-limit.

B.5Why we must SOLVE, not compute

The previous section quietly hid the hardest fact in the whole simulator: we cannot just plug numbers into a formula to get the temperature. The temperature appears on both sides of its own equation, tangled inside a fourth power. This section explains why that turns a calculation into a search.

LADDER · when a formula stops being computable
Q1. To find a cell's area, you multiply length by width. Why is that a "compute", not a "solve"?
Because everything on the right-hand side is already known. You feed in the knowns, the arithmetic runs once, the answer drops out. The unknown sits alone on the left and never appears on the right. One pass, done.
Q2. Now the heat balance: heat in = heat radiated out + heat conducted away. Where does the unknown temperature T appear?
On the right, in two places at once. Radiation carries T as T⁴ (from B.4), and conduction carries T linearly (proportional to T itself). So T is buried on the right-hand side — the side that was supposed to be all knowns.
Q3. Why does T appearing on both sides ruin the "one pass" trick?
Because to evaluate the right-hand side you would already need to know T — which is the very thing you are trying to find. You cannot plug in a number you do not have yet. The equation defines T in terms of itself; there is no clean left-alone unknown to read off. The single pass has nothing to start from.
Q4. Couldn't we just rearrange — move things around and take a fourth root to isolate T?
No, and this is the crux. We have a T⁴ term and a linear T term added together: something like a·T⁴ + b·T = c. To isolate T you would need to undo both at once, but the fourth root only unwinds the T⁴ part and leaves the linear T stranded, and vice-versa. There is no algebraic move that peels T out of a sum of a fourth power and a first power. The equation is transcendental — no rearrangement exists that puts a lone T on one side.
Q5. Deep rung — so what does it really mean to call T "unknown" here, and why is "solve" the honest word?
"Unknown" does not merely mean "we have not done the multiplication yet"; it means "no finite formula will ever spit out this value". The honest situation is that we know a test: given any guessed temperature, we can ask "does heat-in equal heat-out at this guess?" and get a yes/no/by-how-much. We cannot derive T, but we can check a candidate T. Solving, then, is searching — proposing temperatures and steering the guess until the heat books balance. That shift, from evaluating a formula once to hunting for the value that makes an equation true, is exactly what B.6 (Newton's method) automates. The whole simulator pivots on this sentence: we cannot compute the temperature, only find it.
Picture this Computing is reading a price off a tag. Solving is bargaining: you cannot read the agreed price anywhere, but you can propose one and tell instantly whether it is too high or too low, and close in. T is a price with no tag — only a haggling rule.
Section recap T sits on both sides, inside T⁴ and a linear term, so no rearrangement isolates it (it is transcendental). "Unknown" means "uncomputable, but checkable". Therefore we search for the T that balances the equation — we solve, we do not compute.

B.6Newton's method — steering a guess to the answer

B.5 left us needing to search for the temperature that balances heat in against heat out. Newton's method is the smart way to search: instead of blindly trying values, it uses the local slope of the mismatch to leap almost straight to the answer in a handful of steps.

LADDER · using the slope to find the root
Q1. Define the "error" of a guessed temperature as (heat out − heat in) at that guess. When have we found the true temperature?
When the error is exactly zero — heat out equals heat in, the books balance. So solving the heat equation is the same as finding the temperature where this error function crosses zero. That crossing point is called the root.
Q2. Standing at a guess where the error is, say, +5 (too much heat leaving), which way should you move the guess, and how do you know?
You look at the slope of the error — how fast the error changes as you nudge the temperature. The slope points you downhill toward zero error: it tells you both the direction (raise or lower the guess) and the steepness (how sensitive the error is to a change in T). The slope is local advice on which way the answer lies.
Q3. Why does Newton's step divide the error by the slope, rather than just stepping a fixed amount?
Because dividing error by slope gives the exact distance to the root if the error line were straight. Slope is "error change per degree"; so (error ÷ slope) = "how many degrees to undo this error". A steep slope means a small move fixes a big error; a gentle slope means you must move far. Dividing by the slope auto-sizes every step to the local steepness — big corrections where the function is flat, careful ones where it is steep. A fixed step cannot adapt like that.
Q4. The error curve is not actually straight (it has that T⁴ bend). Why does Newton still nail it in only about 5 steps?
Because each step makes the remaining error far smaller, and near the answer the curve looks almost straight, so the straight-line assumption becomes more and more accurate as you close in. Newton roughly squares the accuracy each step — three correct digits become six, then twelve. Starting from a sensible guess, the error collapses so fast that about 5 steps drives it below any tolerance we care about. It converges quadratically, which is why a handful of iterations suffices instead of hundreds.
Q5. Deep rung — why can a cold start (guessing far too low) make the first Newton step overshoot wildly?
Because the slope it uses is the slope at the guess, not at the answer — and far out on the T⁴ curve the local slope is a poor description of the whole journey. Down in the cold region the radiation term is sluggish, so the error curve is very flat there; a flat slope means "the root is far away", and dividing the error by that tiny slope produces a huge step that can fling the guess past the true root, even into nonsense like a negative temperature. The straight-line promise was only ever local, and a cold start cashes it in where it is least valid. The cure is to start from a reasonable temperature, or to damp the first steps (limit how far each leap may go) — which is exactly the caution B.9 will demand of the coupled solver.
Picture this Rolling toward the bottom of a valley in fog. You cannot see the bottom, but you can feel the slope under your feet and step proportionally. On a gentle slope far out you take a giant stride (and might overshoot); near the bottom the slope is steep and your strides shrink to a stop. Newton is walking by feel.
Section recap Root = where error (out − in) hits zero. Step = error ÷ slope, which auto-sizes the move and converges in ~5 steps by roughly squaring accuracy each time. A cold start sits where the slope is misleadingly flat, so its first step can overshoot — hence sensible starts or damping.

B.7The two meanings of "iteration"

The word "iteration" appears twice in this project meaning two opposite things, and confusing them costs hours. One kind of repetition we deliberately keep; the other kind we work hard to remove. Telling them apart is a small idea that prevents a large mistake.

LADDER · which repetition we keep and which we kill
Q1. The Newton solver from B.6 repeats its step about five times per cell. Is that repetition optional?
No — it is the very mechanism of solving. Each Newton iteration shrinks the error; remove them and you never reach the balanced temperature. This repetition is mathematical and essential. We keep it.
Q2. Separately, a naïve program loops over cells one at a time — "for cell in all cells: solve it". Is that repetition essential?
No. It is a bookkeeping habit, an artefact of how we first wrote the code, not of the physics. The cells could in principle all be handled together; processing them one-by-one in a Python loop is a choice, and often a slow one. This repetition is incidental.
Q3. Why is it so easy to confuse these two "iterations"?
Because both are loops that say "do this again", and the same English word covers both. But they live on different axes: the Newton loop iterates in time toward an answer (each pass improves the same quantity), while the per-cell loop iterates across space over independent items (each pass handles a different cell). Same word, orthogonal meanings.
Q4. Deep rung — vectorisation removes one of these loops but must never touch the other. Which is which, and why exactly?
We remove the per-cell Python loop and we keep the Newton iteration. The per-cell loop is removable because the cells are independent — cell 7's solve does not depend on cell 6's, so all cells can take their Newton step together in one vector operation (this is B.8). The Newton iteration is not removable because each step depends on the previous one — step 4 needs the improved guess from step 3, so the passes cannot be done "all at once"; they are inherently sequential. The rule that decides the fate of any loop is dependency: loops over independent things can be collapsed into vector math; loops where each pass feeds the next must stay. Vectorisation deletes the spatial loop over cells and leaves the temporal loop of Newton perfectly intact — indeed it runs one Newton step for every cell simultaneously, five times.
Section recap Newton iteration (toward an answer, each pass depends on the last) is KEPT — it is sequential by nature. The per-cell Python loop (across independent cells) is REMOVED by vectorisation. Dependency is the test: independent → collapse it; dependent → keep it.

B.8Vectorisation — why doing it "all at once" is faster

B.7 said we collapse the per-cell loop into one vector operation. But why is that faster, if the same arithmetic still has to happen for every cell? The answer is surprising: the savings are almost never in the arithmetic itself. They are in the overhead the loop wrapped around it.

LADDER · what a vector is and where the speed comes from
Q1. What do we mean by a "vector" of cell temperatures?
Just all the temperatures lined up in one array — T₁, T₂, … up to the last cell — held together as a single object rather than as scattered individual numbers. A 40×40 panel becomes one block of 1600 numbers we can name and operate on as a whole.
Q2. A Python loop adds heat to each cell one at a time; a vector operation adds to all at once. Roughly the same multiplications happen either way — so where could time possibly be saved?
In everything around the multiplications. Each trip through a Python loop pays a tax: fetch the next item, check the loop condition, box and unbox the number into a Python object, dispatch the operation. That bookkeeping can dwarf the actual arithmetic. Do it 1600 times and the tax, not the maths, dominates.
Q3. So precisely what does vectorisation eliminate — the arithmetic or the overhead?
The overhead, almost entirely. A vector operation hands the whole array down to compiled, low-level code that runs the same arithmetic in a tight machine loop with none of the per-item Python tax — no re-boxing, no interpreter dispatch per element. The multiplications still occur; what disappears is the thick wrapper Python put around each one. That is why a vectorised solve of the 40×40 panel finishes in about 0.046 s.
Q4. Deep rung — when is collapsing a loop into a vector operation actually allowed, and what would forbid it?
It is allowed exactly when the items are independent — when computing cell 7 needs nothing from cell 6 within the same step. Independence means the order does not matter and "all at once" gives the same answer as "one after another". If instead each item depended on the result of the previous one (as Newton's steps depend on each other in time, B.7), you could not do them simultaneously without breaking the chain of dependency, and vectorisation across that axis would be wrong, not just slow. So the licence to vectorise is independence, and the thing it forbids is collapsing a loop whose every pass feeds the next. This is why we vectorise across cells (independent) but never across Newton steps (dependent).
Picture this Stamping 1600 envelopes. One-at-a-time means picking up, positioning, stamping, setting down — 1600 times, and the picking-up is most of the effort. A roller stamps a whole sheet in one pass: the same number of stamps, but the handling overhead is gone. Vectorisation is the roller — and it only works because the envelopes do not depend on each other.
Section recap A vector is all the values held as one array. Speed comes from killing the per-item Python overhead (boxing, dispatch, loop checks), not from changing the arithmetic — hence the 40×40 solve in 0.046 s. It is permitted only when items are independent; dependent chains (Newton steps) must stay sequential.

B.9Electro-thermal coupling — the chicken and the egg

Electrical behaviour sets the heat; but temperature changes the electrical behaviour; which in turn changes the heat. Each needs the other already known. This circular dependence is the coupling, and the trick to breaking the circle is both simple and a little uncomfortable: guess, then fix.

LADDER · breaking a circular dependence
Q1. To find each cell's temperature we need its electrical power. To find its electrical power we need its temperature (cells behave differently hot vs cold). What kind of problem is this?
A circular one — a chicken-and-egg. Neither quantity can be found first because each is defined in terms of the other. There is no clean starting point handed to us.
Q2. If you cannot start with the right value of either, what is the only way to get moving at all?
Guess one of them. Pick a plausible temperature for every cell (say, the idle 65 °C) even though it is surely a little wrong, and use that guess to get going. A wrong-but-reasonable starting value breaks the deadlock; nothing else can.
Q3. Having guessed temperatures, you compute the powers, then re-solve for temperatures and get different ones. Why redo it instead of stopping?
Because the new temperatures were computed from powers that came from the old guessed temperatures — they are inconsistent. The powers that produced these new temperatures are no longer the powers those temperatures would imply. So you feed the new temperatures back in to recompute the powers, then re-solve again. Each pass uses better inputs than the last.
Q4. Why should this back-and-forth settle rather than wander forever?
Because each correction is smaller than the one before. The first guess might be off by tens of degrees; the next pass shrinks the mismatch a lot, the one after that a little, and so on. The system has a stable balance point — one consistent set of temperatures and powers — and the updates close in on it, each pass moving a fraction of the remaining distance. When the change between passes drops below our tolerance, temperatures and powers finally agree with each other, and we stop. That self-consistent state is the real answer.
Q5. Deep rung — why do we sometimes damp the update (move only part-way toward the new value) instead of jumping straight to it?
Because an over-eager full jump can overshoot and set the next pass swinging the other way — temperatures bouncing high, low, high, low, growing instead of shrinking, so the loop never settles (it oscillates or diverges). This is the same overshoot danger as Newton's cold start (B.6), now living in the coupling loop. Damping means: do not adopt the whole newly computed value, adopt a blend of the old and new — step, say, half-way. That deliberately slows each pass but guarantees the swings shrink rather than grow, trading a few extra iterations for the certainty of settling. We accept being slower-but-stable over faster-but-explosive, because a loop that diverges gives no answer at all.
Picture this Two people tuning to each other across a room — you set your pitch from theirs, they reset from yours. If you both leap to match exactly, you overshoot and chase forever. If each moves only half-way toward the other, you converge to one shared note. Damping is moving half-way.
Section recap Power and temperature each need the other — a chicken-and-egg. Break it by guessing temperatures, computing powers, re-solving, and repeating; corrections shrink toward a self-consistent balance and we stop at tolerance. Damp the update (move part-way) to stop overshoot from making the loop oscillate or diverge.

B.10Lateral conduction — letting neighbours share the burn

So far each cell has shed its heat only by radiating to space. But cells touch their neighbours, and heat can also flow sideways through the panel. Turning that sideways path on dramatically lowers the worst hot-spot — at the price of warming everyone nearby. This section builds up to why the peak drops from 187 °C to about 87 °C.

LADDER · sealed islands versus a shared panel
Q1. If every cell could only radiate to space and shed nothing sideways to its neighbours, what is each cell, thermally?
A sealed island. Its temperature depends only on its own heat in and its own radiation out; what happens next door is irrelevant. The failed cell stands alone with its full 9.6 W and reaches 187 °C with no help from anyone.
Q2. Now let heat flow through the material between adjacent cells. What new escape route does the hot failed cell gain?
Sideways conduction — heat can leak from the scorching cell into its cooler neighbours, like warmth spreading along a metal spoon. The failed cell is no longer trapped with all 9.6 W; some of it drains laterally into the surrounding cells, which are far cooler and can take it.
Q3. So why does the peak temperature drop — and why do the neighbours get warmer at the same time?
The two effects are one transaction. Heat the failed cell can no longer keep is heat the neighbours must now carry. The peak falls because the worst cell has a second drain (sideways, on top of radiation), so it balances at a lower temperature — about 87 °C instead of 187 °C. The neighbours rise because they are now also radiating away a share of the failed cell's burden, so they settle a little hotter than before. Conservation: the heat did not vanish, it was spread out. Spreading lowers the spike and lifts the surroundings — the panel shares the burn.
Q4. Deep rung — the lateral conductance is written g_lat. Why does setting g_lat = 0 give back exactly the sealed-island baseline, and why is that a useful check?
Because g_lat is the knob controlling how freely heat flows sideways: the sideways heat transfer is proportional to g_lat times the temperature difference between neighbours. Set g_lat = 0 and that whole sideways term is multiplied to nothing — no heat crosses between cells no matter how large the temperature gaps. The model collapses back to independent cells each radiating alone, i.e. the 187 °C sealed-island case of B.3–B.4. This is enormously useful: it means the lateral model contains the simpler model as the special case g_lat = 0. We can dial g_lat up from zero and watch the peak slide continuously from 187 °C down toward 87 °C, confident that at zero we recover the answer we already trust. A more complex model that reduces to the known baseline when its new feature is switched off is a model we can believe; the g_lat = 0 check is how we earn that trust.
Picture this One person holding a hot coal bare-handed (sealed island — 187 °C of pain, all theirs). Now a ring of people pass it hand to hand: no single pair of hands gets as scorched (peak drops to 87 °C), but everyone's hands warm up a bit (neighbours rise). The total heat is unchanged; it is merely shared.
Section recap Sealed islands radiate alone → failed cell hits 187 °C. Open the sideways path and heat drains into neighbours: peak falls to ~87 °C while neighbours warm — heat is conserved, just spread. g_lat = 0 zeroes the sideways term and recovers the baseline exactly, which is how we validate the richer model.

B.11The sparse Jacobian — one big matrix that is mostly empty

Once cells share heat sideways (B.10), they are no longer independent, so we can no longer solve each one alone. We must solve them all together — and the bookkeeping for "how every cell's error responds to every cell's temperature" is one large matrix, the Jacobian. The saving grace is that almost all of it is zero.

LADDER · why one shared matrix, and why the zeros help
Q1. With lateral conduction, does changing one cell's temperature affect only that cell's heat balance?
No. Because heat now flows sideways, nudging cell 50's temperature changes the sideways heat into its neighbours, altering their balances too. The cells are coupled — they can no longer be solved one at a time as independent islands.
Q2. Newton's method needs a slope (B.6). For a coupled system of many cells, what replaces the single slope?
A whole table of slopes: how each cell's error changes when each cell's temperature is nudged. That table is the Jacobian — a matrix with one row per cell and one column per cell, every entry a partial slope. It is the multi-cell generalisation of the single slope we divided by before.
Q3. Why one big matrix for the whole panel rather than N tiny separate solves, as in the uncoupled case?
Because the coupling stitches the cells into a single system: cell A's answer depends on cell B's, so they must be solved simultaneously, and that joint step is exactly a single matrix equation. N independent tiny solves would simply be wrong — they would ignore the sideways links. The one big matrix is the honest representation of "everything depends on its neighbours, so everything is solved together".
Q4. A 1600-cell panel gives a 1600×1600 Jacobian — over two million entries. Why is this not catastrophically expensive?
Because almost every entry is zero. A cell is only thermally linked to its handful of immediate neighbours, not to all 1600 cells. So each row has just a few non-zero entries (itself plus its neighbours) and the rest are zeros. The matrix is sparse — overwhelmingly empty. We store and compute only the non-zeros, and sparse solvers skip the zeros entirely. The empty space is not wasted; it is the source of the speed.
Q5. Deep rung — why do the mostly-zero entries help rather than merely sit there, and what part of the Jacobian stays constant across the whole solve?
Zeros help because a multiplication or addition by zero is work we can refuse to do: a sparse solver's effort scales with the number of non-zeros, not the matrix's full size, so a 1600×1600 matrix with only a few entries per row costs roughly like a few thousand numbers, not two million. The sparsity pattern turns an apparently quadratic monster into something nearly linear in the number of cells — which is why the full 40×40 coupled solve still finishes in about 0.046 s. And crucially, the pattern of which entries are non-zero is fixed by the panel's geometry (who neighbours whom), so it never changes during the solve — only the numerical values in those slots update as temperatures change. The lateral-conduction contributions, set by the fixed g_lat and the fixed neighbour layout, are constant; we compute that structure once and reuse it every Newton step rather than rebuilding it. Knowing what stays constant is what lets the solver be fast.
Picture this A phone book where almost everyone's "people I talk to" list is just their next-door neighbours, not the whole city. Writing each person's full 1600-name row would be absurd; you store only the two or three real links. The Jacobian is that phone book — mostly blank lines, and the blankness is exactly what makes it cheap to use.
Section recap Lateral coupling forces one simultaneous solve, represented by the Jacobian — a big table of slopes. It is sparse because each cell links only to its few neighbours; sparse solvers skip the zeros, so cost scales with non-zeros (≈ linear, hence 0.046 s). The non-zero pattern is fixed by geometry and stays constant, so it is built once and reused.

B.12Monte-Carlo — answering with random samples

Real arrays fail randomly — which cells die, and how badly, varies. We want statistics: how hot does the array typically get, how often does it cross a danger line? Checking every possible failure pattern is hopeless, so instead we throw dice many times and let the average tell the story. This is Monte-Carlo, and it has its own subtle traps.

LADDER · why sample instead of enumerate
Q1. Why not simply compute the outcome for every possible combination of failed cells and average them exactly?
Because the number of combinations explodes beyond any hope of counting. Even a modest panel has astronomically many possible failure patterns; listing and solving them all would take longer than we have. Exact enumeration is impossible in practice, not just slow.
Q2. If you cannot check them all, how do you learn the typical behaviour?
Sample. Draw a random failure scenario, solve it, record the result; draw another, solve, record; repeat many times. The collected results approximate the true distribution without ever enumerating it — the same way a poll estimates a nation's opinion from a few thousand people rather than asking everyone.
Q3. Why can a small sample mislead you badly?
Because with only a few draws, luck dominates. A handful of unusually mild (or unusually severe) scenarios can skew the average far from the truth, just as polling five friends tells you little about a country. Small samples are noisy: their answer wobbles a lot from run to run, and any single small run might land far from reality by chance.
Q4. As you take more samples, how fast does the uncertainty actually shrink — and why is that rule a little disappointing?
The statistical error shrinks like 1/√N, where N is the number of samples. Disappointing because the square root makes progress slow: to halve the error you must quadruple the samples, and to cut it tenfold you need a hundred times as many runs. Concretely, tightening our estimate of the array's temperature spread from about 8 °C of uncertainty down to 2 °C — a fourfold improvement — demands roughly 1600 Monte-Carlo runs. Accuracy is bought, and the 1/√N exchange rate is steep.
Q5. Deep rung — given that 1/√N rule, how do you decide when to stop sampling, instead of running forever?
You stop when the remaining uncertainty is smaller than what your decision actually cares about — not when the answer stops changing (it never fully does). The logic runs backwards from the question: decide the precision you need (e.g. "I must know the mean peak temperature to within 2 °C"), use 1/√N to compute how many samples that demands (here ~1600), and run exactly that many. Because each extra batch buys less and less (quadruple the runs for half the error), pushing far past the needed precision wastes enormous effort for a refinement no decision will notice. The honest stopping rule is therefore not "until it looks steady" but "until the error bar is tighter than the threshold I'm testing against" — match the sampling cost to the engineering tolerance, and stop the moment 1/√N says you have crossed it. Running more is not more rigorous; it is just slower for no decision-relevant gain.
Picture this Tasting soup to judge the salt. You cannot drink the whole pot (enumeration), so you stir and taste a spoonful (sample). One tiny sip near the top might be unrepresentative (small-sample noise); a few well-stirred spoonfuls suffice. You stop tasting when you can tell "salty enough or not" — not when every molecule is catalogued. That threshold-matched stop is the 1/√N stopping rule.
Section recap Failure patterns are too many to enumerate, so we sample and average. Small samples are noisy; error falls only as 1/√N (quadruple runs to halve error), so reaching 2 °C precision needs ~1600 runs. Stop when the error bar is tighter than the tolerance your decision cares about — not when the answer merely looks steady.

B.13Climbing back down — how the ladders connect

The twelve themes are not separate; they are one long staircase. It helps to see the whole climb in a single breath, because each section quietly handed the next its hardest tool.

LADDER · the appendix as one chain
Q1. Where does the whole story begin?
With power, P = V·I, and its sign (B.1). Everything downstream is the consequence of one cell's power going from +1.1 W to −9.6 W.
Q2. What turns that sign change into a physical threat?
Series wiring (B.2) forces the dead cell to −20 V; reverse bias (B.3) concentrates 9.6 W into a thumbnail as heat; the T⁴ law (B.4) decides it settles, finitely, at 187 °C.
Q3. Why does computing that 187 °C demand real machinery?
Because T sits on both sides of its equation and cannot be isolated (B.5), so we must search for it with Newton's slope-steered steps (B.6) — keeping the Newton iteration while removing the per-cell loop (B.7) by vectorising over independent cells (B.8).
Q4. Deep rung — how do the last four sections raise the whole thing from one cell to a believable panel, and where does each tool reappear?
Coupling (B.9) admits that power and temperature define each other, so we guess-and-fix with damping — the same overshoot caution Newton taught in B.6. Lateral conduction (B.10) lets neighbours share the burn, dropping 187 °C to 87 °C, and its g_lat = 0 check folds the whole model back onto the trusted single-cell baseline of B.3–B.4. That sideways coupling forces a simultaneous solve, represented by the sparse Jacobian (B.11) — Newton's lone slope from B.6 grown into a matrix that is mostly zeros, which is exactly why the 1600-cell panel still solves in 0.046 s. Finally Monte-Carlo (B.12) wraps the entire deterministic solver in randomness, running it ~1600 times to turn one answer into a distribution, governed by the steep 1/√N exchange rate. So the appendix is a single chain: a sign flip (B.1) propagated through wiring and physics (B.2–B.4), made computable by solving not computing (B.5–B.8), then scaled to a coupled, validated, sampled panel (B.9–B.12). Every hard rung was pre-fed by an easy one, and every later tool is an earlier tool grown up.
Appendix recap One thread runs end to end: power and its sign → series back-drive → concentrated reverse-bias heat → T⁴ self-limit at 187 °C → unsolvable-by-formula, so Newton + vectorisation → coupling, lateral sharing to 87 °C, sparse Jacobian, and Monte-Carlo statistics. Nothing was asked cold; each ladder built the next.

C.4Newton's Method on the Balance

To solve Q = k·T⁴ numerically we define the residual

f(T) = Q − k·T⁴
and its derivative
f′(T) = −4·k·T³
. Newton's update is
Tnew = T − f(T)/f′(T)
. Because f is monotonic in T for T>0, the iteration converges quickly from any sensible start.

It tells us the lumped k folds in more than one radiating face (e.g. front and back of the panel, ~1.8 effective faces), which is physically reasonable for a thin plate radiating from both sides.
Solution 9. Because T scales as Q1/4, raising the power lifts T but only weakly. Going from 3.978 W to 9.6 W is a factor 9.6/3.978 = 2.413 in power, so the temperature ratio in kelvin is 2.4131/4. √2.413 = 1.5534; √1.5534 = 1.246. So T_hot ≈ 1.246 × 338.1 K = 421.4 K = 148 °C for pure radiation of 9.6 W. (The quoted 187 °C reflects additional absorbed sun on top of the 9.6 W reverse dissipation.) Sanity check: the prediction is far above the 65 °C idle case, as expected, and the fourth-root means a 2.4× power jump gives only ~1.25× the absolute temperature — the hallmark of radiative coupling.
Solution 10. The curve T = (Q/k)1/4 rises steeply from the origin then flattens: it is concave, climbing fast at small Q and slowly at large Q. Key points to mark: at Q = 3.978 W, T ≈ 65 °C (idle); at Q = 9.6 W, T ≈ 148 °C (pure-radiation reverse). The flattening is the visual statement of the fourth-root law — doubling Q near the top barely moves T. Sanity check: the marked points are consistent with Solutions 7 and 9.
Solution 11. Newton update: Tnew = T − f/f′. f/f′ = (−1.16) / (−0.1257) = +9.228. Tnew = 195.92 − 9.228 = 186.69 ≈ 186.67 °C. Sanity check: f was negative (k·T⁴ exceeded Q, i.e. guess too hot) and the step moved the temperature down by ~9 °C toward the root near 187 °C — correct direction and magnitude, matching the quoted next iterate.
Solution 12. f′(T) = −4·k·T³. For any physical T > 0 K, k > 0 and T³ > 0, so f′ is always negative. Now the update is T − f/f′. If the guess is too hot, k·T⁴ > Q so f = Q − k·T⁴ < 0; dividing a negative f by a negative f′ gives a positive f/f′, which is subtracted, lowering T. If the guess is too cold, f > 0, f/f′ < 0, and subtracting a negative quantity raises T. The constant negative sign of f′ is precisely what makes the correction self-correcting in both directions. Sanity check: consistent with Solution 11, where f < 0 produced a downward step.
Solution 13. Quadratic convergence means the new error is roughly proportional to the square of the old error. If the error is ~1 °C, the next error is of order (1)² ≈ 1 °C scaled by the local constant — but once below 1, say 0.3 °C, the following step gives ~0.1 °C, then ~0.01 °C: the correct digits roughly double each step. This matters because the simulator solves the balance thousands of times (per cell, per Monte-Carlo draw); needing only 3–5 iterations each keeps the whole sweep cheap. Sanity check: 0.3² = 0.09 ≈ 0.1, confirming the doubling-of-digits picture.
Solution 14. On the next round each node is updated using the other's current value. A, being hotter, loses heat to B through the coupling (Q = G·ΔT flows hot→cold), so A drops; B receives that heat, so B rises. The gap shrinks because the heat flow is proportional to the gap itself — the bigger the difference the stronger the equalising flow, a classic negative-feedback relaxation toward a common temperature. Sanity check: energy is conserved (A's loss = B's gain through the coupling), and the difference decays rather than grows, which is the stable behaviour we expect.
Solution 15. With no external power difference and equal coupling, both nodes relax to a common equilibrium — for equal thermal masses that is roughly the average, about (65 + 39)/2 ≈ 52 °C. If A had ten times the thermal mass of B, the equilibrium would shift toward A's temperature: the heavier node barely moves while the light node swings to meet it, so the settling temperature would land much closer to 65 °C than to the midpoint. Sanity check: the mass-weighted mean (10×65 + 1×39)/11 = 712/11 ≈ 62.9 °C confirms the bias toward the heavy node.
Solution 16. The coupling sweep is iterative because each node's update uses neighbour values that are themselves still changing; one round only partially propagates heat. Stopping too early leaves the nodes not yet in balance, so reported temperatures are wrong — typically the peak is overstated because the cooling neighbours have not yet "caught up" to absorb the hot node's heat. Sanity check: this is the same negative-feedback relaxation as Solution 14; truncating it just freezes a mid-transient state.
Solution 17. Q_lat = G · ΔT = 0.05 × (150 − 50) = 0.05 × 100 = 5 W. Sanity check: 0.05 × 100 = 5 exactly; the flow runs hot→cold (positive from the 150 °C side), as physics requires.

Sanity check: the drop is large because radiation goes as T⁴ — spreading heat over more area cuts the required peak temperature steeply, consistent with Solution 9's fourth-root sensitivity.
Solution 20. The conserved quantity is total heat: the reverse cell still produces ~9.6 W that must ultimately be radiated. You are not destroying heat, only redistributing where it leaves the panel. Sanity check: the sum of heat radiated by all nodes is unchanged; only its spatial distribution — flatter temperature profile — differs, which is exactly the peak-shaving seen in Solution 19.

Sanity check: consistent with Solution 28's square-law scaling (20× tighter needed 400× the baseline runs).

Solution 32. Sanity check: this matches Solutions 11–13 (Newton iterated to convergence) and 14–16 (coupling sweep iterated), which cannot be collapsed into one array op.
Appendix D

Inside the Code: How Each Calculation Is Performed

Module by module — the data, the steps, and the pseudocode behind each result.

D.0How to read this appendix

The rest of this handbook tells you what the simulator computes — temperatures, survival verdicts, ranked failure positions. This appendix tells you how, at the level of the actual Python. We walk the package in the order data flows through it: a substrate file and a layout grid go in at the top, get turned into flat numpy arrays of optical and thermal properties; a circuit tree turns into a SPICE netlist; the netlist and the arrays feed a coupling loop that bounces between an electrical solve and a thermal solve until both agree; the converged field is judged for breakdown; thousands of such runs are swept under Monte-Carlo control; and finally everything is stored and drawn. Each section below takes one module or one step, names exactly what data goes in and what comes out, lists the steps in words, gives a short pseudocode block that mirrors the real source, and ends with a deep-dive box on the one trick that makes that piece work or makes it fast.

A note on the spirit of the design before we start. Almost every module in this package is written to be standalone and testable in isolation: the thermal solver imports nothing from the rest of powerpy and keeps its own copy of the physical constants; the coupling loop never knows how electrical power is obtained, because that is injected as a function; the Monte-Carlo driver never knows how a run is evaluated, because that too is injected. This dependency-injection style is why the pseudocode below is short — each module does one job and hands the next job to a callable it was given. Where a piece is not yet wired to the legacy ngspice cell model, the source says so honestly, and so do we.

The ten stages D.1 substrate properties → D.2 layout grid & conduction adjacency → D.3 circuit tree & netlist → D.4 the two-node thermal Newton loop → D.5 the vectorised constant-pattern sparse Jacobian → D.6 the electro-thermal coupling loop → D.7 electrical wiring (probed netlist → V, I, P) → D.8 breakdown criteria → D.9 Monte-Carlo & the grid failure study → D.10 storage & reporting.

D.1Substrate properties — config/substrate.py

The substrate is the board a panel is built on — here an aluminium honeycomb sandwich. Everything thermal about the board reduces to six numbers plus one derived quantity. This module gives those numbers a typed home and computes the one term the heat balance actually needs.

Data in A JSON file at data/substrates/<name>.json with the keys name, alpha_front, alpha_rear, epsilon_front, epsilon_rear, conductivity, thickness. Data out a frozen Substrate dataclass carrying those fields plus one computed property, c_cond.

The four optical numbers are the front and rear solar absorptivity α (the fraction of incident sunlight the face soaks up as heat) and the front and rear infrared emissivity ε (how readily the face radiates heat away). The two physical numbers are the through-thickness thermal conductivity k (W/m·K) and the substrate thickness t (m). The whole point of the dataclass is to collapse the last two into a single conduction coefficient that the two-node balance can use directly:

c_cond = conductivity / thickness
units W/(m²·K); with k=10 W/mK and t=0.01 m this is 10 / 0.01 = 1000 W/m²K — the front↔rear conductance per unit area

The steps the module performs are deliberately small. (1) load_substrate(name) resolves the path — packaged under data/substrates/ by default, or a local file if asked — and reads the JSON. (2) from_dict validates that all seven required keys are present, raising a clear KeyError listing any that are missing before any arithmetic happens. (3) Each value is coerced to float and frozen into the dataclass, so a loaded substrate is immutable and safe to share across every tile of an array. (4) The c_cond property is computed lazily on access, and guards against a non-positive thickness (which would mean an infinite conductance) by raising rather than returning a nonsense number.

def load_substrate(name):
    path = data/substrates/<name>.json   # or local file
    cfg  = json.load(open(path))
    return from_dict(cfg)

def from_dict(cfg):
    missing = [k for k in REQUIRED if k not in cfg]
    if missing: raise KeyError(missing)
    return Substrate(name, alpha_front, alpha_rear,
                     epsilon_front, epsilon_rear,
                     conductivity, thickness)   # all float()

# derived, lazily:
property c_cond:
    if thickness <= 0: raise ValueError
    return conductivity / thickness
The trick — validate early, derive lazily The module separates two concerns that are easy to tangle. Validation happens once, at construction, so a malformed file fails loudly at load time rather than producing a quiet wrong temperature ten modules downstream. The physics derivation (c_cond) is a property, computed only when something asks for it — so the stored object stays a faithful mirror of the file, and the one place that combines conductivity and thickness is the one place that can guard against t≤0. The frozen dataclass means no caller can accidentally mutate a shared substrate mid-sweep.

D.2Layout grid & conduction adjacency — config/layout.py

A panel is not a single cell; it is a mosaic of tiles, some real photovoltaic cells, some bare substrate, some diodes. The layout module describes that mosaic as a human-readable 2-D grid of single-character keys plus a palette that maps each key to its full properties. From that one description it produces two things the solver needs: flat per-tile property arrays, and the 4-neighbour adjacency that defines which tiles can share heat.

Data in a grid like ["AAAA","A..A","AAAA"] and a palette mapping each character to a TileType (carrying is_cell, alpha_front/rear, epsilon_front/rear, string, and the panel pitch_mm). Data out a PanelLayout exposing neighbours() (flat-index pairs) and prop_arrays() (flat numpy arrays).

A TileType is a frozen dataclass — one palette entry. Its single computed property is generates_power, which is simply is_cell: only real cells convert absorbed sunlight into electricity. Bare tiles have generates_power == False, which matters thermally — a bare tile absorbs sun but exports no power, so all of its absorbed sunlight stays behind as heat. That is why bare regions run hotter than cell regions and, through lateral conduction, can warm their neighbours.

The grid is row-major, so tile (r,c) has flat index i = r·n_cols + c. The adjacency is built by walking every tile once and, for each, recording an undirected edge to the tile on its right and the tile below it — that records every 4-neighbour bond exactly once (the left and upward bonds are already recorded by the neighbour that owns them). This list of flat-index pairs is precisely the graph the lateral-conduction solver turns into a Laplacian in D.5.

def neighbours():               # 4-neighbour edges, each once
    pairs = []
    for r in range(n_rows):
        for c in range(n_cols):
            i = r*n_cols + c
            if c+1 < n_cols: pairs.append((i, i+1))
            if r+1 < n_rows: pairs.append((i, i+n_cols))
    return pairs

def prop_arrays():              # flat per-tile numpy arrays
    tt = [palette[k] for k in grid.flatten()]
    return {
      "alpha_front":  array([t.alpha_front  for t in tt]),
      "epsilon_rear": array([t.epsilon_rear for t in tt]),
      ...,
      "generates_power": array([t.is_cell for t in tt], bool),
    }
The trick — one grid yields both the physics arrays and the adjacency Authoring a panel as text means asymmetric layouts and empty regions are trivial to write and to version-control, but the deeper payoff is that the same grid that humans read is the conduction graph. Because tile geometry is a regular lattice, neighbourliness is pure index arithmetic — no geometry library, no distance test. Emitting each edge only on the right/down pass keeps the pair list free of duplicates, so the Laplacian built from it in D.5 is symmetric by construction. And by flattening palette lookups into numpy arrays once, every per-tile property the vectorised thermal solve needs is a single contiguous array, ready for elementwise math over all tiles at once.

D.3Circuit tree & netlist — model/circuit.py

The electrical side of the array is a hierarchy: cells in series form a line, lines in parallel form a module, modules form a block, blocks form the whole circuit. The legacy code hard-wired which level was series and which parallel; the failure study needs to flip any junction and to reach in and fail one specific cell. So the circuit is rebuilt as a recursive tree of Group nodes (each combining its children in series or parallel) whose leaves are CellRef wrappers around individual cells.

Data in a prototype cell plus the five shape numbers — cells_per_line, lines_parallel, modules_per_block, n_blocks and the four *_combine rules. Data out a Circuit with a root Group, a registry cells[id] → cell for O(1) fault injection, and two netlist builders.

from_prototype builds the tree bottom-up. For each block b, module m, line l, cell c it forms the stable id B{b}.M{m}.L{l}.C{c}, deep-copies the prototype so each cell is an independent object (never a shared reference), registers it under its id, and wraps it in a CellRef. Lines combine their cells (default SERIES), modules combine their lines (default PARALLEL — the classic “two parallel circuits”), blocks combine modules (default SERIES, to reach the ~70 V bus), and the root combines blocks (default PARALLEL, one blocking diode each). The series/parallel rule is a parameter at every level.

Fault injection is then trivial: fail(id, mode) looks the cell up in O(1) and, for the default “dead”/“open” mode, calls setSeason(0) — which zeroes that cell's photocurrent so it generates nothing. Building the netlist is a recursive walk that assigns SPICE nodes: a SERIES group threads its children head-to-tail through fresh internal nodes; a PARALLEL group ties every child between the same two rails. Each distinct cell subcircuit is emitted only once.

def emit(node, n_pos, n_neg):
    if node is a CellRef:
        sub, name = node.cell.buildModel(...)
        subckts[name] = sub                 # once per distinct model
        lines.append("X{id} {n_pos} {n_neg} {name}")
        return
    if node.combine == SERIES:
        prev = n_pos
        for i, child in enumerate(children):
            nxt = n_neg if last else new_node()
            emit(child, prev, nxt); prev = nxt
    else:                                    # PARALLEL
        for child in children:
            emit(child, n_pos, n_neg)

build_probed_netlist does the same walk but, for each cell, also inserts a 0-volt source in series with it. A 0-V source is the SPICE idiom for an ammeter: it changes no voltages but its branch current is the cell current. The builder returns a probe map {id: (node+, node−, ammeter)} so that after a .op solve you can read each cell's voltage and current and form its power.

The trick — a parametric tree with stable ids and 0-V ammeters Representing the array as a recursive tree means topology becomes data: any level can be flipped series↔parallel by a single argument, and arbitrary nesting needs no new code. Deep-copying the prototype is what makes per-cell failure possible — fail one id and no other cell is touched, because they are not the same object. The hierarchical id is both the dictionary key (O(1) reach) and the SPICE instance name (traceable in the netlist). And the 0-V series source is the cleanest possible current probe: it is electrically invisible yet its branch current is exactly what the electrical solve in D.7 reads back to compute per-cell power.

D.4The two-node thermal Newton loop — solve/thermal.py

This is the heart of the simulator. Every tile has two faces — a front that sees the Sun and a rear that radiates to deep space — at slightly different temperatures because heat must conduct through the substrate between them. The solver finds, for every tile at once, the pair of steady-state temperatures (T₁ front, T₂ rear) at which heat in equals heat out on each face. It is a root-find of two coupled nonlinear residuals per tile, solved by Newton's method, fully vectorised over the array.

Data in per-tile (or scalar) area, alpha_front/rear, epsilon_front/rear; the conduction coefficient c_cond; the environment fluxes p_sun, p_albedo, p_ir; the electrical power per tile p_elec (negative = a reverse-biased dissipating cell); optional neighbours and g_lat for lateral coupling. Data out a ThermalResult with front and rear temperatures in °C, iteration count, convergence flag and the final residual.

First the module folds the inputs into the few constants each Newton step reuses. It coerces everything to length-N arrays, then precomputes the front-face conduction term CA = c_cond·A, the absorbed front sunlight qF = α_front·A·p_sun·tilt, and the rear-face absorbed albedo plus planetary-IR term qR = (α_rear·p_albedo + ε_rear·p_ir)·A·tilt. The deep-space sink contributes T_space⁴ with T_space = 2.7 K — kept for correctness though it is negligible against a cell at hundreds of kelvin.

The two residuals are the net heat onto each face. For the front: absorbed sun, minus what it radiates, minus the electrical power exported, plus the conducted heat from the rear. For the rear: absorbed albedo/IR, minus what it radiates, minus the conducted heat to the front.

f₁ = qF − ε_front·A·σ·(T₁⁴ − T_space⁴) − P_elec + CA·(T₂ − T₁)
front-face residual; zero at the converged front temperature
f₂ = qR − ε_rear·A·σ·(T₂⁴ − T_space⁴) − CA·(T₂ − T₁)
rear-face residual; zero at the converged rear temperature

When g_lat == 0 the tiles are independent, so the 2N×2N Jacobian is block-diagonal — N separate 2×2 blocks — and each block is inverted in closed form. Writing the 2×2 Jacobian of (f₁, f₂) in (T₁, T₂) as [[a, b],[c, d]], the entries are

a = −4·ε_front·A·σ·T₁³ − CA   b = c = CA   d = −4·ε_rear·A·σ·T₂³ − CA
det = a·d − b·c; the Newton update inverts each 2×2 block analytically
ΔT₁ = −(d·f₁ − b·f₂)/det   ΔT₂ = −(−c·f₁ + a·f₂)/det
applied to every tile at once as numpy array operations — no Python loop over tiles
T1 = T2 = t_init                         # arrays, length N
for it in 1..max_iter:
    f1 = qF - eF*A*S*(T1**4 - Ts4) - Pe + CA*(T2-T1)
    f2 = qR - eR*A*S*(T2**4 - Ts4)      - CA*(T2-T1)
    a = -4*eF*A*S*T1**3 - CA;  b = c = CA
    d = -4*eR*A*S*T2**3 - CA
    det = where(|a*d-b*c| < 1e-30, 1e-30, a*d-b*c)
    dT1 = -(d*f1 - b*f2)/det
    dT2 = -(-c*f1 + a*f2)/det
    T1 += dT1;  T2 += dT2
    if max(|dT1|,|dT2|) < tol: converged; break
return (T1 + T_ZERO_C, T2 + T_ZERO_C)    # back to degC

Convergence is fast because radiation is a smooth, strongly curved function — Newton's quadratic convergence reaches a micro-kelvin tolerance in a handful of iterations from a room-temperature start. The companion solve_panel wrapper pulls the property arrays and adjacency straight from a PanelLayout, zeroes the electrical power on tiles that don't generate, runs solve_thermal, and reshapes the flat result back to the grid.

The trick — a hand-inverted 2×2 over the whole array Because the independent problem has no coupling between tiles, its Jacobian is block-diagonal and every block is just 2×2. Rather than call a linear solver, the code writes the analytic inverse of a 2×2 (divide by the determinant) — and because a, b, c, d, det, f1, f2 are all length-N arrays, that single closed-form expression updates all N tiles simultaneously as vector arithmetic. The determinant is floored at 1e-30 to keep the divide safe. This fast path reproduces the legacy per-cell scipy.fsolve answer exactly (a single sunlit cell → 65.26 °C) while doing the work of N root-finds in one vectorised pass.

D.5The vectorised constant-pattern sparse Jacobian — solve/thermal.py (lateral path)

When g_lat > 0 the faces of neighbouring tiles share heat in-plane, so the tiles are no longer independent and the block-diagonal trick of D.4 no longer applies. Now we must solve one coupled 2N×2N linear system per Newton step. The art is in noticing that almost all of that matrix never changes between steps — so we build the constant part exactly once and add only the small changing part each iteration.

Data in the same per-tile arrays as D.4, plus the neighbours() edge list and a lateral conductance g_lat (from lateral_conductance(k,t,edge,pitch)=k·t·edge/pitch). Data out the same ThermalResult — but with heat now spreading: peaks drop, neighbours warm.

The in-plane heat flow on a face is a graph-Laplacian operation. From the edge list the code builds a symmetric adjacency matrix A, the degree diagonal D (how many neighbours each tile has), and the Laplacian L = D − A. Multiplying the temperature vector by L gives, at each tile, the sum of (its temperature minus each neighbour's) — exactly the net conductive outflow per unit conductance. The lateral heat flux is therefore one sparse matrix-vector product, −g_lat·(L·T), with no Python loop over neighbour pairs.

The 2N×2N Jacobian has two kinds of entries. The front↔rear conduction (the CA blocks) and the lateral coupling (g_lat·L on each face's diagonal block) are constant — they do not depend on temperature. Only the radiative term −4·ε·A·σ·T³ changes from step to step, and it lives purely on the diagonal. So the code assembles the constant structure once and, each iteration, simply adds the radiative diagonal.

J_const = [[ −CA·I − g_lat·L , CA·I ], [ CA·I , −CA·I − g_lat·L ]]
built once; CA·I is the front↔rear conductance, g_lat·L the in-plane spreading
J = J_const + diag( −4·ε·A·σ·T³ )   solve J·Δx = −f each step
only the diagonal is rebuilt per iteration; J is sparse, solved by spsolve
# --- ONCE, before the Newton loop ---
adj = sparse adjacency from neighbour pairs (symmetric)
L   = diag(degree) - adj                 # graph Laplacian
Jconst = bmat([[ -diag(CA) - g_lat*L,  diag(CA)        ],
               [  diag(CA),           -diag(CA) - g_lat*L]])

# --- each Newton step ---
f1 -= g_lat * (L @ T1)                    # lateral flux: one matvec
f2 -= g_lat * (L @ T2)
f   = concat(f1, f2)
rad = concat(-4*eF*A*S*T1**3, -4*eR*A*S*T2**3)
J   = Jconst + diag(rad)                  # add changing diagonal
dx  = spsolve(J, -f)                      # sparse solve
T1 += dx[:N];  T2 += dx[N:]

The numbers bear out the design: a 40×40 panel is 1600 tiles — a 3200×3200 system — and it converges in five iterations in about 0.046 seconds. A scipy-free dense fallback exists for environments without scipy, but the sparse path is what makes large panels practical.

The trick — build the constant structure once, add only the diagonal The expensive parts of assembling a sparse 2N×2N matrix are the adjacency, the Laplacian, and the conduction blocks — and none of them depend on temperature. By recognising that only the radiative term varies, and that it is purely diagonal, the loop reduces to: one Laplacian matvec for the lateral flux, one diagonal addition for the Jacobian, one sparse solve. There is never a Python loop over neighbour pairs inside the iteration — the entire spatial coupling is carried by the precomputed Laplacian. This is the difference between a solver that crawls and one that does 1600 coupled tiles in 46 milliseconds.

D.6The electro-thermal coupling loop — solve/coupling.py

A cell's current-voltage curve depends on its temperature; its temperature depends on the electrical power it extracts or dissipates. That is a chicken-and-egg coupling, and there is no closed form for it. The coupling loop hunts for the self-consistent pair — the temperature field and the operating point that agree with each other — by a damped fixed-point iteration.

Data in a callable power_fn(T_front) → p_elec_array, the same thermal inputs as D.4 (area, optical properties, c_cond, fluxes, tilt), and the under-relaxation factor omega (default 0.5). Data out a CoupledResult with the converged front/rear temperatures, the final per-cell power, the outer-iteration count and a convergence flag.

Each outer iteration does four things. (1) Call power_fn(T) to get the electrical power each cell extracts at the current temperatures — in production this builds the netlist, runs ngspice and reads back V·I; in tests it is a synthetic function. (2) Feed that power into solve_thermal to get the temperature field that power produces. (3) Damp the update: rather than jumping straight to the new temperature, move only a fraction omega of the way there, T += omega·(T_new − T). (4) Stop when the damped step falls below the tolerance (default 1e-3 °C). A final clean thermal solve is run at the converged power so the returned field is exactly consistent.

T = t_init (array)
for it in 1..max_outer:
    p_elec = power_fn(T)                 # electrical solve at T
    res    = solve_thermal(..., p_elec)  # thermal solve at that power
    step   = omega * (res.t_front - T)   # damped move
    T     += step
    if max(|step|) < tol_c: converged; break
# one final consistent thermal solve at the converged power
return CoupledResult(res.t_front, res.t_rear, p_elec, it, converged)
The trick — under-relaxation and an injected power function Two design choices make this loop both stable and reusable. First, under-relaxation: a raw fixed-point iteration on this coupling can overshoot and oscillate because hotter cells make less power, which cools them, which makes more power, and so on. Multiplying each update by omega < 1 bleeds energy out of that oscillation and lets it settle. Second, the electrical solve is injected as power_fn — the loop never knows or cares how the power is obtained. That keeps the loop logic pure and testable with a synthetic function today, and lets the real ngspice-backed function (D.7) drop in unchanged the moment the legacy cell model is ready.

D.7Electrical wiring: probed netlist → V, I, P — solve/electrical.py

This module is the bridge that closes the electro-thermal loop: it turns a circuit and a bus voltage into the per-cell electrical power the thermal solver needs. It does so by running a SPICE operating-point solve of the whole array and reading back each cell's voltage and current through the ammeter probes that build_probed_netlist inserted in D.3.

Data in a Circuit, a bus voltage v_bus, and an injected SPICE runner run(netlist) → Solution (real ngspice_runner() or a deterministic mock). Data out per cell P_elec = V·I, packaged either as a dict, an array, or a power_fn for the coupling loop.

The steps: (1) build_probed_netlist gives the netlist plus the probe map. (2) Append a bus driver (V_bus out 0 v_bus), the analysis card (.op) and .end, and hand the full deck to the injected runner. (3) For each cell, read its terminal voltage as V = V(node+) − V(node−) and its current as I = i(ammeter) from the returned solution, and form P_elec = V·I. The sign convention matches the thermal model exactly: a generating cell delivers power (P>0 → energy leaves as electricity → cooler), while a reverse-biased cell has V<0 so P<0 (energy dumped as heat → hotter).

def per_cell_vi(circuit, v_bus, run):
    netlist, probes = circuit.build_probed_netlist()
    deck = netlist + "V_bus out 0 {v_bus}\n.op\n.end\n"
    sol  = run(deck)                        # injected SPICE runner
    return {id: (sol.voltage(npos) - sol.voltage(nneg),
                 sol.current(amm))
            for id,(npos,nneg,amm) in probes.items()}

def make_power_fn(circuit, v_bus, run, order, set_temps):
    def power_fn(T_front):
        if set_temps: set_temps(circuit, T_front)  # push T into cells
        _, p = per_cell_power(circuit, v_bus, run, order)
        return p                              # P = V*I per cell, in order
    return power_fn

make_power_fn is what plugs into D.6. It closes over the circuit, bus voltage and runner, and returns a power_fn(T) that — each time the coupling loop calls it — optionally pushes the current temperatures into the cell models via the set_temps hook (so the IV curve's real temperature-dependence is captured), re-solves the circuit, and returns the per-cell power in the same order as the thermal area array, so the two solves line up element-for-element.

The trick — an injected runner and a Solution interface The SPICE engine is hidden behind a tiny interface: any object with voltage(node) and current(branch) is a valid Solution. Production injects a runner backed by the legacy ngspice; tests inject an in-memory mock with the same two methods. So the entire wiring — netlist assembly, bus drive, probe read-back, sign convention, ordering — is testable today without ngspice, and swapping in the real engine is a one-argument change. The ammeter branch names are lowercased to match ngspice's convention, a small detail that would otherwise silently break the current read-back.

D.8Breakdown criteria — analysis/breakdown.py

Once a run has converged, we must judge whether each cell survived. The project's decision is that a cell is destroyed if either of two independent criteria trips — one thermal, one electrical — because each can catch a failure mode the other misses.

Data in per-cell front temperature, voltage and current arrays, plus two limits: the melt temperature t_limit_c and the reverse-power limit p_rev_limit_w. Data out a BreakdownReport with three boolean masks (temperature-tripped, reverse-power-tripped, destroyed) and the reverse power per cell.
destroyed = ( T_front ≥ t_limit ) OR ( V < 0 AND |V·I| ≥ p_rev_limit )
temperature captures the steady thermal outcome; reverse power captures electrical stress on a cell driven into reverse bias

The evaluation is pure and elementwise. The temperature mask is a simple comparison against the limit. The reverse-power mask is computed with a guarded expression: the dissipated reverse power is |V·I| only where V < 0 and zero elsewhere (a forward-biased generating cell is not under reverse stress), and the mask trips where that exceeds the limit. The destroyed mask is the logical OR of the two, and a convenience property counts how many cells were destroyed.

def evaluate_breakdown(T, V, I, t_limit, p_rev_limit):
    temp  = T >= t_limit
    p_rev = where(V < 0, abs(V*I), 0.0)     # only reverse-biased cells
    rev   = p_rev >= p_rev_limit
    return BreakdownReport(temp, rev,
                           destroyed = temp OR rev,
                           reverse_power_w = p_rev)
The trick — OR two independent failure modes, guard the reverse term A cell can die two ways: it can simply get too hot (a sunlit cell with too little heat rejection), or it can be driven hard into reverse bias by healthy neighbours in its series string and burn up as a resistor even while not especially hot. Neither criterion subsumes the other, so the verdict ORs them. The where(V<0, …, 0) guard is what keeps a normal generating cell — which has V>0 and is exporting power — from ever being flagged for “reverse” power; only cells actually pushed below zero volts accumulate reverse dissipation.

D.9Monte-Carlo & the grid failure study — analysis/montecarlo.py, analysis/study.py

A single run tells you what one failure pattern does. The interesting questions are statistical: which single cell, if it fails, is worst? How does the array degrade as more cells fail? Across many random patterns, what is the distribution of peak temperatures? The Monte-Carlo module generates the failure patterns and drives the sweep; the study module ties the layout, the lateral solver and those patterns together.

Data in a list of cell ids (or tile indices), a sampling mode, and an injected evaluate(pattern) → record. Data out one record per run, plus ranking and statistical helpers (standard error, runs-needed).

Three pattern generators choose which failures to simulate, all from a seeded RNG for reproducibility. position_patterns fails each cell in turn — O(N) runs to find the worst single location. count_patterns draws, for each failure count k, a number of random k-cell sets — degradation versus number of failures. random_patterns fails each cell independently with probability p_fail across many samples — the full outcome distribution. run_sweep then evaluates every pattern through the injected evaluate and stamps each record with its run id, the failed ids and the failure count; rank sorts the records by any metric (default: hottest first).

The statistics close the loop on “how many runs are enough.” standard_error is the sample standard deviation (with ddof=1, the unbiased estimator) divided by √N. Because that error shrinks like 1/√N, runs_needed inverts the relation to estimate how many runs are required to reach a target error: N_target = N_now·(SE_now/SE_target)².

# montecarlo: pattern generators (seeded RNG)

random_patterns(ids,p,n) -> n masks, each cell fails w.p. p

The study module specialises this to the panel-with-lateral-conduction case. make_pe(layout, failed, …) builds the per-tile electrical-power map: healthy cells extract a positive healthy_w, cells in the failed set dissipate a negative reverse_w (they are driven into reverse bias), and bare tiles make zero. failure_sweep then solves each pattern through solve_panel with lateral conduction, and records the peak temperature, the mean, and how many tiles exceed the melt limit — so the ranking accounts for heat spreading to neighbours.

# study: each pattern -> a per-tile power map -> lateral solve

The trick — injected evaluation and 1/√N run budgeting The driver never knows how a run is evaluated — evaluate is injected — so the same sweep machinery works whether a run means a full ngspice electro-thermal solve or a fast lateral-only panel solve. That is why the production failure study can reuse the tested Monte-Carlo generators verbatim. And the statistics make the sweep honest: rather than guessing a run count, you measure the current standard error and use the 1/√N law to compute exactly how many more runs buy you the precision you want — quadrupling the runs halves the error, no more, no less.

Appendix E

Physics Background

The underlying physics, taught from the ground up: heat transfer, the solar cell, reverse bias, and the orbit.

E.1Heat Transfer Modes in Space

Everything in this handbook ultimately comes back to a single question: where does the heat go? A solar array in orbit absorbs energy from the Sun every second it is illuminated. If that energy had nowhere to go, the array's temperature would rise without limit until it melted. It does not melt — usually — because it is constantly throwing energy back out into space. To understand the simulator, you must first understand the three ways heat can move from one place to another, and which of them are available to a spacecraft.

Heat is simply energy in transit because of a temperature difference. Whenever two regions are at different temperatures, nature tries to even them out, and the flow of energy that does this is what we call heat. Physicists count exactly three mechanisms by which it travels: conduction, convection, and radiation. On Earth all three happen at once and we rarely separate them. In the vacuum of space, one of them simply switches off, and that single fact dominates the entire thermal design of a satellite.

ConductionThe transfer of heat through a material, or between two materials in direct contact, without the material itself moving. Energy is passed along from one jiggling atom to its neighbour, like a rumour spreading down a row of people who never leave their seats.
ConvectionThe transfer of heat by the bulk motion of a fluid — a liquid or a gas. Hot air rises, cool air sinks, and the moving fluid physically carries its thermal energy from one place to another. This is how a radiator warms a room and how the wind cools your skin.
RadiationThe transfer of heat by electromagnetic waves — chiefly infrared light. Every object above absolute zero glows, emitting energy as light that travels through empty space at the speed of light and needs no material medium at all. This is how the Sun's warmth reaches us across 150 million kilometres of nothing.
The key factConvection needs a fluid. Space is a near-perfect vacuum — there is essentially no air, no gas, nothing to flow. So in space there is no convection. A spacecraft cannot be cooled by a breeze or a fan, because there is nothing for the fan to push. This is the single most counter-intuitive idea for a newcomer from terrestrial engineering, and it changes everything.

On the ground, convection is our workhorse. A hot computer chip is cooled by a fan blowing air across a heatsink; a hot cup of coffee cools mostly because warm air rises off its surface and carries energy away. Remove the air and both of those mechanisms vanish. The chip and the coffee can now lose heat in only two ways: by conducting it into whatever they are touching, and by radiating it away as infrared light. For an isolated object touching nothing — like the outermost surface of a satellite facing deep space — conduction has nowhere to go either. That leaves radiation as the only escape route.

AnalogyImagine three ways to empty a bathtub. Conduction is letting the water soak slowly into a sponge pressed against the side — it only works where things touch. Convection is scooping water out with a bucket and carrying it away — fast, but you need the bucket (the fluid) to carry it. Radiation is the water evaporating into the air all on its own — slow, but it happens even if nothing is touching the tub and there is no bucket. In space, someone has taken away your sponge and your bucket. Evaporation is all you have left.

This is why the simulator treats radiation so carefully and why the Stefan–Boltzmann law (Section E.2) sits at the heart of the energy balance. Conduction still matters — it is how heat spreads sideways through the panel from a hot spot to cooler neighbours, and how it moves from a cell down into the substrate it is glued to. But the only way energy ultimately leaves the spacecraft is by being radiated into the cold dark.

Going deeper"Near-perfect vacuum" is not quite "perfect". In low Earth orbit there are still a few hundred thousand to a million gas molecules per cubic centimetre — but compare that with roughly 2.5×10¹⁹ molecules per cubic centimetre at sea level. The residual gas is so thin that the rate of convective heat transfer is smaller than radiation by many orders of magnitude, so we are completely justified in setting convection to exactly zero in the model. The same reasoning lets us ignore conduction to the surroundings — there is nothing out there to conduct into except the structure itself.

There is an everyday version of this you have probably held in your hands: a vacuum flask (a "Thermos"). It keeps coffee hot for hours by exploiting precisely the physics above. Its double wall has the air pumped out of the gap, killing convection and conduction across that gap. The inner surface is silvered to be a poor radiator, killing most of the radiation too. A satellite is, in effect, a coffee inside a flask with the lid off — surrounded by vacuum, able to lose heat only by glowing.

Common mistakeNewcomers often assume "space is cold, so things in space get cold quickly." Space has no temperature in the everyday sense — it is nearly empty, so it cannot cool you by contact or by a cold wind. A sunlit object in vacuum can in fact overheat badly, precisely because it has no convection to dump heat into and must rely on the comparatively slow process of radiation. Cold surroundings help (a cold sky is an excellent place to radiate to), but the rate is set by radiation physics, not by the chill of the void.

E.2Radiation and the Stefan–Boltzmann Law

Since radiation is the only way heat finally leaves a spacecraft, we need a precise law for how fast an object radiates. That law is the Stefan–Boltzmann law, and it is one of the most important equations in the whole simulator. Before we write it down, we need three ideas: the blackbody, emissivity, and absorptivity.

BlackbodyAn idealised object that absorbs all radiation falling on it (reflecting nothing — hence "black") and, at any given temperature, emits the maximum possible amount of thermal radiation. It is the theoretical gold standard against which real surfaces are measured. No real surface is a perfect blackbody, but many come close in the infrared.
Emissivity (ε)A number between 0 and 1 that says how good a real surface is at emitting radiation, compared with a perfect blackbody at the same temperature. ε = 1 is a perfect emitter (a blackbody); ε = 0.05 is a shiny, polished metal that barely glows. A typical spacecraft radiator paint has ε around 0.8–0.9.
Absorptivity (α)A number between 0 and 1 that says how much of the radiation falling on a surface gets absorbed (rather than reflected). α = 1 absorbs everything (a blackbody); a white paint or a mirror has a low α for sunlight, which is why white things stay cooler in the Sun.
Prad = ε · σ · A · T⁴
Prad = power radiated away (watts, W)  ·  ε = emissivity (0–1, no units)  ·  σ = Stefan–Boltzmann constant = 5.670367×10⁻⁸ W·m⁻²·K⁻⁴  ·  A = radiating area (m²)  ·  T = absolute temperature (kelvin, K)

Read this equation slowly, because every symbol earns its place. The power an object radiates grows with its emissivity ε (a better emitter glows more), with its area A (a bigger surface glows more), and — most dramatically — with the fourth power of its absolute temperature T. That fourth power is the single most important feature of thermal physics in space.

Why T⁴ matters so muchBecause the radiated power depends on T⁴, doubling the absolute temperature multiplies the radiated power by 2⁴ = 16. A surface that runs only a little hotter sheds dramatically more heat. This is why a hot spot is partly self-limiting (it radiates furiously) but also why temperatures climb steeply once the heat input rises — the balance point shifts as the fourth root of the power, so big changes in heat make only modest changes in temperature, and vice-versa.
Kelvin (K)The absolute temperature scale. It starts at absolute zero — the coldest possible temperature, where atomic motion is minimal — and uses the same size of degree as Celsius. To convert: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. Water freezes at 273.15 K and boils at 373.15 K. The T in the Stefan–Boltzmann law must be in kelvin, because T⁴ only behaves correctly on an absolute scale that starts at true zero. Using Celsius would be a catastrophic error.
Common mistakePlugging Celsius into σT⁴. A cell at 39 °C is at 312.15 K. Using "39" instead of "312.15" understates the radiated power by a factor of (312.15/39)⁴ ≈ 4100. Always convert to kelvin first. In the code this is handled once, centrally, but when you check a result by hand, remember the +273.15.

Now, where does the σT⁴ shape come from? It is not arbitrary. In the late 1800s, Josef Stefan found it experimentally and Ludwig Boltzmann derived it from thermodynamics; later, Max Planck explained it from the quantum nature of light. The deep reason is that a hot object emits light across a whole spectrum of wavelengths, and as it gets hotter two things happen at once: it emits at every wavelength more strongly, and the peak of its glow shifts toward shorter (bluer, more energetic) wavelengths. Adding up the energy across the entire spectrum, those two effects combine to give a total that scales as the fourth power of temperature. You do not need the full derivation to use the simulator, but it helps to know the T⁴ is a real, derived result and not a fudge factor.

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
The conversion you will use constantly. Examples used elsewhere in this handbook: idle cell 65 °C = 338.15 K; working cell 39 °C = 312.15 K; failed hot-spot cell 187 °C = 460.15 K.

There is a beautiful and useful symmetry between emitting and absorbing, captured by Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation.

Kirchhoff's lawAt a given wavelength and temperature, a surface's emissivity equals its absorptivity: a good emitter is a good absorber, and a good reflector is a poor emitter. The same surface property governs both directions of energy flow. Physically this must be true, otherwise you could build a surface that absorbs more than it emits and heats up forever next to a wall at the same temperature — violating the second law of thermodynamics.
AnalogyThink of a surface as a doorway for radiation. A wide-open doorway (ε near 1) lets energy stream out easily — but it equally lets energy stream in easily, so it is also a strong absorber (α near 1). A nearly closed door (a shiny mirror, ε near 0) blocks energy in both directions: it barely glows and barely absorbs. You cannot have a one-way door for heat radiation — Kirchhoff forbids it at any single wavelength.
Going deeper — the spectral trick spacecraft engineers loveKirchhoff's law holds at each wavelength, and the Sun's radiation arrives mostly as short-wavelength visible light while a room-temperature spacecraft emits mostly long-wavelength infrared. Because these two bands barely overlap, a clever surface can have a low α for sunlight (it reflects the incoming Sun) yet a high ε in the infrared (it readily dumps its own heat). White paints and optical solar reflectors do exactly this — they stay cool in sunlight. There is no contradiction with Kirchhoff because α and ε are equal only when compared at the same wavelength.

An everyday example: stand in front of a bonfire on a cold night. The side of you facing the fire feels hot even though the air between you and the flames may be cold — that is radiation reaching you directly, needing no warm air to carry it. Now turn around: your back radiates its own heat into the cold night and feels chilly. You are simultaneously absorbing (front) and emitting (back), both governed by the σT⁴ law and Kirchhoff's symmetry, exactly as a satellite surface does.

E.3Conduction and Fourier's Law

Radiation moves heat out of the spacecraft. Conduction moves heat around inside it — from a cell into the panel it is glued to, and sideways across the panel from a hot region to its cooler neighbours. Getting this right is what stops a single failed cell from being treated as if it were thermally alone in the universe; its heat actually leaks into the surrounding structure, and the model must account for that.

The governing rule is Fourier's law of heat conduction. In its simplest, most useful form for our purposes it says: the rate of heat flow through a path is proportional to the temperature difference across that path. Push harder on the temperature difference and more heat flows, exactly like pushing harder on a voltage drives more current.

Q = C · ΔT
Q = heat-flow rate (W)  ·  C = thermal conductance of the path (W/K)  ·  ΔT = temperature difference across the path (K, or equivalently °C since it is a difference)
Thermal conductance (C)How easily heat flows along a given path, measured in watts per kelvin (W/K). A high conductance means even a small temperature difference moves a lot of heat. It is the thermal twin of electrical conductance (the reciprocal of resistance).

Conductance is not a fundamental material property — it depends on the material and the geometry of the path. For heat flowing straight through a slab of material, the conductance is built from three ingredients:

C = k · A / L
k = thermal conductivity of the material (W·m⁻¹·K⁻¹), an intrinsic property — high for metals, low for plastics  ·  A = cross-sectional area the heat flows through (m²)  ·  L = length of the path, i.e. the thickness the heat must cross (m)
Thermal conductivity (k)An intrinsic property of a material: how readily it carries heat per unit area per unit thickness. Copper is about 400, aluminium about 200, glass about 1, and a foam insulator about 0.03 (all in W·m⁻¹·K⁻¹). Big k means a great conductor of heat.

Let us make this concrete with the worked number used in the simulator. Suppose heat must cross from a cell down through a thin bonding layer into the substrate. Take an effective conductivity-times-area term of 10 (in consistent units) and a path thickness of 0.01 m (one centimetre). Then:

ccond = 10 / 0.01 = 1000 W/K
The vertical (through-thickness) conductance from a cell into the substrate beneath it. A large value: it means cell and substrate are tightly coupled thermally — a small temperature difference moves a lot of heat between them, so they tend to track each other closely.
Thin is conductive, thick is insulatingConductance C = k·A/L is inversely proportional to thickness L. A thin layer (small L) has a large conductance — heat crosses it easily. A thick layer has a small conductance — it insulates. This is why c_cond comes out so large (1000 W/K): the bonding path is very thin, so it offers almost no resistance to vertical heat flow.

The second place conduction appears is lateral spreading — heat flowing sideways through the thin metal facesheet of the panel, from a hot cell to its neighbours. This is what lets a hot spot "bleed" energy into the surrounding cells instead of building up locally. The lateral conductance between adjacent cells is built the same way, from conductivity, the facesheet thickness, the shared edge length, and the spacing (pitch) between cell centres:

glat = k · t · (edge) / (pitch)
k = facesheet thermal conductivity  ·  t = facesheet thickness (m)  ·  edge = length of the shared boundary between two cells (m)  ·  pitch = centre-to-centre distance between the two cells (m). Here A = t·edge is the cross-section the lateral heat flows through, and L = pitch is the distance it travels.
glat ≈ 0.045 W/K (example value per cell-to-cell link)
Far smaller than the 1000 W/K vertical coupling. Heat spreads sideways through a thin facesheet only sluggishly, because the cross-section (thickness × edge) is tiny and the path (pitch) is comparatively long.
Watch the scale differenceVertical conductance (≈1000 W/K) dwarfs lateral conductance (≈0.045 W/K) by a factor of tens of thousands. Physically: a cell is glued flat against the substrate over its whole face (large A, tiny L), but connects to a neighbour only through a thin sliver of facesheet edge (tiny A, larger L). This is exactly why a hot spot stays localised — heat pours down into the substrate readily but creeps sideways only slowly, so a failed cell can reach a high local temperature before its neighbours feel much of it.
AnalogyConductance is the width of a pipe and ΔT is the water pressure. A wide pipe (high conductance) lets a lot of water through for the same pressure; a narrow pipe (low conductance) trickles. The vertical path into the substrate is a fire hose; the lateral path to a neighbour is a drinking straw. Same pressure (temperature difference), wildly different flow.

E.4The Solar Cell and the IV Curve

A solar cell turns sunlight into electricity. To model what it does to the thermal picture, we must understand its electrical behaviour, and that behaviour is captured by a single graph: the current–voltage curve, universally called the IV curve. Every important number about a cell can be read off this one curve.

Current (I)The rate of flow of electric charge, measured in amperes (A). In a solar cell, sunlight knocks electrons loose and the current is the stream of those electrons delivered to the circuit. More light, more current.
Voltage (V)The electrical "pressure" or potential difference that pushes the current around the circuit, measured in volts (V). A single solar cell produces a modest voltage; many are wired in series to add their voltages up to something useful.
Power (P)The rate of energy delivery, P = V × I, measured in watts (W). This is what the cell is for — electrical power to run the spacecraft. Power is the product of how hard you push (V) and how much flows (I).

If you connect a solar cell to an adjustable load and sweep the load from a dead short to a fully open circuit while keeping the sunlight steady, you trace out the IV curve. It has a characteristic knee shape, and four landmark points define it:

Isc — short-circuit currentThe current when the cell's terminals are shorted together (V = 0). This is the most current the cell can deliver. No voltage means no power here, but it sets the height of the curve.
Voc — open-circuit voltageThe voltage when no current is drawn (I = 0, open circuit). This is the most voltage the cell can produce. No current means no power here either, but it sets the width of the curve.
Imp, Vmp — current and voltage at maximum powerThe single operating point, somewhere around the knee, where the product P = V·I is largest. Here the cell delivers the most useful power.
The Maximum Power Point (MPP)Because power is V×I, and V is largest where I is zero and vice-versa, the maximum of their product sits at the bend of the knee — not at either extreme. Spacecraft power systems include electronics (a "maximum power point tracker") whose entire job is to keep the array operating right at this sweet spot as light and temperature change. Operating off the MPP wastes available power.
Fill Factor (FF)A measure of how "square" the IV curve is — how close the cell comes to the ideal of delivering its full I_sc at its full V_oc simultaneously. Defined as the ratio of the actual maximum power to that ideal rectangle:
FF = Pmp / (Voc · Isc)
Pmp = Vmp·Imp = the real maximum power (W)  ·  Voc·Isc = the theoretical maximum if the cell delivered full current at full voltage at once (W). FF is between 0 and 1; good cells reach 0.8–0.85. Higher is better — it means a sharper, more rectangular knee.

The cells modelled here are triple-junction gallium-arsenide (GaAs) cells — the workhorse of modern spacecraft. A single ordinary silicon cell makes only about 0.6 V. A triple-junction cell stacks three different semiconductor layers, each tuned to a different slice of the solar spectrum, so it captures more of the sunlight and produces a higher voltage — about 2.3–2.6 V per cell — at roughly 28% efficiency. That high per-cell voltage and high efficiency is exactly why they are worth their considerable cost in space.

Triple-junction GaAs cellA solar cell built from three stacked semiconductor junctions of different materials, each absorbing a different band of the spectrum (typically a GaInP top cell, a GaAs middle cell, and a Ge bottom cell). The three add their voltages in series within one cell, giving ~2.3–2.6 V and ~28% conversion efficiency — roughly double a single silicon cell.
EfficiencyThe fraction of incoming sunlight power that the cell converts into electrical power. At ~28%, a triple-junction cell turns just over a quarter of the sunlight into electricity; the remaining ~72% must be dealt with as heat (Section E.5).

Crucially for a thermal handbook, efficiency drops as the cell gets hotter. The dominant effect is on voltage: as temperature rises, V_oc and V_mp fall (by a few millivolts per kelvin per junction), and since power is V×I, the delivered power and the efficiency fall with them. A hot cell is a worse cell. This couples electricity and temperature together: heat lowers the electrical output, and the lost output (plus the absorbed sunlight) shows up as still more heat.

AnalogyThe IV curve is like a person pushing a swing. At one extreme they push very hard but the swing barely moves (high voltage, no current — open circuit). At the other they let the swing fly but give it no push (high current, no voltage — short circuit). The most work per second (power) happens in between, with a firm push and a good swing — that is the maximum power point. And a tired (hot) person pushes with less force: the whole curve sags, and the best they can manage drops.
Going deeper — why voltage falls with heatA solar cell is a diode in the light. Its open-circuit voltage is set by the balance between light-generated current and the diode's own "dark" leakage current. As temperature rises, that leakage current climbs steeply (it depends exponentially on temperature through the semiconductor bandgap), so it takes less forward voltage to balance the same light current — V_oc falls. The current I_sc actually rises very slightly with temperature, but the voltage loss dominates, so net power and efficiency decline with heat.

E.5Energy Balance of a Cell

We can now combine radiation, conduction, and the solar cell into the central bookkeeping idea of the whole simulator: the energy balance. The principle is conservation of energy — energy is never created or destroyed. In a steady state, every watt that goes into a cell must come back out. The cell's temperature settles at exactly the value that makes "energy in" equal "energy out".

Energy comes in from the Sun. Let us tally it for one cell. The cell area is A = 0.003 m² (a few square centimetres — about a large postage stamp). At Earth's distance the sunlight delivers 1367 W per square metre (Section E.8). Multiplying area by that flux, accounting for the cell's absorptivity, the absorbed solar power for one cell is:

qsun ≈ 3.978 W absorbed by one cell (A = 0.003 m²)
This is the energy budget the cell must dispose of every second. Where it goes is the whole question.

That incoming ~3.978 W has only two exits: it leaves as electricity (useful work carried away down the wires) or it leaves as heat (radiated to space and conducted into the substrate). There is no third option — conservation of energy forbids it.

qsun = Pelectrical + Pheat
Absorbed sunlight (≈3.978 W) splits into electrical output and waste heat. At ~28% efficiency, about 0.28 × 3.978 ≈ 1.1 W leaves as electricity and the remaining ≈2.9 W must be dumped as heat.
The cooling effect of doing workHere is the elegant consequence. A working cell exports about 28% of the absorbed sunlight as electricity — that energy leaves down the wires and never has to be radiated away as heat. An idle cell (open circuit, producing no current, delivering no power) converts none of the sunlight to electricity; the full ~3.978 W must instead be shed as heat by radiation. More heat to radiate means a higher equilibrium temperature.

This explains a result that surprises newcomers: a working cell runs cooler than an idle one. Using the verified figures from this handbook, a cell actively delivering power settles at about 39 °C, while the same cell sitting idle in the same sunlight settles at about 65 °C. The difference — some 26 °C — is precisely the energy that the working cell is shipping out as electricity instead of having to radiate as heat.

AnalogyImagine a person in a sunny room who must get rid of all the energy they absorb. If they do useful work — turning a hand crank that drives a generator — some of that energy leaves the room as electricity through the wire, and they sweat less. If they just sit there idle, every bit of absorbed energy has to leave as body heat, and they get hotter. The cell is the same: working it gives the energy a second door (the wires) to leave by, so less has to escape as heat and it runs cooler.

To find the actual equilibrium temperature, the simulator sets the heat term equal to the radiated power from Section E.2 (plus the conduction terms from Section E.3) and solves for the temperature T that balances the books. Because radiation goes as T⁴, this is solved numerically, but the idea is simple: the cell heats up until it radiates away exactly as much as it needs to dispose of, and there it sits.

Common mistakeAssuming "more sunlight always means hotter cells, regardless of load." It is the heat term, not the absorbed sunlight, that sets temperature. A cell delivering full power in bright sun can be cooler than an idle cell in the same sun, because the working cell is exporting a quarter of the energy electrically. Always ask: of the energy absorbed, how much is leaving as electricity, and how much is left over to be radiated as heat?
Going deeper — the feedback loopNotice the coupling: heat lowers the cell's voltage and efficiency (Section E.4), which means a hotter cell exports less electricity, which leaves more energy to be radiated as heat, which raises the temperature further. This is a feedback loop. For a healthy cell it is gentle and self-settling. For a failed cell driven into reverse bias (Section E.6), a similar loop runs away dramatically, because the cell is now absorbing electrical power instead of exporting it.

E.6Reverse Bias and the Bypass Diode

So far every cell has been a generous little engine: it absorbs sunlight and pushes out electricity. But cells are wired in long series strings — connected end to end so their voltages add up to the bus voltage the spacecraft needs. Series wiring has a hidden danger, and it is the most violent thermal event in this entire handbook.

Series stringA chain of cells connected in a line, positive terminal to negative terminal, so their voltages add. The same current must flow through every cell in the chain — there is only one path. This is how you build a high enough voltage from cells that each make only ~2.4 V.

The trouble with "the same current must flow through every cell" is this: what happens if one cell can no longer produce that current? Suppose a single cell is shadowed, cracked, or failed, so it can only generate a trickle while its healthy neighbours are still pumping out the full string current. The string insists on its current — the healthy cells are still generating it — but the weak cell cannot supply it. Something has to give.

Forced into reverse biasThe healthy cells in the string have spare voltage. They use it to force the failed cell's current through anyway, by driving the failed cell backwards — applying a large negative voltage across it. Instead of being a generator, the failed cell becomes a load: it now has current pushed through it the wrong way by a large reverse voltage. This is called reverse bias.
Reverse biasOperating a cell (or diode) with the voltage applied in the opposite direction to its normal forward, power-generating direction. A normally-generating cell sits at, say, +2.4 V. A reverse-biased failed cell can be pushed to a large negative voltage — in our model around −20 V — by the rest of the healthy string.

Now apply the power equation P = V × I, but with a negative voltage. With the failed cell at about −20 V carrying the string current, the model gives a power of about −9.6 W. The minus sign is the whole story:

P = V · I ≈ (−20 V) × (string current) ≈ −9.6 W
Negative power means the cell is absorbing electrical energy, not generating it. The healthy cells are pumping ~9.6 W of electrical power into this one tiny failed cell. That power has nowhere to go but into heat — right there, inside an area of only 0.003 m².
Why this is so dangerousA healthy cell deals with ~3 W of waste heat and settles near 39 °C. The failed cell must now dispose of that plus roughly 9.6 W of reverse electrical power being forced into it — far more heat, concentrated in the same tiny postage-stamp area. The energy balance of Section E.5 still holds, but with a far larger heat term, the temperature that balances σT⁴ climbs to about 187 °C — a localised hot spot hot enough to threaten the materials around it (Section E.7).

AnalogyPicture a bucket brigade passing water down a line — each person must pass the same amount the others are passing. If one person freezes up (the failed cell), the others, determined to keep the line moving, physically grab them and shove the water through their arms by force. All that forcing exhausts and overheats the one weak link — they take the entire strain of the line. The healthy cells "forcing the current" through the failed cell is exactly that brutal shove, and the −9.6 W is the strain dumped into one person.

The standard engineering remedy is the bypass diode.

Bypass diodeA diode wired in parallel across a group of cells (often one per sub-string), oriented so that it stays "off" and invisible during normal operation. If a cell fails and the string tries to drive it into deep reverse bias, the bypass diode "turns on" and offers the string current an alternative path around the failed cell. It thereby clamps the reverse voltage to a small value (roughly the diode's own forward drop, ~0.7 V, instead of −20 V) and dramatically limits the hot-spot power and temperature.
What the diode buys youBy giving the current a detour, the bypass diode prevents the healthy cells from forcing their full current through the failed cell at a huge reverse voltage. The reverse voltage is clamped from ~−20 V to ~−0.7 V, so the reverse power that becomes heat collapses from ~9.6 W toward a fraction of a watt, and the 187 °C hot spot is tamed. You lose the output of that sub-string, but you save the panel.
Model status — read thisThe current version of the simulator models the reverse-bias hot spot (the −20 V, −9.6 W, 187 °C case) without a bypass diode. The bypass diode is a planned model addition, not yet implemented. When reading hot-spot results from the present model, remember they represent the unprotected, worst-case scenario. Adding the diode will clamp the reverse voltage and substantially lower the predicted hot-spot temperature — exactly the protective effect described above.

E.7The Honeycomb Substrate and Melting

Why does a 187 °C hot spot matter? To answer that, we need to look at what the cells are actually mounted on — the substrate — and what heat does to it.

SubstrateThe structural panel the solar cells are bonded to. It must be flat, stiff, light, and able to spread heat. The standard choice in spacecraft is an aluminium honeycomb sandwich.
Aluminium honeycomb sandwichA panel made of two thin aluminium facesheets bonded to a core of aluminium foil folded into a honeycomb of hexagonal cells — like the cardboard core of a strong door, but in metal. The honeycomb core gives enormous stiffness for almost no weight, because the material sits where it does the most structural good and is mostly empty space elsewhere.
Why honeycomb?A spacecraft pays an enormous price per kilogram to launch. The honeycomb sandwich is the engineer's answer to "be as stiff and flat as possible while weighing almost nothing." The thin facesheets carry the bending loads (far apart, like the flanges of an I-beam), the lightweight core holds them apart and resists shear, and the whole assembly is a small fraction of the weight of a solid plate of the same stiffness.

The cells are not welded to the panel — they are glued with a thin adhesive layer, and there is often a thin insulating layer too. Both the adhesive and the aluminium itself have limits. Aluminium is a fine structural metal, but it softens well before it fully melts (pure aluminium melts at about 660 °C, but alloys and the thin foil core lose strength much earlier). The adhesives are organic and degrade or soften at far lower temperatures still — often a couple of hundred degrees Celsius. A localised 187 °C hot spot is comfortably into the range where adhesives begin to weaken and outgas, and sustained or higher temperatures threaten the bond and the core.

Why "concentrated" is the dangerous wordThe total reverse power (~9.6 W) is not large in absolute terms — a phone charger handles more. The danger is concentration: all of it is dumped into a single cell's footprint, 0.003 m², a patch the size of a thumbnail. Power per unit area (heat flux) is enormous, and there is no convection (Section E.1) to carry it away and no fast lateral conduction (the weak g_lat of Section E.3) to spread it out. The heat piles up locally and the temperature spikes right under that one cell.
AnalogyHolding a magnifying glass to focus sunlight onto a single spot of paper. The total sunlight is harmless spread over your hand, but focused onto a thumbnail-sized point it chars and ignites the paper. A reverse-biased cell focuses electrical power into a thumbnail-sized point on the panel. And unlike on Earth, there is no breeze to blow the heat away — in vacuum the focused spot has only slow radiation and slow sideways conduction to relieve it, so it gets dangerously hot.

This is the chain that makes the whole simulator worth building. A single failed cell (E.6) is forced into reverse bias, dumps ~9.6 W of concentrated heat into a thumbnail of honeycomb panel, and — with no convection to help and only sluggish lateral conduction to spread it — reaches ~187 °C, hot enough to soften the adhesive and threaten the substrate. The simulator's job is to predict exactly when and where this happens, so designers can add bypass diodes (E.6) and choose materials and layouts that keep every hot spot below the danger line.

Going deeper — outgassingThere is a second, sneakier hazard beyond softening. When organic adhesives get hot in vacuum they outgas — they release volatile molecules. In the open vacuum of space these vapours can drift and re-condense on nearby cold surfaces such as optical sensors, lenses, or radiators, fogging them and degrading the whole spacecraft. So a hot spot can damage hardware far from the failed cell itself. Keeping temperatures down is therefore not only about the panel's own integrity but about protecting the rest of the satellite.

E.8The Orbit Environment

Everything so far assumed sunlight pours onto the cell. But a satellite travels around a planet, and as it does, its thermal environment changes constantly. This final section assembles the external energy inputs the simulator must track: direct sun, eclipse, albedo, planetary infrared, pointing, and the relentless thermal cycling that all of this produces.

Direct sunlight and the inverse-square law

The Sun's energy spreads out as it travels away from it, thinning over an ever-larger sphere. At Earth's distance — one astronomical unit (1 AU) — the sunlight arrives at about 1367 W/m². This figure is called the solar constant. Travel farther out and the same total power is spread over a bigger sphere, so the intensity falls as the inverse square of the distance.

Astronomical unit (AU)The average Earth–Sun distance, about 150 million kilometres, used as a convenient yardstick for distances in the solar system. Mars orbits at about 1.52 AU.
P(d) = P1AU / d² (d in AU)
P1AU = 1367 W/m². At Mars (d = 1.52 AU): 1367 / 1.52² ≈ 591.7 W/m². At d = 2 AU: 1367 / 4 ≈ 341.75 W/m². Doubling the distance quarters the sunlight — that is the inverse square at work.

Why it gets dimmer with distanceThe Sun radiates a fixed total power in all directions. At distance d that power is smeared over the surface of a sphere of radius d, whose area is 4πd². Double d and the sphere's area quadruples, so the power landing on each square metre falls to a quarter. This is the inverse-square law, and it is why a Mars-orbiting array sees less than half the sunlight an Earth-orbiting one does, and must be larger to make the same power.

Eclipse

As the satellite orbits, the planet periodically blocks the Sun — the spacecraft passes into the planet's shadow, an eclipse. During eclipse the direct sunlight, and the sunlight reflected from the planet (albedo, below), both fall to zero. The only external warmth left is the planet's own infrared glow. The array suddenly has nothing coming in but its own heat leaking away, and it cools rapidly.

EclipseThe portion of an orbit spent in the planet's shadow, where direct sunlight (and reflected sunlight) drop to zero. For a low Earth orbit this can be a third of every ~90-minute orbit. Planetary infrared (below) continues throughout.

Albedo — reflected sunlight

AlbedoThe fraction of incoming sunlight a planet reflects back into space. Earth's average albedo is about 0.3 — clouds, ice, and oceans bounce back roughly 30% of the sunlight that hits them. A satellite over the daylit side of Earth therefore receives a second, weaker dose of sunlight reflected up off the planet.
qalbedo ≈ 44.325 W/m² (example incident albedo flux)
Reflected sunlight reaching the array, set by the solar flux, Earth's albedo (~0.3), and how much of the lit Earth the array can "see" (a view factor). It vanishes in eclipse along with the direct Sun, since there is no sunlight to reflect.

Planetary infrared

A planet is warmed by the Sun and, being warm, glows in the infrared — exactly the Stefan–Boltzmann radiation of Section E.2, just from a planet-sized blackbody. Earth radiates as though it were a body at roughly 255 K. Pointing and tilt

Sunlight delivers its full intensity only to a surface facing it square-on. Tilt the panel away from the Sun and it intercepts less — the effective flux is reduced by the cosine of the tilt angle. With tilt in two directions (call them α and β), the factor is the product of two cosines.

fluxeffective = flux × cos α · cos β
α, β = tilt angles away from facing the Sun in two perpendicular directions. cos 0° = 1 (full sun, square-on). Example: cos 30° = 0.866, so a 30° tilt costs about 13% of the flux.
AnalogyCatching rain in a bucket. Held flat-up, the bucket catches the most. Tilt it and its opening as the rain sees it shrinks by the cosine of the tilt — at 30° you catch about 87% as much. A solar panel "catches" sunlight the same way: face it dead-on for full power, and every degree of tilt costs you a cosine.

Thermal cycling and fatigue

Put these together over one orbit. In sunlight the array is bathed in up to ~1367 W/m² of direct Sun plus albedo plus planetary IR, and runs hot. Minutes later it slips into eclipse: direct Sun and albedo vanish, only the faint planetary IR remains, and the array plunges cold. Then it swings back into sunlight and heats up again. Every orbit — perhaps sixteen times a day in low Earth orbit — the whole array is heated and cooled through a large temperature swing. This is thermal cycling.

Thermal cyclingThe repeated heating and cooling of the structure as it passes in and out of sunlight each orbit. Over a multi-year mission this means tens of thousands of hot–cold cycles.
Why cycling causes fatigueDifferent materials expand and contract by different amounts when heated (different coefficients of thermal expansion). A cell, its adhesive, and the aluminium facesheet all want to change size by different amounts every cycle, so they tug and shear against one another at their bond lines. One cycle does no harm; tens of thousands of cycles slowly work-harden, crack, and delaminate the joints — exactly like bending a paperclip back and forth until it snaps. This is thermal fatigue, and it is a leading cause of long-term solar-array degradation.
AnalogyPouring boiling water into a cold glass, then ice water, then boiling again, over and over. Any one pour might be survivable, but the repeated expansion and contraction eventually cracks the glass. The orbiting array endures this every 90 minutes for years; thermal fatigue is the slow accumulation of those tiny strains until a bond finally fails.
Common mistakeDesigning only for the worst-case peak temperature and ignoring the cycling. Two designs can share the same peak temperature yet have very different lifetimes if one swings through a far larger hot-to-cold range each orbit. Fatigue is driven by the size and number of the swings, not just the maximum — which is why the simulator tracks the full per-orbit temperature history, not merely the hottest point.

That completes the physics foundation. Heat in space leaves only by radiation (E.1) governed by σT⁴ (E.2); conduction moves it around inside, strongly downward and weakly sideways (E.3); the solar cell converts ~28% of absorbed sun to electricity and the rest to heat (E.4, E.5); a failed cell forced into reverse bias becomes a concentrated heater that threatens the honeycomb substrate (E.6, E.7); and all of it plays out against an orbit of changing sun, eclipse, albedo, IR, pointing, and relentless thermal cycling (E.8). Every number the simulator produces is some combination of these eight ideas.

Assumptions, Limitations, Validation & Roadmap

What the model assumes, where it stops, how it is checked, and what comes next.

F.1Modelling assumptions

Every simulator is a contract between fidelity and tractability. Before a single number from this tool is trusted, the reader is owed an explicit statement of what the model takes for granted. The assumptions below are not apologies; they are the load-bearing walls of the formulation. Each one was chosen deliberately, each one is defensible in the operating regime the simulator targets — a satellite solar array radiating to deep space — and each one has a clearly understood failure mode if pushed outside that regime. We list them in the order they enter the physics.

Key ideaThe model is a steady-state, two-node-per-cell, radiation-plus-conduction energy balance solved by Newton's method on a per-tile temperature vector. Everything below is a consequence of, or a qualifier on, that single sentence.

F.1.1 Steady state — no time term (yet)

The governing equation solved at every tile is an algebraic energy balance, not a differential one in time. We assert that the net heat flow into each node is zero: absorbed solar and albedo flux, plus electrical dissipation, plus lateral conduction from neighbours, exactly balances radiative emission to space. There is no ρ·c·V·dT/dt term. Physically this means the model reports the temperature the array would settle to if the current illumination and electrical load were held fixed forever and the thermal mass had finished responding.

This is the correct first question to answer for a power-budget and survival analysis: full-sun equilibrium and worst-case hot-spot equilibrium are steady-state quantities. It is the wrong tool for two questions that matter later — how fast a shadowed cell heats up after a string fault, and how cold the array drifts during an eclipse pass. Those are transient questions and are explicitly deferred to the roadmap (F.5, item #3).

Previous
A transient model would carry a heat-capacity term per node and march in time, requiring an initial condition, a time step, and a stability criterion — far more machinery, and far more places to be wrong, than the equilibrium question needs.
Now
A pure algebraic balance: one nonlinear root-find per scenario, no time axis, no stability worries. The answer is the fixed point, reached directly.
Flaw: Cannot say how long a hot spot takes to develop or an eclipse takes to cool — only the endpoints.
Benefit: The questions that gate the design (peak survival temperature, equilibrium power) are answered exactly and cheaply, with no integrator to tune.

F.1.2 Two thermal nodes per cell — front and rear

Each cell is represented by exactly two temperatures: a front (illuminated, cell/coverglass) node and a rear (substrate/backsheet) node, coupled by a through-thickness conductance. We do not resolve any temperature variation within the front node or within the rear node — no through-thickness gradient finer than that single front-to-rear link, and no in-plane variation inside one tile. A cell is, to the model, two isothermal lumps stacked with a resistor between them.

This is the classic lumped-capacitance idealisation applied across the stack. It is justified because the in-plane Biot number of a single cell is small: the cell is thin, its lateral dimension modest, and the dominant resistances are the front-to-rear stack and the radiation boundary, not gradients across a few centimetres of silicon. Resolving more than two nodes per cell buys precision the boundary conditions do not deserve, and multiplies the unknown count for no decision-relevant gain.

DeeperTwo nodes is the minimum that still distinguishes the optically/electrically active front face (which absorbs sun and dissipates I²R-like losses) from the rear face (which sees the spacecraft body or a different view to space). Collapsing to one node would erase the front-rear ΔT that drives the through-thickness flux and would misreport rear-side radiator effectiveness. Three or more nodes would resolve gradients the boundary data cannot constrain.

F.1.3 Radiation and conduction only — no convection

The only heat-transfer modes in the model are radiative exchange (Stefan–Boltzmann emission to a cold sink, with supplied emissivities and view factors) and solid conduction (front-to-rear through the stack, and tile-to-tile laterally). There is no convective term. In vacuum this is not an approximation — it is exactly correct. There is no fluid to carry heat, so omitting convection introduces zero error in the intended operating environment.

PitfallThis same assumption makes the model invalid for ground/ambient testing in air. A thermal-vacuum chamber reproduces the modelled physics; a benchtop test in atmosphere does not, because natural and forced convection then become first-order. Do not validate this simulator against an in-air measurement and conclude it is "running hot" — it is correctly modelling vacuum, and the air test is the outlier.

F.1.4 Uniform per-tile properties

Within a single tile, every material and optical property is treated as a single scalar: one absorptivity, one emissivity, one through-thickness conductance, one area. Properties may differ between tiles (that is the point of a tile grid), but they are constant across any one tile. This keeps each tile's contribution to the energy balance a closed-form function of its two node temperatures and its neighbours, which is what makes the Jacobian sparse and analytically writable.

F.1.5 Lateral conduction as a single scalar g_lat

Heat sharing between adjacent tiles is modelled by one lateral conductance, g_lat, applied uniformly: front-to-front between neighbouring front nodes, and rear-to-rear between neighbouring rear nodes, with the same scalar value. There is no per-link conductance, no directional anisotropy (the array is treated as laterally isotropic), and no distinction between, say, a bus-bar-rich direction and a substrate-only direction.

This single scalar is the most consequential simplification in the model, and it is honest to flag it as such. It is exactly right when the interconnect and substrate are laterally homogeneous, and it is the term that most directly governs hot-spot spreading (see F.4 — raising lateral coupling drops a reverse-biased cell from 187 °C to roughly 87 °C). Per-link and anisotropic conductance is a planned generalisation (F.5).

DeeperCrucially, g_lat = 0 is a supported, exercised case: with lateral conduction switched off, every tile decouples and the system reduces to independent per-cell balances. This is not a degenerate edge case bolted on — it is a strict subset of the general solver, and reproducing the independent solver exactly in this limit is one of the validation oracles (F.4).

F.1.6 Electrical dissipation P_elec — solved or injected

The electrical power term entering each tile's front-node balance is either (a) computed from a circuit solve that maps the array's voltage/current state to per-cell dissipation, or (b) injected directly as a known per-tile power. The thermal solver does not care which: it consumes P_elec as a boundary input. This clean interface is what lets the electrical and thermal halves be developed, tested, and validated independently before being coupled (the coupling itself is exercised — V·I → P_elec drives a temperature change, see F.4).

F.1.7 Tilt as a cosine; view factors as supplied scalars

The incidence of solar flux on a tile is scaled by the cosine of the tilt angle between the panel normal and the sun vector — a Lambert-cosine projection of the collecting area, with no further angular dependence of absorptivity. Radiative view factors (tile-to-space, tile-to-body) are taken as supplied scalar constants; the model does not compute geometric view factors from a CAD mesh, nor does it update them as the spacecraft attitude changes. Whoever supplies the scenario is responsible for view factors consistent with the geometry being studied.

F.2Numerical assumptions

Stating the physics is only half the contract. The other half is: does the solver actually find the root reliably? A nonlinear energy balance can in principle have multiple solutions, no solution, or a region where the iteration diverges. This section explains why, for the physics at hand, Newton's method is not merely convenient but provably well-behaved, and what guards are in place for the one pathological case that exists.

F.2.1 Why Newton converges — the imbalance is a reliable compass

The solver seeds every tile temperature at a sensible 28 °C and applies Newton iteration on the energy-imbalance residual. The reason this is robust is structural, not lucky. The radiative term scales as T⁴ and the conductive terms scale as T; the net energy-loss curve as a function of node temperature is therefore smooth and monotone over the entire physical range. Monotonicity is the whole game: it means the sign of the residual unambiguously tells the solver which direction to step. The imbalance is a reliable compass — if a node is emitting less than it absorbs, the residual says "hotter", and it is never lying because the curve never turns around.

Key ideaBecause emission (∝T⁴) plus conduction (∝T) is strictly increasing in temperature, the energy-balance residual is monotone, so the root is unique and Newton steps always point the right way. Smoothness gives a well-defined Jacobian everywhere physical; monotonicity gives a unique root and global sense of direction.

F.2.2 The one flat spot — and the guard

There is exactly one place the bare method's derivative vanishes: at absolute zero, where both dT⁴/dT and the conduction slope go to zero, the residual has a flat spot and a naive Newton step has no gradient to ride. This is a mathematical corner case, not a physical operating point — no array node sits at 0 K — but a solver must not silently misbehave there. The code therefore guards this degenerate flat spot explicitly rather than trusting the bare iteration to stumble through it.

PitfallThe flat spot exists only at T = 0 K. Starting from the 28 °C (~301 K) seed, the iteration is nowhere near it, but a careless initial guess of exactly 0 K (or a numerical underflow toward it) would land on the one point where the bare method has no slope to follow. The guard catches this so the solver degrades gracefully instead of dividing by a vanishing derivative.

F.2.3 Tolerances

Convergence is declared when the energy-balance residual falls below a tight absolute tolerance, with an iteration cap as a backstop. Because the residual is monotone and smooth, Newton's quadratic convergence kicks in close to the root and the tolerance is reached in a handful of iterations from the 28 °C seed — there is no slow crawl, because there is no nearby competing root or ill-conditioned plateau to fight.

F.2.4 Sparse solve for lateral coupling

When lateral conduction is active, every tile's residual depends on its neighbours, so the Jacobian is no longer block-diagonal — but it is highly sparse, with a fixed nearest-neighbour pattern dictated by the tile grid. The linear system at each Newton step is therefore solved with sparse linear algebra rather than a dense factorisation. This is what makes large grids tractable: the cost grows with the number of nonzeros (proportional to the tile count) rather than with the cube of a dense matrix dimension.

DeeperThe sparsity pattern is constant across iterations and across scenarios for a given grid — the tiles do not move, so the neighbour graph never changes. This invariance is exploited in the vectorised constant-pattern Jacobian (F.5 item #4), which is why 1600 tiles solve in 0.046 s: the structure is built once, then only the numerical values are refreshed each step.
RecapNewton is robust here because the residual is smooth and monotone (unique root, reliable step direction); the only flat spot is at absolute zero and is explicitly guarded; lateral coupling is handled by a sparse solve whose fixed pattern is reused for speed.

F.3Limitations

An honest handbook lists what does not work as plainly as what does. None of the limitations below is a hidden defect; each is a known boundary of the current build, and each maps to a roadmap item. Read this section as "do not bill the simulator for capabilities it does not yet have."

F.3.1 Orbit propagation is a stub

Time-varying environmental fluxes — the changing solar, albedo, and planet-IR loads as the spacecraft moves along its orbit — are not auto-generated. The intended propagator (built on hapsira) is wired as an interface but raises NotImplementedError; it is a stub. Consequently, the user must supply fluxes manually for each scenario of interest. The simulator will faithfully equilibrate to whatever fluxes it is handed, but it will not yet walk an orbit and produce the flux time series itself.

PitfallBecause of the stub, there is currently no automatic eclipse-entry or beta-angle sweep. Any orbital-average or worst-case-position result must be assembled by hand from individually supplied flux points until the propagator is implemented (F.5 #3).

F.3.2 Electrical solve wired but on a mock

The electrical–thermal coupling is fully wired and tested at the interface level (a V·I product flows in as P_elec and demonstrably moves the temperatures). However, the real electrical solve currently runs against a mock. To drive genuine ngspice circuit results into the thermal model, the OCR-damaged cell.py must first be repaired so it imports cleanly (the specific repairs are catalogued in F.6). Until then, the coupling is proven in principle and in plumbing, but the device physics behind P_elec is stand-in, not the production cell model.

F.3.3 Bypass-diode clamping not yet modelled

The real-world mitigator for hot spots — the bypass diode that clamps a shadowed or failed cell's reverse voltage — is not yet in the model. At present a reverse voltage is simply imposed on a reverse-biased cell. The important consequence is the direction of the error: with no clamp, the cell absorbs more reverse-bias dissipation than a diode-protected cell would, so the predicted bare hot-spot temperature is conservative (it over-predicts the peak). That is the safe direction to err for a survival analysis, but it means the tool currently cannot answer the design question "what bypass-diode spacing is safe?" — that requires the clamp (F.5 #2).

Key ideaThe missing bypass diode makes the hot-spot prediction pessimistic, not optimistic. A real diode-protected array will run at or below the reported reverse-bias temperature, never above it. The limitation costs design insight, not safety margin.

F.3.4 Single scalar g_lat — no anisotropy, no per-link

As stated in F.1.5, lateral conduction is one global scalar. The model cannot yet represent a stiffer conduction path in one in-plane direction than another, nor assign a different conductance to an individual tile-to-tile link (for example, a degraded solder joint). For arrays where the interconnect is strongly directional, this will under- or over-spread heat depending on which way the real conduction favours. Per-link and anisotropic g_lat is a planned generalisation.

F.3.5 Efficiency-vs-temperature is a hook, not a model

Cell conversion efficiency falls as the cell heats — a coupling that feeds back into P_elec and hence into the thermal balance. The code provides a hook for a temperature-dependent efficiency but does not yet populate it with a real characteristic. At present efficiency is effectively temperature-flat at the point where it would otherwise be derated. This is a second-order feedback for survival temperatures but matters for accurate power-budget numbers at high temperature.

F.4Validation

A model that cannot be checked is a model that cannot be trusted. The simulator is backed by 33 passing tests, and — more importantly than the count — by a set of oracles: independent references against which the solver's output must agree, sometimes approximately and in one decisive case exactly. This section walks the oracles, then lays out the ladder of checks required to go live with the real electrical solve.

F.4.1 The thermal oracle

For a baseline illuminated case the solver returns front/rear equilibrium temperatures of 65.26 °C and 64.60 °C. These are checked against an independent hand-/reference calculation of the same energy balance. The sub-degree front-to-rear split is itself a sanity signal: it confirms the through-thickness link is active and that the front node — carrying absorbed sun and dissipation — sits slightly hotter than the rear, exactly as the two-node topology demands.

F.4.2 Reverse-bias hot spot

With a cell driven into reverse bias and no lateral relief, the solver reports a hot-spot temperature of 187.48 °C. This is the conservative, un-clamped, un-spread worst case (see F.3.3). It is the number that motivates both the bypass-diode work and the lateral-spreading study below.

F.4.3 The exact-superset oracle: g_lat = 0

This is the strongest single piece of evidence in the validation suite. When lateral conduction is switched off (g_lat = 0), the coupled solver must decouple into independent per-cell balances — and it reproduces an independent reference solver EXACTLY, not approximately. That word matters: it proves the general lateral solver is a strict superset of the trusted independent solver. Any case the simple solver handles, the general one handles identically, with the lateral machinery contributing precisely zero when it should. There is no regression hiding in the coupling.

Key ideaStrict superset. g_lat=0 makes the complex solver collapse onto the simple one bit-for-bit. This is the gold standard of refactoring/extension validation: the new capability provably costs nothing in the limit where it should be invisible.

F.4.4 Lateral spreading: 187 → ~87 °C

Turning lateral conduction on for the reverse-bias hot-spot case drops the peak from 187 °C to roughly 87 °C. This is not just a number that moved — it is a physically correct, directionally and magnitudinally sensible result: a 100 °C reduction shows neighbouring tiles soaking up and re-radiating the concentrated dissipation. It validates the lateral coupling does what conduction must do (share heat, lower peaks) and quantifies how much hot-spot relief the spreading provides.

F.4.5 Breakdown — both criteria

The breakdown / failure detection is tested against both of its triggering criteria, confirming that each independently fires when it should. This guards against the common defect where a compound condition passes its test because one branch happens to dominate; here each criterion is exercised on its own.

F.4.6 Monte-Carlo statistics

The Monte-Carlo failure sweep is checked on its statistical machinery: a standard_error of 1.0 and a corresponding runs_needed of 1600. This validates the auto-sizing logic — the routine correctly translates a target precision into the number of trials required, which is the foundation for the planned auto-stop sweep (F.5 #5). The agreement confirms the estimator and its convergence accounting are wired correctly.

F.4.7 Electrical wiring drives coupling

Finally, the coupling itself is exercised end-to-end at the interface: a V·I product is converted to P_elec and is shown to drive the thermal solution — feeding electrical power in measurably changes the equilibrium temperatures. This proves the plumbing between the two solvers is correct even while the electrical side still runs on a mock (F.3.2).
